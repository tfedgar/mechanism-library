Human status
Widely used in humans and FDA-approved. Approved 1992 at 5 mg (Proscar) for benign prostatic hyperplasia and 1997 at 1 mg (Propecia) for male-pattern hair loss. Decades of large randomized trials (Kaufman AGA trials n=1553; PCPT prostate-cancer prevention n=18,882; PLESS BPH n=3040). Efficacy for hair maintenance and BPH is Established/human-proven. At a glance

  • Blocks ~65-70% of circulating DHT (partial, because type-1 5α-reductase is largely spared) — the mechanistic OPPOSITE of the DHT→scalp/prostate axis the library invokes
  • Slows/halts male-pattern hair loss and produces modest regrowth at the vertex over 1-2 years (maintenance only; reverses off-drug)
  • Shrinks the prostate ~20%, relieving BPH urinary symptoms; roughly halves PSA (must be doubled when screening)
  • Reduces overall prostate cancer detection but carries a debated high-grade-cancer signal, largely reframed as a detection artifact (true induction not fully excluded)
  • Small rise in serum testosterone (usually within normal range) and slight rise in estradiol; DHT rebounds within days of stopping
  • May lower some 5α-reduced neurosteroids (e.g., allopregnanolone) — a plausible but unproven, largely rodent-anchored basis for sexual/mood side effects and the proposed post-finasteride syndrome
  • Common on-drug adverse effects: decreased libido, erectile/ejaculatory dysfunction, gynecomastia (usually reversible for most men)
  • Category-X teratogen risk: can feminize a male fetus, so pregnant partners must not handle crushed/broken tablets

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps6 human-evidenced 2 speculative

  1. DHT is the villain the androgen library keeps invoking Established

    Testosterone is potent, but in a handful of tissues (scalp follicles, prostate, genital skin) an enzyme called 5α-reductase upgrades it into DHT, which binds the androgen receptor more tightly and lets go more slowly. DHT is what miniaturizes genetically susceptible scalp follicles (male-pattern baldness) and drives prostate overgrowth. Finasteride's whole job is to shut off that upgrade. PMID 9777765; PMID 32202088

  2. Finasteride jams the enzyme almost permanently Established

    It isn't a simple reversible blocker. Finasteride is a fake substrate: the enzyme grabs it, tries to process it, and gets locked into a dead-end complex (a covalent NADP-dihydrofinasteride adduct). That enzyme molecule is effectively destroyed and the body has to build a new one. This is why a single dose suppresses DHT for around 4 days even though the drug itself clears the blood in hours. PMID 8846625; PMID 32202088

  3. It hits type 2 hard and type 1 weakly — that's the whole design Established

    There are three 5α-reductase isoenzymes (SRD5A1, SRD5A2, SRD5A3). Finasteride is selective for type 2, the isoform dominant in prostate and the crucial scalp/genital compartments. Because type 1 (broadly expressed in liver, skin and central nervous system) is largely spared, whole-body DHT falls by roughly 65-70% rather than 90%+ — that partial-blockade is the key contrast with dutasteride, which blocks both type 1 and type 2. PMID 8846625; PMID 34291720; PMID 41718149

  4. Serum and scalp DHT crash; testosterone drifts up a little Established

    On a standard hair-loss dose, circulating DHT drops about 65-70% and scalp DHT falls substantially. Testosterone rises modestly (the T that isn't converted has to go somewhere) but usually stays inside the normal range. Estradiol can nudge up slightly because more T is available for aromatase. This is the mechanistic mirror-image of the DHT axis the androgen library repeatedly invokes. PMID 8846625

  5. Less DHT at the follicle = follicles stop shrinking (and some regrow) Established

    With DHT pressure lifted, susceptible follicles stop miniaturizing. Miniaturized-but-alive follicles can re-enlarge, so over 1-2 years men typically hold ground and gain a measurable count back at the vertex. It's maintenance, not a cure: stop the drug and DHT returns, and the genetic program resumes over the following months. PMID 9777765

  6. In the prostate, shrinkage — plus a PSA and cancer twist Established

    Prostate epithelium is DHT-dependent, so the gland shrinks around 20%, easing urinary obstruction in BPH. Two important knock-ons: PSA (the prostate-cancer blood marker) is roughly halved, so doctors must double a finasteride-user's PSA to interpret it. And in the prevention trial, finasteride cut overall prostate cancer detection about 25% but showed a signal of MORE high-grade tumors — later pathology work argued this was largely a detection artifact (smaller gland = easier to find high-grade disease), though true induction cannot be fully excluded. PMID 10510925; PMID 12824459; PMID 17848673

  7. The proposed second job: 5α-reductase also helps make calming brain steroids Contested

    The same enzyme family that makes DHT also performs the first (5α-reduction) step in building neurosteroids — most importantly allopregnanolone (from progesterone). Allopregnanolone is one of the brain's strongest positive modulators of the GABA-A receptor (the main 'calm-down' system). Importantly, in the brain this synthesis leans on the type 1 isoenzyme (SRD5A1) that finasteride only weakly inhibits, so the link from a type-2-selective drug to lowered calming steroids is plausible but not clean. Most of the mechanistic detail comes from rodent work; human support is limited to small cerebrospinal-fluid studies. Treat this as a biologically plausible — not proven — bridge between a hair drug and mood/anxiety/sexual complaints. PMID 23890183; PMID 41718149

  8. Post-finasteride syndrome: real signal, contested cause, unclear frequency Contested

    A subgroup of men report sexual, cognitive and mood symptoms that persist for months-to-years AFTER stopping the drug. Small studies found altered neurosteroid levels in the cerebrospinal fluid and even SRD5A2 gene-methylation changes in these patients, and the FDA has added persistent sexual dysfunction and suicidal ideation to the label. But there is no controlled proof of causation, no diagnostic test, and it is unknown how common it is or how much is nocebo/susceptibility. Take it seriously; don't overclaim it. PMID 28408350; PMID 31272082; PMID 39953145

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Competitive, mechanism-based (near-irreversible) inhibitor of 5α-reductase type 2 (SRD5A2). Acts as an alternate substrate: the enzyme reduces finasteride and becomes trapped in a stable bisubstrate analog — a covalent NADP–dihydrofinasteride adduct — that inactivates that enzyme molecule. Very high affinity for type 2 and markedly weaker activity against type 1; not a meaningful type-3 inhibitor. This 'suicide-substrate' behavior explains why DHT suppression outlasts the drug's plasma presence.

Signalling cascade. Finasteride → traps SRD5A2 → blocks testosterone→DHT conversion → ↓DHT in serum, scalp, prostate, genital skin (~65-70% systemic) → reduced androgen-receptor activation in DHT-dependent tissues → follicle miniaturization halts / prostate epithelium regresses. Parallel proposed branch: 5α-reduction is also the first step in making allopregnanolone (from progesterone) and other 5α-reduced neurosteroids, so inhibition may lower these GABA-A-potentiating steroids in the CNS — though this step is largely type-1-driven, which finasteride spares, so the CNS effect is uncertain.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral
Absorption / bioavailability
Well absorbed; oral bioavailability ~65%. Food slows the rate of absorption but does not change total bioavailability.
Half-life
Terminal elimination half-life roughly 5-8 h in adult men (somewhat longer in the elderly, but no dose change advised). Pharmacodynamic effect greatly outlasts this: a single dose suppresses serum DHT for up to about 4 days due to the near-irreversible enzyme adduct.
Metabolism
Extensive hepatic metabolism, predominantly via CYP3A4, to two major and essentially inactive metabolites.
Excretion
Metabolites eliminated in both bile/feces and urine. No dose adjustment needed for renal impairment (compensatory increase in fecal excretion); effect of hepatic impairment not well characterized.
Notes
Dose–DHT relationship is flat above a threshold: very low doses suppress DHT poorly, while doses above the approved range add little further systemic DHT suppression. Serum testosterone rises modestly (usually within normal range); PSA falls ~50%. Slow accumulation with repeated dosing. No clinically significant drug interactions reported. (No specific dosing is provided, by design.)

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

A defining feature is the SHALLOW, saturable dose–response for DHT: below a low threshold, DHT suppression is weak; the curve then rises and plateaus, so the systemic DHT reduction (~65-70%) is broadly similar across the approved hair-loss and BPH strengths — the lower hair-loss strength was chosen because higher amounts add prostate/urinary and PSA effects without much extra scalp benefit. This plateau is why 'more is not more' with finasteride, and why the meaningful escalation step in the field is switching agents (dutasteride, which blocks type 1 AND type 2 and drops DHT >90%) rather than raising finasteride. Onset: DHT falls within about 24 hours; clinical hair/BPH benefit accrues over months and is maximal by roughly 1-2 years. Offset: DHT rebounds within days of stopping, and hair/prostate benefits are lost over the following months. (No specific human doses are provided, by design.)

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Dutasteride
    same: Both are 5α-reductase inhibitors used for BPH and (dutasteride off-label) hair loss; both lower DHT, halve PSA, and share the same class side-effect profile and PFS concern.
    different: Dutasteride blocks BOTH type 1 and type 2 isoenzymes → drops systemic DHT >90% (vs ~65-70% for finasteride) and has a much longer half-life (weeks vs hours). More potent for hair/BPH by meta-analysis, but longer washout and, by the same logic, a potentially bigger neurosteroid footprint.
  • vs Minoxidil
    same: Both are first-line for male-pattern hair loss and are often combined.
    different: Completely different mechanism — minoxidil is a topical/oral vasodilator/K-ATP channel opener that prolongs the follicle growth phase; it does NOT touch DHT or 5α-reductase, so it carries none of the androgen/neurosteroid side-effect biology.
  • vs Spironolactone (as an anti-androgen)
    same: Both reduce androgen signaling and are used in androgen-driven skin/hair conditions.
    different: Spironolactone is an androgen-receptor antagonist (blocks DHT/T at the receptor) plus aldosterone antagonist — it doesn't lower DHT synthesis; used mainly in women (feminizing, so not used for male-pattern loss in men the way finasteride is).
  • vs Exogenous androgens / the DHT axis the library invokes
    same: Operates on exactly the same testosterone→DHT→androgen-receptor pathway the PED/androgen library repeatedly references.
    different: It is the ancillary that OPPOSES that axis at the 5α-reductase step — the mechanistic counterweight, not an androgen. It cannot 'boost' anabolism; the small testosterone rise is a byproduct of blocked conversion, not an anabolic effect.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Anastrozole counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    These act on parallel, opposing arms of testosterone metabolism. By blocking the T→DHT drain, finasteride leaves slightly more testosterone available to aromatase, modestly raising estradiol (a documented driver of finasteride-associated gynecomastia/breast tenderness). Anastrozole inhibits aromatase (CYP19A1) and lowers estradiol, directly counteracting that finasteride-driven E2 rise. They do not touch the same enzyme, but they pull the T:E2 balance in opposite directions. finasteride side_effect_mechanisms (gynecomastia via raised E2); PMID 22879203 (finasteride raises E2/E1 in humans); anastrozole monograph (aromatase inhibition lowers E2) Watch: Watch estradiol and breast tissue: finasteride can nudge E2 up while an aromatase inhibitor pulls it down; over-suppressing E2 brings its own harms (joint pain, low libido, adverse bone/lipid effects). Interpret each lab in context of both drugs.
  • Human chorionic gonadotropin opposes Established
    hCG raises not only testosterone and estradiol but also dihydrotestosterone (DHT), because some of the extra Leydig-cell testosterone is 5-alpha-reduced (human hCG-stimulation studies show plasma DHT rising alongside T and E2). Finasteride blocks 5-alpha-reductase type 2, cutting circulating DHT by roughly 65-70%, so it opposes the DHT component of hCG's output — blunting androgenic scalp/prostate/skin effects. It does NOT reduce the testosterone or estradiol that hCG raises. PMID 1264608 and PMID 2943108 (hCG raises DHT alongside T and E2 in men); finasteride monograph (SRD5A2 inhibition, ~65-70% DHT reduction) Watch: DHT and androgenic symptoms (scalp shedding, acne, prostate/urinary changes); note estradiol and testosterone are unaffected by finasteride, so hCG's estrogen/gynecomastia risk persists and must be tracked separately.
  • Nandrolone opposes Contested
    Inverse case that reverses finasteride's usual role. Unlike testosterone, nandrolone's 5α-reduced metabolite (dihydronandrolone) is WEAKER at the androgen receptor than the parent, so 5α-reductase normally DETOXIFIES nandrolone in high-5-AR tissues like scalp follicles. Finasteride blocks that conversion, removing a protective step rather than a harmful one, leaving more intact parent nandrolone to act at the follicle. This is the mechanistic basis for the claim that a 5-AR inhibitor does not protect (and may theoretically worsen) nandrolone-associated hair loss, the OPPOSITE of its role with testosterone. Finasteride also does nothing for nandrolone's separate progestogenic/prolactin activity. nandrolone monograph mechanism_step 4 + pharmacokinetics notes (5-ARIs do NOT reduce and may slightly potentiate nandrolone's androgenic action; PMID 4021486, 19861292 Established for the weaker-metabolite basis); the specific 'worsens hair loss' clinical claim is anecdote-level (MESO-Rx thread 134315052) with no human RCT Watch: Do not assume finasteride protects the hairline on a nandrolone-containing stack the way it does with testosterone, the metabolite logic is reversed. Watch scalp/hairline changes; note finasteride does nothing for nandrolone's progestogenic/prolactin effects (e.g. nipple changes, low libido/erectile dysfunction), which involve a different pathway.
  • Boldenone undecylenate shares target Speculative
    Boldenone is a 5α-reductase substrate: its reductive metabolite is 1-testosterone (dihydroboldenone/DHB), a MORE potent androgen. Finasteride sits on the same enzyme node and would partially blunt the type-2 fraction of that conversion, lowering local production of the stronger metabolite, directionally analogous to how it lowers DHT from testosterone. This is inference from shared substrate biology only: boldenone's human metabolism actually leans on 5β-reduction, and there is no direct finasteride+boldenone data. Boldenone's separate aromatization to estradiol and its strong hematocrit-raising effect are pathways finasteride does not touch. boldenone monograph mechanism_step 7 + pharmacokinetics (5α/5-ene reduction to potent DHB; 5β-reductase predominates in humans); finasteride 5-AR substrate logic, monograph mechanism_steps 1-3, no direct combination data Watch: Even if finasteride blunts the 5α-reduced fraction, it does not affect boldenone's androgen-receptor drive or its aromatization/hematocrit effects. Watch hematocrit and estradiol independently, since finasteride offers no protection there.
  • Testosterone shares target Established
    Direct enzyme-substrate relationship at the same node. Testosterone is the substrate that 5α-reductase type 2 converts into DHT; finasteride forms a near-irreversible dead-end complex with that enzyme, so exogenous testosterone can no longer be efficiently converted to DHT in scalp, prostate and genital skin. Serum/scalp DHT falls ~65-70% while the unconverted testosterone drifts slightly higher, and because more T is available to aromatase, estradiol (and estrone) nudge up. Finasteride is thus a mechanistic counterweight sitting one step downstream of testosterone on the same pathway: it blunts DHT-driven hair loss and prostate growth from TRT but does NOT touch testosterone's own androgen-receptor effects (muscle anabolism, hematocrit). finasteride monograph mechanism_steps 1-4; PMID 8846625; PMID 22879203 (finasteride raises T, E1, E2) Watch: Distinguish DHT-mediated signs (scalp/prostate) from systemic androgen effects finasteride does NOT block (hematocrit, libido from T itself). Watch estradiol and breast tissue, since the small E2 rise can add to testosterone's own aromatization. On finasteride, know that PSA is roughly halved and must be interpreted accordingly for prostate-cancer screening.
  • DHB redundant Established
    Finasteride works by blocking 5-alpha-reductase (SRD5A2), the enzyme that converts testosterone to the stronger DHT, to reduce DHT-driven scalp and prostate effects of 5AR-substrate androgens. DHB is already 5-alpha-reduced (it is dihydroboldenone) and is NOT a 5-alpha-reductase substrate, so finasteride has no enzyme step to block in DHB's pathway. Adding finasteride is therefore a pointless/redundant intervention here - it does NOT blunt DHB's androgenic hair-loss, acne, or prostate effects, a common but mechanistically mistaken expectation. (The same limitation applies to dutasteride.) Finasteride monograph (SRD5A2 inhibitor, T to DHT); DHB is 5-alpha-reduced / not a 5AR substrate (DHB monograph pharmacokinetics notes + scalp side_effect_mechanisms; Friedel 2006 PMID 16621347) Watch: Do not rely on finasteride/dutasteride to protect the scalp or prostate while on DHB; track hairline, acne, and prostate/urinary symptoms directly, since 5AR inhibitors will not modify DHB's action.
  • Dianabol redundant Evidence-human
    Finasteride blocks 5a-reductase (SRD5A2), the enzyme that converts testosterone to DHT - how it protects hair and prostate on testosterone. Against Dianabol this lever is largely a non-event: methandienone is a delta-1 17a-methyl-boldenone derivative whose delta-1 A-ring makes it a poor 5a-reductase substrate, so its androgenic action is not meaningfully DHT-dependent. Finasteride therefore does little to blunt Dbol's scalp/prostate/acne androgenicity - an important negative interaction to teach (a 5AR inhibitor that works against testosterone is redundant/ineffective against this compound) - and it does nothing for Dbol's real drivers (17aa hepatotoxicity, HDL crash, estrogen). Dbol monograph pharmacokinetics/comparisons notes (delta-1 double bond reduces 5a-reduction; methandienone poor 5AR substrate, muscle effect not DHT-dependent); finasteride.json primary_target (SRD5A2, T->DHT, mechanism-based inhibitor) Watch: Do not rely on finasteride to control Dbol-driven acne or hair changes; track those clinically and address Dbol's actual mechanisms (liver enzymes/bilirubin, lipid panel, estradiol/SHBG) with the appropriate bloodwork rather than a 5AR inhibitor.
  • Dutasteride redundant Established
    Both drugs act on the identical target class, 5α-reductase, and produce the same downstream effect (DHT suppression, halved PSA, shared class side-effect and post-5ARI concern). Finasteride selectively traps type 2 (SRD5A2) and drops systemic DHT ~65-70%; dutasteride traps BOTH type 1 and type 2 and drops DHT ~90-95% with a half-life of weeks rather than hours. Because dutasteride's blockade fully encompasses finasteride's, combining them adds essentially no extra DHT suppression, dutasteride simply supersedes finasteride at the enzyme. dutasteride monograph mechanism_steps 3-4 and comparisons (vs Finasteride); PMID 15126539 Watch: There is no additive DHT benefit to combining two 5-AR inhibitors; the more complete blockade (dutasteride) carries a much longer washout and, by the same neurosteroid logic, a potentially larger CNS footprint plus a dutasteride-specific liver-fat/insulin-resistance signal. Track mood, libido and sexual function as class effects; note PSA is halved by either drug.
  • Halotestin redundant Established
    Finasteride blocks 5-alpha-reductase (T->DHT), the strategy used to blunt DHT-mediated hair loss/prostate effects from androgens that depend on 5a-reduction. Fluoxymesterone is not a 5-alpha-reductase substrate — its 9a-fluoro / 11b-hydroxy / 17a-methyl structure blocks 5a-reduction, so it acts directly at the androgen receptor without needing conversion to a more potent DHT species. Finasteride therefore cannot lower or soften Halotestin's androgenic signal; adding it to 'tame' Halotestin is mechanistically redundant. Finasteride only affects the 5a-reducible components of a stack (e.g. testosterone). Halotestin monograph expand.pharmacology (NOT a 5a-reductase substrate; 9a-fluoro/11b-OH/17a-methyl blocks 5a-reduction); finasteride monograph (SRD5A2-selective, blocks T->DHT) Watch: No Halotestin-specific benefit to expect from 5-AR inhibition; if androgenic scalp/skin effects appear, recognize they are direct-AR (not DHT-conversion) driven. Track scalp/hairline and skin as AR-mediated signs.
  • Masteron redundant Established
    Finasteride blocks 5-alpha-reductase type 2 to stop testosterone becoming DHT, reducing DHT-driven hair loss and prostate stimulation. Masteron IS ALREADY a 5-alpha-reduced steroid (2a-methyl-DHT), so it is not a substrate for the enzyme; its androgenic scalp/prostate/skin action is delivered directly to the androgen receptor. Finasteride therefore does NOT blunt Masteron's hair loss, acne or prostate effects - a widely misunderstood point and a key harm-reduction correction. Finasteride still lowers DHT made from any co-administered testosterone, but it offers no protection against Masteron itself. History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Developed by Merck as a 4-azasteroid 5α-reductase inhibitor. FDA-approved 1992 as Proscar (5 mg) for symptomatic BPH, exploiting DHT-driven prostate growth. In 1997 approved as Propecia (1 mg) for male-pattern hair loss after dose–response work showed the 1 mg strength captured most scalp benefit. The 2003 PCPT (NEJM) both validated chemoprevention (fewer cancers detected overall) and ignited the high-grade-cancer controversy, later softened by the 2007 pathology re-analysis. From around 2011 onward, patient advocacy drove label changes: the FDA added reports of persistent erectile dysfunction, and later depression and suicidal ideation, to the labeling; the NIH added a PFS entry to its rare-disease information resource. It remains one of the most-prescribed dermatologic/urologic drugs worldwide, and has been monitored in some sports contexts historically because it can mask steroid metabolites.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Is post-finasteride syndrome a genuine causal entity, and if so how common — no controlled trial, no diagnostic test, no established denominator.
  • Who is susceptible? Whether SRD5A2 methylation/genetic variants or baseline neurosteroid biology predict persistent effects is only pilot-level.
  • Whether the observed CSF neurosteroid changes are cause, consequence, or coincidence of the symptoms.
  • The true magnitude (if any) of depression/suicidality risk — pharmacovigilance and cohort data conflict.
  • Whether long-term type-2 blockade meaningfully raises insulin resistance / fatty-liver / metabolic disease in humans (largely hypothesis- and rodent-anchored).
  • Whether the high-grade prostate cancer signal reflects any true induction beyond the detection-artifact explanation.
  • How much of reported symptomatology is nocebo/expectancy versus pharmacology — essentially unquantified.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Educational monitoring points only — no dosing, no protocol. (1) PSA: know that finasteride roughly HALVES PSA; any interpretation for prostate-cancer screening should double the measured value, and a rising PSA on finasteride is a red flag warranting urologic evaluation. (2) Mood/mental health: because the neurosteroid pathway may be affected, watch for new or worsening depression, anxiety, or suicidal thoughts — these are labeled warnings; new suicidal ideation is an emergency and warrants stopping and seeking help. (3) Sexual function: track libido, erectile and ejaculatory function as a symptom baseline; most resolves off-drug but persistence is the PFS concern to discuss with a clinician. (4) Gynecomastia/breast changes: any new breast lump, tenderness, or discharge should be evaluated (rare male breast cancer reports exist). (5) Pregnancy safety, not lab monitoring but critical: pregnant or potentially-pregnant partners must not handle crushed/broken tablets (fetal male-genital teratogen). (6) There is NO routine 'DHT level' monitoring that guides therapy, and no blood test confirms or excludes post-finasteride syndrome — framing any such test as diagnostic would be misleading.

Caveats

This monograph is mechanism education for an accuracy-first library, NOT medical advice and NOT a protocol — it contains no dosing and none should be inferred. Key honesty flags: (1) The high-grade prostate cancer finding from PCPT is real in the raw data but the leading interpretation is a detection artifact (smaller prostates are easier to biopsy accurately) — true induction cannot be fully ruled out, and this nuance is routinely mis-stated in both directions. (2) Post-finasteride syndrome is where evidence is weakest: the persistence reports are real and the neurosteroid biology is plausible, but the human data are small, uncontrolled, and cannot establish causation or frequency; nocebo and selection effects are unquantified. Do not present it as proven, and do not dismiss it as fake. (3) The 'calming neurosteroid' and depression-mechanism story is anchored mainly in rodent work plus small human CSF studies, and the key CNS neurosteroid (allopregnanolone) is made largely by the type-1 isoenzyme that finasteride only weakly inhibits — flagged Contested accordingly. (4) Large pharmacoepidemiology datasets disagree on whether finasteride raises depression/suicide risk; report the conflict, not one side. (5) Testosterone rising into the normal range is a group-average finding — it does not license any 'it boosts test' framing.

DHT is the villain the androgen library keeps invoking. Testosterone is potent, but in a handful of tissues (scalp follicles, prostate, genital skin) an enzyme called 5α-reductase upgrades it into DHT, which binds the androgen receptor more tightly and lets go more slowly. DHT is what miniaturizes genetically susceptible scalp follicles (male-pattern baldness) and drives prostate overgrowth. Finasteride's whole job is to shut off that upgrade.
