Follistatin

The body's own "brake-release" for muscle: follistatin mops up myostatin and activin before they can tell muscle to stop growing.

Rodent / preclinical 20 citations
Class
Endogenous secreted glycoprotein; myostatin/activin ligand trap (TGF-β superfamily antagonist)
Primary target
Circulating TGF-β superfamily ligands — chiefly myostatin (GDF8) and activin A (also GDF11, some activins/BMPs)
Human status
PRECLINICAL-DOMINANT with only small, early human gene-therapy data. The strongest human evidence is a phase 1/2a trial of AAV1.CMV.FS344 (follistatin gene) injected directly into the quadriceps of Becker muscular dystrophy and sporadic inclusion-body myositis patients: it was well tolerated, some patients improved on the 6-minute walk test (e.g., +58 m, +125 m, +108 m in responders) and biopsies showed muscle hypertrophy, reduced fibrosis and more normal fiber size — but N was tiny (6 BMD subjects in the report; ~3 in the DMD arm), uncontrolled, with non-responders, so this is proof-of-concept, not proof of efficacy (PMID 25322757; PMID 22609847). Newer human work is mostly small/early-phase or unproven: ClinicalTrials.gov lists Minicircle's follistatin plasmid trials in healthy/frail/aging adults (NCT06411366 completed Phase 1; NCT07285629 follistatin+klotho, EARLY_PHASE1) and an AAV9-follistatin + VEGF combo for age-related muscle decline (NCT07443826, Phase 1/2, recruiting, not yet started). NO large randomized controlled trial in healthy people for muscle/performance exists. Crucially: injectable 'follistatin peptide' sold in the fitness/research-peptide market is NOT the gene therapy studied above, has essentially no published human efficacy/safety data, and forum claims of muscle gains are anecdote, not evidence.

At a glance

  • Increases skeletal muscle mass and fiber size by removing the myostatin/activin brake (strong in mice; small/early human gene-therapy signal)
  • Improves muscle regeneration and reduces fibrosis after injury or in dystrophic muscle (rodent + human muscle biopsy in BMD trial)
  • Supports muscle mitochondrial function and fat oxidation (rodent)
  • Potentially preserves muscle in wasting conditions: muscular dystrophy, sarcopenia, cachexia (target rationale; human data thin)
  • Broad TGF-β blockade also affects bone and vascular (BMP9/10) signaling — a double-edged effect, not purely beneficial

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Myostatin is muscle's built-in growth limiter Established

    Your muscles constantly secrete a protein called myostatin (also named GDF8), a member of the TGF-beta family. Its whole job is to put a ceiling on muscle size. When it's knocked out by mutation in animals (and rare humans), muscle mass increases dramatically; when it's overexpressed, muscle wastes away. So the system follistatin acts on is a genuine, well-mapped 'brake' on growth. PMID 32911580 (J Bone Metab review); PMID 42123420 (Int J Mol Sci review)

  2. Myostatin/activin switch on a receptor that signals 'stop growing' Established

    Myostatin (and the related ligand activin A) dock onto a cell-surface receptor called ActRIIB on the muscle fiber. That recruits a partner receptor (the type-I kinases ALK4/ALK5), forming a signaling complex. This is the molecular 'doorbell' that, once rung, tells the muscle cell to hold back. PMID 38136649 (Biomolecules, 'Myostatin and the Heart'); PMID 31285507 (Sci Rep, ActRIIB-ALK4/5)

  3. The signal travels inside via SMAD2/3 to suppress muscle building and trigger breakdown Established

    Once ActRIIB/ALK4-5 fires, it phosphorylates the intracellular messengers SMAD2 and SMAD3. Activated SMAD2/3 go to the nucleus and (a) block the muscle-building program (myogenic differentiation) and (b) turn up atrophy/breakdown genes. Knocking down SMAD2/3 in muscle cells does the opposite — it promotes muscle differentiation — which confirms this is the pathway that produces the atrophic response. PMID 40735858 (Laryngoscope, SMAD2/3 mediates atrophy; siRNA knockdown promotes myogenesis); PMID 31285507

  4. Follistatin works as a 'ligand trap' — it grabs myostatin/activin before they reach the receptor Established

    Follistatin is a secreted protein that binds myostatin and activin A with very high affinity and essentially wraps them up, preventing them from ever touching ActRIIB. No ligand at the receptor means no SMAD2/3 signal. So follistatin doesn't push an accelerator — it removes the brake. This is why it's called a myostatin antagonist even though it never touches the receptor itself. PMID 41272451 (Mol Med, 'Follistatin is a potent regulator of...myostatin and activin A'); PMID 32911580

  5. Releasing the brake lets satellite cells and fibers grow (myogenesis + hypertrophy) Evidence-rodent

    With myostatin/activin neutralized, the muscle's stem-like cells (satellite cells / fibro-adipogenic progenitors) and existing fibers are freed to proliferate, fuse, and enlarge. In mice, deleting follistatin (the reverse experiment) re-activates the myostatin/activin-SMAD pathway and causes muscle loss, atrophic fibers, and weaker exercise performance — direct proof that follistatin's normal role is to protect and build muscle. PMID 41272451 (FAP-specific follistatin knockout mice → atrophy, reduced muscle mass and function)

  6. It also supports muscle metabolism — mitochondria and fat-burning Evidence-rodent

    Beyond raw size, the same mouse studies show follistatin helps maintain mitochondrial biogenesis, oxidative phosphorylation (energy production), and fatty-acid oxidation in muscle. Losing follistatin degraded these, suggesting it keeps muscle metabolically healthy, not just bigger. This part is rodent data and shouldn't be overstated for humans. PMID 41272451 (Mol Med, follistatin KO altered mitochondrial biogenesis, OXPHOS, fatty-acid oxidation)

  7. Because it's broad, it hits related signals too — that's the catch Established

    Follistatin is not laser-targeted to myostatin. It also neutralizes activins and some bone morphogenetic proteins (BMPs). That broad reach is partly why it's potent, but it's also the source of off-target risks (e.g., effects on bone and on vascular BMP9/10 signaling) and is exactly why drug developers often prefer more selective tools (antibodies, decoy receptors) over follistatin itself. PMID 32911580 (J Bone Metab: follistatin antagonizes several TGF-β members; bone-weakness and vascular concerns)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Follistatin (FST) is NOT a receptor ligand — it is a secreted glycoprotein LIGAND TRAP that physically encircles and neutralizes TGF-beta superfamily ligands. Two follistatin molecules wrap around one activin/myostatin dimer, burying ~1/3 of the ligand's residues and occluding BOTH the type II receptor (ActRIIB/ActRIIA) binding site AND, via the follistatin N-terminal domain, the type I receptor (ALK4/ALK5/ALK7) binding site — so it is a pure ANTAGONIST that blocks all receptor engagement, not a competitive ligand-mimic [Established; PMID 16198295, 16482217]. HIGHEST-affinity target = activin A: follistatin binds activin essentially irreversibly with subnanomolar/picomolar-range affinity (described in the literature as one of the tightest known protein-protein interactions; the minimal Fs12 fragment alone forms a stable 2:1 complex) [Established; PMID 16198295, 16482217]. It also binds myostatin (GDF-8) and GDF-11 with high affinity, and binds bone morphogenetic proteins (BMP-2/4/6/7) and other activins with lower/variable affinity — i.e. it is a relatively PROMISCUOUS trap, which is the root of several off-target concerns [Established; PMID 32911580]. Selectivity caveat: because the trap is broad, follistatin antagonizes multiple TGF-beta family members simultaneously (myostatin + activin A + GDF-11 + some BMPs), unlike a selective anti-myostatin antibody [Established; PMID 32911580, 34520530]. Distinct from follistatin-like-3 (FSTL3/FLRG), which in serum is the dominant carrier specifically of myostatin and binds with a different N-terminal-domain mode [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 22052913].

Signalling cascade. Normally, mature myostatin and activin A dimers bind the type II serine/threonine-kinase receptor ActRIIB (or ActRIIA) on the muscle-cell surface; the type II receptor then recruits and trans-phosphorylates the type I receptor ALK4 or ALK5, whose activated kinase phosphorylates the receptor-SMADs SMAD2 and SMAD3. Phospho-SMAD2/3 partner with SMAD4, translocate to the nucleus, and drive a CATABOLIC transcriptional program — up-regulating atrophy-associated ubiquitin ligases (atrogin-1/MAFbx, MuRF-1) and repressing myogenic differentiation [Established; PMID 32911580, 34520530]. Follistatin sits UPSTREAM of the receptor: by sequestering the ligand before it can reach ActRIIB, it prevents type I receptor recruitment and abolishes SMAD2/3 phosphorylation [Established; PMID 16198295]. The downstream consequence of relieving this SMAD2/3 brake is a pro-anabolic shift: de-repression and activation of the PI3K/Akt/mTOR/p70S6K (and eIF4) axis that drives ribosomal protein synthesis, increased expression of myogenic regulatory factors (MyoD, myogenin, myosin heavy chain), enhanced satellite-cell/myoblast differentiation and myotube fusion, and reduced atrogin-1/MuRF-1-mediated proteolysis — net muscle-fiber hypertrophy and hyperplasia [Evidence-rodent / Evidence-in vitro; PMID 41272451, 26059377, 17726519]. Follistatin's effect EXCEEDS pure myostatin blockade: Mstn-null mice given a follistatin transgene reach ~4x normal muscle mass vs ~2x for Mstn-null alone, proving follistatin additionally neutralizes activin/GDF-11 to amplify growth beyond myostatin's ceiling [Established (rodent); PMID 17726519]. Follistatin also modulates some non-SMAD activin-receptor signaling and supports mitochondrial biogenesis/oxidative phosphorylation and fatty-acid oxidation in muscle [Evidence-rodent; PMID 41272451].

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Native follistatin is a SECRETED PROTEIN, not an orally bioavailable small molecule — it cannot be taken as a pill (it would be digested). Therapeutic forms studied in humans are delivered by (1) AAV gene therapy = intramuscular injection of adeno-associated virus serotype 1 carrying the FS344 isoform cDNA (rAAV1.CMV.huFS344 / AAV1.CMV.FS344), which turns the injected muscle into a local follistatin factory; and (2) ACE-083, a recombinant follistatin-Fc fusion protein (follistatin FS288 domain region fused to human IgG Fc) given by direct intramuscular injection to act LOCALLY in the injected muscle [Established; PMID 25322757, 28279643, 35428982]. The 'follistatin' marketed in the grey-market peptide/PED scene as injectable powder is unregulated, not characterized in any human PK study, and not the same as either clinical agent [Speculative/Contested — no peer-reviewed PK].
Absorption / bioavailability
Not applicable in the oral sense — both clinical strategies are deliberately designed for LOCAL muscle exposure, not systemic distribution. AAV gene therapy produces follistatin in situ within the injected quadriceps/limb muscle; ACE-083 is engineered (Fc fusion + intramuscular route) specifically to stay in the injected muscle and minimize spillover into the circulation, precisely to avoid systemic activin/myostatin blockade [Established; PMID 35428982, 35545446]. Systemic bioavailability of the native protein given peripherally is poorly characterized in humans [Key gap].
Half-life
GENUINE DATA GAP for the human therapeutic forms. No peer-reviewed human serum half-life for recombinant follistatin or ACE-083 was retrievable in this search, and follistatin is not catalogued in ChEMBL (it is a protein trap, not a small-molecule with ADMET). Mechanistically, the AAV-FS344 strategy is intended to give DURABLE expression (months to years) from a single intramuscular dose because the transgene persists in post-mitotic muscle — the Becker/sIBM trials measured functional benefit out to ~1 year+ [Evidence-human, durability inferred from clinical timecourse; PMID 25322757, 28279643]. Circulating native follistatin protein is generally regarded as having a SHORT plasma residence, which is a major reason gene therapy and Fc-fusion engineering were pursued — but a precise t1/2 number should be treated as unestablished [Contested/Key gap].
Metabolism
As a glycoprotein, follistatin is cleared by proteolytic degradation/lysosomal catabolism after receptor- or heparan-sulfate-mediated cellular uptake rather than by cytochrome-P450 enzymes; no CYP metabolism is relevant [Established (general protein biology); mechanism not follistatin-specific in retrieved sources]. Isoform behavior matters: the FS288 (membrane-bound) isoform binds cell-surface heparan-sulfate proteoglycans avidly and is taken up/cleared at the cell surface, whereas FS315 (the C-terminal-extended, more acidic isoform) circulates more freely — FS344 was chosen for gene therapy partly to manage this targeting [Evidence-in vitro / mechanistic; framework from PMID 25322757, 32911580]. Quantitative isoform clearance constants were not retrievable [Key gap].
Excretion
Not directly characterized in retrieved human sources; as a degraded protein its constituent amino acids are recycled and small peptide fragments handled renally — there is no intact renal/biliary excretion pathway analogous to a small drug [inferred general protein handling; not follistatin-specific in sources].
Notes
There is no oral form, no ester/prodrug, and follistatin does NOT meaningfully cross the blood-brain barrier (large glycoprotein). The 'forms' that matter therapeutically are the ISOFORMS and engineered constructs: FS288 (heparin/cell-surface-bound, autocrine/paracrine, locally retained), FS315 (longer C-terminal acidic tail, less heparin binding, the main circulating form), and FS344 (precursor that yields FS317; used in AAV gene therapy specifically to avoid off-target cell-surface binding) [Established (isoform framework); PMID 25322757, 32911580]. ACE-083 = Fc-fusion engineered for local retention [PMID 35428982].

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human dose-response is GENUINELY SPARSE and confined to two construct types; there is no validated dose-response curve for systemic recombinant follistatin in humans. (1) AAV-FS344 gene therapy (intramuscular, ascending vector dose): Becker MD phase 1/2a used 3x10^11 vg/kg/leg (cohort 1) and 6x10^11 vg/kg/leg (cohort 2); the HIGHER dose produced clearer histology (greater muscle hypertrophy, more normalized fiber-size distribution, less fibrosis/central nucleation) and several responders gained +58 to +125 m on the 6-minute walk test, while some patients showed no change — i.e. a suggestion of dose-dependent tissue effect but inconsistent functional response, n=6 total [Evidence-human, small/uncontrolled; PMID 25322757]. (2) sIBM trial used 6x10^11 vg/kg delivered to quadriceps of both legs (n=6): treated subjects improved a median +56.0 m/year on 6MWT vs -25.8 m/year decline in matched untreated controls (n=8), with 4/6 robust responders [Evidence-human, controlled comparison but tiny n; PMID 28279643]. (3) ACE-083 (recombinant follistatin-Fc, local IM): 240 mg/muscle every 3 weeks robustly increased MRI total muscle volume — +16.4% (biceps) and +9.5% (tibialis anterior) in FSHD, and +13.5% in CMT vs placebo — a clear, reproducible STRUCTURAL dose effect, but with NO consistent improvement in strength or function in either disease [Evidence-human RCT; PMID 35428982, 35545446]. (4) Preclinical magnitude ceiling: follistatin overexpression quadruples muscle mass in Mstn-null mice (~4x) and roughly doubles-or-more in wild-type, far exceeding selective myostatin blockade — establishing that the achievable hypertrophy is large in rodents but does NOT translate to proportional human functional gain [Evidence-rodent; PMID 17726519]. Bottom line for a lay audience: more follistatin reliably makes muscles BIGGER (volume/histology), but human data repeatedly show bigger did not equal stronger or more functional — and no safe human systemic dose-response exists.

How it compares

  • vs Selective anti-myostatin monoclonal antibodies (e.g. domagrozumab, landogrozumab/LY2495655)
    same: Both aim to relieve the myostatin brake on muscle, raise SMAD2/3-repressed anabolic signaling, and increase muscle mass; both repeatedly grew muscle in trials
    different: Antibodies are SELECTIVE for myostatin (and sometimes GDF-11) and given systemically; follistatin is a BROAD trap that also neutralizes activins and BMPs, giving larger potential hypertrophy but more off-target risk. Like follistatin's ACE-083 trials, selective antibodies generally failed to convert mass gains into functional benefit
  • vs Soluble ActRIIB-Fc decoy receptors (e.g. ACE-031/ramatercept, bimagrumab is an anti-ActRII antibody analog)
    same: Both are ligand-trapping biologics that block the activin-receptor pathway upstream of SMAD2/3 and produce robust muscle hypertrophy; both are broad (hit myostatin + activins + GDF-11)
    different: ActRIIB-Fc/anti-ActRII act at the RECEPTOR (decoy or receptor antibody) whereas follistatin traps the LIGAND directly; the broad receptor decoys carried notable off-target effects (e.g. ACE-031 development was halted partly over epistaxis/telangiectasia/vascular bleeding from BMP9/10 effects) that motivated follistatin's locally-acting designs
  • vs FSTL3 / FLRG (follistatin-like 3), the endogenous relative
    same: Same follistatin-domain architecture; both trap activin and myostatin and antagonize TGF-beta family signaling; FSTL3 is the principal serum carrier of myostatin
    different: FSTL3 lacks the heparin-binding/cell-surface targeting of follistatin's FS288 and binds the ligand via a distinct N-terminal-domain mode with different affinity geometry; follistatin has broader ligand coverage (including BMPs)
  • vs Anabolic androgenic steroids / SARMs (what a PED audience compares it to)
    same: Both can increase skeletal muscle mass; both are used off-label for physique/performance
    different: AAS/SARMs work through the androgen receptor to up-regulate protein synthesis broadly and systemically (with hormonal/cardiovascular/HPTA suppression); follistatin works by removing a specific myostatin/activin growth-brake, is a protein (not orally active), acts mainly locally in studied forms, and crucially has shown big muscle VOLUME but no proven strength/function gain in humans

History & legal status

"Follistatin was originally identified as a gonadal protein that suppresses pituitary FSH secretion (hence the name) and was later recognized as a high-affinity activin- and then myostatin-binding antagonist. Se-Jin Lee's lab established follistatin overexpression as a route to dramatic muscle hypertrophy (2007 quadrupling-muscle paper), catalyzing therapeutic interest [PMID 17726519]. The leading clinical translation has been AAV1-FS344 intramuscular GENE THERAPY developed at Nationwide Children's Hospital/Ohio State (Mendell group), tested in Becker MD (2015), sporadic inclusion body myositis (2017), and Duchenne/BMD programs [PMID 25322757, 28279643, 22609847]. A parallel approach, Acceleron Pharma's ACE-083 (locally acting follistatin-Fc fusion), reached randomized phase 2 in FSHD and CMT (both 2022) [PMID 35428982, 35545446]. REGULATORY STATUS: follistatin has NO approved therapeutic product anywhere — every human use to date is investigational (clinical trials) or unapproved. ACE-083's program did not advance to approval after phase 2 grew muscle volume but failed functional endpoints. Separately, follistatin sold as an injectable 'peptide'/research chemical in the bodybuilding grey market is UNAPPROVED, unregulated, and not a characterized pharmaceutical; it is not FDA/EMA-approved for any use, and using it for performance falls outside any regulatory framework and would be prohibited under anti-doping rules (myostatin-pathway inhibitors are banned by WADA). [Established; regulatory landscape from PMID 32911580, 34520530 and trial records]"

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Why does follistatin reliably increase muscle VOLUME but not strength or function in humans? Both ACE-083 RCTs and selective myostatin agents grew muscle without functional benefit — the disconnect between hypertrophy and performance is unresolved and central to whether follistatin is ever useful (PMID 35428982, 35545446).
  • What is the actual human pharmacokinetics — plasma half-life, isoform-specific clearance (FS288 vs FS315 vs FS344), and the duration/decay of AAV-driven expression? No peer-reviewed human PK numbers were retrievable; this is a genuine gap, especially for the grey-market injectable form which has zero PK data.
  • What are the long-term endocrine/reproductive consequences of trapping both activin and myostatin given the newly described muscle-myostatin to pituitary-FSH axis? Fertility effects are flagged mechanistically in rodents but unquantified in humans (PMID 39818879).
  • How large is the real off-target/safety burden in humans from follistatin's promiscuity (BMP-9/10 vascular effects, bone weakening, activin trapping) versus the locally-retained designs meant to avoid it? Class concerns are established but follistatin-specific human incidence is not (PMID 32911580).
  • Does chronic follistatin exposure carry oncogenic risk given activin/TGF-beta's context-dependent tumor-suppressor role? Trials are too short and small to detect a signal.
  • Is there any safe, effective SYSTEMIC follistatin therapeutic, or is the locally-acting/gene-therapy route the only viable path? No systemic recombinant follistatin has a validated human dose-response.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only — NOT an endorsement or protocol. Anyone exposed to follistatin (especially unregulated injectable forms) and a clinician should watch: (1) MUSCULOSKELETAL — track whether any size gain actually comes with strength/function, since human data show volume can rise without functional benefit; watch for tendon/joint strain from rapid disproportionate hypertrophy. (2) INJECTION SITE — local pain, swelling, induration, signs of infection or abscess (the dominant adverse event in trials; grey-market injectables add sterility risk). (3) ENDOCRINE/REPRODUCTIVE — given the activin/myostatin-FSH axis, consider baseline and follow-up FSH/LH, testosterone, and fertility-relevant symptoms; menstrual changes in women. (4) VASCULAR/BLEEDING — be alert for epistaxis, telangiectasia, or unusual bleeding (BMP-9/10 off-target concern seen with related activin-pathway blockers). (5) BONE — broad TGF-beta/BMP trapping can affect bone; clinically watch for unusual fractures and consider bone-density evaluation with chronic use. (6) GENERAL SAFETY LABS — CBC, CMP (liver/renal function), and for any gene-therapy context, vector/transgene immune responses and pre-existing AAV antibodies. (7) ANTI-DOPING — for athletes, note follistatin/myostatin-pathway agents are WADA-prohibited. Red flags warranting immediate medical review: signs of systemic infection at an injection site, abnormal bleeding, new fractures, or marked reproductive/menstrual disruption."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE. (1) Almost all 'follistatin builds muscle' mechanism comes from cell culture, transgenic mice, and a handful of tiny, uncontrolled human gene-therapy patients — not from healthy-athlete RCTs. (2) Follistatin is NOT selective: it neutralizes activins and BMPs as well as myostatin, raising real concerns about bone weakening and vascular effects (BMP9/10); this is why pharma often pursues more selective myostatin antibodies/decoy receptors instead (PMID 32911580). (3) The whole myostatin-blockade class has a sobering track record: the related decoy receptor ACE-031 (soluble ActRIIB) raised muscle in animals and healthy volunteers but had its program halted over off-target safety signals (nosebleeds, gum bleeding from BMP-related vasculature) (PMID 23169607, PMID 41686840) — a cautionary tale for ANY broad myostatin/activin trap. (4) Gene therapy is irreversible: you cannot 'dose down' an AAV/plasmid that keeps expressing follistatin, and unregulated overexpression is an unknown long-term risk. (5) Injectable 'follistatin peptide / follistatin-344' from peptide vendors is unverified in identity, purity, and biological activity; recombinant follistatin is a large glycoprotein and a short peptide is unlikely to reproduce trial results. (6) Anecdote ≠ evidence: YouTube/Reddit muscle-gain claims are uncontrolled, often confounded by training, diet, and concurrent anabolics, and must be kept strictly separate from the trial data. (7) Myostatin also has roles outside muscle (e.g., it drives pituitary FSH synthesis, PMID 39818879; affects heart and bone) — systemic blockade could have endocrine consequences that haven't been characterized in humans.
