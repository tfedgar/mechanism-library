The DHT kill-switch: it jams both 5-AR isoenzymes and drains ~95% of your dihydrotestosterone — then lingers in your body for months after the last pill.

Class
5α-reductase inhibitor (dual type 1 + type 2); 4-azasteroid
Primary target
5α-reductase isoenzymes type 1 (SRD5A1) AND type 2 (SRD5A2) — near-total blockade of testosterone→DHT conversion, producing ~90-95% serum DHT suppression
Human status
Approved and widely used in humans. FDA-approved November 2002 (Avodart) for symptomatic benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), alone and in combination with tamsulosin (Jalyn, 2010). It is NOT FDA-approved for hair loss, though it is approved for androgenetic alopecia in a few countries (e.g. South Korea, Japan) and used off-label for hair elsewhere. Human evidence is extensive: multiple large randomized controlled trials (dose-ranging, 2-4 year BPH trials, the ~8,200-man REDUCE prostate-cancer prevention trial), plus mechanistic human RCTs on DHT suppression, anabolism, and metabolism. Contraindicated in women of childbearing potential and in pregnancy (teratogen).

At a glance

  • Serum DHT suppressed ~90-95%+ (deeper and less variable than finasteride's ~70%)
  • Prostate shrinks; BPH urinary symptoms, acute-retention and surgery risk fall
  • Hair regrowth / reversal of miniaturization in male-pattern hair loss (off-label in most countries)
  • PSA roughly halved - screening interpretation must be adjusted
  • Muscle anabolism from testosterone is UNAFFECTED by blocking DHT
  • Sexual side effects (low libido, ED, reduced ejaculate) and gynecomastia in a minority
  • Dutasteride-specific metabolic signal: increased liver fat and reduced insulin sensitivity (type 1 effect, not seen with finasteride)
  • Very long half-life (~weeks) - suppression and any side effects persist months after stopping
  • Potent teratogen - male-fetus genital malformation risk; blood-donation deferral required

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps7 human-evidenced 1 speculative

  1. The enzyme it blocks makes your 'strong' androgen Established

    Testosterone is the headline androgen, but in skin, scalp, and prostate an enzyme called 5-alpha-reductase (5-AR) converts it into dihydrotestosterone (DHT) - a more potent version that binds the androgen receptor more tightly and stays bound longer. 5-AR comes in two main working forms: type 1 (broadly expressed - skin, liver, and much of the brain; dominant in sebaceous glands) and type 2 (dominant in prostate, genital skin, and hair follicles). Dutasteride is built to jam BOTH. PMID 15126539; PMID 12022710

  2. It's a slow, near-irreversible enzyme trap - not a quick on/off blocker Established

    Dutasteride is a 4-azasteroid that mimics the enzyme's natural substrate. It slips into the active site and forms an extremely tight, long-lived complex with 5-AR (drug reviews describe it as functionally irreversible). That's why the effect is so deep and durable: the enzyme is effectively taken out of service. That same sticky binding, together with the drug's very long half-life, is why its action outlasts the last pill by weeks to months. PMID 19879888; PMID 18318566

  3. Blocking BOTH isoenzymes crushes DHT harder than finasteride Established

    This is the whole point of dutasteride. Finasteride blocks mainly type 2 and drops serum DHT by about 70%. Dutasteride blocks type 1 AND type 2: in a randomized dose-ranging trial, 0.5 mg/day suppressed serum DHT by ~94.7% and 5 mg by ~98.4%, versus ~70.8% for 5 mg finasteride - and with much less person-to-person variability. Over a year, DHT was reduced by >90% in about 85% of men on 0.5 mg. PMID 15126539; PMID 14565784

  4. Testosterone rises a bit and can get 'rerouted' Evidence-human

    When testosterone can't be converted to DHT, it accumulates slightly - serum testosterone typically rises modestly but stays inside the normal range (seen across all doses in the dose-ranging trial). With one downstream exit blocked, some backed-up testosterone can be shunted toward other pathways, including aromatization to estradiol - the mechanistic rationale offered for the gynecomastia signal. Note: a large estradiol rise was NOT a headline measured finding in the pivotal trials, so this shunting is more inference than a firmly quantified effect. PMID 15126539; PMID 14565784

  5. Less DHT in the follicle = slower hair miniaturization Evidence-human

    In male-pattern hair loss, DHT progressively shrinks (miniaturizes) genetically susceptible scalp follicles. Because both 5-AR isoenzymes sit in the follicle and dutasteride suppresses DHT more completely than finasteride, it can regrow hair and reverse miniaturization somewhat more effectively - a randomized, open-label, evaluator-blinded trial found dutasteride superior to finasteride for hair regrowth (though open-label design limits certainty). This is off-label in most countries (approved for hair only in a few, e.g. South Korea, Japan). PMID 27549867; PMID 32279398

  6. Less DHT in the prostate = shrinkage - and a distorted PSA Established

    DHT is the main androgen driving prostate growth. Near-total DHT suppression shrinks the prostate, easing urinary symptoms of benign enlargement (BPH) and cutting the risk of acute urinary retention and surgery. A consequence: dutasteride roughly HALVES a man's PSA blood test, so a measured PSA must be roughly doubled to interpret it - miss this and a real cancer can be masked. PMID 14565784; PMID 15072886

  7. DHT is NOT required for testosterone's muscle-building effect Evidence-human

    A key myth for the PED audience: blocking DHT does NOT blunt the muscle gains from testosterone. In a rigorous randomized trial giving graded testosterone doses with or without dutasteride, gains in fat-free mass, strength, and other anabolic outcomes were essentially identical whether or not DHT was suppressed. So DHT drives prostate, skin, scalp, and some sexual effects - but testosterone itself, acting on the androgen receptor in muscle, does the anabolic work. PMID 22396515

  8. The enzyme also handles other steroids - so effects may spread beyond DHT Contested

    5-AR types 1 and 2 don't only make DHT. They also A-ring-reduce steroids like glucocorticoids and progesterone, and are the first step in making neurosteroids such as allopregnanolone (a calming, GABA-boosting steroid). Blocking the enzyme can therefore ripple into steroid handling and neurosteroid levels - a leading hypothesis for the mood, anxiety, and sexual symptoms some men report. Dual (type 1 + type 2) blockade may widen this footprint versus finasteride because type 1 is the isoenzyme in liver, fat, and much of the brain. Note: a controlled human study found dutasteride reduced urinary glucocorticoid metabolites but did NOT lower plasma or salivary cortisol - so 'altered handling' is not the same as 'less cortisol.' Much of the neurosteroid-to-mood link is extrapolated from finasteride and rodent work. PMID 24823464; PMID 26574953; PMID 32435662

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Dutasteride is a synthetic 4-azasteroid that acts as a competitive, extremely slow-dissociating (drug reviews call it functionally irreversible) inhibitor of both 5-alpha-reductase isoenzymes: type 1 (SRD5A1) and type 2 (SRD5A2). It occupies the enzyme's steroid-substrate/NADPH pocket and forms a stable enzyme-inhibitor complex with a very long dissociation time, which is why suppression is deep and durable. Its dual coverage is the pharmacologic distinction from finasteride, which is a potent type 2 and only weak type 1 inhibitor.

Signalling cascade. Enzyme blockade -> testosterone can no longer be reduced to DHT in target tissues -> serum and intraprostatic/intrafollicular DHT collapse (~90-95%+ serum) -> reduced androgen-receptor activation specifically in DHT-dominant tissues (prostate, scalp, genital skin, sebaceous glands) -> prostate involution, slowed follicular miniaturization, reduced sebum; testosterone accumulates modestly and may be partly rerouted (some aromatization to estradiol, though a large estradiol rise was not a headline trial finding). Because 5-AR also A-ring-reduces glucocorticoids and is upstream of GABAergic neurosteroids (e.g. allopregnanolone), blockade can extend into hepatic/peripheral steroid handling and CNS neurosteroid tone.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral soft-gelatin capsule (0.5 mg is the marketed strength); highly lipophilic
Absorption / bioavailability
Absolute bioavailability ~60%; absorption largely unaffected by food. Peak plasma concentration ~1-3 hours after dosing.
Half-life
Very long terminal half-life at steady state, on the order of weeks (label figure roughly ~5 weeks). This is the headline PK fact vs finasteride (whose half-life is measured in hours). Steady state takes months to reach; after stopping, serum DHT recovers only gradually over weeks-to-months, and by the 5-half-lives rule the drug can remain detectable for roughly 6 months.
Metabolism
Extensively hepatically metabolized by CYP3A4/CYP3A5 to hydroxylated and other metabolites; strong CYP3A4 inhibitors can raise dutasteride exposure. Highly protein-bound (~99%).
Excretion
Mainly fecal as metabolites and unchanged drug; minimal renal excretion of parent.
Notes
Because of the long half-life, DHT suppression is smooth and non-fluctuating during therapy, but it also means the effect (and any side effects) cannot be rapidly reversed by stopping. Men are advised not to donate blood until several months after their last dose to avoid exposing a pregnant transfusion recipient.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

The pivotal dose-ranging RCT (Clark 2004, n=399 BPH men, 24 weeks) tested dutasteride 0.01-5.0 mg/day against finasteride 5 mg and placebo. Mean serum DHT suppression: ~94.7% at 0.5 mg and ~98.4% at 5.0 mg dutasteride, versus ~70.8% for finasteride 5 mg - the dutasteride effect being both deeper AND less variable between individuals. Suppression is near-maximal at and above the 0.5 mg marketed dose, so going higher adds little DHT suppression; testosterone rose modestly but stayed within the normal range at all doses. NO dosing protocol is offered here; the point is that the approved dose already produces near-complete DHT blockade.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Finasteride
    same: Both are 4-azasteroid 5-AR inhibitors that lower DHT to treat BPH and male-pattern hair loss; overlapping sexual-side-effect and teratogenicity profiles.
    different: Dutasteride blocks BOTH isoenzymes (type 1 + type 2) -> ~90-95%+ serum DHT suppression vs finasteride's ~70% (mainly type 2). Dutasteride's half-life is on the order of WEEKS vs finasteride's HOURS, so its effects persist far longer after stopping. Dutasteride uniquely shows a liver-fat / insulin-resistance signal (type 1 effect). Finasteride is FDA-approved for hair; dutasteride is not (approved for hair only in select countries).
  • vs Testosterone / anabolic androgenic steroids (AAS)
    same: Both act on the androgen-receptor axis and both can affect prostate, hair, and sexual function.
    different: Testosterone/AAS ADD androgen; dutasteride SUBTRACTS the potent DHT metabolite. Crucially, blocking DHT does not blunt testosterone's muscle-building effect (Bhasin 2012), so 5-ARIs are used alongside T mainly to spare hair/prostate - not to change anabolism. Note some 19-nor and DHT-derived AAS are not 5-AR substrates, so dutasteride won't modify them the way it modifies testosterone.
  • vs Aromatase inhibitors (e.g. anastrozole)
    same: Both are enzyme inhibitors used to steer testosterone's downstream fate and both tend to raise the parent hormone slightly.
    different: Aromatase inhibitors block testosterone -> ESTRADIOL; dutasteride blocks testosterone -> DHT. They target opposite branches of testosterone metabolism, so they have largely non-overlapping effect and side-effect profiles (dutasteride can even nudge estradiol upward by shunting substrate toward aromatization).

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Dianabol compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    The genuine shared axis is metabolic/hepatic, not estrogenic. Dianabol (oral 17α-alkylated) sharply worsens the lipid profile (HDL crash, raised LDL/ApoB) and is hepatotoxic; dutasteride independently raises hepatic lipid and reduces insulin sensitivity (its dual-blockade metabolic signal). Stacked, they push liver/lipid/glucose in the same adverse direction. Note the estrogenic angle is weak: Dianabol's estrogen problem is a potent, SHBG-freed methyl-estradiol rather than high aromatization flux, so dutasteride's testosterone-to-estradiol shunt does not meaningfully add to it; and dutasteride does little to blunt Dianabol's androgenic action (Dianabol is only a minor 5-AR substrate), so it should not be relied on to protect scalp/skin. PMID 26574953; PMID 24823464 Watch: Liver enzymes (Dianabol is hepatotoxic, dutasteride raises hepatic lipid); lipids and fasting glucose; do not rely on dutasteride to protect scalp against Dianabol's androgenic component.
  • Insulin compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Dutasteride (dual blockade, not finasteride) reduces whole-body insulin sensitivity and blunts insulin's suppression of non-esterified fatty acids in controlled human clamp studies — i.e. it makes peripheral tissues less responsive to insulin. In someone using exogenous insulin, this added insulin resistance works against insulin's glucose-lowering action, making glucose control more erratic and shifting the effective insulin response. The interaction is metabolic (peripheral insulin signaling), not hormonal-androgenic. PMID 24823464; PMID 26574953 Watch: Blood glucose patterns and HbA1c; watch for a reduced glucose-lowering response; liver fat/enzymes given dutasteride's hepatic-lipid signal.
  • MK-677 compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Two independent routes to worsened glucose handling converge. Dual 5α-reductase inhibition is uniquely (vs finasteride) linked to reduced insulin-stimulated glucose disposal, increased body fat and hepatic lipid accumulation — a type-1-5AR peripheral effect shown in controlled human clamp studies. MK-677 raises GH/IGF-1, and GH excess drives insulin resistance and higher fasting glucose. Stacked, both push insulin sensitivity in the same wrong direction and both promote hepatic/ectopic lipid, compounding the risk of impaired glucose tolerance. PMID 24823464; PMID 26574953 Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c; fasting insulin/insulin-resistance markers; liver enzymes and any signs of fatty-liver progression; lipid panel.
  • Anastrozole counteracts ↓ Contested
    The two enzymes sit on opposite branches of testosterone's fate: aromatase converts T→estradiol (anastrozole blocks it), 5α-reductase converts T→DHT (dutasteride blocks it). Because dutasteride closes the DHT exit, some backed-up testosterone can be shunted toward aromatization — the mechanistic rationale offered for dutasteride's gynecomastia signal, though this shunt is inference rather than a firmly quantified trial finding. An aromatase inhibitor counteracts that estrogenic drift by cutting estradiol synthesis. Otherwise the two drugs act on non-overlapping pathways. PMID 14565784; PMID 27672412 Watch: Estradiol (dutasteride may nudge it up, an AI cuts it — watch for over-suppression if combined); breast tissue/gynecomastia; joint/libido/lipid signs of estrogen that is too low.
  • Letrozole counteracts ↓ Contested
    Same opposite-branch logic as anastrozole but more extreme: letrozole is a very potent aromatase inhibitor (>98% E2 suppression). Dutasteride blocks the DHT exit and can shunt some testosterone toward estradiol (its inferred gynecomastia rationale); letrozole aggressively counteracts that by crushing estrogen synthesis. The enzymes are non-overlapping, so the specific interaction is that letrozole erases the small estrogenic drift dutasteride may cause — with a real risk of driving estradiol far too low. PMID 14565784; PMID 27672412 Watch: Estradiol (high over-suppression risk); symptoms of estrogen deficiency (low libido, joint pain, mood, adverse lipids/bone markers), since letrozole's effect dwarfs dutasteride's small estrogen push.
  • Nandrolone opposes Evidence-rodent
    The counter-intuitive, textbook-flagged case. Nandrolone is a 5α-reductase substrate, but reduction yields 5α-dihydronandrolone (DHN), which binds the androgen receptor MORE WEAKLY than nandrolone itself — the opposite of testosterone→DHT. So in 5-AR-rich tissues like the hair follicle, 5α-reduction normally SOFTENS nandrolone's androgenic action. Dutasteride blocks that softening step, leaving intact, full-strength parent nandrolone acting on follicular androgen receptors. The 'a 5-ARI protects hair' logic is therefore reversed here: adding dutasteride can worsen rather than protect against androgenic hair loss and skin effects. Nandrolone's separate progestogenic/prolactin-driven effects are unrelated to and unaffected by this interaction. PMID 4021486; PMID 19861292 Watch: Scalp hair (pattern/rate of loss) — the usual '5-ARI protects hair' expectation does not apply; skin/sebaceous androgenic effects.
  • Testosterone shares target Established
    5α-reductase (type 1+2) converts testosterone into DHT in skin, scalp, prostate and genital tissue; dutasteride blocks ~90-95% of that conversion. On testosterone it therefore removes the DHT arm specifically — reducing DHT-driven scalp miniaturization, prostate growth and sebum — while leaving testosterone's own androgen-receptor signaling intact. Critically this does NOT blunt the anabolic (muscle/strength) effect of testosterone, which acts on the AR directly rather than via DHT (Bhasin's graded-testosterone-plus-dutasteride RCT showed identical fat-free-mass and strength gains). Serum testosterone rises modestly (backed-up substrate, stays in-range) and some of that backed-up testosterone can be shunted toward aromatization to estradiol, though a large estradiol rise was not a headline trial finding. PMID 22396515; PMID 15126539; PMID 32279398 Watch: PSA (dutasteride roughly halves it — a measured value must be interpreted as ~double, or a real cancer can be masked); scalp/prostate DHT-dependent effects; estradiol and breast tissue given the possible extra shunt toward aromatization; do not expect changes to muscle gains.
  • Boldenone undecylenate redundant Established
    Dutasteride inhibits both 5-AR isoenzymes for near-total (~90-95%) DHT suppression, but boldenone is not a 5-AR substrate and its potent metabolite DHB is not produced by 5-AR, so dutasteride does not blunt boldenone's androgenic action. On an EQ-based regimen it only suppresses DHT from co-run testosterone; boldenone/DHB androgenic side effects proceed unaffected. (Dutasteride's own monograph notes muscle anabolism from testosterone is likewise unaffected by DHT blockade.) EQ monograph (non-5AR substrate; DHB metabolite); dutasteride monograph (dual isoenzyme block; anabolism DHT-independent) Watch: Do not expect dutasteride to mitigate boldenone-driven androgenic hair loss/acne/prostate effects; it acts only on the testosterone-to-DHT arm. Monitor DHT and androgenic symptoms accordingly.
  • Finasteride redundant Established
    Both drugs act on the identical target class, 5α-reductase, and produce the same downstream effect (DHT suppression, halved PSA, shared class side-effect and post-5ARI concern). Finasteride selectively traps type 2 (SRD5A2) and drops systemic DHT ~65-70%; dutasteride traps BOTH type 1 and type 2 and drops DHT ~90-95% with a half-life of weeks rather than hours. Because dutasteride's blockade fully encompasses finasteride's, combining them adds essentially no extra DHT suppression, dutasteride simply supersedes finasteride at the enzyme. dutasteride monograph mechanism_steps 3-4 and comparisons (vs Finasteride); PMID 15126539 Watch: There is no additive DHT benefit to combining two 5-AR inhibitors; the more complete blockade (dutasteride) carries a much longer washout and, by the same neurosteroid logic, a potentially larger CNS footprint plus a dutasteride-specific liver-fat/insulin-resistance signal. Track mood, libido and sexual function as class effects; note PSA is halved by either drug.
  • Halotestin redundant Established
    Dutasteride inhibits both 5-alpha-reductase isoforms for near-total (~90-95%) DHT suppression, used to curb DHT-driven androgenic effects of 5a-reducible androgens. Because fluoxymesterone is not a 5-AR substrate and signals directly at the AR, dutasteride cannot reduce Halotestin's androgenic action or its side-effect profile. History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Developed by GlaxoSmithKline; FDA-approved November 2002 as Avodart for symptomatic BPH. A fixed-dose combination with the alpha-blocker tamsulosin (Jalyn) was approved in 2010. The large REDUCE prostate-cancer chemoprevention trial (NEJM 2010) showed reduced overall cancer detection but a high-grade-tumor signal; an FDA advisory committee in 2011 voted against a cancer-prevention indication and the class received a labeling warning about high-grade prostate cancer. Dutasteride is approved for androgenetic alopecia in a limited set of countries (notably South Korea and Japan) but remains off-label for hair in the US and EU. Generic dutasteride became available in the 2010s. Post-marketing, patient advocacy around 'post-finasteride syndrome' led the NIH to list it in its Genetic and Rare Diseases Information Center, and several national agencies added persistent-side-effect language to 5-ARI labeling, keeping the concern on the regulatory radar.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • True incidence and biological reality of persistent post-drug symptoms (post-5ARI/post-finasteride syndrome) - reported but not established causally, and dutasteride-specific data are especially thin.
  • Whether the REDUCE high-grade prostate cancer signal reflects a real tumor-promoting effect or a detection artifact from prostate shrinkage.
  • Long-term clinical consequences of the dutasteride-specific liver-fat / insulin-resistance signal (does it translate into diabetes or NAFLD risk over years?).
  • How much of the neurosteroid/mood mechanism, largely extrapolated from finasteride and rodents, actually applies to dutasteride in humans.
  • Long-term cardiovascular and metabolic outcomes of near-total lifelong DHT suppression in otherwise healthy younger men using it off-label for hair.
  • Whether adding type 1 blockade (vs finasteride) meaningfully changes the risk of neuropsychiatric or metabolic effects at the population level.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

HARM-REDUCTION MONITORING ONLY - no dosing guidance. What a clinician would watch: (1) PSA - obtain a baseline, and remember dutasteride HALVES PSA; any measured value should be roughly doubled to stay valid, and a confirmed rise while on the drug warrants urologic evaluation even if the absolute number looks 'normal.' (2) Sexual function and libido - track at baseline and over time; know that because of the weeks-long half-life, symptoms will not resolve quickly if the drug is stopped. (3) Mood, anxiety, and cognition - screen and take new depressive or anxiety symptoms seriously given the neurosteroid rationale and labeling. (4) Breast changes - any new breast lump, tenderness, or nipple discharge should be evaluated (rare male breast cancer reports on 5-ARIs). (5) Metabolic markers - fasting glucose/HbA1c and lipids/liver enzymes are reasonable to follow given the human liver-fat and insulin-resistance signal. (6) Pregnancy/handling - it is a teratogen: women who are or could become pregnant must not handle leaking capsules, and men should not donate blood until several months after the last dose. (7) Set expectations up front that the very long half-life makes this a slow-in, slow-out drug - important context for anyone weighing whether to start.

Caveats

This monograph explains mechanism and monitoring only - it is NOT a how-to and contains no dosing protocols. Key honesty points: (1) The single most under-appreciated fact is the very long terminal half-life (on the order of weeks) - dutasteride and its DHT suppression persist for MONTHS after the last dose, unlike finasteride's ~day-scale half-life, so 'just stop if I get sides' does not give fast relief. (2) The 'post-finasteride/post-5ARI syndrome' - persistent sexual, mood, and cognitive symptoms after stopping - is real as a reported phenomenon and is taken seriously enough that regulators (and NIH's rare-disease center) list it, but causation and frequency remain CONTESTED; most controlled data show side effects are uncommon and usually reversible, while the persistent subset is documented mainly in case reports and pharmacovigilance, not controlled trials, and dutasteride-specific data are especially thin. (3) The REDUCE trial found dutasteride reduced overall (mostly low-grade) prostate cancer detection (~23% relative reduction) but showed a signal of MORE high-grade (Gleason 8-10) tumors in years 3-4 - the FDA declined a cancer-prevention indication and added a class warning; whether this is a true biological effect or a detection artifact is debated. (4) It is a potent teratogen and men on it must not donate blood until months after the last dose. (5) Much of the neurosteroid/mood mechanism is extrapolated from finasteride and rodent work; dutasteride-specific human causal data are thin. (6) A controlled human study found dutasteride altered glucocorticoid metabolism but did NOT lower plasma/salivary cortisol - do not overstate the cortisol angle. (7) This is not medical advice; anyone using it should be monitored by a clinician.

The enzyme it blocks makes your 'strong' androgen. Testosterone is the headline androgen, but in skin, scalp, and prostate an enzyme called 5-alpha-reductase (5-AR) converts it into dihydrotestosterone (DHT) - a more potent version that binds the androgen receptor more tightly and stays bound longer. 5-AR comes in two main working forms: type 1 (broadly expressed - skin, liver, and much of the brain; dominant in sebaceous glands) and type 2 (dominant in prostate, genital skin, and hair follicles). Dutasteride is built to jam BOTH. PMID 15126539; PMID 12022710
