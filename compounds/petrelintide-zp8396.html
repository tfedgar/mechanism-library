Petrelintide

Main target: Amylin receptors. Backed by human data.

EvidenceHuman evidence
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A once-weekly, stabilized human-amylin analog betting that "the other satiety hormone" can match GLP-1-class weight loss with less nausea — early human data are promising but Phase 3 has not started.

Strong human data 15 citations
Class
Long-acting amylin analog (dual amylin / calcitonin-receptor agonist); once-weekly subcutaneous injectable peptide
Primary target
Amylin receptors (AMY1R, AMY3R) — heterodimers of the calcitonin receptor (CTR) plus receptor-activity-modifying proteins RAMP1/RAMP3 — and the calcitonin receptor itself, expressed on Calcr+ neurons in the hindbrain dorsal vagal complex (area postrema and nucleus tractus solitarius)
Human status
PIPELINE / Phase 2 (as of mid-2026). Petrelintide (Zealand Pharma; global partnership with Roche/Hoffmann-La Roche) has completed Phase 1 single- and multiple-ascending-dose trials in adults with overweight/obesity (PMID 42017294): ~10-day half-life, dose-proportional PK, and up to 8.6% weight loss at 16 weeks with mostly mild GI adverse events. It has advanced to Phase 2: the monotherapy dose-finding ZUPREME trial (NCT06662539, n=493, COMPLETED; 42-week treatment with primary endpoint at week 28; topline results reported by sponsor but full peer-reviewed efficacy publication pending at this writing), a Phase 2 in obesity + type 2 diabetes (ZUPREME 2, NCT06926842, n=221, active/not recruiting, primary completion Aug 2026), and a Roche-sponsored Phase 2 combination trial (ZYNERGY, NCT07589686, n=486, not yet recruiting, start mid-2026) co-administering petrelintide with the dual GLP-1/GIP agonist enicepatide (RO7795068). Phase 1 renal-impairment (NCT07076030, n=39, completed) and formulation-PK studies (NCT07338214, n=48, completed) are done. No Phase 3 efficacy data, no cardiovascular-outcomes trial, and no long-term (multi-year) safety data exist yet. It is not approved by any regulator for any indication.

At a glance

  • Once-weekly subcutaneous dosing; long ~10-day half-life supports weekly injection (Phase 1 PK)
  • Dose-dependent weight loss — up to 8.6% at 16 weeks in Phase 1 (a short, early safety/PK study, not a definitive efficacy readout)
  • Appetite/satiety suppression via a central (brainstem) mechanism independent of GLP-1
  • Mostly mild GI side effects in Phase 1; nausea the most common, low discontinuation — a hoped-for tolerability edge over incretins that is not yet proven head-to-head
  • Designed for co-formulation/co-administration with incretin drugs (the Roche combination strategy, with the dual GLP-1/GIP agonist enicepatide)
  • Being tested both as monotherapy (ZUPREME Phase 2) and in obesity with type 2 diabetes (ZUPREME 2 Phase 2)
  • NOT approved; NO Phase 3 efficacy data; NO cardiovascular-outcome or long-term safety data yet

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps5 human / established 1 rodent / preclinical 1 contested / speculative

  1. A stabilized copy of the body's own satiety hormone Established

    Amylin is a hormone released with insulin from pancreatic beta-cells after a meal; it tells the brain you've eaten enough. Native human amylin is useless as a drug because it clumps into amyloid fibrils and is cleared within minutes. Petrelintide is an engineered human-amylin analog rebuilt for chemical stability and a long half-life, formulated at near-neutral pH so it can be co-formulated with other peptides. It is designed to reproduce amylin's natural 'stop eating' signal in a once-weekly injection. PMID 41217931 (Development of Petrelintide, J Med Chem 2025; DOI 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.5c01185); PMID 41747885 (Peptides 2026)

  2. Binds amylin/calcitonin receptor complexes in the brainstem Established

    Amylin acts on receptors built from the calcitonin receptor (CTR) paired with accessory proteins called RAMPs (RAMP1 or RAMP3), forming the AMY1 and AMY3 receptors; it also hits the plain calcitonin receptor. These receptors sit on neurons in the hindbrain 'dorsal vagal complex' — the area postrema and nucleus tractus solitarius — a brain region that samples blood-borne signals and controls appetite and nausea. Petrelintide is an agonist at this receptor family, the same target class as cagrilintide. Receptor complex/AMY1R-AMY3R RAMP dependence shown for cagrilintide: PMID 40609154 (EBioMedicine 2025), PMID 41443331 (Mol Metab 2026); target class for petrelintide: PMID 41747885, PMID 41217931

  3. Activates satiety circuits centrally, not through the gut Evidence-rodent

    Once bound, amylin-receptor agonists activate specific Calcr-expressing neuron populations in the hindbrain. Preclinical mapping of the closely related agonist cagrilintide shows that long-term dosing works largely through a subset of nucleus-tractus-solitarius neurons that make prolactin-releasing hormone (Calcr/Prlh cells); knocking those neurons down abolishes the amylin agonist's effect but not semaglutide's. This is why amylin's satiety signal is described as 'independent of GLP-1' — a parallel wire into the same appetite-control hub. PMID 42260119 (cross-species DVC atlas — rat/mouse knockdown, macaque + human conservation, Nat Metab 2026); PMID 39426704 (CNS pathways of amylin, review, Biochimie 2025). Direct neuron-level data are for cagrilintide/amylin, not petrelintide specifically.

  4. Reduces food intake and promotes satiation Evidence-human

    The net physiological effect of amylin-receptor activation is enhanced meal-ending satiation and reduced food intake, plus slowing of gastric emptying. In humans, petrelintide's once-weekly dosing produced dose-dependent weight loss consistent with this appetite-suppressing mechanism, though the trial measured weight (a downstream marker) rather than food intake directly. PMID 42017294 (petrelintide Phase 1 SAD/MAD — dose-dependent weight loss, Diabetes Obes Metab 2026); appetite/gastric mechanism (class-level, preclinical) PMID 39426704, PMID 41747885

  5. Weight loss with, so far, a gentler nausea profile than GLP-1 drugs Evidence-human

    In the Phase 1 multiple-ascending-dose study, 16 weeks of once-weekly petrelintide reduced body weight by up to 8.6%, with mostly mild gastrointestinal side effects: nausea in roughly 17-33% of treated participants (vs ~17% placebo in the highest-dose cohort), rare diarrhea, and vomiting in only the single participant who discontinued. The authors frame potentially better GI tolerability than incretin drugs as a hoped-for advantage — a claim that still needs head-to-head confirmation. PMID 42017294 (Phase 1, up to 8.6% weight loss at 16 wks; nausea 16.7-33.3% vs 16.7% placebo in MAD part 2, mostly mild; 1 GI discontinuation)

  6. Rationale for combination with incretins (Roche pipeline) Evidence-human

    Because amylin and GLP-1 act through separate receptors and partly separate neurons, the two signals can add together. Cagrilintide + semaglutide (CagriSema) showed greater weight loss than either component alone, establishing proof-of-concept for the combination logic. Petrelintide was deliberately engineered to be co-formulable, and Roche is running a Phase 2 study (ZYNERGY) pairing it with enicepatide (RO7795068 / CT-388), a dual GLP-1/GIP receptor agonist that works through a mechanism independent of amylin. This is the strategic reason amylin is called 'the next mechanism after incretins.' Combination proof-of-concept (cagri+sema phase 1b): PMID 33894838 (Lancet 2021), review PMID 41747885; petrelintide co-formulation design: PMID 41217931; enicepatide/CT-388 dual GLP-1/GIP class: PMID 41319798 (Mol Metab 2026); combo trial: NCT07589686 (Roche Phase 2, ZYNERGY)

  7. Possible leptin-resensitization angle (hypothesis, preclinical only) Speculative

    A long-standing preclinical idea is that amylin can restore the brain's responsiveness to leptin, the fat-derived satiety hormone that stops working in obesity ('leptin resistance'). Amylin-leptin synergy has been shown in rodents. Whether petrelintide meaningfully exploits this in humans is unproven and should be treated as a mechanistic hypothesis, not an established benefit. PMID 20413324 (amylin-leptin synergy review, Trends Endocrinol Metab 2010; preclinical). No human petrelintide leptin data.

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Agonist at the amylin receptor family — heterodimers of the calcitonin receptor (CTR, gene CALCR) with receptor-activity-modifying proteins RAMP1 and RAMP3, forming AMY1R and AMY3R respectively — and at the calcitonin receptor itself (a dual amylin/calcitonin-receptor agonist profile, the same target class as cagrilintide). It is a stabilized analog of native human amylin (islet amyloid polypeptide, IAPP), engineered to resist amyloid aggregation and proteolysis while retaining in-vitro and in-vivo potency.

Signalling cascade. These are class B Gs-coupled GPCRs; agonism raises intracellular cAMP in Calcr-expressing neurons of the hindbrain dorsal vagal complex (area postrema, which lacks a full blood-brain barrier, and the nucleus tractus solitarius). For the related agonist cagrilintide, acute signaling alters gene expression in area-postrema Calcr/Ramp3 neurons; chronic dosing up-regulates prolactin-releasing hormone in NTS Calcr/Prlh neurons, a population conserved across rodent, macaque and human and required for the drug's sustained anorectic effect in rats. Downstream, appetite is suppressed and gastric emptying slowed. Petrelintide-specific neuron-level data are not published; these mediators are inferred by class.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection, once weekly.
Absorption / bioavailability
Slowly absorbed after SC injection (Phase 1). Exact absolute bioavailability not reported in the abstract; an IV 0.35 mg arm was included in the SAD study for PK characterization.
Half-life
~10 days, supporting once-weekly dosing (Phase 1).
Metabolism
As a peptide, expected to undergo proteolytic catabolism to amino acids/small peptides rather than CYP-mediated metabolism (typical for engineered peptide analogs; petrelintide is specifically stabilized against proteolysis).
Excretion
Peptide clearance (catabolic). A dedicated renal-impairment PK study was conducted (NCT07076030, n=39, completed Nov 2025); results not yet published in detail.
Notes
Steady-state dose proportionality observed across the tested range; formulated near neutral pH to enable co-formulation with other peptides. SAD tested SC 0.04-2.4 mg + 0.35 mg IV; MAD tested once-weekly regimens escalated every 2 weeks up to 9.0 mg target dose.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

In the Phase 1 MAD trial, once-weekly SC petrelintide produced dose-dependent weight loss, up to 8.6% at 16 weeks in the escalated high-dose cohorts (target doses of 2.4, 4.8 and 9.0 mg reached via every-2-week escalation). GI adverse events also rose with dose but remained mostly mild. Definitive dose-response for efficacy and tolerability is the explicit purpose of the completed Phase 2 dose-finding ZUPREME study (NCT06662539), which compared 5 once-weekly doses versus placebo over a 42-week treatment period with the primary endpoint at week 28 — those full dose-response results are the key awaited data. Per harm-reduction scope, no specific dosing/titration protocol is provided here.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Cagrilintide (Novo Nordisk)
    same: Both are long-acting, once-weekly dual amylin/calcitonin-receptor agonists targeting the same brainstem satiety circuitry.
    different: Cagrilintide is more clinically advanced (Phase 2 monotherapy ~10% weight loss over 26 wks [PMID 34798060], and the CagriSema combination in Phase 3). Petrelintide is engineered as a stabilized human-amylin analog formulable at neutral pH specifically to enable co-formulation, and its sponsor emphasizes a hoped-for GI-tolerability edge — unproven head-to-head.
  • vs Semaglutide / tirzepatide (GLP-1 and GLP-1/GIP incretins)
    same: All are once-weekly SC injectables that reduce appetite via hindbrain circuits and cause dose-related GI side effects.
    different: Petrelintide works through amylin receptors, a mechanism independent of GLP-1 — so it can be combined with incretins for additive effect, and may (unproven) cause less nausea. Incretins currently have far larger, longer, Phase 3 and cardiovascular-outcome evidence; petrelintide has none of that yet.
  • vs Eloralintide (LY3841136, Eli Lilly)
    same: Competing long-acting once-weekly amylin-receptor agonist for obesity, now with early Phase 2 data.
    different: Eloralintide is described as a selective amylin-receptor agonist (PMID 41207310, PMID 41559929); petrelintide is a broader dual amylin/calcitonin-receptor agonist and a stabilized-human-amylin analog. Both are early-phase; a direct efficacy comparison is premature.
  • vs Pramlintide (approved first-generation amylin analog)
    same: Same hormone family and satiety mechanism.
    different: Pramlintide is short-acting (multiple daily injections), based on rat amylin, and used as an adjunct to insulin with only modest weight effect. Petrelintide is the once-weekly, human-sequence, obesity-focused successor.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Petrelintide (development code ZP8396) was discovered and developed by Zealand Pharma (Denmark), building on the second-generation, non-aggregating long-acting amylin-analog design paradigm; a Boehringer Ingelheim team is co-credited on the medicinal-chemistry development paper (PMID 41217931). Zealand entered a global collaboration/license with Roche (Hoffmann-La Roche) to co-develop petrelintide, including combination with Roche's dual GLP-1/GIP agonist enicepatide (RO7795068 / CT-388) — reflected in Roche sponsoring the Phase 2 combination trial ZYNERGY (NCT07589686) while Zealand sponsors the monotherapy and diabetes Phase 2 studies. Regulatory status: investigational only, no approvals anywhere; the program is at Phase 2 with Phase 3 not yet initiated as of mid-2026.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Actual Phase 2/3 efficacy magnitude and how it compares to semaglutide and tirzepatide (Phase 1 8.6% at 16 wks is not a pivotal readout)
  • Whether the 'less nausea than GLP-1' advantage holds at therapeutic doses and head-to-head
  • Body-composition effects — does it preserve lean mass better than incretins? No petrelintide data yet
  • Long-term (multi-year) safety, immunogenicity/ADA impact, and any pancreatitis/gallbladder/thyroid signals
  • Cardiovascular and metabolic outcomes (no CVOT)
  • Added benefit and tolerability of the enicepatide + petrelintide combination vs either alone
  • Durability of weight loss and weight-regain trajectory after stopping
  • Whether human petrelintide meaningfully engages the leptin-resensitization mechanism seen in rodents

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For an investigational drug there is no approved monitoring protocol; the following reflect what the trials track and what the drug class implies (informational, not medical advice). GI tolerability (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea), especially during initiation/up-titration, is the principal near-term watch-item and the main driver of discontinuation seen in Phase 1. Body weight and waist circumference are the efficacy endpoints. Metabolic labs followed in trials include HbA1c, fasting glucose, fasting lipids, and hsCRP (inflammation). Anti-drug antibodies are monitored as a safety endpoint. Renal function is relevant enough that a dedicated renal-impairment PK study was run. Trial exclusion criteria flag the populations where caution is greatest: history of pancreatitis, significant gastroparesis/gastric-outlet obstruction, uncontrolled thyroid disease, significant cardiovascular disease or clinically significant arrhythmia, and known hypersensitivity to amylin analogs. Anyone considering trial participation should do so only under investigator supervision. No self-directed dosing or titration guidance is provided by design.

Caveats

Petrelintide is investigational — do not treat any figure here as a settled efficacy claim. (1) The headline "up to 8.6% weight loss" comes from a 16-week Phase 1 study designed for safety/PK, not a pivotal efficacy trial; larger, longer, placebo-controlled Phase 2/3 data are what will actually establish how much weight it takes off and how that compares to semaglutide/tirzepatide. (2) The "better tolerability than GLP-1" narrative is a hypothesis supported by low Phase 1 GI event rates, not a head-to-head result — nausea still occurred, and up-titration tolerability at higher/therapeutic exposures needs confirmation. (3) The detailed neuron-level mechanism (Calcr/Prlh cells, RAMP dependence) is derived largely from amylin and cagrilintide in rodents (with cross-species conservation shown into macaque and human tissue), then reasoned onto petrelintide by class; petrelintide-specific circuit data are limited. (4) The leptin-resensitization angle is preclinical and speculative for this drug. (5) Long-term risks that only emerge in large trials — gallbladder events, pancreatitis signals, lean-mass loss, immunogenicity from a peptide, thyroid C-cell signals seen with some agents — are simply not yet characterized for petrelintide. (6) This entry synthesizes published evidence and is not medical advice; no dosing guidance is provided by design.
