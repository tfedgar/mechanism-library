Tongkat Ali

A Malaysian root that nudges your own testosterone system to run more efficiently — mostly by stopping testosterone from being converted into estrogen — rather than adding hormone from outside.

Limited human data 24 citations
Class
Herbal adaptogen / phytoandrogen (quassinoid-rich root extract); active marker = eurycomanone
Primary target
Aromatase (CYP19A1) in the testes' Leydig cells — the enzyme that turns testosterone into estrogen
Human status
HONEST READ: Unusually for a "T-booster," Tongkat Ali has REAL human RCTs and a 2022 meta-analysis (Leisegang, PMID 36013514, 5 RCTs pooled) showing a statistically significant rise in total testosterone (SMD ~1.35) — but the effect is largest in hypogonadal/older/stressed men and much weaker or absent in young healthy men with normal T (PMID 33559971). A 4-week RCT in stressed adults showed -16% cortisol and improved mood (Talbott, PMID 23705671). Erectile-function benefit is supported by a meta-analysis of RCTs (Kotirum, PMID 26365449). BUT: a broader systematic review of "testosterone boosters" (PMID 37697053) found the overall T-booster evidence inconsistent and many trials small, industry-funded, or using proprietary extracts (e.g. Physta) that don't generalize. The detailed MECHANISM (aromatase/PDE/Leydig cell) is shown mainly in RODENT cells and molecular docking, not proven in living humans. Longevity/anti-aging claims are marketing, not evidence. Bottom line: a genuine but MODEST hormone-normalizer best evidenced in low-T/stressed men — not a steroid-like surge, and not a proven longevity compound.

At a glance

  • Raises serum total testosterone — meaningfully in hypogonadal/older/stressed men, minimally in young men with normal levels (Evidence-human; PMID 36013514)
  • Lowers cortisol and improves mood/stress markers (-16% cortisol over 4 weeks) (Evidence-human; PMID 23705671)
  • Improves erectile function and libido in men with dysfunction (Evidence-human; PMID 26365449)
  • Increases sperm production / fertility (Evidence-rodent for mechanism; some human fertility signal)
  • Proposed free-testosterone release by lowering SHBG binding (Contested / proposed)
  • Ergogenic/muscle and longevity claims are weak or marketing-driven (Speculative)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Eurycomanone is the active quassinoid Established

    The root's punch comes mainly from a bitter compound called eurycomanone (the standardized marker in quality extracts). This is why dose and standardization matter far more than how much raw 'root powder' is in a capsule. PMID 23810842; PMID 36781893

  2. Blocks aromatase, sparing testosterone Evidence-rodent

    Inside the testes' Leydig cells, an enzyme called aromatase normally converts some testosterone into estrogen. Eurycomanone binds aromatase (like the drug formestane does) and slows that conversion — so more testosterone stays as testosterone instead of becoming estrogen. This is the core, best-documented mechanism. PMID 23810842

  3. Boosts steroidogenesis (and PDE at high dose) Evidence-rodent

    Beyond blocking aromatase, eurycomanone directly pushes Leydig cells to make more testosterone. At higher concentrations it also inhibits phosphodiesterase (PDE), an enzyme that breaks down the cellular 'go' signal (cAMP) — keeping the testosterone-production machinery switched on longer. PMID 23810842; PMID 32901880

  4. Acts through the HPG axis Evidence-rodent

    The effect isn't only local: the extract appears to work along the whole brain-to-testes hormone chain (hypothalamus to pituitary to gonads), supporting both testosterone output and sperm production rather than just dumping in a hormone. PMID 23261482

  5. Proposed: frees testosterone from SHBG Contested

    A popular claim is that Tongkat Ali lowers SHBG — the carrier protein that 'handcuffs' testosterone in the blood — releasing more free, usable testosterone. This is a reasonable and often-repeated idea, but in humans it's still a proposed mechanism, not firmly proven. PMID 29975460; PMID 24386995

  6. Lowers cortisol (stress relief) Evidence-human

    Part of why it helps stressed/older men may be indirect: by reducing the stress hormone cortisol, it improves the testosterone-to-cortisol balance. In a human RCT, 4 weeks dropped cortisol ~16% and improved tension, anger and confusion. This 'adaptogen' angle may explain why benefits are biggest in stressed, not young-healthy, people. PMID 23705671

  7. Net result in humans: a modest top-up Evidence-human

    Put together, in real people the payoff is a real but modest rise in testosterone — clearest in men who are low, older, or stressed, and minimal in young men already running normal levels. It normalizes rather than supercharges. PMID 36013514; PMID 33559971

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Tongkat Ali is a multi-compound root extract; its lead quassinoid is EURYCOMANONE (the most abundant), with eurycomanol, eurycomalactone, and canthin-6-one alkaloids (e.g. 9-methoxycanthin-6-one) as minor actives. CERTAINTY ON BINDING TARGETS IS LOW: eurycomanone is NOT catalogued in ChEMBL (compound_search returned 0 hits this run), so there are NO curated, experimentally measured Ki/Kd/IC50 affinity constants for any human receptor or enzyme. The mechanistic targets below are inferred from cell assays and computational docking, NOT from purified-enzyme inhibition constants. (1) AROMATASE (CYP19A1): Evidence-in vitro — in isolated rat Leydig-cell-rich interstitial cells, eurycomanone raised testosterone and LOWERED oestrogen output, and in molecular docking it bound the aromatase active site 'with similar orientations and free-energy binding values' to formestane (a known steroidal aromatase inhibitor). This is presented as a competitive/steroidal-type inhibition by analogy, but NO numerical Ki was reported [PMID 23810842]. (2) PHOSPHODIESTERASE (PDE): Evidence-in vitro — at high concentration eurycomanone behaved like a PDE inhibitor (its testosterone-raising effect was non-additive with the PDE inhibitor IBMX, and docking placed the two at DIFFERENT sites of the enzyme), implying eurycomanone may raise intracellular cAMP in Leydig cells [PMID 23810842]. (3) Eurycomanone showed NO reversal by aminoglutethimide, ketoconazole or nifedipine and was NON-responsive to the oestrogen-receptor blocker tamoxifen — arguing AGAINST it acting as a direct ER ligand/SERM and against blocking the early P450scc/CYP17 steroidogenic enzymes [PMID 23810842]. (4) SHBG: Contested/Evidence-human-mixed — the doping-control literature notes the testosterone-boosting effect 'might be caused by release of free testosterone from sex-hormone-binding globulin' [PMID 29975460], but the human RCTs that measured SHBG found NO significant change (young men [PMID 33559971]; ageing men on Physta [PMID 34262417]), so a direct SHBG-displacement mechanism is unproven in vivo. (5) Rho-kinase II (ROCK-II): Evidence-in vitro — an aqueous extract inhibited ROCK-II with IC50 651.1 ± 32.9 ng/mL, a plausible contributor to erectile/smooth-muscle effects [PMID 31223329].

Signalling cascade. Proposed net cascade (each step certainty-labelled). At the LEYDIG CELL (Evidence-rodent/in vitro): eurycomanone increases testosterone biosynthesis — partly by inhibiting local aromatase so that synthesized testosterone is NOT converted to oestradiol (shifting the T:E balance upward), and at higher concentrations by PDE inhibition that elevates cAMP, the second messenger downstream of LH receptor signalling that drives StAR-mediated cholesterol transport and steroidogenic enzyme activity [PMID 23810842]. A standardised extract (F2) raised Leydig-cell testosterone as strongly as eurycomanone or formestane, and transcriptomics flagged CDKL2 (a cyclin-dependent-kinase-like gene) and cell-cycle genes as differentially expressed, with reduced Leydig-cell viability at 72 h that recovered by 96 h — i.e. the extract perturbs Leydig-cell proliferation, not only hormone output [PMID 32901880]. At the HPG/HPA AXIS (Evidence-human, mixed): in ageing/hypogonadal men total and free testosterone rise [PMID 34262417], whereas in healthy YOUNG men total and free testosterone and oestradiol rose but LH, FSH and SHBG did NOT change — the authors infer the rise is driven more via the hypothalamic-pituitary-ADRENAL axis (and reduced cortisol) than via classic HPG (LH-driven) stimulation [PMID 33559971]. The most reproducible human signal is a CORTISOL-LOWERING / stress-axis effect: salivary cortisol fell ~16% and testosterone status rose ~37% in moderately stressed adults [PMID 23705671], and serum cortisol fell significantly in ageing men on 200 mg Physta [PMID 34262417]. Net: a phytoandrogen whose human testosterone effect is small-to-moderate and appears to work as much by REDUCING cortisol/glucocorticoid drag and shifting the androgen:oestrogen ratio as by directly stimulating gonadal steroidogenesis.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral, as standardised root extract — typically water/aqueous extracts (e.g. Physta, 'F2') standardised to a % of eurycomanone, or powdered whole root. No injectable/clinical formulation exists; it is sold as a dietary supplement.
Absorption / bioavailability
POOR oral bioavailability of the lead compound. Evidence-rodent: in rats the ABSOLUTE oral bioavailability of eurycomanone was only ~10.5% (AUC oral vs IV, dose-normalised); after oral dosing Cmax was ~0.33 ± 0.03 µg/mL at Tmax ~4.4 h. The poor absorption is attributed to low membrane permeability (low logP / high polarity) and/or high first-pass metabolism, NOT chemical instability (eurycomanone is pH-stable) [PMID 16206032]. NO validated human pharmacokinetic parameters for eurycomanone exist — this is a major data gap; human PK is inferred from rat data only.
Half-life
Evidence-rodent only: after IV dosing in rats the elimination half-life (t1/2) of eurycomanone was ~1.0 ± 0.26 h with plasma levels falling to zero by ~8 h; elimination rate constant ~0.88/h, clearance ~0.39 L/h/kg [PMID 16206032]. The short half-life suggests effects depend on repeated/divided dosing. Human half-life is UNKNOWN.
Metabolism
Evidence-in vitro (human/animal liver microsomes): minor canthinone and lactone constituents undergo Phase I hydroxylation and O-demethylation plus Phase II glucuronidation — e.g. 9-methoxycanthin-6-one is O-demethylated and 9-hydroxycanthin-6-one is glucuronidated; these metabolites match those seen in human urine/serum after ingestion and are proposed as doping-control markers [PMID 29975460]. Specific CYP isoenzymes were not definitively assigned. Eurycomanone itself appears subject to high first-pass metabolism inferred from its low oral bioavailability [PMID 16206032].
Excretion
Renal/urinary excretion of metabolites is documented (9-methoxycanthin-6-one, its O-demethyl and glucuronide conjugates detectable in human urine and serum) [PMID 29975460]. Relatively high volume of distribution in rats (Vd ~0.68 L/kg) implies extravascular distribution [PMID 16206032]. Quantitative human excretion balance is not reported.
Notes
Eurycomanone is a polar quassinoid (no ester/prodrug strategy in marketed products; semisynthetic 15-O-acyl esters have been made experimentally to improve lipophilicity/antimalarial activity [PMID 16254833], but these are not the supplement). BBB penetration is not characterised. CLINICALLY RELEVANT HERB-DRUG INTERACTION (Evidence-human RCT): co-administration of a 200 mg water extract reduced the bioavailability of oral propranolol — AUC down ~29%, Cmax down ~42%, Tmax prolonged ~86% — via reduced ABSORPTION (not increased metabolism), so Tongkat Ali can blunt absorption of co-ingested oral drugs [PMID 21054461]. Product standardisation varies enormously between brands [PMID 29154863].

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Human dose-response is only loosely defined and confounded by extract heterogeneity (different brands standardise to different eurycomanone %). HUMAN (Evidence-human): The best dose-controlled trial is the Physta ageing-male RCT — 100 mg vs 200 mg vs placebo daily for 12 weeks in men 50-70 with baseline testosterone <300 ng/dL: 200 mg produced an earlier and more significant rise in total testosterone (significant by week 4, p<0.01-0.001) than 100 mg (significant only by week 12), and only the 200 mg arm significantly lowered cortisol — a genuine dose-effect signal favouring ~200 mg [PMID 34262417]. In healthy YOUNG men, 600 mg/day for 2 weeks raised total and free testosterone and oestradiol without moving LH/FSH/SHBG, and the authors described the steroidogenic effect as 'dose-related' [PMID 33559971]. The stress trial used a standardised hot-water extract for 4 weeks and reported ~+37% testosterone status and ~-16% cortisol [PMID 23705671]. The pooled meta-analytic effect across 5 RCTs was a standardised mean difference of 1.352 (95% CI 0.565-2.138) for total testosterone — a moderate-to-large effect, but heterogeneous and strongest in hypogonadal men [PMID 36013514]. The magnitude in absolute ng/dL terms and a true threshold/ceiling dose remain undefined in humans. RODENT (Evidence-rodent): classic rat studies used 200-800 mg/kg of various solvent fractions, with 800 mg/kg butanol/methanol fractions producing the clearest androgen-like effects on levator ani muscle and sexual behaviour [PMID 11693547; PMID 10803359]. These mg/kg rodent doses are far above human supplement exposures and do not translate directly. NO well-powered human dose-finding study exists.

How it compares

  • vs Pharmaceutical aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole, formestane/exemestane)
    same: Eurycomanone is proposed to inhibit aromatase (CYP19A1), like these drugs, and docked into the active site comparably to formestane — net effect of shifting the testosterone:oestrogen ratio upward [PMID 23810842].
    different: The pharmaceuticals have defined, potent, validated enzyme-level inhibition constants and reliably suppress oestradiol in humans; Tongkat Ali's aromatase inhibition is in-vitro/docking only with NO human oestradiol suppression demonstrated (oestradiol actually rose in young men [PMID 33559971]) and no Ki. It is a weak, multi-target botanical, not a clinical AI, and is not used or approved for oestrogen-driven disease.
  • vs Exogenous testosterone / TRT
    same: Both raise circulating testosterone and are used for symptoms of low testosterone / ADAM (fatigue, libido, erectile function) [PMID 34262417; PMID 33541567].
    different: TRT delivers exogenous hormone with large, predictable increases and suppresses the HPG axis (lowers LH/FSH, can impair fertility); Tongkat Ali produces a small endogenous rise WITHOUT lowering LH/FSH and without SHBG change [PMID 33559971], so it does not shut down the axis. Effect size is far smaller and more variable, and unlike TRT it is an unregulated supplement, not a prescription hormone.
  • vs Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera)
    same: Both are adaptogenic herbal supplements marketed for testosterone/vitality whose most reproducible human signal is CORTISOL reduction and stress/mood improvement rather than a large direct androgenic effect [PMID 23705671; PMID 34262417].
    different: Mechanistically distinct (ashwagandha works via GABAergic/stress-axis modulation; Tongkat Ali via proposed aromatase/PDE/steroidogenic effects). Direct head-to-head comparative trials are lacking, so relative potency is unestablished.
  • vs Fadogia agrestis (the common 'stack' partner)
    same: Frequently combined in PED/biohacker stacks as a 'natural testosterone' duo; both claim pro-androgenic, pro-libido effects in rodents.
    different: Fadogia has essentially NO human RCT evidence and documented testicular-toxicity signals in rodents, whereas Tongkat Ali has multiple human RCTs and a meta-analysis [PMID 36013514] plus reassuring subchronic rodent toxicology [PMID 24062779]. The evidence asymmetry is large; combining them is not studied for safety.

History &amp; legal status

"Origin: Eurycoma longifolia Jack (family Simaroubaceae), 'Tongkat Ali' ('Ali's walking stick') in Malay, also 'Malaysian ginseng' and 'Longjack'; a small understorey tree native to Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand and Indochina. Centuries of traditional South-East-Asian use of the root decoction for fever, hypertension, malaria, fatigue and as an aphrodisiac/male tonic [PMID 26978330; PMID 20434529]. Modern development centres on standardised aqueous extracts (notably 'Physta', a patented water extract from Biotropics Malaysia, and research fraction 'F2'), driven by Malaysian government/university programmes. Regulatory status: sold globally as a DIETARY SUPPLEMENT / botanical, NOT an approved drug anywhere — no FDA/EMA drug approval for any indication; in the US it falls under DSHEA supplement rules (no pre-market efficacy review). It is on no major controlled-substance or banned list and is NOT prohibited by WADA; however, because it raises testosterone and active constituents (e.g. 9-methoxycanthin-6-one) appear in urine, anti-doping labs have characterised metabolite markers to detect use and athletes are cautioned about contamination/over-the-line testosterone effects [PMID 29975460]. In Malaysia it is regulated as a traditional medicine with a defined acceptable daily intake (~1.2 g/day powdered root derived from toxicology) [PMID 24062779]. Major practical issue: wide product variability and adulteration, prompting analytical-standardisation methods [PMID 29154863]."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No human pharmacokinetics: every PK parameter (bioavailability, half-life, Cmax) is from rats; the ~10.5% oral bioavailability and ~1 h half-life of eurycomanone [PMID 16206032] have never been confirmed in humans, so the active plasma exposure achieved by a supplement dose is unknown.
  • Is aromatase inhibition real in vivo? The mechanism rests on in-vitro Leydig-cell data and molecular docking against formestane [PMID 23810842]; there is no human study showing Tongkat Ali lowers oestradiol — and one RCT showed oestradiol RISING [PMID 33559971]. No purified-enzyme Ki/IC50 for human CYP19A1 exists.
  • Which constituent and which target actually drive the human testosterone effect — eurycomanone vs eurycomanol vs canthinone alkaloids — and via HPG vs HPA (cortisol-lowering) routes, remain unresolved [PMID 33559971; PMID 34262417].
  • True dose-response and ceiling in humans: only a 100-vs-200 mg comparison exists [PMID 34262417]; the minimally effective dose, optimal eurycomanone standardisation %, and whether higher doses add benefit or toxicity are undefined.
  • Long-term safety in humans: trials run 2-24 weeks; chronic (>1 year) endocrine, hepatic, haematologic (the rodent clotting-time shortening [PMID 24062779]) and fertility outcomes are unstudied.
  • Magnitude and durability of effect: meta-analytic SMD is moderate-to-large but heterogeneous and partly industry-sponsored [PMID 36013514]; the absolute ng/dL change, who responds, and whether effects persist or wane with continued use are open.
  • Herb-drug interaction breadth: the propranolol absorption interaction [PMID 21054461] is the only one characterised — generalisability to other oral drugs is untested.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (NOT a dosing protocol). Bloodwork worth tracking around use: a full male hormone panel — total and free testosterone, oestradiol (because the net oestrogen direction is unpredictable: aromatase inhibition is only in-vitro, and oestradiol rose in young men [PMID 33559971; PMID 23810842]), SHBG (RCTs show it usually doesn't move [PMID 34262417; PMID 33559971], so an unexpected change is a flag), and LH/FSH (these should stay roughly stable; suppression would be atypical and worth investigating). Watch cortisol/stress markers — a fall is the expected/'on-target' effect [PMID 23705671; PMID 34262417]. Safety labs to baseline and recheck: liver function (AST/ALT) and renal function (BUN/creatinine) — reassuring in rodent toxicology but unmonitored long-term in humans [PMID 24062779]; complete blood count and a coagulation panel (PT/PTT), given the rodent signal of shortened clotting times [PMID 24062779]. Clinical signs to watch: sleep disturbance/over-stimulation, mood or irritability changes, and any signs of androgen excess (acne, aggression, in women virilising signs). Drug-interaction vigilance: because the extract reduced ORAL drug absorption (propranolol AUC -29%, Cmax -42% [PMID 21054461]), anyone on oral medications — especially narrow-therapeutic-index or cardiovascular drugs — should be alert to reduced drug effect and separate timing / consult a clinician. Product-quality vigilance: choose third-party-tested, eurycomanone-standardised products because commercial content and purity vary widely [PMID 29154863]. Athletes: be aware testosterone-raising effects and detectable urinary metabolites can intersect with anti-doping testing [PMID 29975460]."

Caveats

EVIDENCE QUALITY: The detailed mechanism (aromatase + PDE inhibition, Leydig-cell steroidogenesis, HPG axis) is shown in rodent cells and molecular docking (PMID 23810842, 23261482, 32901880) — NOT directly proven in living humans. Human RCTs measure outcomes (testosterone, cortisol, ED) but not the molecular steps. EFFECT SIZE IS POPULATION-DEPENDENT: meta-analysis shows real total-T gains, but driven by hypogonadal/older/stressed men; young men with normal T see little (PMID 36013514, 33559971). SHBG-lowering / free-T release is a widely repeated but still PROPOSED mechanism, not established in humans (PMID 29975460). TRIAL LIMITATIONS: many trials are small, short, use proprietary standardized extracts (e.g. Physta, PMID 34262417) that may not match commercial products, and some are industry-linked; a broad systematic review of T-boosters found the field inconsistent (PMID 37697053). PRODUCT VARIABILITY: commercial 'Tongkat Ali' varies hugely in eurycomanone content and can be adulterated/contaminated (heavy metals) — standardization and third-party testing matter (PMID 36781893, 39369924). LONGEVITY/ANTI-AGING and most ergogenic (muscle/strength) claims are SPECULATIVE — no human longevity data; muscle-mass evidence is thin. NOT MEDICAL ADVICE: don't position as hormone therapy; potential interactions with hormone-sensitive conditions and medications are under-studied.
