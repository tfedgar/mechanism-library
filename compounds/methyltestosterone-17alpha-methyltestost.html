The original oral anabolic steroid: a single methyl group bolted onto testosterone lets it survive the liver and reach the bloodstream by mouth, but that same trick is what makes it notoriously hard on the liver and crushing to your HDL.

Class
Oral 17alpha-alkylated (specifically 17alpha-methylated) anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS); synthetic testosterone derivative
Primary target

Human status
Established human drug. FDA-approved since 1973 and still marketed (e.g. as part of estrogen-androgen HRT in women and for male hypogonadism), so unlike many "designer" steroids there IS genuine human pharmacology and decades of clinical/toxicology data. HOWEVER: the supraphysiologic, stacked, long-term bodybuilding-style use that forum culture revolves around is NOT what those approvals studied. The liver-toxicity, lipid, and insulin-resistance signals below come from real human data, but dose-for-dose performance data in healthy lifters is thin — most "gains" claims are anecdote, and the muscle-building dose-response framework comes from injectable testosterone, not from oral methyltestosterone specifically.

At a glance

  • AR-mediated anabolic signaling: increased muscle protein synthesis and lean mass (effect is testosterone-class and dose-dependent in human RCTs of testosterone; Bhasin PMID 11701431)
  • Androgenic effects: virilization, libido, secondary sex characteristics — same receptor, can't be separated out
  • Oral bioavailability: the 17alpha-methyl group blocks first-pass hepatic breakdown, which is the entire point of the molecule
  • Hepatotoxicity: cholestatic jaundice, peliosis hepatis (blood-filled liver cavities), hepatic adenomas/tumors — directly documented for long-term methyltestosterone in humans (Westaby PMID 69876; Petrovic/WJG PMID 36051334)
  • Adverse lipid shift: marked suppression of HDL ('good') cholesterol — a hallmark of oral 17-alkylated AAS, inversely dose-dependent with androgen exposure (Bhasin PMID 11701431; WJG PMID 36051334)
  • Insulin resistance: reduced whole-body glucose uptake demonstrated in women on short-term methyltestosterone (Diamond PMID 9851788)
  • Possible estrogenic effects via aromatization to 17alpha-methylestradiol (mechanism shown in fish, not confirmed quantitatively in humans — treat as Contested) (Hornung PMID 14687976)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps3 human 3 established 1 contested / speculative

  1. The 17-alpha-methyl group = the oral trick (and the original sin) Established

    Plain testosterone taken by mouth is almost useless — the liver destroys it on the first pass before it can work. Chemists added a single methyl (CH3) group at the 17-alpha position. That bulky group physically shields the molecule from the liver enzymes that would normally inactivate it, so it survives, enters the blood, and stays active orally. This one structural change defines the whole '17-alpha-alkylated' steroid family — and it's also exactly why these compounds are the ones that damage the liver. CHEMBL1395 (17-methyltestosterone, oral=true); El-Desoky 2016, PMID 26639430; Westaby 1977, PMID 69876

  2. It's just testosterone wearing a hat — same receptor Established

    Despite the modification, methyltestosterone is still recognized by the androgen receptor (AR), the same protein natural testosterone binds. It is a full AR agonist. There is no special 'methyltestosterone receptor' — every anabolic and androgenic effect runs through this one shared lock-and-key system, which is why you cannot get the muscle effect without the androgenic baggage. Brinkmann 2011, PMID 21796517; Naamneh Elzenaty 2022, PMID 35595638

  3. The genomic mechanism: the drug becomes a gene switch Established

    Here's the core mechanism. The AR normally sits dormant in the cell, held by chaperone proteins. When methyltestosterone (or testosterone) binds it, the receptor changes shape, releases the chaperones, pairs up with a second copy of itself (dimerizes), and moves into the cell nucleus. There it docks onto specific DNA sequences called androgen response elements and acts as a transcription factor — recruiting co-activator proteins and switching ON genes. In muscle, that means turning up the genes for protein synthesis. This is the 'genomic' pathway: the effect is slow (hours to days) because it works by changing which proteins the cell manufactures, not by an instant chemical jolt. Brinkmann 2011, PMID 21796517; Naamneh Elzenaty 2022, PMID 35595638

  4. Net result in muscle: more protein laid down, dose-dependently Evidence-human

    Because the activated AR ramps up protein-synthesis genes, the muscle builds more contractile protein than it breaks down, so lean mass and strength rise. In rigorously controlled human studies of testosterone, this gain is clearly dose-dependent — higher blood androgen levels produce bigger increases in fat-free mass and strength. Important honesty caveat for the channel: this dose-response was established with injectable testosterone, not with oral methyltestosterone, and supraphysiologic-dose data in healthy lifters is limited. The mechanism is the same drug class, but the precise numbers are an extrapolation. Bhasin 2001, PMID 11701431

  5. Why the liver suffers: the methyl group that saved it orally now poisons the liver Evidence-human

    The same 17-alpha-methyl group that lets the drug survive the liver also makes the liver process it abnormally. In humans on long-term methyltestosterone, this produces a recognizable pattern: cholestasis (bile backs up, causing jaundice), peliosis hepatis (the liver develops blood-filled cavities), and benign or malignant liver tumors. A 1977 Lancet study of 60 patients on long-term methyltestosterone found abnormal liver function in many and early peliosis on biopsy — and explicitly concluded that only the 17-alpha-alkylated androgens cause this. Modern reviews attribute the damage to disrupted antioxidant defenses, up-regulated bile-acid synthesis, and abnormal hepatocyte overgrowth. Westaby 1977, PMID 69876; Petrovic 2022 (World J Gastroenterol), PMID 36051334

  6. The lipid hit: HDL gets crushed Evidence-human

    Oral 17-alkylated steroids are especially brutal on cholesterol. They drive your HDL ('good' cholesterol) down — an effect thought to involve increased hepatic lipase activity in the liver. In Bhasin's controlled human testosterone study, HDL fell as androgen exposure rose (an inverse dose-response). Oral 17-alpha-alkylated agents like methyltestosterone are generally considered worse for lipids than injectable testosterone because they pass through and act heavily on the liver, where lipid handling is controlled. This is a major, often-underappreciated cardiovascular concern that anecdote tends to ignore. Bhasin 2001, PMID 11701431; Petrovic 2022, PMID 36051334

  7. Side branches: insulin resistance and a possible estrogen route Contested

    Two extra threads. (1) Insulin resistance: in women given short-term methyltestosterone, whole-body glucose uptake dropped measurably — direct human evidence that androgen excess can blunt insulin sensitivity. (2) Estrogenic effects: methyltestosterone can in principle be aromatized to 17-alpha-methylestradiol, an estrogen. This conversion was demonstrated mechanistically in fish — NOT quantified in humans — so the degree of estrogenic activity in people is genuinely uncertain. Forums sometimes overstate this; the honest label is Contested, because the strong mechanistic data is non-mammalian. Diamond 1998, PMID 9851788 (insulin, human); Hornung 2004, PMID 14687976 (aromatization, fish/in-vitro)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. PRIMARY TARGET — Androgen receptor (AR / NR3C4), a ligand-activated nuclear-receptor transcription factor; methyltestosterone is a full AR AGONIST acting through the same receptor as testosterone, not a novel target [Established; ChEMBL CHEMBL1395; Brinkmann 2011 PMID 21796517; Naamneh Elzenaty 2022 PMID 35595638]. AFFINITY (the honest numbers): methyltestosterone is a MODERATE-to-WEAK AR binder relative to the natural ligands. In Saartok's classic rat/rabbit competition assay (radioligand [3H]methyltrienolone/R1881), the AR relative-binding-affinity rank was methyltrienolone(R1881) >> nandrolone > methenolone > testosterone > 1α-methyl-DHT, with 17α-methyltestosterone binding the AR only modestly and binding SHBG WEAKLY (rank for SHBG: 1α-methyl-DHT >> DHT > T > 3β-adiol > 3α-adiol ≈ 17α-methyl-T) [Evidence-rodent; Saartok 1984 PMID 6539197]. So two practical points: (1) the 17α-methyl group does NOT boost AR affinity above testosterone — its purpose is oral survival, not potency at the receptor; (2) methyltestosterone binds the carrier protein SHBG poorly, leaving more drug free and also DISPLACING/suppressing SHBG, which raises free fractions of co-circulating androgens. No quantitative human Ki/Kd specific to methyltestosterone-AR is established — published affinities are rodent-tissue RBAs, not human Ki. SECONDARY/OFF-TARGET — methyltestosterone is itself a competitive inhibitor of human aromatase (CYP19A1) in cell culture (complete inhibition near 10^-4 M, kinetics paralleling letrozole) and binds the aromatase active site (type-I docking), i.e. it occupies an enzyme rather than activating it [Evidence-human/in vitro; Mor 2001 PMID 11850230; Hong 2008 PMID 18184606].

Signalling cascade. Canonical AR genomic pathway (class-level for testosterone/AR; assumed shared by this AR-agonist analog): (1) Lipophilic methyltestosterone (ALogP ~4.27, PSA 37.3 Å², MW 302.46) crosses the plasma membrane by diffusion [ChEMBL CHEMBL1395 calculated ADMET]. (2) Unlike testosterone, the 17α-methyl group blocks 17β-HSD oxidation of the 17β-OH, so the molecule stays in its active 17β-ol androgen form and is NOT reduced to a 17-keto product [Evidence-in vitro; class data Saartok 1984 PMID 6539197]. (3) It binds cytoplasmic AR, which is held inactive by HSP90/HSP70 chaperones; ligand binding triggers a conformational change, chaperone release, receptor N/C-terminal interaction and homodimerization [Established; Brinkmann 2011 PMID 21796517; Naamneh Elzenaty 2022 PMID 35595638]. (4) The dimer translocates to the nucleus and docks on androgen-response elements (AREs) in target-gene promoters. (5) It recruits coactivators (e.g. SRC/p160 family) and the basal transcription machinery, switching ON androgen-responsive genes — in skeletal muscle this up-regulates contractile/structural protein synthesis genes, shifting net protein balance toward accretion [Established; Brinkmann 2011 PMID 21796517]. (6) Effect is SLOW (hours-to-days) because it works by changing which proteins the cell manufactures, not by a fast second-messenger jolt — the defining feature of the genomic mechanism. Same receptor drives BOTH anabolic and androgenic outputs, which is why virilization cannot be engineered away from muscle growth.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (tablets) and historically buccal/sublingual; the molecule is the prototype ORAL AAS. Buccal/sublingual routes were used to partly bypass first-pass metabolism. It is NOT an ester and NOT a prodrug — it is itself the active androgen (contrast with injectable testosterone esters or lipophilic oral prodrugs like testosterone undecanoate).
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapidly absorbed from the GI tract; serum concentrations peak ~1–2 h after an oral methyltestosterone dose [textbook/PMC AAS review; Established]. The 17α-methyl group is what confers meaningful oral bioavailability — plain oral testosterone is almost inactive because the liver destroys it on first pass. Physicochemistry is drug-like and orally compatible: ALogP 4.27, PSA 37.3 Å², 0 H-bond donors-excess, 0 rotatable bonds, 0 Lipinski violations, QED 0.73 [ChEMBL CHEMBL1395]. A precise published % oral bioavailability figure specific to methyltestosterone is not well established in the retrieved evidence — the 'orally active' claim is structural/empirical, not a single validated F% number.
Half-life
Short — on the order of a few hours (commonly cited ~2.5–4 h); requires multiple daily dosing to sustain levels, consistent with the 1–2 h peak and rapid clearance. Exact human t1/2 is not pinned to a high-quality citation in the retrieved corpus; treat as approximate [low-certainty].
Metabolism
Hepatic. The 17α-methyl group sterically RETARDS (does not abolish) hepatic inactivation, which is the entire basis of oral activity AND of hepatotoxicity [Established; Bond 2016 PMID 27372877]. It blocks 17β-HSD oxidation of the 17β-OH so no 17-keto metabolites form; the steroid is instead cleared via A-ring reduction (5α/5β reductases, 3-keto reduction) and conjugation. Shares characteristic urinary metabolites (fully reduced A-ring 17,17-dimethyl-18-nor structures) with mestanolone and other 17α-methyl AAS, the basis of doping tests [Drug Test Anal 2023 PMID 37903531; class metabolite work]. Methyltestosterone is itself a competitive aromatase (CYP19A1) inhibitor rather than a good aromatase substrate, so it does not meaningfully raise serum estradiol in men [Evidence-human; Friedl 1990 PMID 2294373; Mor 2001 PMID 11850230].
Excretion
Renal excretion of conjugated (glucuronide/sulfate) reduced metabolites; detectable in urine as the characteristic reduced 17α-methyl metabolites used in sports anti-doping [Drug Test Anal 2023 PMID 37903531].
Notes
Not esterified, not a prodrug — direct oral androgen. Crosses cell membranes freely (lipophilic). CNS access: AAS act centrally (e.g., dorsomedial hypothalamus infusion studies show behavioral effects), consistent with CNS penetration of this lipophilic steroid Evidence-rodent. The same first-pass-resistance that makes it oral also concentrates its action on the liver — the mechanistic root of its hepatotoxic and HDL-crushing profile.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

HUMAN MOOD/BEHAVIOR dose-response IS established for this exact compound: in the NIH placebo-controlled crossover trial (n=20 healthy men), 40 mg/d (low) produced little change, whereas 240 mg/d (high) produced statistically significant increases in positive mood (euphoria, energy, sexual arousal), negative mood (irritability, hostility, violent feelings) and cognitive impairment; 1/20 (5%) had a frank manic episode and another became hypomanic — i.e., a clear dose-threshold for neuropsychiatric activation [Evidence-human; Su 1993 PMID 8492402]. HUMAN LIPID dose-response (compound-specific): methyltestosterone 20 mg BID x12 wk drove HDL-C DOWN (~1.20→0.77 mmol/L by 4 wk), raised hepatic triglyceride lipase ~+102%, lowered apoA-I and raised LDL-C — and notably did NOT raise serum estradiol (behaving like the aromatase-inhibited arm) [Evidence-human; Friedl 1990 PMID 2294373]. HUMAN INSULIN dose-response: methyltestosterone 5 mg TID x10–12 days in women reduced whole-body glucose uptake (~10.96→7.3 mg/kg/min) — androgen excess induces insulin resistance even short-term [Evidence-human; Diamond 1998 PMID 9851788]. HUMAN HYPOGONADISM efficacy: 1-month oral methyltestosterone in 22 hypogonadal impotent men produced supraphysiologic TOTAL testosterone but free testosterone often FELL and clinical response was poor (only ~9% full recovery) — a dose-response disconnect driven by SHBG effects [Evidence-human; Morales 1994 PMID 8072077]. WHERE IT IS UNKNOWN: there is NO rigorous human dose-response for MUSCLE/strength gain from oral methyltestosterone specifically. The dose-dependent fat-free-mass and strength curve (e.g., +3.4/+5.2/+7.9 kg FFM at 125/300/600 mg weekly) comes from INJECTABLE TESTOSTERONE [Bhasin 2001 PMID 11701431] and is a same-class EXTRAPOLATION, not direct methyltestosterone data. RODENT: methyltestosterone restores aggression in castrated male rats to testosterone-propionate levels [Evidence-rodent; PMID 7824579]. Bottom line: mood, lipid and insulin dose-responses are real human signals for this compound; the anabolic dose-response is borrowed from testosterone and should not be presented as measured for methyltestosterone.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Testosterone (injectable/native)
    same: Identical primary target (AR agonist) and identical genomic cascade; both drive anabolic + androgenic effects through one receptor; both aromatize-related discussions apply to the parent ring system
    different: Testosterone is destroyed orally (needs injection/esters); it is freely aromatized to estradiol and is NOT meaningfully hepatotoxic. Methyltestosterone is orally active (17α-methyl), is a competitive aromatase INHIBITOR rather than substrate (does not raise serum E2), binds AR no better than testosterone, and is markedly hepatotoxic + worse for HDL [Friedl 1990 PMID 2294373; Mor 2001 PMID 11850230; Westaby 1977 PMID 69876]
  • vs Methandienone / Dianabol (17α-methyl-boldenone)
    same: Same 17α-methyl oral-activation trick; same 17α-alkyl hepatotoxicity and HDL-crash class profile; both retain 17β-ol (no 17-keto shunt)
    different: Dianabol has a Δ1 A-ring (added double bond) that alters aromatization toward a potent, metabolically-resistant 17α-methylestradiol, making it more estrogenic/gyno-prone in practice; methyltestosterone instead largely inhibits aromatase and does not raise serum estradiol in men [Friedl 1990 PMID 2294373; example file aromatase analysis]
  • vs Stanozolol (Winstrol, 17α-methyl pyrazole)
    same: 17α-methylated oral AAS; hepatotoxic class; directly toxic to hepatocytes in the same rat-hepatocyte assay as methyltestosterone
    different: Stanozolol binds the AR very WEAKLY (RBA <0.05 in Saartok) yet is strongly anabolic via non-classical/SHBG mechanisms; it is non-aromatizable and behaviorally was ineffective at eliciting aggression in castrated rats where methyltestosterone matched testosterone [Saartok 1984 PMID 6539197; Welder 1995 PMID 8527826; PMID 7824579]
  • vs Fluoxymesterone (Halotestin, 9α-fluoro-11β-OH-17α-methyl-T)
    same: Close structural relative — methyltestosterone + 9α-fluoro/11β-hydroxyl; 17α-methylated oral AAS, strongly hepatotoxic, non-aromatizable
    different: Fluoxymesterone is far more potent/androgenic per mg despite weak direct AR binding (RBA <0.05 in Saartok), with a higher androgenic:anabolic punch and reputation for harsh lipid/liver effects [Saartok 1984 PMID 6539197]

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Anadrol adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Two mechanisms compound. (1) Both are 17aa oral AAS, so hepatotoxicity (cholestasis, peliosis) is additive on the liver. (2) Both are AR agonists that stimulate erythropoiesis; Anadrol is a notably strong hematocrit-raiser, so co-use pushes red-cell mass and blood viscosity higher than methyltestosterone alone, compounding thrombosis risk. MoA card Erythropoiesis; MoA card Hepatotoxicity (17aa orals); Non-ischemic cardiomyopathy & polycythemia AAS PMID 39280364; Bhasin 2001 dose-dependent hemoglobin PMID 11701431 Watch: Hemoglobin and hematocrit for erythrocytosis (viscosity/clot risk); full liver panel (ALT/AST/GGT/ALP/bilirubin) and jaundice signs. Watch blood pressure and headaches.
  • Ligandrol adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both suppress HDL cholesterol through overlapping AR-driven hepatic mechanisms. Methyltestosterone (oral 17aa) sharply induces hepatic lipase, dropping HDL ~35-40% and raising LDL; LGD-4033 similarly lowers HDL via AR activation altering hepatic lipase/lipoprotein metabolism in the liver. Co-use compounds the atherogenic HDL drop beyond either alone. Both also suppress the HPG axis, deepening endogenous shutdown. methyltestosterone monograph (hepatic lipase HDL crash); Friedl 1990 methyltestosterone HDL crash + hepatic lipase +102% PMID 2294373; ligandrol monograph side_effect_mechanisms (AR-driven hepatic lipase HDL lowering, RCT) Watch: Fasting lipid panel (HDL, LDL, ApoB) to track the compounded HDL suppression; LH/FSH and testosterone for axis suppression. A collapsing HDL is a signal to seek evaluation.
  • MK-677 adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    MK-677 (GHSR-1a agonist) raises GH/IGF-1, which induces insulin resistance and raises fasting glucose. Methyltestosterone independently reduces whole-body insulin-stimulated glucose uptake (demonstrated directly in humans, ~33% drop). Stacked, the GH-driven and androgen-driven insulin resistance compound, worsening glucose tolerance more than either alone. Diamond 1998 methyltestosterone reduced glucose uptake PMID 9851788; methyltestosterone monograph side_effect_mechanisms (insulin resistance); MK-677 monograph (GH-induced insulin resistance) Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c, fasting insulin/HOMA-IR; symptoms of hyperglycemia. Watch for lethargy or increased hunger from MK-677.
  • Superdrol adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Both are 17-alpha-alkylated oral AAS. The 17aa group that lets each survive first-pass metabolism also forces slow, abnormal hepatic processing that impairs canalicular bile transport (BSEP/ABCB11, MRP2) and drives oxidative stress, producing cholestasis, peliosis, and adenoma risk. Running two 17aa orals together stacks the same hepatotoxic hit on one organ, and Superdrol is among the most hepatotoxic of the class (bland canalicular cholestasis with high bilirubin), so the additive cholestatic/hepatocellular burden is greater than either alone. MoA card Hepatotoxicity (17aa orals) [BSEP/ABCB11, MRP2]; Anabolic Steroid Effect on the Liver PMID 29521706; Welder 1995 rat hepatocyte toxicity PMID 8527826; Westaby 1977 PMID 69876 Watch: Baseline and periodic ALT/AST plus GGT and ALP, total and direct bilirubin; watch for jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, itching, right-upper-quadrant pain. Pair transaminases with GGT to distinguish liver from muscle/training rises.
  • Trenbolone adds to a side effect ⚠ Speculative
    Both are non-aromatizing androgens (trenbolone is a 19-nor that does not aromatize; methyltestosterone acts as an aromatase inhibitor and does not raise serum estradiol), and both suppress the HPG axis. Stacked, neither supplies estrogen, so the pair can drive a combined low-estradiol state (impaired libido, erectile and joint/mood symptoms) while endogenous production is doubly shut down. This is a compounding effect, not a genuine opposition; the forum idea that methyltestosterone can 'supply estrogen' to trenbolone is unfounded because methyltestosterone is itself estrogen-lowering. Trenbolone's gynecomastia risk, when present, is progestin/prolactin-driven and is not offset by methyltestosterone. Friedl 1990 methyltestosterone does not raise estradiol / acts as AI PMID 2294373; Mor 2001 PMID 11850230; MoA card Gynecomastia (19-nor progestin/prolactin route); trenbolone monograph (non-aromatizing, HPG suppression) Watch: Serum estradiol and prolactin, libido, erectile and mood symptoms; LH/FSH and testosterone for combined axis suppression. For trenbolone also monitor renal markers.
  • Metformin counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    Metformin lowers glucose via hepatic complex-I inhibition / AMPK activation and improved insulin sensitivity. Methyltestosterone pushes the opposite direction by inducing peripheral insulin resistance (reduced skeletal-muscle glucose disposal shown in humans). Metformin can partially counteract the androgen-induced glucose-uptake decline, an education point on opposing metabolic effects (not a dosing recommendation). Diamond 1998 PMID 9851788 (androgen insulin resistance); metformin monograph (AMPK/complex-I glucose-lowering) Watch: Fasting glucose/HbA1c and insulin sensitivity to see whether metformin offsets the androgen-driven rise; standard metformin GI tolerance, B12 status, and renal function.
  • Enclomiphene works against Established
    Enclomiphene is a SERM that antagonizes hypothalamic ERalpha to remove estrogen negative feedback and thereby raise LH/FSH and endogenous testosterone. Methyltestosterone opposes this: as an AR agonist it directly suppresses GnRH/LH/FSH via androgen negative feedback, blunting the gonadotropin rise the SERM is meant to produce. The exogenous androgen fights the SERM's axis-restoring intent. Morales 1994 LH/SHBG suppression PMID 8072077; enclomiphene monograph (hypothalamic ERalpha antagonist raising LH/FSH); methyltestosterone monograph side_effect_mechanisms (HPG suppression) Watch: LH/FSH and total/free testosterone (expect a blunted gonadotropin/testosterone response), estradiol, and SHBG (methyltestosterone suppresses it, distorting free-hormone interpretation).
  • Human chorionic gonadotropin works against Established
    hCG is an LHCGR agonist that stimulates testicular Leydig cells to raise intratesticular testosterone and maintain testicular function, whereas methyltestosterone as an AR agonist drives negative feedback that suppresses hypothalamic GnRH and pituitary LH/FSH, shutting down the endogenous axis. The exogenous androgen works against hCG's purpose of preserving testicular output; the two pull the HPG axis in opposite directions. Morales 1994 methyltestosterone suppressed LH/SHBG PMID 8072077; methyltestosterone monograph side_effect_mechanisms (HPG-axis suppression); hCG monograph (LHCGR agonist) Watch: LH/FSH, total and free testosterone, testicular size/symptoms; estradiol (hCG also raises intratesticular aromatization). Interpret suppressed gonadotropins as expected axis shutdown.
  • Proviron shares target Established
    Both act on SHBG to raise the free/active fraction of co-circulating androgens and estradiol, but by different routes: methyltestosterone (a 17aa oral) strongly suppresses hepatic SHBG production and binds SHBG weakly; Proviron (mesterolone) is the strongest SHBG binder among common AAS (~4x DHT) and displaces bound hormone. Together they drive SHBG-bound hormone into the free pool, so a modest-looking total testosterone can mask a high free level and unexpected estrogenic effects, a bloodwork-interpretation trap. methyltestosterone monograph (17aa AAS lower SHBG, weak SHBG binding); Morales 1994 SHBG suppression PMID 8072077; Saartok 1984 mesterolone strongest SHBG binder PMID 6539197 Watch: SHBG together with total AND free/calculated-free testosterone and estradiol; interpret 'normal' totals cautiously when SHBG is low. Watch for libido/estrogenic symptom shifts.
  • Anastrozole overlaps (redundant) Evidence-human
    The intuitive pairing is an AI counteracting an aromatizing androgen, but methyltestosterone is NOT a good aromatase substrate. It is itself a competitive aromatase (CYP19A1) inhibitor and in controlled human study did not raise serum estradiol (behaved like the aromatase-blocked arm). So adding anastrozole to methyltestosterone is largely redundant on the estrogen-suppression axis and risks over-suppressing estradiol (both agents pushing E2 down), which harms lipids, bone, joints, mood, and libido rather than balancing an estrogen rise. Friedl 1990 (methyltestosterone did not raise estradiol) PMID 2294373; Mor 2001 methyltestosterone competitive aromatase inhibition PMID 11850230; Hong 2008 PMID 18184606 Watch: Serum estradiol (watch for crashing it too low), fasting lipid panel (HDL already falls with methyltestosterone; low estrogen worsens it), joint/mood/libido symptoms of estrogen deficiency, and bone-health signs on prolonged use.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

"Methyltestosterone was synthesized in the 1930s (Ruzicka and colleagues) and was the FIRST orally active androgen — the molecule that opened the entire 17α-alkylated oral AAS class. It became a long-standing prescription drug for male hypogonadism/delayed puberty and, in fixed combination with esterified estrogens, for menopausal/HRT use in women (the corpus entry notes FDA marketing status and ATC codes G03BA02 / G03EK01; ChEMBL CHEMBL1395; molecular formula C20H30O2, MW 302.46). It is a DEA Schedule III controlled substance in the US under the Anabolic Steroid Control Acts (1990/2004), and is a WADA-prohibited substance in sport with established urinary detection metabolites [Drug Test Anal 2023 PMID 37903531]. Clinical use has declined sharply because of its hepatotoxicity and the availability of safer transdermal/injectable testosterone, but legacy combination products persisted for decades. Non-medical/bodybuilding use is illegal without prescription; it also appears as an undeclared adulterant in 'bodybuilding supplements,' a documented cause of cholestatic liver injury [Stolz/DILIN 2019 PMID 30934130]."

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • No rigorous human dose-response for MUSCLE/strength gain from oral methyltestosterone itself — the anabolic dose-response framework is borrowed from injectable testosterone (Bhasin 2001 PMID 11701431) and is an unvalidated same-class extrapolation.
  • No high-quality human Ki/Kd for methyltestosterone at the human AR — published affinities are rodent-tissue relative binding affinities (Saartok 1984 PMID 6539197), not human equilibrium constants.
  • Precise human pharmacokinetics are soft: a validated oral bioavailability % and a well-cited terminal half-life specific to methyltestosterone are not established in the retrieved evidence (only the 1–2 h peak is solid).
  • The molecular mechanism of 17α-alkyl hepatotoxicity is still 'poorly understood' — the oxidative-stress/BSEP-MRP2 cholestasis model is a leading hypothesis, not a proven causal pathway (Bond 2016 PMID 27372877).
  • The degree of any estrogenic effect in humans is uncertain: methyltestosterone does not raise serum estradiol and acts as an aromatase inhibitor (Friedl 1990 PMID 2294373; Mor 2001 PMID 11850230), yet aromatization to 17α-methylestradiol is demonstrated mechanistically mainly in fish/in-vitro — the net human estrogenic/anti-estrogenic balance is unresolved (Contested).
  • Long-term cardiovascular outcome data for oral methyltestosterone specifically are absent; hard-endpoint harm (cardiomyopathy, MI, mortality) is inferred from broader AAS cohorts (e.g., Windfeld-Mathiasen 2025 PMID 39945117), not from methyltestosterone trials.
  • Genetic susceptibility to its cholestatic injury is only partly mapped — ABCB11/BSEP variants were over-represented but did not fully explain risk in DILIN cases (Stolz 2019 PMID 30934130).

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (signs/labs to watch — NO dosing or cycle guidance). LIVER is the headline: baseline and periodic ALT/AST, GGT, ALP, total/direct bilirubin; watch for jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, pruritus/itching, and right-upper-quadrant pain (cholestasis can be severe and protracted, and peliosis/adenoma are serious) [Westaby 1977 PMID 69876; Stolz/DILIN 2019 PMID 30934130]. Note an interpretation caveat: AST/ALT can also rise from muscle/training, so pair with GGT and clinical signs. LIPIDS/CARDIOVASCULAR: fasting lipid panel — expect HDL-C to fall and LDL to rise; this is an atherogenic shift, so track HDL/LDL/total cholesterol over time [Friedl 1990 PMID 2294373; Bhasin 2001 PMID 11701431]. HEMATOLOGY: hemoglobin/hematocrit for erythrocytosis (viscosity/thrombosis risk). ENDOCRINE: LH/FSH, total AND free testosterone, SHBG, and estradiol — expect HPG suppression, low free-T despite high total-T, suppressed SHBG; in women watch for virilization (voice change, hirsutism — may be permanent) [Morales 1994 PMID 8072077]. METABOLIC: fasting glucose/HbA1c and insulin sensitivity given documented androgen-induced insulin resistance [Diamond 1998 PMID 9851788]. MENTAL HEALTH: monitor for irritability, hostility, mood swings, hypomania/mania, especially at higher exposure [Su 1993 PMID 8492402]. BLOOD PRESSURE and signs of fluid retention. The single most protective principle from the evidence is that hepatotoxicity and the HDL crash are intrinsic to the oral 17α-alkyl structure — they track with exposure and reverse on cessation; abnormal LFTs, jaundice, or a collapsing HDL are signals to seek medical evaluation, not to self-manage."

Caveats

TEACHING-ACCURACY CAVEATS. (1) Human-data gap: methyltestosterone is FDA-approved and genuinely studied IN HUMANS — but at therapeutic HRT/hypogonadism doses, not at the stacked supraphysiologic doses central to bodybuilding/PED culture. Do not present approval-era safety/efficacy data as if it covers cycle-style use. (2) The dose-response muscle framework (step 4) comes from INJECTABLE TESTOSTERONE (Bhasin PMID 11701431), not oral methyltestosterone; it is a same-class extrapolation, not direct evidence for this compound. (3) The estrogenic/aromatization claim (step 7) rests on FISH data (PMID 14687976) — flag it as Contested, never assert a quantified human estrogenic effect. (4) Hepatotoxicity is the strongest, most uniquely-attributable human signal: the Lancet study (PMID 69876) and the WJG review (PMID 36051334) directly tie 17-alpha-alkylation to cholestasis, peliosis hepatis, and liver tumors — this is the headline harm and should not be softened. (5) Lipid (HDL) harm is real and a cardiovascular concern. (6) HARM REDUCTION: this package explains MECHANISM only and contains NO dosing, sourcing, or cycle guidance — do not add any. (7) All AR-mechanism statements describe the testosterone/AR class generally; methyltestosterone is assumed to share the canonical AR genomic pathway (consistent with its being a testosterone analog and AR agonist), which is well supported but is class-level, not methyltestosterone-specific, molecular biology.

The 17-alpha-methyl group = the oral trick (and the original sin). Plain testosterone taken by mouth is almost useless — the liver destroys it on the first pass before it can work. Chemists added a single methyl (CH3) group at the 17-alpha position. That bulky group physically shields the molecule from the liver enzymes that would normally inactivate it, so it survives, enters the blood, and stays active orally. This one structural change defines the whole '17-alpha-alkylated' steroid family — and it's also exactly why these compounds are the ones that damage the liver.
