Phenylpiracetam

A phenyl-bolted piracetam that crosses into the brain easily and acts like a mild stimulant — the rodent data point to the dopamine transporter as the engine, but almost nobody has confirmed that mechanism in humans.

Limited human data 11 citations
Class
Racetam-class nootropic (a phenylated 2-pyrrolidinone / GABA-derived cyclic compound); functionally a mild CNS stimulant
Primary target
Dopamine transporter (DAT, SLC6A3) — reuptake inhibition
Human status
Not an approved medicine in the US, EU, or UK. It is a registered prescription nootropic in Russia and some CIS countries (brand names Phenotropil, Actitropil). It is on the WADA Prohibited List (banned in sport since 1998 as carphedon/fonturacetam) and is frequently sold illegally as a 'dietary supplement,' where independent testing shows widespread mislabelling, inaccurate dosing, and contamination with other unapproved drugs. Human efficacy evidence is limited to small, mostly Russian-language, high-bias-risk clinical studies (one 2025 meta-analysis, n=549, for fatigue/asthenia); there is essentially NO human mechanistic data — the entire molecular story (DAT inhibition, enantiomer-specific effects, anti-inflammatory/metabolic actions) rests on rodent and in-vitro work, much of it from a single research group tied to the manufacturer Olainfarm. Long-term human safety, abuse/tolerance liability, and the true mechanism in people are unknown.

At a glance

  • Mild stimulant / wakefulness and increased physical stamina — the property that got it banned in sport (WADA, 1998); rodent locomotor increase is the mechanistic correlate (Evidence-rodent; human use is anecdotal/clinical-grade only)
  • Subjective focus and reduced mental fatigue ('antiasthenic') — the main human-tested use, but only in low-quality trials (Evidence-human, low certainty)
  • Memory/cognitive enhancement — clearest for the R-enantiomer in mice; weak/unconfirmed in humans (Evidence-rodent)
  • Antidepressant-like and anxiolytic effects (Evidence-rodent; reviewer-claimed in humans)
  • Anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, anticonvulsant signals (Evidence-rodent / Evidence-in vitro)
  • Reduced weight gain + better glucose handling without a stimulant 'buzz' (Evidence-rodent, S-isomer)
  • CAUTION on hype: 'selective dopamine reuptake inhibitor in humans' and 'upregulates acetylcholine/NMDA receptors' are forum/marketing overreach — the first is rodent-only, the second is borrowed from piracetam and unproven for this compound
  • Supplement-quality reality: products labelled 'phenylpiracetam' are frequently mislabelled, mis-dosed (up to 4x), or stacked with other unapproved drugs (phenibut, vinpocetine, picamilon, omberacetam)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. The phenyl group is the whole trick: it gets the molecule into the brain Evidence-rodent

    Phenylpiracetam is piracetam with a phenyl ring bolted onto the pyrrolidinone core. That extra ring makes it more fat-soluble (more lipophilic), so it crosses the blood-brain barrier far more readily and is active at much lower doses than plain piracetam. Rodent pharmacokinetics confirm it reaches brain tissue within ~15 minutes of dosing. This is why it 'feels like something' where piracetam is subtle. Bobkov 1983 PMID 6403074; Zvejniece 2020 PMID 32279140 (brain uptake 15 min, ~28 ug/g); Malykh 2010 review PMID 20166767

  2. The real engine appears to be blocking the dopamine transporter (DAT) Evidence-rodent

    The most modern, cleanest mechanistic work comes from the Latvian Olainfarm/Institute of Organic Synthesis group. Using radioligand binding across a panel of targets, they found the molecule's only significant hit was DAT — the protein that vacuums dopamine back out of the synapse. Block it and dopamine lingers longer, which is the same general lever amphetamine-type stimulants pull (though by a different, gentler route). Crucially they report it did NOT bind norepinephrine or serotonin transporters/receptors, so it looks like a relatively selective dopaminergic compound — IN RODENTS. Zvejniece 2017 PMID 28743458 (S-isomer = selective DAT inhibitor, no NE/5-HT); Zvejniece 2020 PMID 32279140 (DAT only significant target in binding panel)

  3. The two mirror-image versions do different jobs Evidence-rodent

    Marketed phenylpiracetam is a racemate — a 50/50 mix of two mirror-image molecules (R and S enantiomers). When tested separately in mice, both the R- and S-forms raised locomotor activity and produced antidepressant-like effects, but only the R-enantiomer improved memory (passive-avoidance), even though both reached the brain at similar levels. So the 'stimulant' and 'cognitive' effects may ride on partly different molecular actions. Most human products don't separate the isomers, so users get a blend. Zvejniece 2011 PMID 21689376 (stereoselective: R-isomer carries the memory effect)

  4. The classic 'racetam' glutamate/acetylcholine story is largely borrowed, not proven for this molecule Contested

    Older racetam literature attributes nootropic effects to modulation of glutamate receptors (especially AMPA-type) and cholinergic tone — increasing receptor density/sensitivity rather than flooding the synapse with one transmitter. For phenylpiracetam specifically, this is mostly extrapolated from piracetam; even review authors admit 'the modes of action of piracetam and most of its derivatives remain an enigma.' Treat any confident 'it upregulates ACh/NMDA/AMPA receptors' claim about phenylpiracetam as inherited hype, not direct evidence. Malykh 2010 PMID 20166767 (glutamate-receptor subtype effects implicated; mechanism 'an enigma'); Gromova 2024 review PMID 39269293

  5. Secondary effects: anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, mitochondrial, metabolic — all downstream and all in animals Evidence-rodent

    Because dampened dopamine signalling is tied to neuroinflammation, the DAT action plausibly explains a string of reported secondary effects: in mice, R-phenylpiracetam blunted LPS-induced inflammation (lower TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, iNOS) and reduced paw oedema/pain. The S-isomer reduced body-weight gain and improved glucose handling in obese rodents WITHOUT raising locomotor activity — hinting the metabolic effect is separable from the 'stimulant' feel. Reviews also claim mitochondrial-protective and adaptogenic actions. Important: every one of these is rodent or in-vitro. Zvejniece 2020 PMID 32279140 (anti-inflammatory/neuroprotective); Zvejniece 2017 PMID 28743458 (weight/glucose, no locomotor change); Gromova 2024 PMID 39269293 (mitochondrial/adaptogenic claims)

  6. What humans actually have: low-quality efficacy data, essentially zero mechanistic confirmation Evidence-human

    Human evidence is dominated by Russian-language clinical work on 'asthenia' (fatigue), stroke rehab, and epilepsy. A 2025 meta-analysis pooled 549 patients and reported reduced fatigue scores at 200 mg/day with ~5.5% transient side effects — but it pooled small, mostly Russian studies at high risk of bias, with no placebo-controlled mechanistic readout. No human study has shown DAT occupancy, dopamine changes, or receptor-density shifts. So the human story is 'people report it works for fatigue/focus,' while the WHY is entirely animal-derived. Devlikamova 2025 meta-analysis PMID 40047835 (n=549, high bias risk); Malykh 2010 PMID 20166767

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. They report S-/R-phenylpiracetam as a SELECTIVE dopamine transporter (DAT) inhibitor: in a radioligand-binding + enzymatic target-profiling panel, DAT was 'the only significant molecular target' for R-phenylpiracetam, with NO significant activity at norepinephrine or serotonin receptors/transporters. NOTE FOR SYNTHESIS: neither abstract gives a numeric Ki/IC50 in the retrieved text — a dedicated radioligand affinity-number paper for phenylpiracetam was NOT found indexed (a targeted DAT-affinity PubMed query returned only generic 184k IC50-method hits, none compound-specific). Treat any specific Ki/IC50 as needing the full-text of 28743458/32279140. Enantioselectivity: PMID 21689376 (Zvejniece 2011) shows R-enantiomer carries the memory-improving activity (active at 1 mg/kg in passive avoidance) while both R and S drive locomotor/antidepressant effects.

Signalling cascade. Mechanism per 28743458/32279140: DAT blockade raises synaptic dopamine. Downstream signalling claims (mitochondrial/adaptogenic, anti-inflammatory via reduced TNF-α, IL-1β, iNOS overexpression in LPS endotoxaemia) are in 32279140 (rodent) and the review 39269293. Older Soviet pharmacology (PMID 6403074, Bobkov 1983) describes anticonvulsant + operant-behaviour activation but predates the DAT mechanism. Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (tablet; Phenotropil/Actitropil) and experimental IP/PO in rodents. Human-form data come from Russian clinical-use reviews (39269293), not indexed Western PK trials.
Absorption / bioavailability
SEARCHER NOTE: No human bioavailability number found in indexed abstracts this run. Rodent BBB penetration quantified in PMID 32279140: after 50 mg/kg IP or PO, R-phenylpiracetam reached brain tissue within 15 min; max brain concentration 28 µg/g (IP) and 18 µg/g (PO) — implying good oral absorption and BBB crossing in mice. Flag human bioavailability as UNKNOWN/ungrounded.
Half-life
NOT FOUND in indexed sources this run. No human half-life value retrieved; flag as a genuine data gap. (Commonly-cited ~3-5 h human t1/2 figures could NOT be sourced to an indexed paper here.)
Metabolism
Not characterised in retrieved abstracts; no CYP-enzyme data found. PMID 21689376 used UPLC-MS/MS to measure parent compound in brain but did not report metabolites. Forensic/anti-doping detection assays exist (urine: PMID 10746314 carphedon by SPME-GC; PMID 21049269 GC-MS).
Excretion
Renal/urinary excretion of parent carphedon is the basis of anti-doping urine detection (PMID 10746314), but no quantitative excretion fraction was retrieved.
Notes
Racemic drug; R- and S-enantiomers studied separately (21689376, 28743458, 32279140). Brain levels of R and S were similar despite divergent behavioural effects (21689376), arguing for pharmacodynamic not PK enantioselectivity. No prodrug/ester. ChEMBL had NO record under phenylpiracetam, carphedon, or fonturacetam this run — so no curated calculated ADMET (LogP/PSA/Ro5) available from that source.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

SEARCHER NOTE — what is grounded vs not. RODENT (grounded): PMID 21689376 — R-phenylpiracetam increased locomotion at 10 and 50 mg/kg; antidepressant (forced swim) at 50-100 mg/kg; memory enhancement (passive avoidance) at just 1 mg/kg (R only). PMID 32279140 — anti-inflammatory/neuroprotective at 50 mg/kg single dose; dose-dependent paw-oedema reduction over 7-day pretreatment. PMID 28743458 — chronic dosing (8-12 wk) in obese Zucker rats/Western-diet mice reduced body-weight gain, glucose, leptin WITHOUT increasing locomotor activity (S-enantiomer). HUMAN (grounded only via Russian clinical literature): PMID 40047835 meta-analysis used a fixed 200 mg/day dose for asthenia (all 11 included studies) — so a human dose-RESPONSE curve is effectively UNKNOWN; only a single dose level has pooled human data. SYNTHESIS MUST state that human dose-response is not characterised — only one human dose (200 mg/day) has aggregated evidence, and no Western RCT was found indexed.

How it compares

  • vs Piracetam (parent racetam)
    same: Shared 2-oxopyrrolidine core; both nootropic, low acute toxicity, glutamatergic-modulation class claims (20166767).
    different: Phenylpiracetam adds a phenyl group -> markedly more potent, broader indications, and a distinct DAT-inhibition/stimulant action absent in piracetam (28743458, 20166767). Piracetam lacks meaningful DAT activity.
  • vs Other DAT-targeting agents (e.g. tesofensine, triple-reuptake inhibitors)
    same: Both inhibit DAT to raise synaptic dopamine; explored for weight/metabolic effects.
    different: Tesofensine is a triple monoamine (DAT/NET/SERT) reuptake inhibitor (corpus PMIDs 24239329, 17982477); phenylpiracetam is SELECTIVE for DAT with no NE/5-HT activity (28743458, 32279140) — a cleaner, weaker dopaminergic profile.
  • vs Other 'racetam' nootropics (aniracetam, oxiracetam, omberacetam)
    same: Same chemical family; co-marketed in unapproved 'nootropic' supplements (34484905, 37357012).
    different: Phenylpiracetam uniquely has stimulant/DAT and anti-doping (WADA-banned 1998) status; aniracetam/oxiracetam lack the DAT mechanism and are no longer in clinical use (20166767).

History &amp; legal status

"Origin: developed in the USSR; first characterised as '4-phenylpiracetam' by Bobkov et al. 1983 (PMID 6403074). Marketed in Russia as Phenotropil and (current brand) Actitropil/fonturacetam for asthenia, cerebral ischaemia, etc. (39269293, 40047835). Regulatory: NOT approved for human use in the US — flagged as one of several unapproved drugs found in OTC cognitive supplements (PMID 34484905). EU: an unapproved/unauthorised ingredient (37357012, 40558871). Anti-doping: fonturacetam (4-phenylpiracetam, carphedon) was 'the first nootropic prohibited in sport,' banned by WADA/IOC in 1998 (PMID 37357012); urine detection methods date to 1999 (PMID 10746314)."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Quantitative human DAT binding affinity (Ki/IC50) — NOT found indexed this run; only qualitative 'selective DAT inhibitor' from rodent target-profiling (28743458, 32279140).
  • Human pharmacokinetics — no indexed human half-life, bioavailability, or metabolism (CYP) data found; only rodent brain-PK (32279140).
  • Human dose-response curve — only a single dose (200 mg/day) has pooled human data (40047835); efficacy/safety at other doses unknown.
  • Long-term safety, dependence/tolerance, and abuse liability of chronic use — no indexed study found.
  • Whether Russian-literature efficacy (asthenia) replicates in independent/Western RCTs — none found indexed; bias risk flagged (37357012).
  • Detailed molecular signalling beyond DAT (mitochondrial/adaptogenic, anti-inflammatory cascade) — claimed in review 39269293 but mechanistically resolved only in rodent inflammation models (32279140).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"HARM-REDUCTION MONITORING (no dosing implied). Cardiovascular/stimulant signs: resting heart rate and blood pressure (DAT-mediated dopamine elevation can be sympathomimetic). Sleep: insomnia/overstimulation as a marker of excess dopaminergic tone. Mood: watch for anxiety, irritability, or post-use low mood (biphasic 'psychodepressant' effect at high doses, PMID 6403074). Metabolic (from rodent data): glucose/leptin shifts seen in animals (28743458) — general metabolic awareness, not a validated human signal. Supplement-specific danger: because OTC products are frequently mislabelled and co-formulated with phenibut/vinpocetine/picamilon/omberacetam at up to 4x pharmaceutical doses (34484905, 37357012, 40558871), the biggest practical 'bloodwork' concern is unknown co-ingestants — verify product identity. Athletes: WADA-prohibited since 1998, urine-detectable (37357012, 10746314) — a doping-test positive is a near-certain consequence. SEARCHER NOTE: no validated phenylpiracetam-specific monitoring protocol exists in indexed literature; the above is class-inferred."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE: the proposed mechanism (DAT reuptake inhibition) is entirely rodent-derived and has never been confirmed in humans (no PET occupancy, no microdialysis, no human receptor data). FORUM HYPE TO KILL ON CAMERA: (1) 'It's a clean/selective dopamine reuptake inhibitor' — that selectivity was shown in mouse brain binding assays, not people; (2) 'It upregulates acetylcholine and NMDA/AMPA receptors' — this is inherited from piracetam folklore and is explicitly called 'an enigma' even by review authors for this drug class; (3) racemic vs enantiomer confusion — sold products are 50/50 R/S mixes, and the memory benefit is an R-isomer finding, so 'studies show it boosts memory' overstates what a racemic capsule does. CONFLICT/SOURCE BIAS: most mechanistic and clinical work originates from the Latvian Institute of Organic Synthesis / Olainfarm (the manufacturer) and from Russian clinical groups — independent replication is thin. TOLERANCE: users widely report rapid tolerance to the stimulant effect; this is anecdotal, not characterised in controlled human studies. PRODUCT RISK: independent lab analysis (Cohen 2021 PMID 34484905) found supplements labelled with racetams were mislabelled, over-dosed up to 4x, and stacked with other unapproved drugs — so 'what's in the bottle' is itself unverified. NEVER frame any of this as dosing or usage guidance.
