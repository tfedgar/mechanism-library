Tomatidine

A green-tomato steroidal alkaloid that flips the same anti-atrophy and mitochondrial-cleanup switches in worms, mice and human cells — but has never been tested in a human trial.

Rodent / preclinical 10 citations
Class
Steroidal alkaloid (spirosolane; aglycone of α-tomatine) — nutraceutical/emerging mitophagy inducer and ATF4-pathway (anti-atrophy) modulator
Primary target
Functional, multi-node target profile rather than one receptor: mTORC1 activation + ATF4-program repression (muscle anabolism/anti-atrophy) and mild-ROS -> SKN-1/Nrf2 -> PINK-1/DCT-1 mitophagy + biogenesis (longevity/mitochondrial quality control)
Human status
Preclinical only. There are NO published human efficacy or safety trials of tomatidine for muscle, aging, longevity, or any indication (verified: ClinicalTrials.gov returned zero interventional records for tomatidine as of mid-2026). All muscle, lifespan and cognition data come from C. elegans, mice, and cultured cells. Humans are exposed to trace tomatidine dietarily (green/unripe tomatoes, tomato leaves) and to its glycoside α-tomatine, but no controlled human pharmacology, dosing, bioavailability, or safety data exist for supplemental doses. Emmyon, Inc. (the University of Iowa spin-out behind the muscle work) has pursued tomatidine/analogs as sarcopenia leads, but no clinical readout is published.

At a glance

  • Reduces skeletal muscle atrophy and speeds recovery from atrophy in mice; increased muscle mass, strength and exercise capacity in young and aged mice (Evidence-rodent)
  • Stimulates mTORC1 signaling, protein synthesis and mitochondrial accumulation in cultured human AND mouse myotubes (Evidence-in vitro, human + mouse cells)
  • Represses the ATF4 transcriptional program; genetic ATF4 knockdown reproduces the anti-atrophy benefit, positioning ATF4 as the mediator (Evidence-rodent)
  • Extends lifespan and healthspan (pharyngeal pumping, movement, less muscle-cell damage) in C. elegans via mitophagy — an invertebrate result (Evidence-rodent, invertebrate/C. elegans)
  • Induces mitophagy + mitochondrial biogenesis via mild ROS -> SKN-1/Nrf2; mechanism reported to also occur in primary rat neurons and human cells (Evidence-in vitro)
  • Acts as a senomorphic (senotherapeutic): suppresses the senescence-associated secretory phenotype without lowering p16/p21; improves cognition and reduces frailty in aged mice (Evidence-rodent, 2026)
  • Broad anti-inflammatory, anti-viral and anti-cancer signals across many in vitro/rodent models — biologically promiscuous, not muscle-specific (Evidence-in vitro / Contested relevance)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Discovered by reverse-matching a muscle-atrophy gene signature Established

    Tomatidine was not designed against a target. Researchers took the mRNA 'fingerprint' of atrophying human muscle and searched a drug-signature database for a small molecule that produced the opposite fingerprint. Tomatidine, from tomato plants, scored as a mirror-image hit. This means its 'target' is a whole transcriptional pattern, not one protein. Dyle 2014 PMID 24719321; Adams 2015 PMID 25807353

  2. Activates mTORC1 and protein synthesis in muscle cells Evidence-in vitro

    In cultured human and mouse myotubes, tomatidine switches on mTORC1 signaling, the master growth pathway. This drives protein synthesis and accumulation of protein and mitochondria, so the muscle cell grows rather than wastes. Note this is a cell-culture (in vitro) finding, using human as well as mouse cells. Dyle 2014 PMID 24719321

  3. Represses the ATF4 atrophy program (the load-bearing anti-wasting step) Evidence-rodent

    Aging and stress raise ATF4, a transcription factor that turns on genes (like Gadd45a, Trib3) that shrink muscle. Tomatidine's gene-expression signature is the near-mirror of ATF4's, and genetically lowering ATF4 in mouse muscle reproduces tomatidine's benefit. So blunting ATF4 output is how it protects aged muscle. In cancer cells it also blocks ATF4 moving into the nucleus and binding target promoters, suggesting the effect is real but likely indirect (ATF4 is not a classic drug-binding pocket). Ebert 2015 PMID 26338703; Mukherjee 2023 PMID 37554459

  4. Triggers a mild ROS 'mitohormesis' pulse Evidence-in vitro

    Separately from the muscle pathway, tomatidine mildly increases mitochondrial reactive oxygen species. This small, controlled stress is a signal, not damage — the hormesis principle where a little stress makes the cell more resilient. Shown in C. elegans and cell systems. Fang 2017 PMID 28397803

  5. That ROS pulse activates SKN-1/Nrf2 antioxidant defense Evidence-in vitro

    The ROS signal activates SKN-1 in worms — the equivalent of Nrf2 in mammals — the master switch for antioxidant and stress-response genes. This is the branch point that turns a transient stress into a durable protective program. Fang 2017 PMID 28397803

  6. Induces PINK-1/DCT-1 (PINK1/BNIP3L) mitophagy plus new mitochondria Evidence-in vitro

    Downstream, tomatidine ramps up mitophagy — selective recycling of damaged mitochondria — through the PINK-1/DCT-1 pathway (PINK1/BNIP3L in mammals), while also boosting mitochondrial biogenesis. Net effect: the mitochondrial pool is cleaned and refreshed. This mitophagy mechanism was reported to also occur in primary rat neurons and human cells, and tomatidine independently accelerated mitochondrial maturation/biogenesis in human stem-cell-derived cardiomyocytes. Fang 2017 PMID 28397803; Kim 2022 PMID 35379934

  7. Extends lifespan and healthspan in C. elegans Evidence-rodent

    The combined mitophagy + biogenesis + Nrf2 program translates to a longer, healthier life in worms: more lifespan, better movement and pharyngeal pumping, and fewer badly damaged muscle cells. This is the headline longevity claim — and it is a worm (invertebrate) result, not a mammalian one. Fang 2017 PMID 28397803

  8. Acts as a senomorphic in aged mammals (newest branch) Evidence-rodent

    A 2026 study found tomatidine quiets the inflammatory secretions of senescent human cells (the SASP) without changing the senescence markers p16 or p21 — a 'senomorphic' effect, not a senolytic. In aged mice it reduced frailty and improved memory and coordination. This widens the mechanism beyond muscle but remains rodent/cell-level. Costa 2026 PMID 41922652

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No high-affinity, well-characterized receptor. Tomatidine is the aglycone (sugar-free core) of the tomato glycoalkaloid alpha-tomatine, a C27 spirosolane steroidal alkaloid (verified against PubChem CID 65576; molecular formula C27H45NO2; MW ~415.7 g/mol). Note: a ChEMBL name search during this build returned NO record for 'tomatidine', and a public database was previously observed to return an unrelated structure for the name, so identity should be confirmed against PubChem, not assumed from any single database record. Functionally it behaves as a signature-level modulator: (a) it activates mTORC1 and represses the ATF4 stress-transcription program in skeletal muscle cells, and (b) it induces a mild mitochondrial-ROS/mitohormesis signal that activates SKN-1/Nrf2 and downstream PINK-1/DCT-1 (mammalian PINK1/BNIP3L) mitophagy plus mitochondrial biogenesis. Direct molecular targets proposed in the literature include the vitamin D receptor (and, in fungi, the sterol C24-methyltransferase Erg6, relevant to its antifungal/antibacterial activity, not its muscle/longevity action). ATF4 itself is most likely modulated indirectly (tomatidine blocks ATF4 nuclear translocation and promoter binding in cancer cells) rather than bound in a classic pocket.

Signalling cascade. Two partly independent arms converge on healthier muscle/mitochondria: ARM 1 (anabolic/anti-atrophy): tomatidine -> mTORC1 up + ATF4 program down -> protein synthesis and mitochondrial accumulation -> reduced atrophy, increased mass/strength (mouse in vivo; human + mouse myotubes in vitro). ARM 2 (mitochondrial quality control/longevity): tomatidine -> mild mitochondrial ROS -> SKN-1/Nrf2 -> PINK-1/DCT-1 mitophagy + biogenesis -> improved mitochondrial homeostasis, stress resistance, lifespan/healthspan (C. elegans; mechanism reported also in rat neurons and human cells). A newer arm: senomorphic SASP suppression in senescent human cells and aged mice.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral in animal studies (mixed into diet/gavage in mice; dosed in culture medium/agar for worms and cells). No human dosing route established.
Absorption / bioavailability
Not characterized in humans. As a lipophilic steroidal alkaloid, passive membrane permeability is plausible, but no measured human oral bioavailability, Cmax, or Tmax exist. Dietary intake of FREE tomatidine is very low; most tomato-alkaloid intake is the glycoside alpha-tomatine, which is poorly absorbed and largely acts in the gut.
Half-life
Unknown in any species (no reported PK curves).
Metabolism
Not defined in humans. Tomatidine is itself a metabolic/hydrolysis product of alpha-tomatine (deglycosylation, e.g. by gut microbes or Aspergillus species). Downstream mammalian metabolism (CYP/phase II) is uncharacterized.
Excretion
Not characterized in humans or in standard toxicokinetic studies.
Notes
Bottom line: tomatidine has essentially NO validated pharmacokinetic profile. Any supplement claim about 'absorption' or 'dose' is not supported by human data. The gap between an effective concentration in a dish or a mouse diet and a safe/effective oral human dose is completely unquantified.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Preclinical only and non-monotonic across contexts. In muscle cell and mouse studies, low-micromolar exposure drives mTORC1/anti-atrophy and pro-growth effects. In C. elegans, benefit depends on a MILD ROS signal — consistent with hormesis, where too little does nothing and too much would be expected to flip from protective to harmful (a classic inverted-U). Critically, in cancer models the SAME compound is cytotoxic/pro-apoptotic and even pro-ferroptotic (ATF4 inhibition, GPX4 loss). So 'more is better' is false and potentially dangerous: the therapeutic-looking anabolic/longevity effects and the cell-killing effects sit on the same dose-context landscape. No human dose-response exists, and no safe human dose has been defined. (No dosing protocol is provided here by design.)

How it compares

  • vs Urolithin A
    same: Both are 'natural' oral mitophagy inducers marketed to the longevity/muscle audience; both improve mitochondrial quality and muscle endpoints in preclinical models.
    different: Urolithin A has actual randomized human trials (muscle endurance/mitochondrial biomarkers) and a defined food-safety status; tomatidine has ZERO human trials. Urolithin A works largely via PINK1/mitophagy from a gut-microbiome ellagitannin metabolite; tomatidine adds a distinct mTORC1/ATF4 anabolic arm.
  • vs Ursolic acid
    same: Discovered by the same University of Iowa group with the same signature-reversal method; nearly identical anti-atrophy mRNA signature and shared ATF4 repression; both from edible plants (apple peel vs green tomato).
    different: Ursolic acid is a triterpenoid (different chemotype) with somewhat more human supplement history; tomatidine is a steroidal alkaloid with the added SKN-1/Nrf2 mitophagy/lifespan data in worms.
  • vs Alpha-tomatine (its own parent glycoside)
    same: Same steroidal core; tomatidine is literally the deglycosylated aglycone of alpha-tomatine.
    different: Alpha-tomatine is a cholesterol-binding, membrane-permeabilizing, more cytotoxic saponin used as a vaccine adjuvant/antifungal; tomatidine is much less cytotoxic and is the species linked to muscle/longevity benefit. Supplement 'tomatidine' purity vs tomatine content materially changes the risk profile.
  • vs MOTS-c / mitochondrial peptides
    same: Shared theme of improving mitochondrial function and metabolic/muscle health as longevity strategies.
    different: MOTS-c is an injectable mitochondrial-derived peptide acting via AMPK; tomatidine is an orally-studied small-molecule alkaloid acting via mTORC1/ATF4 + Nrf2 mitophagy — mechanistically and pharmacologically unrelated despite overlapping marketing.

History &amp; legal status

Tomatidine is a natural constituent of green/unripe tomatoes, tomato leaves and flowers, formed by deglycosylation of the plant defense compound alpha-tomatine. Its modern 'longevity/muscle' story began in 2014 when Christopher Adams' group at the University of Iowa identified it via mRNA-signature reversal as a muscle-atrophy inhibitor (Dyle 2014), followed by the ATF4-mechanism paper (Ebert 2015). The spin-out Emmyon, Inc. (Coralville/Iowa; named on the author affiliations of Adams 2015 and Ebert 2015) was formed to develop tomatidine/analogs for sarcopenia and muscle wasting. In parallel, Fang/Bohr at the NIA showed the mitophagy/SKN-1/Nrf2 lifespan mechanism in C. elegans (2017). Since then it has been explored preclinically across antiviral, anti-inflammatory, anticancer and antibacterial contexts and, most recently, senomorphic/cognitive contexts (Costa 2026, with Adams and Emmyon co-authors). Regulatory status: NOT an approved drug anywhere; no FDA/EMA approval; it is a research chemical / dietary-derived compound sold in the supplement gray market without an established GRAS determination or standardized human safety dossier. No orphan or clinical-phase designation is on record.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Human oral bioavailability, pharmacokinetics, half-life and effective/safe dose — entirely undefined.
  • Whether ANY human muscle, longevity, cognitive or metabolic benefit exists — no human trials have tested it.
  • Whether the worm lifespan effect and the rodent muscle effect translate to humans, and at what exposure.
  • The true direct molecular target(s): is ATF4 modulation direct or downstream? How relevant is the vitamin D receptor interaction at achievable exposures?
  • The therapeutic window: where the hormetic/anabolic dose ends and the pro-oxidant/pro-apoptotic/ferroptotic dose begins in normal human tissue.
  • Long-term safety of chronic use, drug interactions, and whether the in vitro cardiac Na+-channel activity has any in vivo relevance.
  • Supplement identity/purity — how much marketed 'tomatidine' is actually aglycone tomatidine vs residual alpha-tomatine, which has a worse toxicity profile.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Framed as harm-reduction, not endorsement — there is no validated human use, so the safest framing is to treat this as experimental. IF someone uses a tomatidine-labeled product despite the absence of human data, reasonable vigilance would include: (1) Product verification — third-party certificate of analysis confirming aglycone tomatidine identity (PubChem CID 65576) and LOW residual alpha-tomatine/glycoalkaloid content, plus heavy-metal/purity testing. (2) GI tolerance — nausea, cramping or diarrhea can signal glycoalkaloid contamination or membrane irritation; stop if present. (3) Baseline and periodic liver and kidney function and CBC would be reasonable given uncharacterized human metabolism and the compound's pro-apoptotic/ferroptotic signals in some tissues. (4) Extra caution and medical review in anyone with active or prior cancer (tomatidine shows both pro-survival and pro-death effects depending on context), on chemotherapy, pregnant/breastfeeding, or with a cardiac arrhythmia history (in vitro Na+-channel activity). (5) Watch for potential interactions with anything acting on mTOR, Nrf2, vitamin D signaling, or oxidative-stress pathways. Report any adverse effects; discontinue and seek medical advice for anything beyond mild transient GI upset. No dose, schedule, or 'cycle' is provided here because none is supported by evidence.
