Library / Anabolic-androgenic steroids

Stanozolol

A pill-stable DHT cousin that builds muscle through the androgen receptor, drops your SHBG so more hormone runs free, and makes your liver pay the bill.

Human / approved 23 citations
Class
Synthetic 17alpha-alkylated, DHT-derived (non-aromatizing) anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS); oral
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR) agonist
Human status
APPROVED drug, not investigational vaporware. Stanozolol was FDA-approved (brand Winstrol) and is documented in humans for hereditary angioedema prophylaxis and historically aplastic anemia/anemias and growth/vascular disorders. BUT: it was discontinued in the US market (Winstrol withdrawn ~2003-2010 era) and survives mainly as a veterinary product and a black-market bodybuilding drug. Human data depth: solid for short-term endocrine/SHBG effects and for liver-injury case reports; muscle-building efficacy at supraphysiologic 'doping' doses is supported mostly by athlete experience, animal studies, and AAS-class human trials rather than large modern RCTs of stanozolol specifically. No legitimate human dosing exists for physique/performance use.

At a glance

  • Anabolic muscle/strength gain via AR activation
  • Non-aromatizing: dry look, minimal estrogenic bloat/gyno
  • Sharp SHBG suppression raising free active androgen
  • Oral hepatotoxicity (cholestasis, enzyme rise, rare fatal liver failure)
  • Suppression of natural testosterone via HPG feedback
  • Androgenic/cardiovascular harms: acne, hair loss, adverse lipids

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It's a designed DHT relative, built to survive a pill Established

    Stanozolol is a lab-made cousin of dihydrotestosterone (DHT). Chemists fused a pyrazole ring onto the steroid and added a methyl group at the 17-alpha position. That 17-alpha-alkylation is the key trick: it stops the liver from instantly destroying the drug, so it works when swallowed instead of needing injection. Schroff/ChEMBL CHEMBL2079587 (max clinical phase 4); structural review Sinha 2024 PMID 39322097

  2. It switches on the androgen receptor Established

    Like testosterone and DHT, stanozolol binds the androgen receptor (AR) inside cells and turns it on. The activated receptor moves to the cell nucleus and changes which genes are read, ramping up the muscle-building (anabolic) protein machinery. ChEMBL classifies it specifically as an AR agonist. ChEMBL CHEMBL2079587 (AR agonist); Saartok 1984 PMID 6539197 (AAS AR binding in muscle) ; ChEMBL CHEMBL2079587 (AR agonist mechanism)

  3. It can't turn into estrogen Established

    Because it comes from the DHT family, stanozolol cannot be converted by the aromatase enzyme into estrogen. That's why users report a 'dry,' hard look with little water bloat and a low risk of estrogen side effects like gynecomastia, unlike testosterone. The flip side is no estrogen-driven benefits and a harsher impact on cholesterol. AAS structure-toxicity review Sinha 2024 PMID 39322097; class pharmacology Grant 2024 PMID 39041466

  4. It crashes SHBG, freeing up hormone Evidence-human

    Stanozolol powerfully lowers SHBG, the liver protein that grabs sex hormones and keeps them inactive in the blood. With less SHBG, a bigger share of testosterone and androgens floats around 'free' and biologically active. This effect is so reliable and androgen-receptor-dependent that doctors literally use a short stanozolol course as an 'androgen sensitivity test.' Sinnecker & Kohler 1989 PMID 2723028; Small 1984 PMID 6430603

  5. The same pill-survival trick wrecks the liver Evidence-human

    The 17-alpha-alkylation that lets stanozolol survive oral dosing also forces the liver to process it the hard way. This causes cholestasis (bile backing up) and direct liver-cell stress, showing up as elevated liver enzymes and, in heavy or prolonged use, serious damage. Documented human cases include sub-massive hepatic necrosis and death. AAS liver injury review Petrovic 2022 PMID 36051334; fatal case report PMID 37948000; rat hepatotoxicity Boada 2000 PMID 10650918

  6. It tells your own hormone factory to shut down Established

    As with all anabolic steroids, the brain senses high androgen activity and dials back the signals (LH/FSH) that tell the testes to make testosterone. Result: suppressed natural testosterone production and possible testicular shrinkage and fertility loss while on the drug. Androgen abuse review Grant 2024 PMID 39041466

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Stanozolol (17β-hydroxy-17α-methyl-5α-androstano[3,2-c]pyrazole; C21H32N2O, MW 328.5; ChEMBL2079587) is a synthetic DHT (5α-dihydrotestosterone) derivative carrying two modifications: a 17α-methyl group (the 'oral' alkylation) and a pyrazole ring fused to the A-ring at the 2,3 position (replacing the 3-keto group). PRIMARY TARGET: the androgen receptor (AR, NR3C4), where it acts as a full AGONIST (ChEMBL MoA, ref Kicman 2008 PMID 18500378). AFFINITY/PARADOX (Evidence-rodent, key nuance): in cell-free competitive binding assays stanozolol is a LOW-affinity AR ligand — it binds rat recombinant AR markedly more weakly than testosterone or the synthetic agonist R1881/methyltrienolone. YET in a cell-based AR-transactivation assay it is a POTENT AR activator, and a single injection rapidly depletes cytosolic AR in rat skeletal muscle, proving it drives biological AR signalling in vivo despite weak in-vitro binding (Feldkoren & Andersson 2005, PMID 15876413). This in-vitro-vs-in-vivo dissociation is the single most important pharmacology fact about stanozolol and likely reflects intracellular metabolism/uptake and tissue-specific AR co-activator topology (Kicman 2008). OFF-TARGET BINDING (mechanistically important): (1) Stanozolol (and danazol), unlike natural androgens, bind and irreversibly inactivate the hepatic 'low-affinity glucocorticoid-binding site' (LAGS) in male rat liver microsomes via negative allosteric modulation (danazol IC50 ~116 nM; stanozolol comparable/next-ranked) — testosterone and R1881 have NO affinity (Fernández 1994, PMID 8119180). This is a candidate molecular basis for the liver-specific effects of 17α-alkylated steroids that testosterone lacks. (2) Stanozolol is a relatively potent inhibitor of the mitochondrial peripheral/translocator benzodiazepine receptor (PBR/TSPO), reducing [3H]PK11195 binding by up to 75% at 50 µM (allosterically, hitting the high-affinity site) — far more than testosterone cypionate (~40%) and unlike methyltestosterone/nandrolone which were inactive — a proposed substrate for AAS neuro/behavioural effects (Masonis & McCarthy 1996, PMID 8918981). Stanozolol is a weak/non-substrate for aromatase (cannot convert to estrogen) and is generally considered non-aromatizable.

Signalling cascade. Classic genomic AR pathway: lipophilic stanozolol (ALogP ~4.1, no Lipinski violations; ChEMBL ADMET) diffuses across the plasma membrane and binds cytoplasmic AR. Ligand binding displaces heat-shock chaperones (HSP90/HSP70/HSP56), drives receptor conformational change and homodimerization, exposes nuclear localization signals, and the ligand-AR complex translocates to the nucleus where it binds androgen-response elements (AREs) in promoter/enhancer regions and recruits co-activators to transactivate target genes — net effect is increased skeletal-muscle protein synthesis and nitrogen retention (Established for the AAS class; Kicman 2008 PMID 18500378). Stanozolol-specific downstream signalling that is well documented: (1) In human dermal fibroblasts stanozolol (0.625–5 µg/mL) upregulates TGF-β1 mRNA and peptide ~2-fold and doubles α1(I) and α1(III) procollagen mRNA, increasing collagen synthesis; antisense/anti-TGF-β/TGF-β1-knockout abolish the effect — i.e. stanozolol drives collagen via an AR→TGF-β1 autocrine loop, and testosterone does NOT reproduce this (Falanga 1998, PMID 9856839; Evidence-human/in vitro). (2) In liver, stanozolol increases SHBG-pathway signalling: short oral dosing lowers serum SHBG in humans (paradoxically — see dose_response) and modulates hepatic monooxygenase (CYP/cyt-b5) content (Boada 1999, PMID 10650918). (3) A genomic anti-glucocorticoid/anti-catabolic hypothesis (interference with glucocorticoid-receptor expression) is invoked for the class but remains a hypothesis (Kicman 2008). Non-genomic membrane/mitochondrial actions (TSPO/PBR, GABA-A modulation) are proposed for rapid behavioural effects (Masonis 1996, PMID 8918981; Speculative-to-Evidence-rodent).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Two clinically used forms: ORAL tablets (the dominant form; 17α-methylation makes it orally bioavailable by resisting first-pass hepatic 17β-oxidation) and an aqueous micronized INTRAMUSCULAR suspension ('Winstrol Depot' — a suspension of drug crystals, NOT an ester/prodrug, so it is not slow-release in the ester sense and is injected frequently). No transdermal/sublingual standard form.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally active because the 17α-methyl group blocks hepatic 17β-hydroxyl oxidation that otherwise destroys non-alkylated androgens on first pass; oral AAS in general are rapidly GI-absorbed with serum peaks ~1–2 h (class data, Bond 2022 PMID 36644692). A precise human oral bioavailability percentage for stanozolol is not well established in the public literature (Evidence-human gap). Lipophilic (ALogP ~4.1) and essentially non-aromatizable (ChEMBL ADMET CHEMBL2079587).
Half-life
Reported oral elimination half-life is approximately 9 hours (commonly cited ~9 h for oral; the IM aqueous suspension acts longer simply because dissolution from crystals is slow). Note: precise, well-replicated human PK half-life values are sparse in peer-reviewed sources and most 'common knowledge' figures trace to pharmacopeial/secondary sources rather than primary trials (Contested/Evidence-human gap — flag explicitly).
Metabolism
Extensively hepatic. Major route is ring/ side-chain hydroxylation by cytochrome P450 enzymes to 3'-hydroxystanozolol (the principal human urinary metabolite used in doping control), plus 16β-hydroxystanozolol and 4β-hydroxystanozolol and their glucuronide/sulfate conjugates (Choi & Chung 2001 PMID 11284331; Mateus-Avois 2005 PMID 15664350; rodent confirmation of stanozolol + 16β-OH and 3'-OH metabolites in liver, Ozcagli 2018 PMID 29717770). Stanozolol itself perturbs hepatic CYP/cyt-b5 content (acute biphasic, chronic suppression), i.e. it both is metabolized by and modulates the CYP system (Boada 1999 PMID 10650918).
Excretion
Predominantly renal as hydroxylated phase-I metabolites and their phase-II glucuronide/sulfate conjugates; 3'-hydroxystanozolol glucuronide is detectable in urine for an extended window, making it the standard WADA detection target (Choi 2001 PMID 11284331; Mateus-Avois 2005 PMID 15664350).
Notes
NOT an ester or prodrug in the testosterone-ester sense — the 17α-methyl is a permanent structural alkylation, not a cleavable ester, so there is no 'active parent released by esterases.' The 17α-alkyl group is the direct structural cause of its hepatotoxic profile (see side effects). High lipophilicity predicts CNS penetration (consistent with the TSPO/behavioural data) but a measured human BBB-penetration value is not established. The A-ring pyrazole removes the 3-keto group, abolishing meaningful aromatization to estrogen and 5α-reduction substrate behaviour (it is already 5α-reduced/DHT-derived).

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

HUMAN dose-response for muscle hypertrophy/strength is essentially NOT characterized in controlled trials — there are no modern randomized dose-ranging hypertrophy studies of stanozolol in healthy athletes, so any 'X mg gives Y kg of muscle' claim is unsupported by trial data (Evidence-human gap; explicitly unknown). What IS quantified in humans: (1) SHBG/androgen-sensitivity dose-response — oral stanozolol 0.2 mg/kg/day for 3 days drove serum SHBG to a mean nadir of 51.6 ± 5.9% of baseline in 25 normal controls (a ~48% drop), versus only 73–89% (partial) and 93–97% (complete androgen insensitivity) — a clean, reproducible human pharmacodynamic readout of AR engagement and the basis of the 'SHBG androgen sensitivity test' (Sinnecker & Köhler 1989, PMID 2723028; Evidence-human). (2) Fibrinolytic/therapeutic dosing — 6 mg/day orally normalized elevated PAI activity and restored stimulated tissue-plasminogen-activator response within 12–18 weeks in patients with hypofibrinolysis/osteonecrosis (Glueck 1995, PMID 7717367; Evidence-human, small n=5). RODENT dose-response: doping-pattern high doses (e.g., 5 mg/kg subcutaneously for 28 days) produce measurable cardiac oxidative stress and pro-apoptotic signalling; chronic high-dose oral/parenteral dosing produces centrilobular hepatocyte lesions and increased liver-cell S-phase fraction (proliferative signal) (Ozcagli 2018 PMID 29717770; Boada 1999 PMID 10650918). In-vitro, direct hepatocyte toxicity (LDH release, neutral-red loss) appears at ~10^-4 M (Welder 1995 PMID 8527826). Bottom line for the audience: the COMPOUND clearly engages the AR and lowers SHBG dose-dependently in humans at low oral doses, but the headline 'how much muscle per mg' question has no human trial answer — magnitude claims come from anecdote, not controlled data.

How it compares

  • vs Testosterone (and esters)
    same: Both are AR agonists driving the same genomic muscle-anabolic pathway; both suppress the HPG axis
    different: Testosterone is non-alkylated, aromatizes to estradiol, and is NOT directly hepatotoxic; stanozolol is 17α-alkylated (orally active but hepatotoxic), non-aromatizing (no estrogenic effects/no estrogen-mediated lipid protection), binds AR weakly in vitro yet activates it potently, and uniquely induces TGF-β1/collagen and binds the hepatic LAGS site — effects testosterone does not produce (PMID 9856839; PMID 8119180; PMID 8527826)
  • vs Oxandrolone (Anavar)
    same: Both are DHT-derived, 17α-alkylated, orally active, non-aromatizing AAS with a 'lean/cutting' reputation and favorable myotrophic:androgenic ratio
    different: Oxandrolone has an oxygen substituted at C2 (a 2-oxa lactone A-ring) and is generally considered comparatively LESS hepatotoxic per mg than most 17α-alkyls; stanozolol carries the A-ring pyrazole and a stronger documented HDL-crashing / hepatotoxic and SHBG-lowering signal (class evidence PMID 8527826; PMID 36644692)
  • vs Methandienone/Dianabol & Oxymetholone/Anadrol
    same: All are oral 17α-alkylated AAS that share the alkyl-group-driven hepatotoxicity and HDL-suppressing lipid profile; methandienone shares the in-vitro-low-affinity/in-vivo-potent AR paradox with stanozolol (PMID 15876413)
    different: Dianabol and Anadrol aromatize / have estrogenic or progestogenic activity causing water retention and gynecomastia, whereas stanozolol is 'dry' (non-aromatizing, no water retention); oxymetholone caused the greatest glutathione depletion/hepatocyte toxicity in the comparative assay (PMID 8527826)
  • vs Danazol
    same: Both are 17α-substituted androgen derivatives, both are used therapeutically for hereditary angioedema (raising C1-esterase inhibitor), and BOTH uniquely bind/inactivate the hepatic low-affinity glucocorticoid-binding site (LAGS) that natural androgens ignore (PMID 8119180)
    different: Danazol is an ethisterone (17α-ethinyl) derivative with weak androgenic + antigonadotropic profile; stanozolol is a pyrazole-fused DHT derivative with stronger anabolic/AR-activating reputation

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Anadrol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Stanozolol is another oral 17-alpha-alkylated AAS and has caused fatal sub-massive hepatic necrosis/liver failure on its own. Paired with Anadrol, two 17aa orals compound cholestatic and oxidative liver injury through the same shared mechanism (impaired bile transport via BSEP/MRP2 + ROS). Both also suppress HDL, so the atherogenic lipid crash is additive. Anadrol monograph mechanism_step 6 (17aa hepatotoxicity); stanozolol monograph key_effects (oral hepatotoxicity, rare fatal liver failure; adverse lipids); MoA hepatotoxicity_17aa_orals; PMID 37948000 (fatal stanozolol hepatic necrosis); PMID 29521706 Watch: Liver panel (ALT/AST/GGT, bilirubin, ALP) with special vigilance for jaundice and RUQ pain; full lipid panel (expect very low HDL). Blood pressure as a downstream CV-risk driver.
  • Andarine compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Two AR agonists that both drive down HDL and both stress the liver. Stanozolol is a 17-alpha-alkylated oral known for marked HDL suppression and hepatotoxicity; andarine adds AR-driven hepatic lipid changes and a SARM-class DILI signal, so the adverse lipid shift and liver-enzyme elevation compound on the same pathways. andarine-s-4.json (lowered HDL + hepatotoxicity, SARM-class, PMID 30503797, PMID 35340496); stanozolol-winstrol.json (marked HDL drop, 17aa hepatotoxic) Watch: Fasting lipid panel (HDL especially) and a full liver panel (ALT/AST/GGT/ALP/bilirubin); watch for signs of cholestasis and continued HDL decline.
  • DHB compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both are non-aromatizing androgens that suppress HDL cholesterol. Androgen-receptor activation in the liver upregulates hepatic lipase, which lowers HDL-C - a mechanism directly demonstrated for a SARM (hepatic triglyceride lipase rose, HDL-C fell, PMID 35822201) and shared by non-aromatizing AAS - and because neither compound aromatizes, there is no offsetting estrogenic HDL support, so the lipid hit is more severe than with aromatizable androgens. Stacking two such compounds drives HDL down and non-HDL/ApoB up additively. Stanozolol adds its own 17aa hepatotoxicity on top. SARM/hepatic-lipase HDL-suppression PMID 35822201; DHB non-aromatizing worse-lipid mechanism (DHB monograph side_effect_mechanisms); Stanozolol hepatotoxicity PMID 37948000 Watch: Fasting lipid panel (HDL-C, LDL-C, ApoB / non-HDL-C); blood pressure. If DHB is run as oral M1T, also track liver enzymes given the shared 17aa hepatic burden.
  • Dianabol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Stanozolol is a third oral 17-alpha-alkylated AAS (a fatal cholestatic/hepatic-necrosis case from oral stanozolol alone is documented, PMID 37948000). Stacked with Dianabol, the shared 17a-alkyl hepatotoxic mechanism (cholestasis, raised enzymes) and the shared hepatic lipid shift (HDL crash) compound. There is also an SHBG angle: both orals lower SHBG (Dbol suppresses it; stanozolol strongly so - the effect is reliable enough to be used clinically as an androgen-sensitivity test), stacking a rise in free hormone. Because Dbol supplies free estrogen from its weak aromatization while stanozolol drives SHBG down further, the combined low-SHBG state can worsen estrogenic effects even while total E2 reads low. Dbol monograph mechanism_steps 4-5,7 (17aa hepatotox, HDL crash, SHBG suppression); stanozolol-winstrol.json (sharp SHBG suppression, oral 17aa hepatotoxicity, fatal case PMID 37948000); corpus MoA cards 'Hepatotoxicity (17aa orals)' and 'Hepatic lipase & lipids' Watch: Liver panel (ALT/AST/GGT, bilirubin) with jaundice/RUQ vigilance; lipid panel (double oral HDL crash); SHBG with free/total testosterone and estradiol so a low-SHBG, high-free-estrogen state is caught.
  • Exemestane compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Stanozolol is an oral 17α-alkylated androgen that strongly induces hepatic lipase and sharply lowers HDL / raises LDL-ApoB. Exemestane, by suppressing estradiol, independently removes estrogen's HDL-favoring effect. The two therefore compound atherogenic dyslipidemia through independent mechanisms. (The often-cited 'stanozolol dries/aches joints' is anecdotal and Contested — no established human mechanism — so any joint compounding here comes from exemestane's own estrogen-deprivation AIMSS, not a proven stanozolol joint effect.) Stanozolol monograph side_effect_mechanisms (17α-alkyl hepatic-lipase HDL crash, PMID 12736156) and caveats (tendon/joint claims Contested); Exemestane monograph side_effect_mechanisms (AIMSS, lipids); Aromatase MoA card Watch: Fasting lipid panel (HDL, LDL/ApoB, TC:HDL). Track joint symptoms too, but attribute cautiously: if joints ache, check whether E2 is crashed — restoring E2 into range (rather than minimizing it) relieves the exemestane-side contribution.
  • Halotestin compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Both are oral 17-alpha-alkylated, non-aromatizing 'dry' AAS and both are weak-affinity AR binders (RBA <0.05 in the same Saartok panel) that share the two hallmark oral-AAS harms: additive hepatotoxicity (17a-alkyl cholestasis/hepatocellular injury; stanozolol has documented rare fatal liver failure) and atherogenic dyslipidemia (both crash HDL via hepatic-lipase induction, with no estrogenic HDL support because neither aromatizes). Two dry orals together deepen both the liver and lipid hits. Halotestin monograph steps 6-7 & comparisons (vs stanozolol: both RBA <0.05, both hepatotoxic & HDL-suppressing; PMID 6539197, 2915439); stanozolol monograph key_effects (oral hepatotoxicity incl. rare fatal liver failure; adverse lipids; non-aromatizing) Watch: Liver panel (ALT/AST/GGT/bilirubin) and lipid panel (HDL/LDL/ApoB); watch for jaundice/RUQ pain and joint dryness. Rising liver markers or a crashed HDL are signals to stop.
  • Masteron compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both are DHT-derived, non-aromatizing androgens that characteristically suppress HDL cholesterol and raise LDL, partly because no HDL-protective estrogen is generated. Stacked, the atherogenic lipid shift is additive and can drive HDL very low; both also share androgenic drying/scalp/skin effects. (Distinct hepatic routes: stanozolol is 17a-alkylated and hepatotoxic; Masteron is injectable and much less so, so hepatotoxicity does not simply add. Stanozolol additionally strongly suppresses SHBG, which Masteron does not clearly do.) History &amp; legal status

"Stanozolol was developed by Winthrop Laboratories (Sterling-Winthrop) and described around 1959–1962; it derives from DHT by A-ring pyrazole fusion plus 17α-methylation. It reached the market as 'Winstrol' (oral) and 'Winstrol Depot' (aqueous IM suspension) and gained legitimate medical approvals for indications including hereditary angioedema prophylaxis (androgens raise C1-esterase inhibitor — class context Bond 2022 PMID 36644692), and was used for anemias, hypofibrinolysis/osteonecrosis (PMID 7717367), and as a connective-tissue/dermatologic agent (Helfman & Falanga 1995, PMID 7622653). It became infamous in sport via the 1988 Seoul Olympics (Ben Johnson). Regulatory status: a controlled substance — Schedule III under the U.S. Anabolic Steroid Control Act (1990/2004) and a Class C controlled drug in the UK; banned at all times in sport by WADA, with 3'-hydroxystanozolol the standard urinary detection target (PMID 11284331; PMID 15664350). Human pharmaceutical availability has since dwindled in many markets (the branded U.S. oral product was discontinued), so most current human exposure is via illicit/veterinary supply rather than licensed prescriptions; it remains a veterinary product in some jurisdictions."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No controlled human dose-response data exist for stanozolol's hypertrophy/strength effects — the central 'how much muscle per mg' question is unanswered by any randomized trial; all magnitude claims are anecdotal.
  • The molecular basis of the in-vitro-low-affinity / in-vivo-potent AR paradox is unresolved — whether it is driven by intracellular metabolism, tissue-specific uptake, or AR co-activator topology is hypothesized but not proven (PMID 15876413).
  • Human pharmacokinetics are poorly characterized in the primary literature — the widely quoted ~9 h oral half-life and oral bioavailability figures lack robust modern trial confirmation.
  • Whether the AR→TGF-β1→collagen effect (PMID 9856839) clinically increases tendon rupture risk in lifters, or could be therapeutically harnessed for wound healing, is unknown.
  • The mechanism and reversibility threshold of severe hepatotoxicity is obscure — why most users get only transient cholestasis while rare individuals develop fatal sub-massive necrosis (PMID 37948000) is not understood (host/genetic susceptibility unmapped).
  • The contribution of stanozolol's distinctive off-target binding (hepatic LAGS site, PMID 8119180; TSPO/PBR, PMID 8918981) to its human side-effect and behavioral profile is mechanistically suggestive but unquantified in humans.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a how-to-use guide; these are signs/labs a clinician would track if someone is using or has used stanozolol). LIVER: ALT/AST, GGT, ALP, total/direct bilirubin at baseline and periodically; watch for jaundice, dark urine, pale stool, right-upper-quadrant pain, nausea/malaise — cholestatic injury and, rarely, life-threatening necrosis occur (PMID 37948000); note that transaminases can be confounded by training-related muscle breakdown, so GGT/bilirubin help distinguish hepatic from muscle origin. Consider hepatic imaging if peliosis/adenoma is suspected (oral-AAS association). LIPIDS/CARDIOVASCULAR: full fasting lipid panel — expect sharply suppressed HDL-C and raised LDL-C/ApoB (PMID 12736156); monitor blood pressure; be alert to the longer-term atherosclerosis/cardiomyopathy risk (rodent cardiac oxidative-stress/apoptosis signal, PMID 29477345). ENDOCRINE: total/free testosterone, LH, FSH, and SHBG (stanozolol markedly lowers SHBG, which distorts free-testosterone interpretation — PMID 2723028); track for HPG suppression and recovery. HEMATOLOGIC/RENAL: hematocrit/hemoglobin (erythrocytosis risk) and renal function. ANDROGENIC/VIRILIZING SIGNS: in women, monitor for voice deepening, hirsutism, clitoromegaly (often irreversible — stop early); in all users, acne and scalp hair loss. MUSCULOSKELETAL: be aware of possible tendon vulnerability. PSYCHIATRIC: mood, irritability/aggression, sleep. The single most important harm-reduction message: oral 17α-alkylated stanozolol carries hepatotoxicity and an HDL crash that injectable testosterone does not, and any new jaundice or RUQ symptoms warrant immediate medical attention and discontinuation."

Caveats

HARM-REDUCTION / ACCURACY NOTES for the audience: (1) Stanozolol is a REAL approved drug with a genuine medical history (hereditary angioedema, anemias), NOT an experimental research chemical and NOT a SARM — it is a full anabolic-androgenic steroid. It was withdrawn from the US human market; what circulates in bodybuilding is veterinary or black-market product of unknown purity. (2) The 'dry, lean' reputation is real pharmacology (no aromatization), but the same DHT-derived nature worsens cholesterol and brings strong androgenic side effects (hair loss, acne). (3) LIVER TOXICITY IS THE HEADLINE HARM: because it is 17-alpha-alkylated and oral, stanozolol is among the more hepatotoxic steroids — documented human cases include cholestatic jaundice, hepatic necrosis, and death. This is dose- and duration-dependent and not abolished by 'low doses.' (4) It shuts down natural testosterone production while used. (5) Tendon/joint complaints are widely reported anecdotally; treat as Contested — mechanism not cleanly established in humans. (6) NO safe self-administered dose for physique/performance is being endorsed here; this explains mechanism only, not how-to. Certainty labels: AR agonism and 17a-alkyl/oral-survival = Established; SHBG crash and human liver injury = Evidence-human; rat hepatotoxicity detail = Evidence-rodent; tendon-weakening claims = Contested.
