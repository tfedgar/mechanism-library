SRT2104

The most selective synthetic SIRT1 activator to reach human trials — repeatedly safe and lipid-lowering, but hobbled by poor, erratic drug absorption and no confirmed benefit on its headline metabolic endpoints.

Human / approved 12 citations
Class
Small-molecule sirtuin-activating compound (STAC); selective allosteric SIRT1 activator (chemically unrelated to resveratrol)
Primary target
SIRT1 (NAD+-dependent class III deacetylase; ChEMBL target CHEMBL4506) — curated direct allosteric ACTIVATOR acting through the Glu230-dependent STAC mechanism; proposed downstream axis is PGC-1alpha-driven mitochondrial biogenesis plus deacetylation of NF-kB and FOXO3a (downstream steps largely rodent/cell, not measured in SRT2104 human trials)
Human status
Pipeline / clinically stalled. SRT2104 (Sirtris, then GlaxoSmithKline after GSK's 2008 acquisition of Sirtris) is the most selective and clinically advanced synthetic SIRT1 activator, reaching Phase 2 (ChEMBL max_phase 2). It has an unusually broad human safety database — 14 registered trials (confirmed on ClinicalTrials.gov) spanning first-in-human PK, healthy elderly, type-2 diabetes, cigarette smokers, endotoxemia/sepsis, psoriasis, and ulcerative colitis (plus a withdrawn immobilization-atrophy study) — and was consistently safe and well tolerated. It never reached approval for any indication. Two problems dominate: (1) poor and highly variable pharmacokinetics — mean absolute oral bioavailability ~14%, sub-dose-proportional exposure, and up to a ~4-fold food effect — which the diabetes team explicitly blamed for the lack of glycemic benefit; and (2) mixed efficacy — clear lipid and anti-inflammatory biomarker effects, but negative results on its flagship metabolic endpoint (glucose in T2D) and in ulcerative colitis. There is no evidence of ongoing GSK development. Interest persists in academia for rare/degenerative diseases (Duchenne muscular dystrophy, Huntington's), at the preclinical/patient-cell stage. It is NOT an approved drug and is used in humans only within clinical trials.

Mechanism, step by step

  1. SRT2104 binds SIRT1 and acts as an ACTIVATOR (not an inhibitor) Established

    SRT2104 is a first-in-class synthetic small molecule (MW ~517, formula C26H24N6O2S2; ChEMBL CHEMBL4297436) that binds SIRT1 and acts as an ACTIVATOR, not an inhibitor. Unlike a drug that blocks an enzyme, it speeds SIRT1's deacetylation of certain substrates. ChEMBL curates it as a direct SIRT1 activator (target CHEMBL4506, action_type ACTIVATOR, direct_interaction true, disease_efficacy true). ChEMBL CHEMBL4297436 mechanism record (target CHEMBL4506, action_type ACTIVATOR, direct_interaction true); PMID 22616762 (first-in-class small-molecule SIRT1 activator descriptor)

  2. Activation works through a shared allosteric site (Glu230), rebutting the fluorophore-artifact critique Established

    Sirtuin activators were controversial because early assays used a fluorophore-tagged peptide, raising the worry that 'activation' was an assay artifact. Hubbard et al. (Science 2013) showed chemically diverse STACs activate SIRT1 through a structured N-terminal domain, and that a single residue (Glu230) is required for activation by all previously reported STAC scaffolds plus a new chemically distinct class. In primary cells reconstituted with activation-defective SIRT1, the metabolic effects of STACs were blocked. This is the mechanistic backbone for the class. (The paper demonstrates the common Glu230 mechanism for STACs generally; it does not report SRT2104 by name, though SRT2104 is a Sirtris STAC of this class and Sirtris authors co-wrote the paper.) PMID 23471411 (Hubbard et al., Science 2013)

  3. Activated SIRT1 deacetylates PGC-1alpha, a driver of mitochondrial biogenesis Established

    SIRT1's best-characterized metabolic output is deacetylating PGC-1alpha, a master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis (via NRF-1/2 and TFAM), increasing oxidative (fat-burning) capacity. This PGC-1alpha axis is well-established SIRT1 biology at the pathway level and PGC-1alpha is a validated STAC substrate. For SRT2104 SPECIFICALLY, however, the 'more mitochondria' data are rodent/cell; the only human signal is indirect and trend-level (see step 4). PMID 23471411 (PGC-1alpha as a hydrophobic-motif STAC substrate; Glu230 mechanism)

  4. In elderly humans, SRT2104 produced only a TREND toward faster muscle mitochondrial recovery Contested

    In the first elderly trial, 31-phosphorus MRS (a non-invasive readout of muscle energy metabolism) showed TRENDS toward more rapid ADP and phosphocreatine recovery after exercise, which the authors describe as consistent with increased mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation and with SIRT1 activation. These were explicitly trends, not statistically established effects — the closest human evidence for the 'mitochondrial biogenesis' headline, and it is weak. PMID 23284689

  5. SRT2104 reduces inflammatory cytokines in a human endotoxemia model (proposed via NF-kB deacetylation) Evidence-human

    In a human LPS (endotoxin) challenge, 7-day or single-dose SRT2104 lowered peak IL-6 (to ~59-81% of placebo) and IL-8 (to ~57-77% of placebo) and reduced C-reactive protein, while NOT changing TNF-alpha or IL-10 — a selective effect, not blanket immunosuppression. This is direct human proof of an anti-inflammatory action consistent with SIRT1 activation. The commonly cited molecular step (SIRT1 deacetylating NF-kB p65) is the presumed mechanism from rodent/cell biology; it was NOT measured in this human study, which quantified cytokine outputs, not p65 acetylation. PMID 25978169 (human cytokine/CRP outcomes); NF-kB p65 deacetylation step: rodent/in vitro mechanism, not measured here

  6. SRT2104 improves the serum lipid profile — the most reproducible human effect Evidence-human

    Across independent trials (elderly, smokers, type-2 diabetics), 28 days of SRT2104 lowered total cholesterol, LDL, and triglycerides versus placebo. In smokers the reductions were statistically significant (e.g., LDL -10 vs +3 mg/dL, TG -40 vs +13 mg/dL, p<0.05). This is the most consistently reproduced human signal and fits SIRT1's role in lipid handling. It appeared even in the diabetes trial where glucose control did not improve. PMID 23284689; PMID 23770971; PMID 24446723

  7. SRT2104 reduces a marker of thrombin generation during acute inflammation Evidence-human

    In the same human endotoxemia study, SRT2104 lowered prothrombin fragment F1+2 (a marker of thrombin generation) to ~58-64% of placebo, indicating reduced activation of clotting, while leaving fibrinolysis (t-PA, PAI-1) and endothelial activation (von Willebrand factor) unchanged. This is the first human demonstration of an anticoagulant tilt with a SIRT1 activator. The molecular link to tissue-factor/NF-kB deacetylation is inferred biology, not measured in this trial. PMID 25978169 (F1+2 outcome); tissue-factor/NF-kB mechanism: inferred, not directly measured

  8. In animal/cell disease models, SRT2104 acts as an exercise mimetic and is neuro/muscle protective Evidence-rodent

    Beyond humans, SRT2104 crossed the blood-brain barrier and, in a Huntington's disease mouse model, attenuated brain atrophy, improved motor function, and extended survival. In Duchenne muscular dystrophy models (flies, mdx mice, patient-derived myoblasts) it was anti-inflammatory, anti-fibrotic and pro-regenerative and reshaped the muscle proteome/acetylome to mimic exercise. These are animal/cell findings — promising but not human-validated for these indications. PMID 25574479 (Huntington's mice); PMID 40195304 (DMD flies/mice/patient myoblasts, in vitro + in vivo)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Direct, selective allosteric activator of SIRT1 (ChEMBL CHEMBL4297436 -> target CHEMBL4506, action_type ACTIVATOR, direct_interaction=true, disease_efficacy=true). Chemically unrelated to resveratrol (formula C26H24N6O2S2, MW ~516.65; an imidazothiazole/thiazole-carboxamide-type scaffold). Per Hubbard et al., STAC activation requires a structured N-terminal SIRT1 domain and specifically Glu230, and is facilitated by hydrophobic motifs in substrates such as PGC-1alpha and FOXO3a; the Glu230 mechanism was shown for the STAC class generally rather than for SRT2104 by name.

Signalling cascade. SIRT1 activation -> NAD+-dependent deacetylation of PGC-1alpha (pro-mitochondrial biogenesis via NRF-1/2, TFAM), NF-kB p65 (anti-inflammatory), FOXO3a (stress resistance), and lipid-regulatory programs. In SRT2104 HUMAN trials the measured outputs were lower LDL/TG/total cholesterol, reduced IL-6/IL-8/CRP, and reduced F1+2 (thrombin marker); the specific deacetylation steps are inferred from rodent/cell SIRT1 biology, not directly demonstrated in those human studies. Rodent/cell models additionally show enhanced oxidative capacity and tissue regeneration.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsule/tablet; multiple formulations tested including slow/intermediate/fast release). An IV/oral radiolabeled (carbon-14) microtracer study was used for the absolute-bioavailability and preliminary-metabolism work.
Absorption / bioavailability
Low and erratic. Mean absolute oral bioavailability ~14%. Exposure increases sub-dose-proportionally (doubling the dose gives less than double the exposure). Large between-subject variability (AUC %CV ~51-89% in the psoriasis trial). Pronounced food effect: up to ~4-fold higher exposure with food.
Half-life
Terminal elimination half-life ~15-20 hours; Tmax ~2-4 hours. Accumulation on repeat dosing is modest (<=3-fold over 7 days).
Metabolism
Hepatic metabolism (characterized preliminarily via a carbon-14 radiolabeled microtracer study); mean systemic clearance ~400 mL/min.
Excretion
Cleared via metabolism with subsequent excretion (radiolabeled microtracer study); high systemic clearance relative to modest bioavailability.
Notes
Colonic tissue exposure was ~140-160x plasma exposure in the ulcerative colitis study — high local delivery still failed to produce clinical benefit, arguing the UC failure was not simply insufficient tissue drug. The poor/variable systemic PK is the single most important practical limitation and the leading explanation for negative systemic (glycemic) endpoints.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

No clean efficacy dose-response was established, largely because exposure was not dose-proportional. In the Phase 2 type-2 diabetes trial, doses of 0.25, 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0 g/day for 28 days (once daily, on background metformin) produced no consistent, dose-related change in glucose or insulin, and plasma concentrations did not rise proportionally with dose. Lipid improvements appeared across dosing but without a tidy dose-gradient. In psoriasis, average exposure rose dose-dependently but with high intra-subject variability (AUC %CV 51-89%), and histologic (not clinical PASI) improvement was seen across escalating doses. Practically, the flat/erratic exposure-response means 'more mg' did not reliably mean 'more effect.' No dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs Resveratrol (and SRT501)
    same: Both are SIRT1-activating compounds (STACs) that improve lipids and engage the PGC-1alpha/mitochondrial axis in models
    different: SRT2104 is chemically unrelated to resveratrol, more selective/potent for SIRT1, and has better-characterized (though still poor) PK; resveratrol has notoriously low bioavailability and many off-target effects. SRT2104 was a successor designed to overcome resveratrol's limitations.
  • vs SRT1720 / SRT2379 (sister Sirtris STACs)
    same: Same synthetic-STAC class, same Glu230-dependent allosteric mechanism, similar PGC-1alpha/mitochondrial pharmacology
    different: SRT2104 advanced furthest in humans (Phase 2, broad safety database); SRT1720 is largely a rodent longevity/metabolic tool compound and did not achieve comparable clinical development.
  • vs NAD+ precursors (NMN / NR)
    same: Both aim to boost the SIRT1 pathway and mitochondrial function for 'longevity/metabolic' goals
    different: NMN/NR raise the SIRT1 co-substrate (NAD+) upstream and act broadly across all seven sirtuins and other NAD+-dependent enzymes; SRT2104 directly and selectively activates SIRT1 regardless of NAD+ level. Mechanistically complementary, not equivalent; NMN/NR are sold as supplements whereas SRT2104 is an investigational drug.
  • vs Metformin (as a metabolic/geroprotective comparator)
    same: Both explored for metabolic and aging-related benefit; both engage AMPK-SIRT1-PGC1alpha-adjacent biology
    different: Metformin is an approved, orally reliable drug with hard outcome data; SRT2104 failed to improve glucose in T2D and has no approved indication or outcome data. Metformin is the pragmatic benchmark SRT2104 did not beat metabolically.

History & legal status

SRT2104 was discovered at Sirtris Pharmaceuticals, the company built on sirtuin/resveratrol work, as a second-generation, resveratrol-independent SIRT1 activator. GlaxoSmithKline acquired Sirtris in 2008, and most later trials are registered under GSK or 'Sirtris, a GSK Company.' The pivotal mechanistic defense of the class — showing STACs activate SIRT1 through a common Glu230 allosteric site rather than as a fluorophore assay artifact — was published in Science in 2013 (Hubbard et al.; co-authored by David Sinclair and Sirtris/GSK scientists) and remains the key rebuttal to that critique. Despite a broad Phase 1/2 program (diabetes, elderly, smokers, endotoxemia, psoriasis, ulcerative colitis) and a clean safety record, SRT2104 was not advanced to approval; GSK wound down the Sirtris sirtuin-activator program. It has never received FDA/EMA marketing authorization for any indication. Contemporary interest is academic and preclinical, focused on rare neuromuscular/neurodegenerative disease (DMD, Huntington's).

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether an improved formulation with reliable, dose-proportional exposure would rescue efficacy on metabolic endpoints (the PK problem may have masked real pharmacology)
  • Whether the human 'mitochondrial biogenesis' signal is real and clinically meaningful, or just a trend-level 31P-MRS finding from one small elderly study
  • Whether the reproducible lipid-lowering translates into any hard cardiovascular outcome benefit (no outcome trials exist)
  • Long-term safety, carcinogenicity, and net effect on aging biology in humans — all untested beyond weeks-to-months
  • Whether the promising rodent/cell DMD/Huntington's exercise-mimetic and neuroprotective effects will replicate in humans
  • Optimal target tissue and indication — high colonic exposure did not help UC, so tissue exposure alone does not predict benefit
  • How selective SIRT1 activation compares head-to-head with NAD+ repletion (NMN/NR) for the same longevity/metabolic goals

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Framed for harm-reduction and clinical-trial context only — this is not a protocol and there is no approved use. If SRT2104 is encountered in a research/gray-market setting, sensible monitoring given its known biology would include: (1) a fasting lipid panel (its most reproducible effect is on cholesterol/LDL/TG — track for both intended change and any unexpected shift); (2) liver function tests (hepatic metabolism; any oral experimental small molecule warrants LFT surveillance); (3) awareness of the strong food effect — inconsistent intake conditions produce very different blood levels, making any apparent effect or side effect hard to interpret; (4) in people with inflammatory bowel disease, extra caution given flare events seen on trial; (5) coagulation awareness if there is any bleeding/clotting history, since SIRT1 activation reduced thrombin generation (F1+2) in endotoxemia; (6) for anyone with a personal/family cancer history, recognize that long-term oncologic safety of sustained SIRT1 activation is simply unknown. Product-quality is a major unaddressed risk: there is no legitimate consumer SRT2104, so identity, purity and dose of any gray-market material are unverified. The safest course is to use it only within a regulated clinical trial. No doses, titration, cycling, or administration instructions are provided here by design.

Caveats

Treat SRT2104 as a mechanistically interesting but clinically unproven tool compound, not a validated therapy. Critical caveats: (1) Its pharmacokinetics are a genuine liability — ~14% bioavailability, non-dose-proportional exposure, large between-subject variability (AUC %CV ~51-89% in the psoriasis trial), and a ~4x food effect — so blood levels are hard to control; the negative diabetes result is best read as 'under-dosed/erratic exposure' rather than 'SIRT1 activation doesn't help metabolism.' (2) The headline 'mitochondrial biogenesis' claim rests on trend-level 31P-MRS data in one small elderly trial plus rodent/cell work — suggestive, not established in humans. (3) The most robust human effects are on lipids and inflammatory/coagulation biomarkers, which are surrogate markers, not proven clinical outcomes (no mortality, event, or hard-outcome data). (4) The molecular deacetylation steps (NF-kB p65, tissue factor, PGC-1alpha) are inferred from rodent/cell SIRT1 biology and were not directly measured in the SRT2104 human trials, which read out cytokines, F1+2, lipids and imaging. (5) Efficacy is indication-dependent and includes clear failures (ulcerative colitis; glucose in T2D). (6) There is no long-term human safety, carcinogenicity, or aging-outcome data — SIRT1's biology is context-dependent and can be double-edged (e.g., in some cancer contexts). (7) This is not a supplement and there is no legitimate consumer product; gray-market 'SRT2104' has no quality assurance.
