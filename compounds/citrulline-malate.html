Citrulline malate

Main target: The L-arginine to nitric oxide pathway. Backed by human data.

Works in humans?Human evidence
Approved?Not FDA-approved

The amino acid that sneaks past your liver to top up the nitric-oxide system and fuel the "pump."

Human / approved 20 citations
Class
Conditionally non-essential amino acid (L-citrulline) as a malate salt; ergogenic dietary supplement / NO precursor
Primary target
The L-arginine to nitric oxide pathway: citrulline raises plasma L-arginine, the substrate for endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS), increasing NO-mediated vasodilation
Human status
REAL human RCTs exist — this is not a rodent-only story. Multiple systematic reviews/meta-analyses in humans show a small-to-moderate benefit for resistance-training repetition volume and reduced post-exercise muscle soreness/perceived exertion (PMID 34010809, 33308806, 42356270, 34176406). BUT effects are inconsistent: the proposed blood-flow/vasodilation mechanism is poorly confirmed in vivo (acute FMD/perfusion data are scarce and mixed — PMID 31977835, 34417881), and aerobic/anaerobic cycling benefits are often null (PMID 31994989). The PGC-1alpha "muscle adaptation" mechanism is rodent-only (PMID 29797700). Endurance meta-analysis found no clear citrulline benefit (PMID 34965876). Honest bottom line: modest, real ergogenic signal for high-volume resistance work and recovery; the headline "more blood flow / bigger pump" mechanism is plausible but under-proven in humans, and longevity/healthspan claims are speculative.

At a glance

  • Modestly increases repetitions-to-failure / total training volume in resistance exercise (small-moderate effect; meta-analytic)
  • Reduces post-exercise muscle soreness and rating of perceived exertion
  • Raises plasma L-arginine and NO availability — more reliably than swallowing arginine directly
  • Promoted for the 'muscle pump' via vasodilation, but in-vivo blood-flow evidence is weak/inconsistent
  • Malate may modestly support aerobic energy metabolism and ammonia handling (mechanistic, not well quantified in humans)
  • Little-to-no consistent benefit for pure aerobic/anaerobic endurance time-trials

Mechanism, step by step

7 steps6 human-evidenced 1 preclinical

  1. You swallow citrulline (not arginine) on purpose Established

    The active molecule is L-citrulline; malate is the carrier acid. Here's the clever part: if you just eat arginine directly, much of it is destroyed by the enzyme arginase in your gut and liver before it reaches the bloodstream. Citrulline slips past that first-pass breakdown intact. PMID 28179487 (Agarwal 2017, J Nutr); PMID 31977835 (Gonzalez & Trexler 2020)

  2. The kidney converts citrulline into arginine Established

    Citrulline travels to the kidneys, where enzymes turn it back into L-arginine and release it into the blood. Because it took the back door, oral citrulline ends up raising blood arginine MORE effectively than the same dose of arginine itself. PMID 28179487 (Agarwal 2017); PMID 25445598 (Morita 2014)

  3. More arginine feeds nitric oxide production Established

    L-arginine is the raw material the enzyme eNOS uses in blood-vessel walls to make nitric oxide (NO), a gas signaling molecule. More arginine substrate means more NO can be produced. PMID 31977835 (Gonzalez & Trexler 2020); MoA-gen Citrulline (PMID ) (eNOS-substrate; over-attributed cite removed)

  4. Nitric oxide relaxes blood vessels (the 'pump') Evidence-human (NO causes vasodilation is established; that citrulline meaningfully boosts muscle blood flow in vivo is Contested)

    NO tells the smooth muscle around arteries to relax, widening them (vasodilation) and increasing blood flow to working muscle. This is the basis of the 'pump' and the theory that it delivers more oxygen/nutrients and clears waste faster. PMID 24277765 (FMD is NO-mediated); PMID 34417881 (Trexler 2021, mechanism review); PMID 41867246 (acute FMD study) (eNOS-substrate; over-attributed cite removed)

  5. Malate may pitch in on energy and ammonia Speculative (in humans)

    The malate half isn't just filler: malate is a TCA-cycle (Krebs cycle) intermediate, so it could modestly support aerobic ATP production and help buffer ammonia, a fatigue-related byproduct. This contribution is plausible but not well-quantified in people. PMID 34417881 (Trexler 2021, critical review)

  6. Net result: more reps, less soreness Evidence-human

    Across human trials, the most consistent payoff is squeezing out a few more repetitions in high-volume resistance training and feeling less sore afterward — a small-to-moderate effect, not a dramatic one. The endurance and pure-power benefits are shakier. PMID 34010809 (Varvik 2021, meta-analysis); PMID 33308806 (Rhim 2020, meta-analysis); PMID 42356270 (Wang 2026, 3-level meta-analysis)

  7. Rodent-only bonus: muscle-adaptation signaling Evidence-rodent

    In mice, citrulline raised PGC-1alpha, a master switch for mitochondrial and muscle adaptation, hinting at longer-term training benefits. This has NOT been demonstrated in humans — treat it as a hypothesis, not a proven human effect. PMID 29797700 (Villareal/Sureda 2018, Mol Nutr Food Res, mice)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Citrulline malate is NOT a receptor ligand — it has no direct binding target, no Ki/EC50, and is neither agonist nor antagonist. It is a metabolic substrate/precursor. The active moiety, L-citrulline (free-base MW 175.19; formula C6H13N3O3; ChEMBL444814), is delivered as a 1:1 (or labeled 2:1) salt with DL-malic acid (combined MW ~309.27; ChEMBL4297667; brand 'Stimol'). Its pharmacological 'targets' are enzymes it feeds, not receptors it occupies: (1) argininosuccinate synthase (ASS) + argininosuccinate lyase (ASL), which convert citrulline → arginine; and (2) the three nitric oxide synthases (nNOS/NOS1, iNOS/NOS2, eNOS/NOS3), which consume the resulting arginine to make NO + regenerate citrulline. Malate is a substrate for the TCA-cycle enzymes (malate dehydrogenase, fumarase). Because there is no occupancy pharmacology, 'selectivity' and 'affinity' are not defined for the parent compound. [Established]

Signalling cascade. The intended cascade is the citrulline–arginine–NO pathway, valued specifically because it BYPASSES hepatic/intestinal arginase and splanchnic first-pass that cripple oral L-arginine. Steps: (1) Oral citrulline is efficiently absorbed across enterocytes (b0,+ / amino-acid transporters) and, unlike arginine, is largely NOT metabolized by intestinal arginase or extracted by the liver — the liver lacks the ASS capacity to trap circulating citrulline, so it passes through (Curis 2005, PMID 16082501; Bahadoran 2021, PMID 33089645). (2) Circulating citrulline is taken up chiefly by the kidney (and endothelial/other NO-producing cells) where ASS + ASL convert it to L-arginine — citrulline thus acts as a 'masked', liver-sparing form of arginine, raising plasma arginine MORE effectively than arginine itself (Schwedhelm 2008, PMID 17662090). (3) In endothelium, eNOS oxidizes L-arginine to L-citrulline + NO; cytosolic ASS/ASL recycle that citrulline back to arginine (the 'citrulline–NO cycle'), and co-localization of citrulline transporters with NOS lets cells channel exogenous citrulline preferentially into NO (Bahadoran 2021, PMID 33089645). Functionally this raises the arginine/ADMA ratio (ADMA = endogenous NOS inhibitor), increasing NOS flux. (4) NO diffuses to vascular smooth muscle, activates soluble guanylate cyclase → cGMP → PKG → smooth-muscle relaxation/vasodilation, increasing muscle blood flow. Schwedhelm 2008 showed citrulline raised arginine/ADMA ratio (186→278) and downstream urinary cGMP (38→50 nmol/mmol creatinine) and nitrate (92→125) — i.e., the full signalling chain moved (PMID 17662090). (5) The malate half is a separate, parallel mechanism: as a TCA-cycle anaplerotic intermediate it can refill cycle intermediates and support oxidative ATP production and ammonia/lactate handling, independent of NO (Bendahan 2002, PMID 12145119). Note the important gap: biochemical markers move reliably, but acute downstream vasodilation (flow-mediated dilation) frequently does NOT change in healthy young men, suggesting a physiological ceiling (Grannes 2026, PMID 41867246).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (powder/capsule). Forms: citrulline malate (1:1 or 2:1 citrulline:malate salt; brand Stimol) and plain L-citrulline. ~1.75 g of a 1:1 salt delivers ~1 g free citrulline; the 'malate ratio' is a real QC problem — some products are mislabeled, so a stated '8 g CitMal' may not deliver the assumed citrulline dose (Gough 2021, PMID 34417881). [Established]
Absorption / bioavailability
Citrulline is readily and efficiently absorbed and, crucially, escapes intestinal arginase and hepatic first-pass — so oral citrulline raises plasma ARGININE AUC and Cmax dose-dependently and MORE than an equivalent dose of arginine (Schwedhelm 2008, PMID 17662090). Quantified human serum peaks: 6 g CitMal → citrulline ~505 µM, arginine ~70 µM, ornithine ~28 µM; 12 g → citrulline ~882 µM, arginine ~102 µM, ornithine ~57 µM (dose-dependent; Grannes 2026, PMID 41867246). [Evidence-human]
Half-life
Plasma citrulline half-life is short, on the order of ~0.7–1.1 h in human studies; peak plasma citrulline/arginine typically occurs ~0.5–1.5 h post-dose, consistent with the 40–60 min pre-exercise timing used in ergogenic trials (Schwedhelm 2008, PMID 17662090; meta-analysis dosing window, Vårvik 2021, PMID 34010809). Exact t½ varies by dose/assay. [Evidence-human]
Metabolism
Not metabolized by liver to a meaningful degree (the whole point of using it over arginine). Converted to arginine mainly in the KIDNEY by argininosuccinate synthase (ASS) + argininosuccinate lyase (ASL); in NO-producing cells the same enzymes recycle it. Some conversion overflow appears as ornithine (via arginase acting on the newly formed arginine), seen as the rise in serum ornithine. Malate enters the TCA cycle (malate dehydrogenase/fumarase). [Established]
Excretion
Renal. The kidney is the principal site of citrulline→arginine conversion and also reabsorbs filtered citrulline efficiently (high renal reabsorption is part of why citrulline is a better NO precursor than arginine; Bahadoran 2021, PMID 33089645). Downstream NO metabolites (nitrate) and cGMP are excreted in urine and were used as pharmacodynamic readouts (Schwedhelm 2008, PMID 17662090). [Established]
Notes
Not an ester/prodrug in the classical sense — citrulline is the active species; malate is a co-substrate salt, not a carrier. CNS/BBB penetration of citrulline is limited and not the therapeutic intent. Because conversion is renal, advanced renal impairment could alter arginine yield (untested specifically). The malate counter-ion is the variable, lower-QC component. [Established / Evidence-human]

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

HUMAN ergogenic doses cluster at 6–8 g of citrulline malate taken 40–60 min before exercise (Vårvik 2021 meta-analysis, PMID 34010809). Biochemistry is clearly dose-dependent: 6 g vs 12 g roughly scaled peak serum citrulline (~505 → ~882 µM) and arginine (~70 → ~102 µM) (Grannes 2026, PMID 41867246), and in Schwedhelm citrulline raised plasma arginine and the arginine/ADMA ratio dose-dependently up to 3 g twice daily (PMID 17662090). PERFORMANCE dose-response, however, is poorly defined and NOT cleanly dose-graded. Landmark acute strength result: a single 8 g dose increased bench-press repetitions to failure, with the effect GROWING across sets — +52.9% more reps by the final set and 40% less soreness at 24–48 h (Pérez-Guisado 2010, PMID 20386132). But the pooled meta-analysis of 8 trials (n=137) found only a SMALL effect: roughly +3 repetitions, ~+6.4% (SMD 0.196), with a non-significant upper-body subgroup (PMID 34010809). Chronic/aerobic dosing: 6 g/day for 15 days raised oxidative ATP production ~34% and sped phosphocreatine recovery ~20% by 31P-MRS (Bendahan 2002, PMID 12145119). Aerobic-performance trials are largely null/equivocal (e.g., PMID 31994989). Key honesty point: there is NO established human dose–response curve linking citrulline dose to a reliable performance magnitude — markers rise dose-dependently while functional vascular (FMD) and many performance endpoints show ceiling effects or no change (PMID 41867246; critical review PMID 34417881). Doses above ~8–10 g mainly increase GI complaints without proven added benefit. [Evidence-human; dose-response for performance: Contested]

How it compares

  • vs L-arginine
    same: Both feed the arginine→NO→cGMP vasodilation pathway and are marketed as 'pump'/NO supplements.
    different: Oral arginine is heavily destroyed by intestinal arginase and hepatic first-pass, so it raises plasma arginine LESS than citrulline does on a per-gram basis; citrulline bypasses this and is converted to arginine in the kidney. Arginine can also induce arginase and raise urea. Citrulline is the more efficient, better-tolerated NO precursor (PMID 17662090; PMID 33089645).
  • vs Plain L-citrulline (no malate)
    same: Identical active moiety and identical citrulline–arginine–NO mechanism; same PK for the citrulline portion.
    different: CM adds malate, a TCA-cycle anaplerotic that may independently support oxidative ATP/ammonia handling (Bendahan 2002, PMID 12145119). CM carries the QC 'ratio' problem (mislabeled citrulline content); plain citrulline gives a cleaner, known dose (PMID 34417881). Whether malate adds real performance benefit over plain citrulline is unresolved.
  • vs Dietary nitrate / beetroot juice
    same: Both ultimately aim to raise NO bioavailability and improve blood flow/efficiency; sometimes stacked or compared head-to-head (e.g., PMID 31456449).
    different: Nitrate works via the NOS-INDEPENDENT nitrate→nitrite→NO pathway (enhanced in hypoxia/low pH), whereas CM works through the NOS-DEPENDENT arginine route. Nitrate has arguably stronger/more consistent evidence for endurance economy; CM's signature claim is anaerobic reps + soreness.
  • vs Malic acid / malate alone
    same: Shares the malate moiety and its TCA-cycle anaplerotic, ATP-supporting role.
    different: Malate alone provides no NO-precursor (citrulline) activity, so it lacks the vasodilation/'pump' rationale; CM bundles both an NO precursor and an energy-cycle intermediate.

History & legal status

"Citrulline was first isolated from watermelon (Citrullus) in the early 20th century, hence the name. Citrulline malate has a long European pharmaceutical history as the prescription/OTC anti-asthenia (anti-fatigue) product 'Stimol' (Laboratoires Biocodex), used in France and elsewhere for functional fatigue — the antiasthenic use predates and motivated the Bendahan muscle-energetics work (PMID 12145119). In the US and most markets it is sold as a dietary supplement, NOT an approved drug; the FDA has not approved citrulline malate for any disease indication, and supplement-grade product quality (citrulline:malate ratio) is inconsistent (PMID 34417881). L-citrulline itself is also used clinically in inborn urea-cycle disorders and as a gut-function biomarker (PMID 16082501). It is NOT a controlled substance and is not on the WADA prohibited list — legal and freely sold for athletes. [Established]"

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does the malate component add anything beyond plain L-citrulline for performance, or is the 'malate' branding mostly marketing? No clean head-to-head bioavailability-controlled trial has isolated malate's contribution.
  • Why does Pérez-Guisado's large +53% rep effect (PMID 20386132) so badly outsize the meta-analytic ~+6% (PMID 34010809)? Is the original an outlier, a protocol artifact, or dose/QC-specific?
  • What is the true human dose–response for PERFORMANCE? Markers rise dose-dependently to 12 g (PMID 41867246) but functional endpoints plateau — where (if anywhere) is the ergogenic optimum, and is >8 g ever worthwhile?
  • Does the acute FMD 'ceiling effect' in healthy young men (PMID 41867246) mean real vascular benefit only appears with chronic dosing, an exercise stimulus, or in endothelial-dysfunction/older/hypertensive populations?
  • How much does citrulline:malate ratio mislabeling (PMID 34417881) explain the field's inconsistent results — i.e., how many 'null' trials simply under-dosed citrulline?
  • Are there clinically meaningful interactions when stacked with PDE5 inhibitors, nitrates, or antihypertensives (additive NO/cGMP hypotension)? Essentially unstudied for CM specifically.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (no dosing guidance). Symptoms: watch for GI upset, bloating, loose stools/diarrhea, and reflux — the most common and dose-related complaints; and for dizziness, lightheadedness, or headache that could reflect excess vasodilation/hypotension, especially around exercise or if taken with blood-pressure-lowering drugs, nitrates, or PDE5 inhibitors. Vitals: it is reasonable to note resting and post-exercise blood pressure, since CM can deepen post-exercise hypotension in hypertensives (PMID 31291417); anyone on antihypertensives or with low baseline BP should be alert to symptomatic drops. Bloodwork is not routinely required in healthy users — controlled trials show no adverse changes in standard clinical safety markers — but where labs are drawn, the mechanistically relevant values are plasma arginine/ornithine and the arginine/ADMA ratio (research markers, not clinical necessities), and basic renal function given that the kidney performs the citrulline→arginine conversion. People with urea-cycle disorders or significant renal impairment should treat citrulline as a metabolically active compound and involve a clinician rather than self-experiment. Stop and reassess if GI symptoms are persistent or if any vasodilation-type symptoms (faintness, palpitations) occur. [Evidence-human]"

Caveats

EVIDENCE QUALITY: Human RCTs are real but small, heterogeneous, and often use a single acute 8 g dose; effect sizes are small-to-moderate, not transformative. MECHANISM GAP: The marketed 'increased blood flow / pump' story is biologically plausible but the in-vivo human data on vasodilation and muscle perfusion after citrulline are scarce and inconsistent (PMID 31977835, 34417881) — NO causing vasodilation is established, but citrulline meaningfully boosting working-muscle blood flow is Contested. CITRULLINE vs CITRULLINE MALATE: most positive resistance-training data use citrulline MALATE (~8 g, ~1.11 g malate per g citrulline ratio in classic studies); pure L-citrulline doses are often lower (~3-6 g) and results differ — don't assume they're interchangeable. ENDURANCE/AEROBIC: meta-analysis of food-source arginine/citrulline found no clear endurance benefit (PMID 34965876); cycling time-trial RCT null (PMID 31994989). RODENT-ONLY claims: PGC-1alpha upregulation and muscle-mass effects are from mice (PMID 29797700) — not demonstrated in humans. LONGEVITY: any healthspan/anti-aging framing is speculative extrapolation from the NO/endothelial-function literature, not direct citrulline longevity trials. PUBLICATION BIAS: supplement-performance literature is prone to small-study and positive-result bias. SAFETY: generally well tolerated; caution with blood-pressure-lowering or PDE5-inhibitor medications due to additive vasodilation.
