Creatine

A rechargeable battery for your muscle cells: creatine stockpiles a fast-release phosphate that regenerates ATP during all-out effort. It is one of the most RCT-proven supplements in existence for strength and power.

Limited human data 19 citations
Class
Naturally-occurring nitrogenous organic acid / dietary ergogenic aid (energy-substrate supplement)
Primary target
The creatine kinase / phosphocreatine (PCr) energy-buffer system in skeletal muscle (and brain); cellular uptake via the SLC6A8 creatine transporter
Human status
REAL, robust human evidence — this is the opposite of the rodent-hyped longevity compounds. The core energy mechanism (raising muscle creatine/phosphocreatine and accelerating ATP resynthesis) was demonstrated directly in human muscle biopsies in 1992 (PMID 1327657). The performance benefit is backed by decades of human RCTs and recent meta-analyses showing small-but-significant strength and power gains with resistance training (PMID 40944139). Effective dose: ~3-5 g/day of creatine monohydrate (the cheapest form is also the best-studied; fancier forms show no proven advantage). Cognitive/memory benefits are genuine but smaller and most reliable in deficient or stressed populations (older adults, vegetarians) — PMID 35984306, 39070254. Be honest about the boundary: broad 'longevity' or disease-treatment claims (Alzheimer's, depression, etc.) are still early-stage human pilots or hypotheses (PMID 41737290), NOT established outcomes. Long-term safety in healthy people is well-supported at standard doses.

At a glance

  • Increases muscle phosphocreatine stores, enabling faster ATP regeneration during short, intense effort (Established, human).
  • Improves strength and power when paired with resistance training: small-but-significant gains in squat, bench, vertical jump and peak power (Evidence-human, PMID 40944139).
  • Enhances high-intensity, repeated-bout exercise capacity (sprints, lifts) more than steady endurance (Established/Evidence-human, PMID 18652080).
  • Causes a few kg of early weight gain that is largely intracellular water, not fat.
  • Largest responders are those with low baseline stores — vegetarians and the elderly (Established, PMID 1327657; 32349356).
  • May modestly improve memory and cognition, most clearly in older adults, vegetarians, or sleep/stress states (Evidence-human, PMID 35984306; 39070254).
  • Being explored for clinical bioenergetic conditions (e.g. an Alzheimer's pilot showed raised brain creatine), but these are early/small trials, not proven therapies (Speculative-to-Evidence-human, PMID 41737290).

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It gets pumped into your cells Established

    Swallowed creatine travels in the blood and is pulled into muscle (and brain, heart, etc.) by a dedicated doorway protein called the SLC6A8 creatine transporter. Insulin (released when you eat carbs) ramps that transporter up, which is why taking creatine with a meal helps loading. MoA-gen corpus (SLC6A8 transporter); PMID 1327657 (Harris/Söderlund/Hultman 1992)

  2. It fills up the muscle's tank Established

    Daily supplementation measurably raises the total creatine and phosphocreatine content of human muscle. The classic 1992 human study showed ~5 g taken several times a day for a few days significantly increases muscle creatine — and the boost is biggest in people who started low (e.g. vegetarians, who get little dietary creatine). You can saturate fast with a loading dose or slowly with ~3-5 g/day. PMID 1327657 (abstract: 'significant increase in total creatine content... greatest in subjects with a low initial total creatine content')

  3. It becomes phosphocreatine: a loaded spring Established

    Inside the cell, the enzyme creatine kinase tacks a high-energy phosphate onto creatine to make phosphocreatine (PCr). Think of PCr as a pre-loaded spring sitting next to your engine, holding a phosphate ready to fire. More creatine in the tank means a bigger PCr reserve. Corpus PMC8401986 (creatine kinase reaction, ATP-CP phosphagen system); PMID 18652080

  4. It instantly regenerates ATP during all-out effort Established

    ATP is the cell's actual energy currency, but muscle only holds a few seconds' worth. During an explosive lift or sprint, ATP is spent (becoming ADP). Creatine kinase instantly snaps the phosphate off PCr onto ADP to rebuild ATP — faster than any other system in the body. This is the single fastest way to resupply energy, so you can push harder before fatiguing. It also soaks up the spent ADP/protons, buffering the cell. Corpus PMC8401986 (PCr donates Pi to ADP to resynthesize ATP, fastest phosphagen system); MoA-gen corpus (PCr pool buffers ATP / rapid resynthesis during intense contraction)

  5. More energy means more reps, which builds more muscle Evidence-human

    Because each set sustains a bit more high-quality effort, you accumulate more training volume over weeks. Pooled human RCT data show creatine plus resistance training produces small-but-real gains over training alone: roughly +5.6 kg squat, +1.4 kg bench, +1.5 cm vertical jump, and higher Wingate peak power. The effect is from training harder, not from a drug pumping up muscle directly. PMID 40944139 (2025 meta-analysis; WMD squat +5.64 kg, bench +1.43 kg, vertical jump +1.48 cm, Wingate +47.8 W; abstract only)

  6. It draws water into the cell (and may signal growth) Contested

    Creatine is osmotically active, so storing more of it pulls water into the muscle cell, increasing cell volume. Part of early scale weight gain (~1-2 kg) is this intracellular water, not fat. Cell swelling is also proposed as an anabolic signal, and creatine may support satellite cells and reduce protein breakdown — but these signalling effects are less nailed-down than the energy mechanism. Corpus Frontiers Sports Active Living review (cell swelling, satellite-cell/myogenic activity, reduced protein degradation — proposed mechanisms)

  7. The same battery runs in the brain Evidence-human

    The brain also uses a phosphocreatine backup during heavy neural firing. Supplementation can raise brain creatine and modestly improve memory, most clearly in people who are deficient or stressed (older adults, vegetarians, sleep-deprived). Benefits in young, well-fed, omnivorous brains are smaller and less consistent. PMID 35984306 (memory meta-analysis: improved memory, esp. older adults 66-76 y); PMID 39070254 (cognition meta-analysis); PMID 21118604 (vegetarian cognition)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Creatine is NOT a classic receptor ligand — it has no agonist/antagonist activity at any GPCR or nuclear receptor. Its 'targets' are a TRANSPORTER and an ENZYME SYSTEM. (1) SLC6A8 (CRT/CreaT, a Na+/Cl--dependent SLC6-family creatine transporter): creatine is the SUBSTRATE, co-transported with 2 Na+ and 1 Cl- down the sodium gradient, which is what makes uptake concentrative and largely one-way into the cell (Established; reported Km for creatine ~15-77 uM in expression systems — micromolar affinity, near typical fasting plasma creatine ~25-100 uM, so the transporter is partly substrate-saturated at rest). SLC6A8 loss-of-function causes cerebral creatine deficiency / X-linked intellectual disability (PMID 33334757, 24953403), proving it is the rate-limiting gate for tissue (especially brain) creatine. (2) Creatine kinase (CK) isoenzymes — cytosolic MM (muscle), MB (cardiac), BB (brain), and mitochondrial mtCK: creatine and phosphocreatine are the SUBSTRATE/PRODUCT pair of the freely reversible reaction PCr + ADP + H+ <-> Cr + ATP. CK is not 'bound' in an inhibitor sense; it is rate-limited only by substrate/product concentrations, so raising the Cr/PCr pool raises buffering capacity. No meaningful Ki/EC50 applies because creatine works by mass action, not occupancy.

Signalling cascade. Mechanistic cascade, building on the existing entry (pumped in -> fills tank -> becomes PCr -> regenerates ATP -> more reps/water/brain battery): STEP 1 Uptake — dietary/supplemental creatine raises plasma creatine; SLC6A8 (driven by the Na+/Cl- gradient, hence ATP-dependent indirectly via Na/K-ATPase) concentrates it intracellularly. Insulin and exercise upregulate SLC6A8 surface expression, increasing uptake (Established; co-ingested carbohydrate/insulin stimulus raises muscle loading, PMID 1327657, 28615996). STEP 2 Phosphorylation — mitochondrial CK at the inner-membrane interface uses ATP made by oxidative phosphorylation to phosphorylate creatine to phosphocreatine (PCr), which diffuses to the cytosol = the 'phosphocreatine shuttle' (spatial energy buffer connecting mitochondria to myofibrils/ion pumps). STEP 3 Temporal buffer — during a burst of demand (sprint, heavy set, high-frequency neuronal firing) cytosolic CK runs PCr + ADP -> Cr + ATP, regenerating ATP in milliseconds and clamping the ADP rise; this keeps the ATP/ADP ratio (free energy of ATP hydrolysis) high right at the myosin ATPase and the SERCA/Na-K pumps. STEP 4 Metabolic signalling — by buffering ADP/AMP, a fuller PCr pool blunts AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) activation and shifts the LKB1/AMPK axis, supporting anabolic/energy-replete signalling and delaying glycolytic flux (Evidence-rodent/in vitro for the LKB1/AMPK arm, e.g. broiler muscle PMID 35007932; the human relevance is inferred). STEP 5 Cell-volume signalling — creatine is osmotically active; concentrative SLC6A8 uptake draws water in, raising intracellular volume, a recognised anabolic/anti-proteolytic 'cell swelling' signal that is proposed to contribute to the lean-mass effect (Contested as a primary driver vs. a correlate). STEP 6 Secondary effects — antioxidant/anti-inflammatory actions, possible myokine (BDNF) and muscle-brain-axis signalling (Evidence-human emerging/Speculative, PMID 40771202). NET: more available rapidly-regenerated ATP -> more work per session -> greater training stimulus -> hypertrophy/strength; in brain, a deeper PCr battery supports cognition under metabolic stress.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (powder/capsule), almost always as creatine monohydrate (CrM) dissolved or suspended in water/juice. The reference form against which all others are measured.
Absorption / bioavailability
Near-complete oral bioavailability of creatine monohydrate (~100% — it is essentially fully absorbed in the small intestine; the body cannot meaningfully exceed muscle saturation so 'extra' is excreted unchanged, not unabsorbed). A 5 g oral dose raises plasma creatine to a mean peak ~795 umol/L at ~1 h in ~80 kg men; repeated 5 g every 2 h sustains plasma ~1000 umol/L (Evidence-human, Harris 1992, PMID 1327657). Co-ingesting carbohydrate/protein (insulin) increases the FRACTION retained in muscle, not the absorption per se. 'Enhanced'/buffered/ester forms claim better uptake but evidence is weak and largely industry-funded (e.g. a single small CreAMP PK study, PMID 40182172).
Half-life
Plasma elimination half-life of an oral creatine dose is short, on the order of ~1-3 hours, as the kidney clears the unincorporated fraction. The functionally relevant 'half-life,' however, is the TISSUE washout: after stopping supplementation, elevated muscle total-creatine returns to baseline slowly over ~4-6 weeks (Established/Evidence-human).
Metabolism
Creatine is NOT cytochrome-P450 metabolized. It is non-enzymatically and irreversibly converted to creatinine by spontaneous cyclization (a slow, pH/temperature-dependent dehydration), at a fairly constant ~1.5-2% of the total body creatine pool per day (~2 g/day in a typical adult). Endogenous synthesis (when not supplementing) is a 2-enzyme pathway: AGAT (arginine:glycine amidinotransferase, kidney/pancreas) makes guanidinoacetate, then GAMT (guanidinoacetate methyltransferase, liver) methylates it to creatine using SAM. Supplementation feedback-downregulates endogenous AGAT/GAMT synthesis (reversible).
Excretion
Renal. Creatinine (the degradation product) is filtered and excreted in urine — this is why supplementing PREDICTABLY raises serum creatinine and lowers eGFR estimates WITHOUT true kidney injury. Unincorporated creatine itself also spills into urine when intake exceeds uptake: in the loading study, renal excretion was ~40-68% of the dose over the first 3 days (PMID 1327657).
Notes
Creatine crosses the blood-brain barrier SLOWLY and poorly (BBB has low SLC6A8 density), so brain creatine rises far more slowly than muscle — weeks, and higher doses (e.g. 20 g/day in the AD pilot, PMID 41737290) are typically used for brain endpoints. Prodrug/ester strategies (creatine ethyl ester, CEE) were designed to improve permeability but CEE is unstable at physiological pH and degrades to creatinine, undermining the rationale (PMID 23957855; PMID 19228401). Monohydrate remains the best-evidenced, cheapest form.

How it compares

  • vs Guanidinoacetic acid (GAA)
    same: Direct biosynthetic precursor of creatine (GAMT methylates GAA -> creatine); raises tissue creatine/PCr and improves muscle energy status, with similar downstream CK-buffer mechanism.
    different: GAA must be methylated in vivo (consumes SAM, can raise homocysteine); it is a livestock feed additive and far less studied for human safety than monohydrate. Creatine monohydrate bypasses this step. (GAA evidence is largely rodent/livestock, e.g. PMID 35007932.)
  • vs Creatine ethyl ester (CEE) and 'buffered'/HCl/nitrate forms
    same: All aim to deliver creatine to the same SLC6A8/CK system; marketed as better-absorbed.
    different: Monohydrate is already ~100% bioavailable, so there is little headroom to improve. CEE is unstable at physiological pH and degrades to creatinine, and head-to-head it did NOT outperform monohydrate on muscle creatine or performance (PMID 23957855, 19228401). No alternative form has beaten monohydrate on hard endpoints — monohydrate is the evidence-based and cheapest choice.
  • vs Beta-alanine (carnosine precursor)
    same: Both are well-evidenced ergogenic aids that buffer the energy/acid-base challenge of high-intensity exercise and require a saturation/loading period in tissue.
    different: Different mechanism and time domain: creatine buffers ATP via the PCr/CK system on the seconds timescale (phosphagen/sprint power, strength); beta-alanine raises muscle carnosine to buffer H+ (pH) over the ~1-4 minute high-intensity range. They are complementary, not interchangeable.
  • vs Caffeine
    same: Both are among the most validated legal ergogenic aids for performance.
    different: Caffeine is an acute, dose-by-dose CNS adenosine-receptor antagonist (works within ~1 h, tolerance develops); creatine is a chronic substrate that must accumulate in tissue over days-weeks and then persists. Some early reports suggested caffeine might blunt creatine's effect, but this is inconsistent.

History & legal status

Creatine was isolated from meat extract by French chemist Michel-Eugene Chevreul in 1832 (named from Greek 'kreas', flesh). Its role in muscle energetics (phosphocreatine, the CK reaction) was worked out across the early-mid 20th century. The modern supplement era was launched by Harris, Soderlund & Hultman's 1992 demonstration that oral creatine loads human muscle (PMID 1327657), followed by visible use at the 1992 Barcelona Olympics. Regulatory status: in the US it is a DIETARY SUPPLEMENT under DSHEA (1994) — not FDA-approved as a drug, not requiring pre-market efficacy approval; it carries no FDA drug approval for any indication. It is legal and NOT banned by WADA, the IOC, the NCAA, or major pro leagues (it is a permitted ergogenic aid), though the NCAA bars member schools from PROVIDING it to athletes. In the EU it has favorable EFSA authorized health claims (e.g. creatine improves performance in successive bursts of short-term high-intensity exercise at 3 g/day). It is one of the most-sold and most-studied sports supplements worldwide. Quality concerns center on manufacturing (synthesis by-products like dicyandiamide), addressed by branded high-purity grades (e.g. Creapure) and third-party testing.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Brain dose-response and clinical benefit: the dose, duration, and populations for which creatine produces RELIABLE cognitive/neuroprotective effects are unresolved — benefits seem concentrated in stressed or low-baseline states (sleep deprivation, vegetarians, aging, AD), and the pivotal trials (e.g. the AD pilot, PMID 41737290) are uncontrolled or small.
  • Non-responders: roughly a quarter of people show minimal tissue-creatine increase or functional benefit; the genetic and dietary determinants (baseline creatine status, SLC6A8 variants, fiber type) are only partly mapped (PMID 41719210).
  • The hair-loss/DHT question: rests on one unreplicated 20-subject study showing a raised DHT:T ratio with no actual measurement of hair loss (PMID 19741313) — whether creatine causes or accelerates androgenic alopecia is genuinely unknown.
  • Cell-volume vs. metabolic signalling: how much of the lean-mass effect is true contractile hypertrophy vs. osmotic water/glycogen, and whether cell swelling is a CAUSAL anabolic signal, remains debated (PMID 37432300).
  • Long-term safety in special populations: pregnancy, adolescents, and people with chronic kidney disease have limited controlled long-term data despite strong safety in healthy adults.
  • Optimal form and timing: whether co-ingestion strategy, micronization, or any 'enhanced' form materially changes outcomes over plain monohydrate is still not convincingly demonstrated.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT dosing advice): (1) Be aware that serum creatinine and any creatinine-based eGFR will read HIGHER/LOWER on creatine — this is expected and usually benign; if a clinician orders kidney tests, tell them you take creatine so a true creatinine rise is not mistaken for injury, and consider cystatin-C-based eGFR (creatine-independent) for an unconfounded read. (2) Watch GI tolerance — bloating, cramping, or loose stool signal too large a single bolus or poor dissolution; these are osmotic and dose-related. (3) Track sudden bodyweight changes — a quick ~1-2 kg early rise is water (intracellular), not fat; useful to distinguish from later genuine lean gains. (4) Hydration — no special risk in healthy people, but maintain normal fluid intake. (5) Pre-existing kidney disease, single kidney, or nephrotoxic-drug use — get individualized clinician guidance before use because the safety data in that setting are thin. (6) Product quality — use third-party-tested / high-purity (e.g. Creapure, USP/NSF-verified) creatine monohydrate to avoid contaminant exposure, since the molecule is safe but manufacturing varies. (7) If on any medication affecting renal handling, flag the supplement to your prescriber.

Caveats

Honesty notes for teaching: (1) Creatine does not directly 'build muscle' — it lets you train harder, and the training builds the muscle; the supplement is a performance enabler, not an anabolic agent. (2) Early weight gain is mostly water inside the cell, which is harmless and expected — coach viewers not to panic. (3) ~20-30% of people are 'non-responders' who already have high muscle creatine and see little extra benefit. (4) The cell-swelling / satellite-cell / anti-catabolic signalling pathways are plausible and supported by reviews but are Contested vs the rock-solid energy mechanism — don't overstate them. (5) Avoid the marketing trap: 'advanced' forms (HCl, nitrate, buffered, 'enhanced absorption') have NOT been shown to beat plain monohydrate — the monohydrate is the gold standard. (6) The kidney-damage scare is not supported in healthy people at normal doses; creatinine rises slightly as a benign lab artifact, not actual kidney injury. (7) Brain/cognitive and clinical claims are real but modest and context-dependent — separate the proven ergogenic story from speculative longevity messaging. Several effect-size claims here are grounded on abstracts only (full text not retrieved); directions are solid, exact magnitudes are approximate.
