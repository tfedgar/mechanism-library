L-carnitine L-tartrate

Main target: CPT1/CPT2 + carnitine-acylcarnitine translocase. Backed by human data.

Works in humans?Human evidence
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A stable salt of a fat-shuttle molecule the body already makes — its human-relevant edge isn't fat burning but faster recovery and a modest, single-study bump in muscle androgen-receptor density.

Strong human data 7 citations
Class
Amino-acid derivative / conditionally-essential nutrient; mitochondrial long-chain fatty-acid transport cofactor (carnitine salt)
Primary target
CPT1/CPT2 + carnitine-acylcarnitine translocase (mitochondrial fatty-acid shuttle); secondary human-observed effect on skeletal-muscle androgen-receptor density
Human status
Widely available, legal, over-the-counter dietary supplement (marketed heavily as "Carnipure" tartrate, Lonza) — not a regulated drug, no approval needed for this indication. Human evidence exists and is genuinely human (multiple small randomized crossover trials, n≈10–18 for the recovery/AR endpoints; n=12–14 for the muscle-loading/fuel studies). However, the flagship findings — the androgen-receptor upregulation and the recovery benefits — rest on a small cluster of studies from largely the same University of Connecticut group (Kraemer/Volek/Ho, several manufacturer-associated). Two newer, larger, non-UConn registered RCTs exist (NCT04420377, n=82; NCT05681806, n=44), but neither has posted results on ClinicalTrials.gov as of this writing, and both used Carnipure — NCT04420377 lists the manufacturer Lonza Ltd. as a study collaborator — so they are NOT manufacturer-independent and their outcomes cannot yet be cited. The core biochemistry of LCLT is textbook and it is unambiguously safe at supplement doses; but its performance/physique claims are modest, partly manufacturer-linked, and not robustly replicated at scale. Treat it as a well-characterized nutrient with a real but small, recovery-focused effect, not a physique-transforming agent.

At a glance

  • Reduced muscle soreness and lower biochemical markers of muscle damage after resistance exercise (CK, myoglobin) — the best-supported human benefit, but from small, mostly single-group trials
  • Reduced markers of oxidative stress and purine/free-radical damage during recovery
  • Modest upregulation of skeletal-muscle androgen-receptor density (single n=10 crossover, unreplicated) — of interest to TRT/AAS users because it means more receptor sites, not more hormone
  • Glycogen sparing and a shift toward fat oxidation at low intensity — but ONLY after ~6 months of combined carnitine + carbohydrate loading
  • No reliable increase in circulating testosterone, GH, or IGF-1 (LCLT raised IGFBP-3 in one study, not free anabolic hormones)
  • No consistent improvement in strength, power, or sprint performance in the acute/short-term dosing trials
  • Not an effective standalone fat-loss agent in humans at supplement doses despite marketing claims

Mechanism, step by step

7 steps6 human-evidenced 1 speculative

  1. Delivers carnitine, the mandatory fatty-acid ferry Established

    Long-chain fatty acids cannot cross into mitochondria on their own — they must first be attached to carnitine. LCLT is just L-carnitine packaged with tartaric acid; once dissolved, it releases free L-carnitine that joins the body's existing carnitine pool. This is textbook, non-controversial biochemistry. Textbook biochemistry of the carnitine shuttle; muscle loading demonstrated in humans Wall 2011 PMID 21224234 (DOI 10.1113/jphysiol.2010.201343)

  2. Feeds the CPT1 → translocase → CPT2 shuttle Established

    At the mitochondrial outer membrane, CPT1 swaps coenzyme-A for carnitine on a fatty acid (making acylcarnitine); a translocase (CACT/SLC25A20) carries it inward; CPT2 swaps carnitine back for CoA so the fatty acid can be burned. Carnitine availability is one lever on this flux. CPT1 is the rate-limiting, malonyl-CoA-regulated step. Carnitine shuttle physiology; in humans carnitine loading raised muscle long-chain acyl-CoA ~200% and upregulated fat-metabolism gene networks Stephens 2013 PMID 23818692 (DOI 10.1113/jphysiol.2013.255364)

  3. Oral loading CAN raise muscle carnitine — but only with insulin present Evidence-human

    Muscle is normally near-saturated with carnitine and resists taking up more. Chronic oral L-carnitine plus enough carbohydrate to spike insulin raised muscle total carnitine ~21% at 24 weeks; without the insulin stimulus (or in some trials even with a different insulin secretagogue), muscle carnitine does not reliably rise. This is the single most important practical caveat — carnitine alone is poorly retained by muscle. Wall 2011 PMID 21224234 (DOI 10.1113/jphysiol.2010.201343); Stephens 2013 PMID 23818692 (DOI 10.1113/jphysiol.2013.255364)

  4. Shifts fuel use and can spare glycogen at low intensity Evidence-human

    When muscle carnitine was successfully raised, subjects spared muscle glycogen (~55% less used at 50% VO2max, consistent with more fat use) and matched glycolytic and mitochondrial flux better at high intensity (44% lower muscle lactate), with an 11% work-output gain. Note: this required 24 weeks of combined carnitine+carbohydrate loading, not a single dose. Wall 2011 PMID 21224234 (DOI 10.1113/jphysiol.2010.201343)

  5. Blunts exercise-induced tissue damage and free-radical / purine markers Evidence-human

    After resistance exercise, ~3 weeks of LCLT lowered markers of muscle breakdown (creatine kinase, myoglobin), oxidative stress (malondialdehyde), and purine-catabolism markers (hypoxanthine, xanthine oxidase), and reduced soreness. Proposed mechanism: better local blood flow / oxygen delivery and reduced hypoxic tissue damage, protecting membranes and receptors. This is the most reproducible LCLT effect in the fitness literature, though the studies are small and largely from one group. Kraemer 2003 PMID 12930169 (DOI 10.1519/1533-4287(2003)017<0455:teolls>2.0.co;2); Ho/Kraemer 2010 PMID 20045157 (DOI 10.1016/j.metabol.2009.11.012)

  6. Upregulates skeletal-muscle androgen receptor content — the community-relevant hook Evidence-human

    In resistance-trained men, 3 weeks of LCLT raised resting muscle androgen-receptor (AR) protein content vs placebo (12.9 vs 11.2 arbitrary units). More intact AR means more docking sites for testosterone. Importantly, LCLT did NOT raise circulating testosterone (total T was actually suppressed during the post-exercise feeding trials) — the effect is on receptor density, not hormone level. This is a single small crossover (n=10) and has not been independently replicated. Kraemer 2006 PMID 16826026 (DOI 10.1249/01.mss.0000227314.85728.35)

  7. Downside pathway: gut bacteria convert carnitine to TMAO Contested

    Gut microbiota metabolize carnitine to trimethylamine, which the liver oxidizes to TMAO — a metabolite that accelerates atherosclerosis in mice and, in a human cohort, predicted cardiovascular events but only when TMAO was also high. Omnivores generate far more TMAO from carnitine than vegetarians/vegans. Causation in humans from supplemental doses is not established, but it is the main theoretical long-term safety flag. Koeth 2013 PMID 23563705 (DOI 10.1038/nm.3145)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. L-carnitine is not a receptor ligand — it is a small zwitterionic substrate/cofactor. It is the obligatory acyl-group carrier for CPT1 (outer mitochondrial membrane, rate-limiting, allosterically inhibited by malonyl-CoA), the CACT/SLC25A20 translocase, and CPT2 (inner membrane). Cellular uptake into muscle is via the high-affinity organic cation transporter OCTN2 (SLC22A5); net muscle loading is insulin-sensitive, which is the basis for why carbohydrate co-ingestion is needed. The androgen-receptor effect is downstream and indirect (increased AR protein content), not carnitine binding AR.

Signalling cascade. carnitine → CPT1 acyl transfer → CACT translocation → CPT2 → beta-oxidation; parallel indirect arm: reduced hypoxic/oxidative tissue damage → more intact/upregulated AR protein → more testosterone docking sites (single small trial).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (tablet/capsule/powder). LCLT is ~68% elemental L-carnitine, ~32% L-tartaric acid by mass.
Absorption / bioavailability
Oral bioavailability of supplemental L-carnitine is LOW and dose-dependent — roughly 15–20% at gram doses, with much of a bolus unabsorbed and partly degraded by gut microbiota to TMA. Endogenous/dietary carnitine (mostly from red meat) is absorbed far more efficiently than supplemental boluses.
Half-life
Plasma elimination half-life of L-carnitine is long (on the order of ~15 hours) due to extensive renal reabsorption, but plasma levels are tightly homeostatically defended; muscle content changes only slowly over weeks–months of insulin-assisted loading.
Metabolism
Largely NOT hepatically metabolized to active forms; the key metabolic fate of the unabsorbed/enterally-exposed fraction is microbial conversion to trimethylamine (TMA) → hepatic FMO3 → TMAO. The body also interconverts free carnitine and acylcarnitines.
Excretion
Renal, with efficient carnitine reabsorption at normal plasma levels; excess supplemental load increases urinary carnitine and acylcarnitine excretion.
Notes
Because plasma carnitine is homeostatically clamped and muscle is normally near-saturated, a single dose does little; meaningful muscle loading requires chronic dosing WITH carbohydrate/insulin over months (Wall 2011: significant effects at 24 weeks; a separate 24-week trial using leucine rather than carbohydrate as the insulin trigger failed to raise muscle carnitine, PMID 36696886). This PK reality is the crux of why acute pre-workout carnitine underperforms marketing claims.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

The human trials showing recovery/AR benefit used ~2 g/day elemental L-carnitine delivered as LCLT (Kraemer 2003/2006, Ho 2010) for ~3 weeks. Muscle-loading/fuel-shift studies used ~1.36–2 g of L-carnitine WITH ~80 g carbohydrate, twice daily, for 12–24 weeks (Stephens 2013 used 1.36 g twice daily for 12 weeks; Wall 2011 used 2 g twice daily for 24 weeks). Interpretation, not a protocol: the recovery/AR effects appear at gram-level chronic dosing; the metabolic/fat-oxidation effects require both larger cumulative exposure AND insulin co-stimulation. There is no evidence that pushing dose higher yields proportionally larger effects, and higher oral boluses mainly increase the unabsorbed fraction available for TMAO generation. This library does not provide dosing instructions.

How it compares

  • vs Acetyl-L-carnitine (ALCAR)
    same: Both deliver L-carnitine to the shared body pool
    different: ALCAR crosses the blood-brain barrier better and is studied for cognition/neuropathy/mood; LCLT is studied for muscle recovery and AR density. Different tissue emphasis; both raise systemic carnitine.
  • vs Glycine propionyl-L-carnitine (GPLC)
    same: Salt/ester variants of the same carnitine molecule
    different: GPLC is marketed for nitric-oxide/blood-flow and pump; LCLT's signature human data are recovery + AR content.
  • vs Plain L-carnitine (base) or other salts (fumarate, etc.)
    same: Same active moiety once dissolved
    different: No convincing head-to-head evidence that the tartrate salt is mechanistically superior — the AR/recovery data simply used LCLT. Differences are largely stability/dissolution/marketing.
  • vs Creatine monohydrate
    same: Both OTC, both target muscle energetics broadly, both generally safe
    different: Creatine has large, robustly replicated strength/hypertrophy/performance effects across hundreds of trials; LCLT's effects are smaller, recovery-focused, and far less replicated. NCT05681806 directly benchmarks Carnipure against creatine (no published results yet).
  • vs Actual androgens (TRT/AAS)
    same: Both intersect the androgen-receptor axis — but at opposite ends (ligand vs receptor number)
    different: Androgens supply hormone (and suppress the HPTA); LCLT supplies zero hormone and only modestly raises receptor density (single study). LCLT is not a substitute, booster, or 'natural steroid'.

History & legal status

L-carnitine was discovered in muscle extracts in the early 20th century; its role as the mitochondrial fatty-acid carrier was elucidated mid-20th century (Fritz). Prescription levocarnitine (Carnitor) is FDA-approved for primary and secondary carnitine deficiency and in dialysis patients — a distinct medical use from the supplement. The L-tartrate salt was commercialized by Lonza as 'Carnipure tartrate' and became the standard form in sports-nutrition research largely because the influential University of Connecticut trials (Kraemer/Volek, 2003–2010) used it, several with industry association. It has never been a controlled or banned substance and is not on the WADA prohibited list. The TMAO cardiovascular hypothesis (Hazen group, Koeth 2013) reframed carnitine's long-term safety debate.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does the androgen-receptor upregulation (single n=10 crossover) replicate in larger, independent, manufacturer-free samples, and does it translate to any real strength/hypertrophy advantage?
  • What did the two larger completed RCTs (NCT04420377, NCT05681806) actually find? Results are not yet posted, so their outcomes are unknown.
  • Is the tartrate salt genuinely different from other carnitine salts for the AR/recovery effect, or incidental to which product researchers happened to use?
  • What is the real-world cardiovascular significance of chronic supplemental carnitine via TMAO in humans (vs mice) — dose, diet, and microbiome-dependent?
  • Whether the recovery benefit requires actual muscle carnitine loading (insulin-dependent, slow) or works through a separate perfusion/antioxidant mechanism at doses that don't raise muscle carnitine
  • How LCLT interacts with concurrent TRT/AAS use in the target community — no direct studies exist
  • Optimal responder profile: vegetarians and older adults (lower baseline muscle carnitine) may respond more than saturated young omnivores

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Educational monitoring context, not medical advice: (1) Watch for a fishy body/breath odor and GI upset as the most common, dose-related, fully reversible signs — reducing the amount typically resolves them. (2) For anyone with cardiovascular risk factors or a heavy red-meat diet, the carnitine→TMAO pathway is the parameter of theoretical interest; plasma TMAO is a research biomarker, not a routine clinical test, and the human causal picture is unsettled — a reasonable person may factor this into long-term use. (3) Because LCLT does not raise circulating androgens or suppress the HPTA, standard hormonal monitoring is not indicated for LCLT itself. (4) Realistic expectation-setting is itself 'monitoring': judge LCLT by recovery/soreness, not by scale weight, testosterone labs, or PRs, since those are where the evidence is weakest or null. Anyone with kidney impairment, seizure history, thyroid disease, or on anticoagulants/thyroid medication should consult a clinician before use, as carnitine has been reported to interact in those contexts. No dosing protocol is provided here.

Caveats

Read the hype filter carefully. (1) LCLT is NOT a testosterone booster — the one AR study raised receptor density, not circulating testosterone (total T was in fact suppressed during the feeding trials), and no LCLT study shows higher free/total T. (2) It is NOT a reliable fat-burner: the widely marketed "carnitine burns fat" story collapses in humans because muscle is already near carnitine-saturated and won't take up more without months of carnitine PLUS high-carbohydrate/insulin co-loading; acute pre-workout doses do essentially nothing to fat oxidation. (3) The strongest, most reproducible signal is reduced post-exercise damage/soreness, not performance — strength, power and sprint outcomes are consistently unchanged in these trials. (4) Much of the favorable literature comes from one research group and manufacturer-linked studies (small n, crossover); the AR finding in particular (single n=10 crossover) has not been independently replicated, and the two larger newer RCTs still involve the Carnipure manufacturer and have no published results yet. (5) The main long-horizon safety question is the carnitine→TMAO→cardiovascular pathway: causal in mice and associational in humans (only when TMAO is concurrently high), unproven as causal from supplement doses, but the reason a heavy-red-meat-plus-supplement user might reasonably think twice. (6) Tartrate is just the counter-ion — claims that the "L-tartrate form" is uniquely superior to other carnitine salts for the AR/recovery effect come from the studies happening to use LCLT, not from head-to-head salt comparisons. This is educational mechanism information, not medical or dosing advice.
