Dasatinib plus Quercetin

Main target: Senescent Cell Anti-Apoptotic Pathways. Backed by human data.

EvidenceHuman evidence
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

The original prescription senolytic: a two-drug "hit-and-run" cocktail that briefly disables the survival wiring senescent cells depend on, so those cells self-destruct — proven to clear senescent cells in humans, but still without a positive Phase 3 disease outcome.

Strong human data 10 citations
Class
Senolytic combination — a repurposed SRC-family/multi-kinase inhibitor (dasatinib, a small-molecule TKI) plus a dietary flavonoid (quercetin)
Primary target
Senescent Cell Anti-Apoptotic Pathways (SCAPs)
Human status
REPURPOSED / INVESTIGATIONAL SENOLYTIC — mechanism proven in humans, disease benefit not yet proven. Dasatinib (Sprycel) is FDA-approved for CML/Ph+ ALL; quercetin is a dietary flavonoid/supplement. Neither is approved as a senolytic, and D+Q is not an approved therapy for any aging or fibrotic indication. Human data to date: proof-of-mechanism (senescent-cell clearance in diabetic kidney disease, N=9, PMID 31542391) and small open-label safety/feasibility pilots (IPF, n=14, PMID 30616998; Alzheimer's SToMP-AD, n=5, PMID 37162971 — in which dasatinib but NOT quercetin was detectable in CSF). More than 20 registered D+Q clinical trials exist across IPF, chronic/diabetic kidney disease, Alzheimer's/MCI, frailty, bone loss, NAFLD, cancer survivorship, HIV, and others (all verified real: e.g., NCT02848131, NCT02874989, NCT04063124, NCT04685590, NCT04313634, NCT06018467, NCT05506488, NCT04733534, NCT07144293) — but as of 2026 no adequately powered randomized Phase 3 has reported a positive clinical disease outcome; the placebo-controlled Phase 2 trials (e.g., NCT04685590) are ongoing. Efficacy claims are early-stage; the strongest human result is that the drugs do what they are designed to do (clear senescent cells), not that this changes disease trajectory.

At a glance

  • Selectively eliminates senescent cells (senolysis) — confirmed in human adipose and skin tissue after just 3 days of dosing (PMID 31542391)
  • Lowers circulating SASP inflammatory factors (IL-1a, IL-6, MMP-9, MMP-12) in humans (PMID 31542391)
  • Reduces senescence-associated macrophage infiltration / crown-like structures in fat tissue (PMID 31542391)
  • Improved physical function (6-minute walk, gait speed, chair-stands) in a small open-label IPF pilot (n=14) — but no change in pulmonary function (PMID 30616998)
  • In aged mice: improved physical function and increased survival after senescent-cell clearance (PMID 29988130) — rodent evidence, not yet replicated in a human outcomes trial
  • 'Hit-and-run' pharmacology: works with brief intermittent dosing because the effect is triggering apoptosis, not maintaining a blood level (PMID 31542391, PMID 30616998)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps4 human / established 4 rodent / preclinical

  1. Senescent cells accumulate and become apoptosis-resistant Established

    With age, stress, radiation and chronic disease, some cells enter senescence — a stable growth arrest. Instead of dying, they up-regulate 'pro-survival' networks (SCAPs), which is why they persist and pile up at disease sites. PMID 25754370 (Zhu 2015, Aging Cell); PMID 29988130 (Xu 2018, Nat Med)

  2. Transcriptomics maps the survival wiring senescent cells depend on Evidence-in vitro

    Kirkland's group profiled senescent human cells and found several up-regulated survival nodes — ephrins (e.g., EFNB1), PI3K-delta, p21, BCL-xL and serpins/PAI. Silencing individual nodes with RNA interference killed senescent cells while sparing normal dividing or quiescent cells. This defined the drug targets. PMID 25754370 (Zhu 2015, Aging Cell)

  3. Dasatinib blocks SRC/tyrosine-kinase survival signaling in senescent progenitors Evidence-in vitro

    Dasatinib is a broad tyrosine-kinase inhibitor (SRC-family kinases, plus BCR-ABL, KIT, PDGFR-beta, ephrin-A receptors — all confirmed in ChEMBL mechanism data). In senescent cells it interrupts kinase-dependent survival signals — in the founding screen it was most effective at eliminating senescent human fat-cell progenitors (preadipocytes). PMID 25754370 (Zhu 2015, Aging Cell); ChEMBL CHEMBL1421 mechanism of action (SRC / BCR-ABL / KIT / PDGFR-beta / EphA2 inhibitor)

  4. Quercetin inhibits BCL-2-family and PI3K nodes, covering a different senescent cell type Evidence-in vitro

    Quercetin, a flavonoid, targets anti-apoptotic BCL-2-family proteins and PI3K/insulin-IGF-1 signaling (and serpins). In the founding screen it was more effective against senescent human endothelial cells and marrow stromal cells. Because dasatinib and quercetin cover different cell types, the combination clears a broader range than either alone. PMID 25754370 (Zhu 2015, Aging Cell); PMID 30616998 (Justice 2019, quercetin targets BCL-2, insulin/IGF-1, HIF-1alpha SCAP nodes)

  5. Disabling SCAPs tips senescent cells into their own apoptotic program Evidence-human

    Senescent cells live in a self-created pro-apoptotic environment held in check only by these survival proteins. Transiently blocking the SCAPs removes the brake, so the cell's own death signals win and it undergoes apoptosis — while normal cells, which don't depend on these nodes, survive. This is why brief, intermittent 'hit-and-run' dosing works: the drugs need only be present long enough to trigger death, not to sit at a steady level. PMID 31542391 (Hickson 2019, EBioMedicine); PMID 25754370 (Zhu 2015)

  6. Fewer senescent cells means less SASP (inflammatory secretion) Evidence-human

    Senescent cells secrete a pro-inflammatory, tissue-degrading mix (the senescence-associated secretory phenotype, SASP: IL-6, IL-1, MMPs and more). Clearing the cells lowers this output. In the human diabetic-kidney-disease trial, circulating IL-1a, IL-6, MMP-9 and MMP-12 fell after treatment. PMID 31542391 (Hickson 2019, EBioMedicine)

  7. In humans, D+Q measurably reduces senescent cell burden in tissue Evidence-human

    This is the key human proof-of-mechanism: 3 days of oral D+Q reduced p16-INK4A+ and p21-CIP1+ cells, senescence-associated beta-galactosidase activity, and associated macrophages in fat and skin biopsies from 9 patients with diabetic kidney disease — despite the drugs clearing the bloodstream (elimination half-lives under 11 hours). PMID 31542391 (Hickson 2019, EBioMedicine)

  8. In aged mice, clearance translates to better function and longer life — not yet proven in humans Evidence-rodent

    In naturally aged mice, intermittent D+Q reduced senescent-cell number, improved physical function, and increased post-treatment survival by 36% (mortality hazard reduced to 65%). Transplanting senescent cells into young mice caused physical dysfunction that D+Q reversed. These functional/lifespan gains are rodent findings; the equivalent disease-outcome benefit has NOT been demonstrated in a positive human Phase 3. PMID 29988130 (Xu 2018, Nat Med)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Dasatinib is an ATP-competitive small-molecule inhibitor of SRC-family kinases (SRC, LCK, YES, FYN) and other tyrosine kinases (BCR-ABL, KIT, PDGFR-beta, ephrin-A receptors), binding the kinase ATP pocket — all of these targets are documented in ChEMBL mechanism-of-action data (CHEMBL1421). In the senolytic context its relevant action is blocking tyrosine-kinase-dependent survival signaling (including ephrin-receptor and PI3K/AKT-linked nodes) that senescent progenitor cells rely on. Quercetin is a flavonol that targets anti-apoptotic BCL-2-family proteins (BCL-2/BCL-xL), PI3K/insulin-IGF-1 signaling, HIF-1alpha and serpins, and also has broad antioxidant/kinase-modulating activity. Neither drug is senescent-cell-specific on its own; specificity for senolysis arises because senescent cells are uniquely dependent on these SCAP survival nodes (Zhu 2015).

Signalling cascade. D+Q -> inhibition of SRC-family/ephrin/PI3K (dasatinib) and BCL-2/BCL-xL/PI3K (quercetin) -> collapse of the Senescent Cell Anti-Apoptotic Pathways (SCAPs) -> the pro-apoptotic environment intrinsic to senescent cells is unmasked -> selective apoptosis of senescent cells -> reduced senescent-cell burden -> reduced SASP and macrophage recruitment -> (rodent) improved tissue function and survival.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral, both agents. Given intermittently ('hit-and-run', e.g., a few days on / weeks off) rather than continuously — the therapeutic goal is a transient apoptotic hit, not steady-state exposure.
Absorption / bioavailability
Dasatinib: rapidly absorbed, Tmax ~0.5-3 h; absorption is pH-dependent (reduced by acid-suppressing agents). Quercetin: low and highly variable oral bioavailability due to extensive first-pass metabolism and poor solubility; various formulations attempt to improve uptake.
Half-life
Both are short-lived in the sense relevant to senolysis. The Hickson human study notes combined D+Q elimination half-lives <11 h, and the IPF report cites individual half-lives <6 h — yet senolytic/functional effects persisted well after the drugs were gone, the basis of intermittent dosing.
Metabolism
Dasatinib: primarily hepatic via CYP3A4 (active and inactive metabolites) — strong CYP3A4 inhibitors raise levels, inducers lower them. Quercetin: extensive phase II conjugation (glucuronidation/sulfation/methylation) to circulating conjugates; quercetin also inhibits CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein, which can raise co-administered drug levels including dasatinib.
Excretion
Dasatinib: predominantly fecal/biliary excretion of metabolites, minor renal. Quercetin conjugates cleared via bile and urine.
Notes
The key PK insight is temporal disconnect: brief exposure (hours) produces a durable biological effect (senescent cells removed for days-plus) because apoptosis is a one-way commitment. This decouples efficacy from continuous drug presence and is the rationale for intermittent regimens (PMID 31542391, PMID 30616998). CNS penetrance is limited and asymmetric: in the SToMP-AD trial dasatinib was detectable in CSF but quercetin was not (PMID 37162971).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Senolytic dosing is deliberately low and intermittent, distinct from continuous oncology dasatinib dosing. The relationship is threshold/'trigger'-like rather than classically dose-linear: enough drug must be present transiently to push senescent cells past the apoptotic tipping point, after which continued exposure adds toxicity without proportional senolytic benefit. Human proof-of-mechanism was achieved with only 3 days of oral dosing in the diabetic-kidney-disease study (PMID 31542391), and the IPF pilot used a 3-days/week over 3-weeks schedule (PMID 30616998). Higher/continuous exposure shifts the risk-benefit toward dasatinib's dose-dependent oncology toxicities (effusions, PAH, cytopenias). No validated dose-response curve for aging/longevity outcomes exists in humans, and no dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Navitoclax (ABT-263) and BCL-2/BCL-xL-selective senolytics
    same: Both classes force apoptosis by disabling anti-apoptotic BCL-2-family proteins; navitoclax and quercetin overlap on the BCL-xL node.
    different: Navitoclax is a potent, targeted BCL-2/BCL-xL inhibitor but causes dose-limiting thrombocytopenia (platelets depend on BCL-xL), limiting systemic use; D+Q is broader-spectrum (covers more senescent cell types via the dasatinib/SRC arm) and repurposes existing agents.
  • vs Fisetin (flavonoid senolytic)
    same: Both are flavonoid-based senolytic strategies; fisetin is frequently studied alongside or instead of quercetin in the same trial programs (e.g., NCT04313634, NCT04733534).
    different: Fisetin is often used as a single agent with a cleaner supplement/tolerability profile and no oncology-drug partner, whereas D+Q pairs quercetin with the prescription TKI dasatinib, adding both broader cell-type coverage and dasatinib's toxicity/interaction burden.
  • vs Senomorphics (e.g., rapamycin/rapalogs, metformin, some SASP inhibitors)
    same: Both aim to reduce the damage from senescent cells and lower SASP-driven inflammation.
    different: Senomorphics suppress the harmful secretory phenotype WITHOUT killing the cells (must be taken continuously); senolytics like D+Q ELIMINATE the cells, enabling intermittent 'hit-and-run' dosing.
  • vs Continuous oncology dasatinib (Sprycel) monotherapy
    same: Same molecule and same kinase targets.
    different: Senolytic use is low-dose, intermittent, and paired with quercetin to trigger transient senolysis; oncology use is higher, continuous, and single-agent — carrying far greater cumulative toxicity exposure.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Cellular senescence as a driver of aging pathology was cemented by transgenic senescent-cell-ablation studies (Baker/van Deursen), which motivated a small-molecule approach. In 2015 the Kirkland/Tchkonia group at Mayo Clinic, with Robbins/Niedernhofer (then Scripps), published the first senolytics paper (Zhu et al., Aging Cell, PMID 25754370), coining 'senolytics' and identifying D+Q by mapping and pharmacologically hitting the SCAP survival network. Xu et al. 2018 (Nat Med, PMID 29988130) provided the influential mouse healthspan/lifespan data. The first human data came in 2019: the IPF open-label pilot (Justice et al., PMID 30616998) and the diabetic-kidney-disease proof-of-mechanism study (Hickson et al., PMID 31542391), the latter being the first direct demonstration that senolytics clear senescent cells in people. Since then the field has expanded to 20+ registered D+Q trials across fibrotic, metabolic, neurodegenerative, skeletal and cancer-survivorship indications (Mayo Clinic, Wake Forest, UT Health San Antonio, Washington University, St. Jude, and international sites). Regulatory status: dasatinib (Sprycel, Bristol-Myers Squibb) has been FDA-approved for CML/Ph+ ALL since 2006; quercetin is marketed as a dietary supplement. As of 2026 the D+Q senolytic combination has NO regulatory approval for any aging, fibrotic, renal, or neurodegenerative indication and remains investigational/off-label.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Does clearing senescent cells with D+Q change hard clinical outcomes in humans (disease progression, mortality, disability) — or only the biomarker of senescent-cell burden? No positive Phase 3 has answered this; placebo-controlled Phase 2 trials are still running.
  • Optimal dosing frequency and interval for durable benefit without cumulative toxicity — the 'hit-and-run' schedule is empirically motivated, not optimized in humans.
  • Long-term safety of repeated intermittent dasatinib exposure in older, non-cancer populations, including cardiopulmonary (effusion/PAH) and hematologic risk.
  • Which patients/tissues benefit vs. are harmed — senescence can be transiently protective in acute injury, and preventive D+Q worsened damage in an acute-kidney-injury mouse model (PMID 40573218).
  • How much of quercetin's contribution is real senolysis versus its low bioavailability and poor tissue/CNS penetrance making it a minor partner to dasatinib in vivo (in SToMP-AD quercetin was undetectable in CSF).
  • Whether adequate CNS exposure is achieved for neurodegenerative indications, and whether that translates to cognitive benefit (SToMP-AD was a tiny feasibility study).
  • Comparative effectiveness vs. fisetin, navitoclax, or newer targeted/next-generation senolytics.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Investigational drug requiring physician supervision — NOT for self-administration. Reasonable monitoring domains reported/relevant in the trial literature (framing what to WATCH, not how to dose): (1) Cardiopulmonary — symptoms/signs of pleural or pericardial effusion (new dyspnea, cough, chest discomfort) and pulmonary arterial hypertension, given dasatinib's endothelial-permeability effect (PMID 29348177, PMID 34776414); baseline and on-treatment assessment for at-risk patients. (2) Hematologic — complete blood counts for cytopenias, and awareness of bruising/bleeding (frequent in the IPF pilot, PMID 30616998). (3) Drug interactions — review for CYP3A4 substrates/inhibitors/inducers and P-gp substrates (quercetin inhibits CYP3A4/P-gp; dasatinib is a CYP3A4 substrate) and for acid-suppressing agents that reduce dasatinib absorption. (4) Renal/hepatic function and glucose in metabolic/kidney indications; caution given evidence that senolytics can worsen acute kidney injury contexts (PMID 40573218). (5) QT/cardiac considerations per dasatinib labeling. Any use should be within a clinical trial or under specialist oversight; this monograph provides no protocol or dose.

Caveats

HYPE-VS-DATA: The lifespan/healthspan headline ("senolytics extend life ~36%") is a mouse result (PMID 29988130), not a human one — do not conflate the two. In humans the confirmed effect is senescent-cell CLEARANCE and lower inflammatory SASP markers (PMID 31542391), plus a functional signal in a tiny (n=14) uncontrolled IPF pilot where pulmonary function did NOT improve (PMID 30616998). No positive Phase 3 disease outcome exists. SAFETY: dasatinib is a chemotherapy-grade kinase inhibitor with well-documented dose-dependent toxicities in oncology use — pleural/pericardial effusions, pulmonary arterial hypertension (via increased endothelial permeability, PMID 29348177; PMID 34776414), cytopenias, bleeding risk, QT effects and drug interactions; the low-dose intermittent senolytic regimen is intended to minimize these but the long-term safety of even intermittent use in non-cancer/older populations is not established. Notably, in a folic-acid acute-kidney-injury mouse model, preventive D+Q did NOT clear senescent cells and INCREASED kidney-damage markers (PMID 40573218) — a reminder that senolytics are not universally protective and context matters. Quercetin has variable, low oral bioavailability and inhibits CYP3A4/P-gp, which can raise levels of co-administered drugs (including dasatinib). This is NOT a self-experimentation protocol: it uses a prescription oncology drug with serious interactions, requires medical supervision, and no dosing guidance is given here.
