Hexarelin

A synthetic 6-amino-acid "growth hormone-releasing peptide" that pulses the pituitary to dump growth hormone — and also grabs a second receptor (CD36) in the heart, giving it cardiovascular effects most GH boosters don't have.

Limited human data 28 citations
Class
Growth hormone secretagogue (GHRP-6 analog), synthetic hexapeptide — unapproved research peptide
Primary target
Growth hormone secretagogue receptor 1a (GHS-R1a, the ghrelin receptor) in the hypothalamus and pituitary; secondary target CD36 (a scavenger receptor) in heart and blood vessels
Human status
Limited and old. In humans, hexarelin reliably and potently releases GH after a single dose, and also bumps ACTH, cortisol, and prolactin (Evidence-human; PMID 11238504, 10404825). Small acute studies in patients reported improved heart pumping (ejection fraction) after IV dosing (Evidence-human, small/old; e.g. bypass-surgery study PMID 12144941). There are NO modern registered clinical trials (ClinicalTrials.gov: 0 results for hexarelin) and it has never been approved for any indication. The headline cardioprotection, anti-fibrosis, and anti-atherosclerosis claims are almost entirely rodent/in-vitro. A known catch: with continuous/repeated dosing the GH response tends to wane (tachyphylaxis/desensitization of GHS-R1a) — short human studies did not see desensitization (PMID 8921821), but this is the central open question for any real-world "stacked daily" use, and it is not resolved in humans. Bottom line for viewers: the GH spike is real in people; the exciting heart-repair story is preclinical hype until proven in humans.

At a glance

  • Strong, pulsatile growth hormone release after a single dose (Evidence-human, PMID 11238504) — comparable to or slightly less than ghrelin, and synergistic when combined with GHRH
  • Raises ACTH and cortisol, and prolactin — i.e. it is NOT a 'clean' GH releaser; it nudges the stress-hormone axis, partly via vasopressin (Evidence-human, PMID 10404825). This is a real downside vs. selective secretagogues
  • Direct heart effects via CD36 independent of GH: increases contractility and changes coronary vessel tone (Evidence-rodent; CD36 identification PMID 11988484)
  • Reduced ischemia/reperfusion injury and infarct damage in rats, linked to IL-1 signaling and GHS-R1a (Evidence-rodent, PMID 28321024; 24747279)
  • Anti-fibrotic in heart — suppressed cardiac fibroblast proliferation and collagen in rat models (Evidence-rodent, PMID 17766487; 22842067)
  • Anti-atherosclerotic signal — competes with oxidized-LDL at the CD36 binding pocket and reduced plaque in rodent models (Evidence-rodent/in vitro, PMID 15176951; 19931584)
  • Risk of waning GH response with repeated dosing (desensitization of GHS-R1a) — not seen in short human studies (PMID 8921821) but unresolved for chronic use (Contested)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a) Established

    Hexarelin is a man-made mimic of ghrelin, your 'hunger/GH' hormone. It docks onto the same receptor ghrelin uses — GHS-R1a — sitting on cells in the hypothalamus and pituitary. Because it's synthetic, it's more chemically stable and longer-lasting than natural ghrelin. PMID 25278975 (review); PMID 11238504

  2. Pituitary releases a pulse of growth hormone Evidence-human

    Activating GHS-R1a triggers a calcium-driven signaling cascade inside pituitary somatotroph cells, which dump stored growth hormone into the blood. In humans a single dose produces a big GH spike — and if you also give GHRH (the other GH trigger) the two work synergistically for an even larger surge. PMID 11238504

  3. Spillover onto the stress axis: ACTH, cortisol, prolactin Evidence-human

    The same receptor activation isn't perfectly selective for GH. In people, hexarelin also drives the adrenal/stress axis — raising ACTH and cortisol (partly by stimulating vasopressin release) and prolactin. So it's not a 'clean' GH switch; it tugs on stress hormones too, which matters for anyone using it regularly. PMID 10404825; PMID 11238504

  4. A SECOND receptor in the heart: CD36 Established

    Here's what makes hexarelin unusual. Researchers tagged it with a label and fished out its binding partner in heart tissue — it turned out to be CD36, a 'scavenger receptor' on heart muscle cells and tiny blood vessels, completely separate from the ghrelin receptor. This gives hexarelin direct actions on the heart that don't depend on growth hormone at all. PMID 11988484

  5. CD36 binding changes heart contraction and vessel tone Evidence-rodent

    Through CD36 (and GHS-R1a present in the heart), hexarelin alters ion currents and calcium handling in heart muscle cells, increasing the force of contraction, and changes coronary blood-vessel tone. In rodent models this is the basis for its 'cardiac' reputation. Important caveat: most of this is in rats and isolated tissue. PMID 11988484; PMID 20610573; PMID 20056829

  6. Tissue protection: less injury, less scarring, less plaque (in animals) Evidence-rodent

    In rodent models, hexarelin reduced heart damage after a simulated heart attack (tied to calming IL-1 inflammatory signaling), suppressed fibroblast scar-tissue formation, and — because its CD36 binding site overlaps the spot CD36 uses to grab oxidized LDL — it blunted atherosclerotic plaque build-up. Promising biology, but this protective story has not been confirmed in humans. PMID 28321024; PMID 17766487; PMID 15176951

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. GHS-R1a: full agonist (Gq-coupled). Hexarelin is the high-affinity prototype peptidyl GHS — in human-tissue radioreceptor assays using [125I]Tyr-Ala-hexarelin as tracer, hexarelin fully displaces the radioligand and is MORE potent than both MK-0677 and ghrelin at peptidyl-GHS binding sites, especially in endocrine tissues and myocardium (Established; PMID 11061542). CD36 binding: identified by photoaffinity cross-linking of rat cardiac membranes — a radioactive photoactivatable hexarelin derivative covalently labeled an 84-kDa glycoprotein whose N-terminal sequence was identical to CD36 (Established/Evidence-rodent; PMID 11988484). The hexarelin binding domain on CD36 was mapped to the Asn132–Glu177 region, with Met169 the contact residue; this OVERLAPS the oxLDL binding site (Gln155–Lys183), so hexarelin can compete with oxidized LDL for CD36 (Established/in vitro; PMID 15176951). The receptor distribution is wide: human radioreceptor mapping found peptidyl-GHS binding mainly in MYOCARDIUM, then adrenal, gonads, arteries, lung, liver, skeletal muscle, kidney, pituitary, thyroid, adipose tissue (PMID 11061542). Specific dissociation constants (Ki/Kd) in nM are NOT robustly established in the human literature retrieved (key gap). Functionally hexarelin acts at low nanomolar concentrations: in rat ventricular myocytes electrophysiologic effects occur at ~10 nM (PMID 20056829).

Signalling cascade. GHS-R1a arm (neuroendocrine): agonism activates Gq → phospholipase C → IP3/DAG → intracellular Ca2+ rise and PKC activation in pituitary somatotrophs → pulsatile GH release. Centrally, hexarelin amplifies GH release partly by stimulating hypothalamic GHRH and by functionally antagonizing somatostatin tone (Evidence-rodent/human; PMID 7731497). Direct cardiac GHS-R1a arm: in isolated rat ventricular myocytes, hexarelin/ghrelin (10 nM) activate PLC→PKC, which (a) increases L-type Ca2+ current I(Ca,L), raising the Ca2+ transient amplitude and producing a positive inotropic effect — abolished by the GHS-R1a antagonist [D-Lys3]-GHRP-6 and by the PKC inhibitor chelerythrine, but NOT by PKA inhibition (Evidence-rodent; PMID 20610573); and (b) inhibits the transient outward K+ current I(to) via PLC/PKC, prolonging action potential duration (Evidence-rodent; PMID 20056829). CD36 arm (cardiovascular/metabolic): CD36 activation in perfused hearts raises coronary perfusion pressure dose-dependently (a vasoconstrictive/coronary tone effect), absent in CD36-null mice and CD36-deficient SHR rats (Evidence-rodent; PMID 11988484). Downstream of CD36, hexarelin signals to PPARγ, driving genes of fatty-acid uptake/oxidation and adipocyte differentiation — the basis of its lipid-lowering and insulin-sensitizing effects (Evidence-rodent/in vitro; PMID 18288286). Additional cardioprotective signalling described in models includes PI3K/Akt and MAPK survival pathways, autophagy modulation, PTEN/MDM2-p53 modulation, anti-fibrotic suppression of cardiac fibroblast proliferation/collagen, and endothelium-dependent prostacyclin (PGI2) release contributing to inotropy and anoxic recovery (Evidence-rodent; PMIDs 34066741, 31091855, 17766487, 11669462).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Parenteral peptide. Most human/clinical data are IV bolus or subcutaneous; nasal and oral forms were studied historically. It is NOT esterified and is not a prodrug. As a small 6-residue peptide it is not orally bioavailable in any practical sense without enhancers.
Absorption / bioavailability
SC bioavailability is high for a peptide: ~64% in rats (Evidence-rodent; PMID 10611139). ORAL bioavailability is very poor — estimated at ~0.3% ± 0.1% in humans (from GH-response modeling), because hexarelin is degraded by intestinal/pancreatic proteases (chymotrypsin-like and trypsin, which deamidate the C-terminal lysine) and has low permeability across intestinal epithelium and Caco-2 monolayers (Evidence-human/in vitro; PMIDs 11578108, 9643439). Intranasal delivery was clinically used to accelerate growth in short children (Evidence-human; PMID 8548949); in rabbits intranasal dosing gave brain/CSF levels ~1.6× higher than IV at equal dose via a direct nose-to-brain (olfactory) route despite lower plasma levels (Evidence-animal; PMID 19501141).
Half-life
Short. In healthy humans (IV bolus dose-response), the GH-response-associated plasma half-life was ~55 min (Evidence-human; PMID 7957536). In rats IV t1/2 ≈ 76 min; in dogs IV terminal t1/2 ≈ 120 min (Evidence-animal; PMIDs 10611139, 8545255). Volume of distribution at steady state is small (~387–744 mL/kg across species), consistent with limited tissue penetration.
Metabolism
Notably proteolytically STABLE in plasma/systemically: in bile-duct-exteriorized rats, 90% of biliary and 71% of urinary radioactivity was UNCHANGED hexarelin, demonstrating resistance to systemic peptidases (the D-amino acids confer this) (Evidence-rodent; PMID 10611139). The major site of degradation is the GUT lumen, where pancreatic/intestinal chymotrypsin- and trypsin-type proteases deamidate the Lys residue (Evidence-in vitro; PMID 11578108). No specific hepatic CYP involvement — it is a peptide, cleared by peptidases/excretion, not oxidative metabolism.
Excretion
Primarily BILIARY/hepatic. In rats, total recovery of label was ~60% bile, 22% urine, 10% feces; biliary excretion is the dominant elimination route, with renal excretion secondary (Evidence-rodent; PMID 10611139). Highest tissue radioactivity in kidney, liver and duodenum. Kinetics are first-order (linear, no accumulation) up to 50 µg/kg.
Notes
Chemically more stable and functionally more potent than ghrelin (Evidence/review; PMID 25278975). Dual-receptor (GHS-R1a + CD36) pharmacology means cardiac/metabolic effects are partly GH-independent. Crosses to brain efficiently via intranasal olfactory transfer. No ester/prodrug chemistry; the D-2-methyl-Trp and D-Phe residues are the source of its protease resistance and oral-route failure (gut proteases still cleave the Lys amide).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

HUMAN (GH release): The pivotal double-blind, placebo-controlled, rising-dose IV study (n=12 healthy adult men; PMID 7957536) gave a clean dose-response. Mean peak GH (Cmax) was 3.9 ng/mL (placebo), 26.9 (0.5 µg/kg), 52.3 (1 µg/kg), 55.0 (2 µg/kg) — i.e. the curve is near-saturated by ~1–2 µg/kg. Modeled ED50 ≈ 0.50 µg/kg (for Cmax) and 0.64 µg/kg (for AUC); Emax ≈ 55 ng/mL. GH peaked ~30 min and returned to baseline by ~240 min (Evidence-human, high certainty for GH endpoint). HUMAN (cardiac): A single IV dose of 2.0 µg/kg in CABG patients with coronary artery disease acutely raised LV ejection fraction, cardiac index and cardiac output within +10 min, lasting to +90 min, with reduced wedge pressure and modestly raised mean arterial pressure — and critically this inotropic effect was NOT reproduced by equipotent-GH doses of GHRH or rhGH, proving a GH-independent direct cardiac action (Evidence-human; PMID 12144941). Human cardiac dose-response across multiple doses is essentially uncharacterized — most human cardiac data are single-dose. CHRONIC HUMAN: 16 weeks of twice-daily SC hexarelin (1.5 µg/kg) in elderly subjects produced partial, reversible attenuation of the GH response (desensitization), with GH AUC falling ~45% by week 16 and recovering after washout (Evidence-human; PMID 10990150). ANIMAL (cardiac/metabolic, much higher doses): rat MI models used 10–100 µg/kg/day SC for 2 weeks to improve post-infarct cardiac function (PMID 10614623); ghrelin-KO mouse MI used 400 nmol/kg/day SC (hexarelin out-performed equimolar ghrelin on EF and dP/dt; PMID 23861368); metabolic benefit in insulin-resistant MKR mice used 200 µg/kg twice daily (PMID 28977588). BOTTOM LINE: human GH dose-response is well defined (ED50 ~0.5 µg/kg, plateau ~1–2 µg/kg); human cardiac/metabolic dose-response magnitudes, and any human anabolic/body-composition dose-response, are NOT established — those endpoints rest on rodent data at substantially higher mg-equivalent doses.

How it compares

  • vs Ghrelin (endogenous GHS-R1a ligand)
    same: Both are GHS-R1a full agonists driving pulsatile GH release; both produce GH-independent cardioprotective/positive-inotropic effects via PLC/PKC→I(Ca,L) in cardiomyocytes.
    different: Hexarelin is a synthetic D-amino-acid hexapeptide — chemically more stable (protease-resistant) and functionally more potent than ghrelin at peptidyl-GHS sites (PMID 11061542); it is NOT acylated (ghrelin needs octanoylation). Hexarelin binds CD36 (cardiac/macrophage) which ghrelin does not engage the same way, and head-to-head it out-performed equimolar ghrelin on post-MI EF/dP/dt (PMID 23861368). Ghrelin also has orexigenic/GI/metabolic roles via its own receptor distribution.
  • vs GHRP-6 (the parent peptide hexarelin is an analog of)
    same: Same GHRP family, same His-D-Trp-Ala-Trp-D-Phe-Lys core, both GHS-R1a agonists and both bind cardiac CD36 (the CD36 work was on 'GHRPs' generally; PMID 11988484).
    different: Hexarelin (His-D-2-methyl-Trp-...) carries a 2-methyl-Trp modification giving greater metabolic stability and higher GH-releasing potency than GHRP-6; GHRP-6 is also more strongly orexigenic per unit GH effect.
  • vs MK-0677 / Ibutamoren (non-peptidyl orally-active GHS)
    same: Both are GHS-R1a agonists that raise GH/IGF-1 with pulsatile, feedback-preserving kinetics.
    different: Ibutamoren is a small non-peptide, ORALLY bioavailable with a long half-life suited to once-daily dosing — unlike hexarelin (parenteral, ~55-min human t1/2, ~0.3% oral bioavailability). In human radioreceptor assays hexarelin was MORE potent than MK-0677 at peptidyl binding sites and, unlike MK-0677, MK-0677/ghrelin do not displace [125I]-hexarelin from non-endocrine (e.g. cardiac CD36-type) sites — i.e. hexarelin engages a peptidyl-specific cardiac receptor MK-0677 does not (PMID 11061542). In isolated papillary muscle MK-0677 actually REDUCED contractility whereas hexarelin was inotropic (PMID 11669462).

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    This blend contains ipamorelin, which is ALSO a GHS-R1a agonist — so pairing it with hexarelin is redundant on GHS-R1a and adds desensitization risk at that receptor, while the CJC-1295 (GHRH-R) component synergizes with hexarelin for a larger GH pulse (GHRP-plus-GHRH synergy). The compounding concern is amplified GH excess. Note ipamorelin is specifically a 'clean' secretagogue with minimal cortisol/prolactin effect, so the HPA/prolactin load in this stack comes from hexarelin's non-selectivity, not from ipamorelin. Hexarelin monograph (GHS-R1a target; GHRH synergy; ACTH/cortisol/prolactin rise, PMID 15177700/11238504); CJC-1295+ipamorelin monograph (CJC GHRH-R, ipamorelin GHS-R1a and 'clean' with minimal cortisol/prolactin) Watch: IGF-1 (watch for a waning response suggesting desensitization); GH-excess signs (edema, arthralgia); fasting glucose; and, because of hexarelin's own non-selectivity, prolactin and a morning cortisol.
  • Trenbolone compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Two independent routes to the same problem — hyperprolactinemia. Hexarelin raises prolactin directly via pituitary GHS-R1a agonism (human-shown), while trenbolone is a 19-nortestosterone with intrinsic progestogenic activity that raises prolactin. Together they stack two prolactin-elevating mechanisms, worsening prolactin-driven effects (low libido, erectile dysfunction, gynecomastia) that an aromatase inhibitor will NOT fix because neither route is estrogen-mediated. Hexarelin monograph (prolactin rise, PMID 10404825/11238504); corpus PMID 15177700 (hexarelin raises prolactin); trenbolone monograph (19-nor progestogenic activity raises prolactin; AI does not fix) Watch: Serum prolactin; prolactin-related symptoms (libido/erectile changes, gynecomastia, galactorrhea). Estradiol control alone does not address this — the prolactin/progestin route is separate.
  • Sermorelin potentiates ↑ Evidence-human
    Hexarelin acts at GHS-R1a while sermorelin is a GHRH-receptor agonist — two independent, complementary triggers on the same pituitary somatotroph. GHRP-plus-GHRH is the classic synergistic pairing: together they produce a markedly larger GH pulse than the sum of either alone, because the GHRP arm also blunts somatostatin tone. This synergy is human-demonstrated with hexarelin plus GHRH(1-29). Hexarelin monograph mechanism step 2 (GHRH synergy, PMID 11238504); corpus PMID 8799695 (combined GHRH + hexarelin on GH secretion in humans) Watch: IGF-1 and symptoms of GH excess from the amplified pulse — fluid retention/edema, joint pain/arthralgia, carpal-tunnel-type paresthesia; fasting glucose (larger GH pulses mean more insulin resistance).
  • Tesamorelin potentiates ↑ Evidence-human
    Same GHRP-plus-GHRH synergy as with sermorelin: tesamorelin is a stabilized GHRH analog acting at the GHRH receptor, a different pathway from hexarelin's GHS-R1a. Combining a GHRH-R agonist with a GHS-R1a agonist drives a supra-additive GH pulse (GHRP arm eases somatostatin tone; GHRH arm directly stimulates). The human synergy data are for GHRH(1-29) plus hexarelin; extending it to tesamorelin is a mechanism-class extrapolation since tesamorelin is a GHRH-R agonist. Hexarelin monograph mechanism step 2 (GHRH synergy, PMID 11238504); corpus PMID 8799695 (GHRH + hexarelin combined in humans); tesamorelin monograph (GHRH-R agonist) Watch: IGF-1 and signs of GH excess (edema, arthralgia, paresthesia); fasting glucose/HbA1c, since the larger GH surge accentuates insulin resistance.
  • Cabergoline counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    Hexarelin is not a clean GH releaser — its GHS-R1a agonism in the pituitary transiently raises prolactin along with GH (shown in humans). Cabergoline is a dopamine D2 agonist that tonically suppresses prolactin at the lactotroph, so it directly counteracts hexarelin's prolactin bump. This is the same mechanism used to manage prolactin elevations from other sources. Hexarelin monograph (prolactin rise, PMID 10404825/11238504); corpus PMID 15177700 (hexarelin raises prolactin in humans); cabergoline monograph (D2-selective agonist lowers prolactin) Watch: Serum prolactin to confirm control; symptoms of high prolactin (low libido, erectile changes, galactorrhea). Note cabergoline itself needs monitoring for mood and, at higher cumulative exposure, cardiac valve effects.
  • Metformin counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    Hexarelin's GH pulses reduce insulin sensitivity and can raise glucose (GH is counter-regulatory). Metformin lowers hepatic glucose output and improves insulin sensitivity, so it pushes in the opposite metabolic direction and can partially offset the GH-driven glucose rise — a counteracting metabolic interaction (opposing net effects on glucose) rather than a shared molecular pathway. Hexarelin monograph (GH-induced insulin resistance, PMID 28400207); metformin monograph (lowers hepatic glucose output / improves insulin sensitivity) Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c to gauge net effect; watch for GI intolerance from metformin. Improved glucose does not prove the GH-axis load is gone — track IGF-1 separately.
  • Insulin opposes Evidence-human
    Hexarelin pulses growth hormone via GHS-R1a, and GH is a counter-regulatory, anti-insulin hormone: it lowers peripheral insulin sensitivity and can raise fasting glucose. That directly opposes insulin's glucose-lowering action, so the same insulin effect is blunted while background glucose drifts up. This is the recognized metabolic pitfall of the GH-secretagogue class, not unique to hexarelin. Hexarelin monograph side_effect_mechanisms (GH-driven insulin resistance, class-level, PMID 28400207); corpus on GH counter-regulation of insulin Watch: Fasting glucose, HbA1c and fasting insulin; watch for creeping hyperglycemia. Because GH raises glucose while insulin lowers it, the net picture is unpredictable and episodes of both high and low glucose warrant physician review.
  • GHRP-2 redundant Established (shared GHS-R1a; near-identical human endocrine spectrum)
    Hexarelin is another GHRP-6 super-analog with a near-identical human endocrine profile to GHRP-2 (GH, prolactin, ACTH, cortisol), acting on the same GHS-R1a. Combining the two is largely redundant for GH but compounds the shared spillover (cortisol/ACTH, prolactin) and accelerates GHS-R1a desensitization. Hexarelin adds a secondary CD36 (scavenger-receptor) action not shared by GHRP-2, but the overlap that matters for stacking is the identical ghrelin-receptor mechanism. GHRP-2 monograph comparisons (hexarelin 'essentially equivalent' human GH/PRL/ACTH/cortisol); Hexarelin monograph (GHS-R1a + CD36; raises ACTH/cortisol/prolactin, PMID 10404825) Watch: Morning cortisol/ACTH and prolactin (compounded HPA and lactotroph stimulation); GH/IGF-1 for evidence of blunting; appetite and weight.
  • GHRP-6 redundant Established
    Hexarelin is a direct GHRP-6 analog (single methyl-Trp substitution) acting on the identical GHS-R1a receptor and also binding CD36. Stacking it with GHRP-6 adds no new pathway and drives shared beta-arrestin desensitization at the receptor, so the GH response does not simply add. Their off-target ACTH/cortisol/prolactin elevations and appetite effects compound on the same mechanism, and hexarelin is a notably strong HPA/sleep-architecture disruptor. Monograph comparisons (hexarelin analog, GHS-R1a + CD36, stronger HPA/desensitization; PMID 15177700, 28321024); receptor desensitization PMID 15905359 Watch: IGF-1 (shared ceiling/desensitization); morning cortisol/ACTH and prolactin for compounded off-target stimulation; sleep quality and appetite/weight.
  • MK-677 redundant Established
    Both are full agonists at the very same receptor, GHS-R1a (the ghrelin receptor), driving the same pituitary somatotroph pathway to raise GH/IGF-1 — so stacking them is largely redundant rather than additive on the GH-releasing target. Chronic co-agonism of one receptor also raises the risk of GHS-R1a desensitization, so the GH output per dose can wane, and both share a GH-driven insulin-resistance liability. (Unlike hexarelin, MK-677 is comparatively GH-selective and does not meaningfully raise prolactin, so the cortisol/prolactin concern here is hexarelin's, not a compounding of the two.) Hexarelin monograph (GHS-R1a primary target; desensitization risk, Contested for chronic use); MK-677 monograph (GHS-R1a full agonist; relatively GH-selective, insulin resistance) Watch: IGF-1 as the integrated GH readout (watch for a plateau or fall despite continued use, suggesting desensitization); fasting glucose/HbA1c since both can worsen insulin sensitivity; appetite/weight and fluid retention (MK-677 is notable for both).

History & legal status

"Hexarelin (Examorelin) was developed in the early-mid 1990s by Romano Deghenghi and colleagues (Mediolanum Farmaceutici, Italy; later studied with Pharmacia & Upjohn) as a more stable, potent GHRP-6 analog — a synthetic GH-releasing hexapeptide. It was investigated in the 1990s–2000s as: (1) a diagnostic test of pituitary GH reserve, (2) a growth-promoting agent (including intranasal use to accelerate growth in short children; PMID 8548949), and (3) a cardiovascular agent after the discovery of its direct CD36-mediated cardiac actions. Despite favorable early data and clinically usable intranasal/oral-with-enhancer formulations, it was NEVER brought to market — partly due to the partial desensitization of the GH response on chronic dosing and the rise of orally-active non-peptide GHS (e.g. MK-0677) and recombinant GH (a 2003 commentary by Laron explicitly asked why these intranasal/oral GHS were not marketed; PMID 12602542). REGULATORY STATUS: Hexarelin is NOT an FDA- or EMA-approved drug for any indication; it has no marketing authorization. It is sold only as a 'research chemical' / gray-market peptide and is not a legitimate pharmaceutical. It is a prohibited substance in sport: WADA bans growth hormone secretagogues / GHRPs (including hexarelin) at all times under category S2 (Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors). It is not a controlled/scheduled narcotic, but human use is unapproved and unregulated."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No published human cardiac or metabolic DOSE-RESPONSE: nearly all human cardiac evidence is single acute IV doses (2 µg/kg); the dose-effect relationship, durability, and whether repeated dosing improves clinical cardiac outcomes in humans are unknown.
  • Quantitative human receptor affinities are not pinned down: Ki/Kd values (nM) for hexarelin at GHS-R1a vs CD36, and the GHS-R1a:CD36 selectivity ratio, are not established in the retrieved human literature — the 'peptidyl-specific' cardiac receptor identity beyond CD36 was even flagged as 'still unknown' (PMID 11061542).
  • Net human glucose/insulin effect is unresolved: GH-driven insulin resistance (a class concern) vs the CD36→PPARγ insulin-SENSITIZING effect seen in rodent metabolic models point in opposite directions, with no controlled human metabolic data.
  • Long-term safety is essentially unstudied: no data on cancer incidence/mortality, cardiac remodeling, or HPA-axis effects beyond ~16 weeks; GHS-class long-term safety is explicitly described as needing study (PMID 28400207).
  • Whether the CD36 coronary-vasoconstriction signal (rodent) translates to any human ischemic/vasospastic risk at GH-releasing doses, versus its anti-atherosclerotic oxLDL-competition benefit, is unknown.
  • Anabolic/body-composition claims in humans (the main reason it is used recreationally) are unvalidated — human lean-mass/fat-mass dose-response and any performance benefit have not been demonstrated in controlled trials.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (not an endorsement; no dosing guidance). Given the pharmacology, reasonable things a clinician would WATCH: (1) Glucose/insulin axis — fasting glucose, HbA1c, and fasting insulin, because GH secretagogues can reduce insulin sensitivity and raise glucose (class effect). (2) IGF-1 and the GH axis — IGF-1 as the integrated readout of GH stimulation; symptoms of GH excess (fluid retention/edema, joint pain/arthralgia, carpal-tunnel-type paresthesia, jaw/soft-tissue changes) warrant stopping and review. (3) Prolactin and a morning cortisol — because acute dosing can transiently raise PRL, ACTH and cortisol; persistent symptomatic hyperprolactinemia (galactorrhea, libido/menstrual changes) or signs of cortisol excess should be evaluated. (4) Thyroid panel (TSH/free T4) as part of pituitary-axis surveillance. (5) Cardiovascular signs — blood pressure and heart rate; given the rodent coronary-tone and action-potential-prolongation signals, any palpitations, chest pain or syncope should prompt ECG and cessation. (6) Lipid panel and body composition as objective markers if metabolic effects are a concern. Also relevant: because product is gray-market/unregulated, identity, purity and contamination cannot be assumed — and because it is WADA-banned, use is disqualifying in tested sport. None of this is medical advice; anyone using it should be under physician supervision."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE. The GH-releasing effect is genuinely proven in people, but it comes from small, mostly 1990s–2000s physiology studies — and those same studies show hexarelin is not selective: it raises cortisol, ACTH, and prolactin, which forums rarely mention. The exciting part viewers hear about — heart repair, anti-fibrosis, anti-atherosclerosis, infarct protection — is almost ENTIRELY rodent and in-vitro. There are zero modern registered clinical trials (ClinicalTrials.gov returned 0), and hexarelin has never been an approved drug; development was abandoned. Two specific cautions to voice: (1) Desensitization — GHS-R1a can downregulate, so the GH pulse may shrink with repeated daily dosing; short human studies did not see this (PMID 8921821) but chronic real-world use is untested. (2) The CD36/cortisol effects mean it is mechanistically NOT interchangeable with 'cleaner' secretagogues, and the same CD36 pathway is involved in lipid uptake and vascular biology, so off-target effects are plausible and unstudied long-term in humans. Do not present rodent cardioprotection as a human benefit. Certainty labels: GH release = Evidence-human; cortisol/ACTH/prolactin rise = Evidence-human; all cardiac protection/anti-fibrosis/anti-plaque = Evidence-rodent or in-vitro; chronic safety and desensitization in humans = Contested/unknown.
