Ecnoglutide

A Chinese-engineered, cAMP-signaling-biased GLP-1 analog that posts semaglutide-league weight loss in Chinese phase 3 trials and is cheap to make - a combination already seeding grey-market interest.

Limited human data 16 citations
Class
cAMP-biased (G-protein-biased) GLP-1 receptor agonist; long-acting acylated GLP-1 analog
Primary target
Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP-1R), a class B GPCR
Human status
Pipeline / late clinical, not yet approved anywhere (as of mid-2026). Developed by Hangzhou Sciwind Biosciences; ex-China oral rights licensed to Verdiva Bio (VRB-101). Completed phase 1 SAD/MAD (NCT04389775, n=64), phase 2 T2D (NCT under CTR20211014; PMID 39333121, n=145), phase 3 obesity (SLIMMER, NCT05813795, n=664), and phase 3 T2D (EECOH-1 monotherapy vs placebo, NCT05680155, n=211; EECOH-2 vs dulaglutide, NCT05680129, n=623). Active/ongoing programs: US oral-tablet phase 2/2b (VRB-101/Verdiva: EVOLVE-2 NCT07281937 and a weight-maintenance study NCT07553299), a phase 2 head-to-head/switch vs semaglutide in China (NCT07073417), two obstructive-sleep-apnea phase 3 trials (NCT07434050 on PAP, NCT07387094 not on PAP), and a completed adolescent phase 1b (NCT07143227, n=48). No regulatory approval, no cardiovascular-outcomes trial (CVOT), and no long-term (multi-year) safety data yet. All pivotal EFFICACY data to date come from Chinese adults.

At a glance

  • Substantial weight loss: at the top 2.4 mg weekly dose, placebo-adjusted body-weight reduction of ~13% at 40 weeks in Chinese adults with overweight/obesity without diabetes (SLIMMER phase 3; LS-mean change -13.2% vs +0.1% placebo, treatment difference -13.3%); ~87% of that group lost >=5% (PMID 40555243)
  • Strong glucose lowering in type 2 diabetes: HbA1c reductions of roughly 1.8-2.4% across doses in phase 2 (PMID 39333121) and phase 3 monotherapy (PMID 41501026), and non-inferior with a statistically-significant-but-not-clinically-meaningful edge over dulaglutide 1.5 mg in phase 3 (PMID 40854315)
  • Appetite suppression / increased satiety and glucose-dependent insulin secretion - the shared class effects of GLP-1R agonism
  • Gastrointestinal side effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, decreased appetite) remain the dominant, dose-related adverse events, as with all GLP-1 drugs - the cAMP-bias 'less nausea' claim is supported in rodents but NOT proven superior head-to-head in humans
  • Long half-life (~124-138 h at steady state) supports true once-weekly injectable dosing; an oral formulation is in development
  • Improvements in lipids and blood pressure consistent with weight loss (secondary endpoints)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Engineered GLP-1 backbone binds GLP-1R Established

    Ecnoglutide is a modified copy of the gut hormone GLP-1. Two changes make it drug-like: swapping alanine-8 for valine blocks the DPP-4 enzyme that normally destroys GLP-1 within minutes, and attaching a C18 fatty-acid diacid lets it hitch a ride on blood albumin so it lingers for days. It docks into the same GLP-1 receptor that natural GLP-1 and semaglutide use. PMID 37364710 (Guo 2023, Mol Metab)

  2. Biased activation: strong cAMP, weak receptor internalization Evidence-in vitro

    When most GLP-1 drugs hit the receptor they trigger two arms: a Gs/cAMP signal (the metabolically useful one) and a beta-arrestin arm that pulls the receptor inside the cell (internalization/desensitization) and blunts the signal over time. Ecnoglutide is 'biased': in cell assays it drives cAMP extremely potently (EC50 ~0.018 nM) but barely causes receptor internalization (EC50 >10 uM). It leans on the useful arm and largely ignores the shut-off arm. This is in-vitro pharmacology; whether it translates to a clinical advantage is unproven. PMID 37364710; biased-agonism framework PMID 40461942, PMID 41570980

  3. Sustained cAMP -> PKA -> beta-cell and satiety signaling Evidence-rodent

    cAMP is the second messenger that, inside insulin-producing beta cells, activates PKA and Epac to amplify glucose-triggered insulin release, and inside brain neurons contributes to reduced appetite. By avoiding rapid receptor internalization, a cAMP-biased agonist is PROPOSED to keep this signal running longer instead of the receptor being pulled off the cell surface and desensitized. The 'reduced internalization sustains signaling' idea is supported by rodent work with other biased incretin agonists, not by direct human data for ecnoglutide. PMID 38738303 (reduced incretin-receptor trafficking enhances glycemic control in DIO mice); PMID 42174184 (cAMP-dependent hindbrain mechanism of GLP-1 weight loss, mouse)

  4. Glucose-dependent insulin secretion + glucagon suppression Established

    Downstream, ecnoglutide produces the classic incretin effect: it boosts insulin only when glucose is high (so hypoglycemia risk is low on its own) and dampens inappropriate glucagon. In rodents it lowered blood glucose and raised insulin; in humans it produced large HbA1c reductions. PMID 37364710 (rodent mechanism); PMID 39333121, PMID 41501026 (HbA1c reductions in humans)

  5. Central appetite suppression -> reduced food intake -> weight loss Evidence-human

    GLP-1R agonists act on hindbrain and hypothalamic circuits (area postrema, NTS, arcuate nucleus) to increase satiety and slow gastric emptying, cutting calorie intake. Recent mouse work shows the weight-loss signal for semaglutide runs through cAMP (and also Gq) in GLP-1R-expressing area-postrema neurons - the general pathway ecnoglutide is biased toward. In humans, ecnoglutide produced up to ~13% placebo-adjusted weight loss. PMID 40555243 (phase 3 obesity, human); mechanistic context PMID 42174184 (mouse)

  6. cAMP-bias as a proposed nausea-sparing strategy Contested

    The commercial pitch is that skewing away from beta-arrestin/internalization reduces the GI side effects (nausea, vomiting) that limit GLP-1 tolerability. In three preclinical mammalian species a GLP-1R biased cAMP agonist kept glucose control while cutting malaise and emesis. But in ecnoglutide's own human trials GI events were still the most common, dose-related adverse events - so the 'less nausea' advantage is a rodent/mechanistic hypothesis, NOT a demonstrated human benefit. Preclinical (mouse/rat/musk shrew): PMID 41508705. Human GI-event data: PMID 40555243, PMID 39333121

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Agonist at the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R), a class B secretin-family GPCR. Structurally a GLP-1 analog with an alanine-8-to-valine substitution conferring DPP-4 resistance and a gamma-Glu-2xAEEA-linked C18 fatty diacid at a lysine, enabling reversible albumin binding for long half-life. Its defining feature is signaling bias: in vitro it is an extremely potent activator of the Gs/cAMP pathway (cAMP EC50 ~0.018 nM) but a very weak inducer of GLP-1R internalization (EC50 >10 uM), i.e. it favors the productive Gs arm over receptor internalization/desensitization. This bias is characterized in cell assays; its clinical consequences are unproven.

Signalling cascade. GLP-1R -> Gs -> adenylate cyclase -> cAMP -> PKA & Epac2. In beta cells this amplifies glucose-stimulated insulin secretion and supports beta-cell function; centrally (area postrema/NTS/hypothalamus) it drives satiety and reduced food intake, with recent mouse data localizing the weight-loss effect of semaglutide to cAMP (and Gq) signaling in GLP-1R-expressing area-postrema neurons. Reduced receptor internalization is proposed to limit desensitization and sustain signaling (rodent-supported hypothesis).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Subcutaneous once-weekly injection (lead formulation); once-weekly oral tablet (VRB-101) in phase 2 development.
Absorption / bioavailability
SC absorption consistent with an albumin-bound acylated peptide; oral bioavailability of peptides is inherently low and is the central challenge for the oral tablet program (formulation-dependent, not yet publicly quantified).
Half-life
Steady-state half-life ~124-138 h in the phase 1 SAD/MAD study (NCT04389775), supporting once-weekly dosing.
Metabolism
Proteolytic degradation to amino acids/small peptides, as expected for a peptide therapeutic; the A8V substitution confers resistance to DPP-4 cleavage. Not reported as a CYP substrate.
Excretion
Peptide catabolism rather than renal/hepatic small-molecule clearance; degradation products handled as peptide fragments.
Notes
Dedicated drug-drug interaction studies (all arms run under NCT06335134) found no clinically meaningful effect of steady-state ecnoglutide on the systemic exposure of warfarin or metformin (PMID 42137314) or rosuvastatin or digoxin (PMID 42310888); the 90% CIs for exposure ratios were within or near the 80-125% no-effect bounds, so no dose adjustment of those co-medications was indicated. Any minor exposure changes are consistent with GLP-1-mediated gastric slowing rather than metabolic-enzyme inhibition, though the studies were not designed to prove that mechanism.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Clear monotonic dose-response for the injectable. SLIMMER obesity phase 3 (40 wk, no diabetes): LS-mean bodyweight change -9.1% (1.2 mg), -10.9% (1.8 mg), -13.2% (2.4 mg) vs +0.1% placebo; placebo-adjusted treatment differences -9.2%, -11.1%, -13.3% (all p<0.0001); >=5% responders 77%/84%/87% (PMID 40555243). Phase 2 T2D (20 wk): HbA1c -1.81%/-1.90%/-2.39% for 0.4/0.8/1.2 mg vs -0.55% placebo (PMID 39333121). EECOH-1 phase 3 T2D monotherapy (24 wk): HbA1c -1.96% (0.6 mg) and -2.43% (1.2 mg) vs -0.87% placebo (PMID 41501026). EECOH-2 phase 3 T2D vs dulaglutide 1.5 mg (primary at wk 32; sustained to 52 wk): HbA1c -1.91% (0.6 mg) and -1.89% (1.2 mg) vs -1.65% dulaglutide - both non-inferior; ecnoglutide 1.2 mg superiority (delta -0.24%, p=0.0002) was statistically significant but characterized as not clinically meaningful (PMID 40854315). GI adverse events also rise with dose - the classic efficacy-vs-tolerability trade-off.

How it compares

  • vs Semaglutide
    same: GLP-1R agonism; once-weekly SC; GI-dominant side-effect profile; appetite suppression
    different: Both are acylated long-acting GLP-1 analogs, but ecnoglutide is explicitly engineered for cAMP-bias (minimal internalization in vitro) whereas semaglutide is a less-biased agonist. Weight loss in ecnoglutide's Chinese phase 3 (~13% placebo-adjusted at 40 wk) is broadly in the semaglutide range, but there is no completed head-to-head weight-loss RCT (a phase 2 switch study, NCT07073417, is ongoing). Ecnoglutide is positioned as far cheaper to manufacture.
  • vs Dulaglutide
    same: Once-weekly GLP-1RA for T2D; comparable AE-driven discontinuation rates (~3-4% over 52 wk)
    different: Head-to-head in T2D (EECOH-2, PMID 40854315): ecnoglutide non-inferior on HbA1c, with a statistically-significant-but-not-clinically-meaningful edge at 1.2 mg. Dulaglutide is an Fc-fusion; ecnoglutide is an albumin-binding acylated peptide.
  • vs Tirzepatide / other biased or dual agonists
    same: cAMP-favoring/biased agonism as a shared design theme aimed at efficacy with better tolerability
    different: Ecnoglutide is a pure GLP-1R mono-agonist; tirzepatide is a GLP-1R/GIPR dual agonist that also shows G-protein-biased signaling. Dual/triple agonists generally achieve greater weight loss; ecnoglutide's differentiation is bias + low cost, not multi-receptor coverage.
  • vs Orforglipron (oral small-molecule GLP-1RA)
    same: GLP-1R agonism; oral ambitions; obesity/T2D targets
    different: Orforglipron is a non-peptide oral small molecule; ecnoglutide is a peptide (injectable lead; oral tablet VRB-101 still in phase 2). Both invoke G-protein bias as a rationale.

History & legal status

Discovered by Hangzhou Sciwind Biosciences (China); first described in the literature in 2023 (Guo et al., Molecular Metabolism, PMID 37364710). Injectable development code XW003; ex-China oral rights out-licensed to Verdiva Bio as VRB-101. Clinical program run predominantly in China, with a phase 3 read-out cluster in 2024-2025 (SLIMMER obesity; EECOH-1/EECOH-2 T2D). As of mid-2026 it is NOT approved by FDA, EMA, or (per available records) marketed in China; regulatory filing is a plausible next step but is not established here. Program expansion includes obstructive sleep apnea (two phase 3 trials), a completed adolescent phase 1b, and the oral formulation (US phase 2/2b via Verdiva). Its low cost of goods and simplified manufacturing - highlighted in the discovery paper - are exactly what make it attractive to grey-market suppliers seeking a cheap semaglutide-alternative peptide.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether cAMP-bias actually delivers better GI tolerability than semaglutide/tirzepatide IN HUMANS - no completed head-to-head tolerability RCT; its own trials still show dose-related GI events
  • Cardiovascular outcomes - no CVOT has been run or reported
  • Long-term (multi-year) safety, durability of weight loss, and weight regain after discontinuation
  • Generalizability beyond Chinese populations (all pivotal efficacy data are Chinese-only; US oral trials are ongoing but not yet reported)
  • Oral bioavailability and real-world efficacy of the oral tablet (VRB-101) - still in phase 2
  • Body-composition specifics (lean vs fat mass) for ecnoglutide specifically
  • Ecnoglutide-specific rates of gallbladder disease, pancreatitis, and any thyroid signal at scale
  • Regulatory status/approval timeline, and whether low manufacturing cost translates to legitimate access versus illicit supply

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For legitimate clinical/supervised use, monitoring mirrors the GLP-1RA class (this is descriptive, not a protocol and not dosing guidance): watch for and manage GI intolerance (nausea/vomiting/diarrhea, with attention to dehydration); screen for symptoms of acute pancreatitis (severe persistent abdominal pain) and gallbladder disease (right-upper-quadrant pain, jaundice); track heart rate; watch for hypoglycemia when combined with insulin or sulfonylureas (low risk as monotherapy given glucose-dependence); assess nutritional adequacy and lean-mass preservation given appetite suppression; note the class contraindication in personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2; and caution in pregnancy. FOR GREY-MARKET MATERIAL the overriding harm-reduction points are: you cannot verify identity, purity, sterility, or actual peptide content of unregulated 'ecnoglutide'; there is no established dosing standard and none should be inferred; non-sterile injectables carry infection/abscess risk; and there is no medical oversight to catch the pancreatitis, gallbladder, dehydration, or cardiovascular events above. The responsible action is to obtain any GLP-1 therapy through a licensed prescriber, not to self-source a pipeline drug.

Caveats

Pipeline compound, not approved by FDA, EMA, or (as of this writing) in China - do not treat trial numbers as a green light. All pivotal EFFICACY data come from Chinese adults; response and safety may differ in other populations, and there is no cardiovascular-outcomes trial and no multi-year safety follow-up. The headline selling point - that cAMP-bias means 'GLP-1 benefits with less nausea' - is grounded in cell and rodent data (Evidence-in vitro / Evidence-rodent) but has NOT been demonstrated superior to standard GLP-1 drugs in a completed head-to-head human tolerability RCT; in its own trials, GI events were still the most common, dose-related adverse events (Contested). Note also that the 'sustained signaling from reduced internalization' mechanism is a hypothesis supported by rodent studies of OTHER biased incretin agonists, not by direct human ecnoglutide data. The low cost of goods / simplified manufacturing that makes it attractive for grey-market copying also means unregulated versions carry the usual research-chemical risks: no identity/purity guarantee, wrong or variable dosing, non-sterile injectables, and no medical monitoring. This is an evidence summary of a drug in development, NOT a recommendation, endorsement, or a how-to. Nothing here is dosing guidance.
