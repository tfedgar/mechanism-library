Pemvidutide

Main target: Balanced co-agonism of the GLP-1 receptor. Backed by human data.

Works in humans?Human evidence
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A balanced GLP-1/glucagon dual agonist that strips liver fat hard and is positioned to spare more lean mass than pure appetite-suppressing incretins — but it is still an investigational Phase 2 drug, not an approved product, and several of its headline selling points (lipid magnitude, muscle-sparing, fibrosis reversal) are not yet proven in peer-reviewed human trials.

Strong human data 10 citations
Class
GLP-1 receptor / glucagon receptor (GCGR) dual agonist peptide (also known as ALT-801); once-weekly subcutaneous, administered without dose titration in trials
Primary target
Balanced co-agonism of the GLP-1 receptor (appetite/satiety, gastric emptying, glycemia) and the glucagon receptor (hepatic fat oxidation, lipogenesis suppression, energy expenditure), tuned toward a ratio intended to emphasize hepatic-fat and lipid reduction rather than maximal weight loss
Human status
Investigational, Phase 2 (not approved anywhere). Developed by Altimmune (Gaithersburg, MD). Human data span Phase 1 (single/multiple ascending doses; a drug-drug interaction study, NCT04972396), a Phase 1b/2a MASLD trial (12 weeks, NCT05006885; PMID 39002641) and its 12-week double-blind extension to 24 weeks (NCT05292911; PMID 41113119), and the Phase 2b IMPACT biopsy trial in F2-F3 MASH (NCT05989711; 24-week results PMID 41237796, Lancet 2025 — met the MASH-resolution primary endpoint, did NOT meet the fibrosis-improvement co-primary at 24 weeks; the trial is ongoing to 48 weeks). An obesity program (MOMENTUM, ~48-week Phase 2) has been reported by the sponsor/at congresses but, per the sources reviewed here, is not yet a peer-reviewed PubMed-indexed primary paper. Active Phase 2 programs are also under way in alcohol use disorder (RECLAIM, NCT06987513; active, not recruiting) and alcohol-associated liver disease (RESTORE, NCT07009860; recruiting). No Phase 3, no cardiovascular-outcome, and no long-term safety data exist yet. It is not available by prescription; any non-trial 'pemvidutide' is unregulated gray-market material of unknown identity/purity.

At a glance

  • Large liver-fat reduction (~75% relative at 1.8 mg over 24 weeks; 53.8% normalize to <=5% liver fat) — steeper than the accompanying weight loss predicts
  • Moderate body-weight loss (~6.2% over 24 weeks in the MASLD extension; larger over 48 weeks in the obesity program) driven by GLP-1 appetite suppression
  • MASH resolution without worsening fibrosis in 52-58% at 24 weeks (Phase 2b IMPACT), but the fibrosis-improvement co-primary was NOT met at that timepoint
  • Reductions in LDL-cholesterol and triglycerides reported for the dual-agonist class and (sponsor/congress-level) for pemvidutide — promising but not yet a peer-reviewed primary lipid outcome for this drug
  • Reported favorable fat-vs-lean loss ratio (the 'preserved lean mass' physique angle) — promising but not proven head-to-head
  • Class-typical GI side effects (nausea, vomiting), plus a glucagon/GLP-1-associated rise in heart rate; generally well tolerated with low discontinuation in trials (IMPACT: <=1-2%)
  • Once-weekly subcutaneous dosing without titration in trials

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps5 human-evidenced 2 speculative

  1. Dual receptor engagement Established

    Pemvidutide is a single peptide engineered to activate two gut/pancreatic-hormone receptors at once: the GLP-1 receptor and the glucagon (GCGR) receptor. It is described by its developers as 'balanced' — retaining meaningful glucagon-arm activity compared with weight-loss-first drugs — so that liver-fat effects are emphasized alongside appetite suppression. PMID 39002641 (Harrison 2025, J Hepatol); PMID 35461369 (Nestor 2022, Sci Rep, preclinical ALT-801 characterization)

  2. GLP-1 arm cuts food intake Established

    Through the GLP-1 receptor, pemvidutide acts on brain appetite centers and the gut to increase satiety, slow gastric emptying, and lower food intake. This is the same appetite-suppressing mechanism class shared by semaglutide and tirzepatide and is the main driver of body-weight loss. PMID 39002641 (Harrison 2025); PMID 40776973 (Stachteas 2025, Rev Cardiovasc Med, class review)

  3. Glucagon arm drives the liver to burn its own fat Evidence-human

    Through the glucagon receptor, pemvidutide is understood to act directly on liver cells to switch on fatty-acid oxidation (burning stored fat) and switch off new fat synthesis (de novo lipogenesis). This is the proposed reason liver fat drops far more than weight loss alone would predict — the liver is being told to consume its fat, not just receive less. The direct hepatic action is mechanistic/class-established glucagon biology; in humans it is inferred from the disproportionate liver-fat reduction rather than from direct human hepatic-flux measurement. PMID 39002641 (Harrison 2025); PMID 41113119 (Browne 2025, JHEP Rep)

  4. Large, fast reduction in liver fat Evidence-human

    In people with fatty liver, 24 weeks of pemvidutide reduced liver-fat content by ~75% at the 1.8 mg dose versus ~14% on placebo, with more than half of participants (53.8% at 1.8 mg) reaching a normal (<=5%) liver-fat level. The dual mechanism produced steeper hepatic-fat reduction than the accompanying weight loss (~6%) alone would predict. PMID 41113119 (Browne 2025, JHEP Rep); PMID 39002641 (Harrison 2025)

  5. Proposed glucagon-driven boost in energy expenditure and lipid clearance Contested

    Glucagon-receptor activation is expected, on class/mechanistic grounds, to raise energy expenditure and lower circulating cholesterol and triglycerides. Pemvidutide's own lipid results in humans (LDL-cholesterol and triglyceride reductions) have been reported mainly at the sponsor/congress level and as a secondary endpoint in ongoing trials, not yet as a peer-reviewed primary lipid outcome — the peer-reviewed MASLD papers measured liver fat, weight, ALT and cT1, not lipids. Treat the lipid magnitude as promising and class-plausible, not established for pemvidutide specifically. PMID 40776973 (Stachteas 2025, dual-agonist class review); PMID 39002641 (Harrison 2025)

  6. Resolves steatohepatitis (MASH) at the tissue level Evidence-human

    In the Phase 2b IMPACT biopsy trial, pemvidutide resolved MASH (the inflammatory, ballooning form of fatty-liver disease) without worsening scarring in 58% (1.2 mg) and 52% (1.8 mg) of patients versus 20% on placebo at 24 weeks (p<0.0001) — meeting that primary endpoint. It did NOT meet the co-primary endpoint of fibrosis (scarring) improvement at 24 weeks (33%/36% vs 28%, non-significant), which is a key limitation. PMID 41237796 (Noureddin 2025, Lancet)

  7. Relative lean-mass sparing — the physique hook (still uncertain) Contested

    Pemvidutide is discussed at regulatory workshops as a candidate 'muscle-protective' incretin, and the sponsor reports a favorable fat-vs-lean loss ratio. This is the appealing physique angle, but head-to-head, DXA/biopsy-controlled proof that it spares lean mass better than semaglutide or tirzepatide does NOT yet exist in peer-reviewed form — treat 'preserves lean mass' as a promising hypothesis, not an established fact. PMID 41362110 (von Haehling 2025, J Cachexia Sarcopenia Muscle — names pemvidutide among emerging muscle-preserving candidates)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Synthetic peptide (ALT-801) acting as a dual agonist at the GLP-1 receptor (class B GPCR) and the glucagon receptor (GCGR, class B GPCR). Described by its developers as a 'balanced' co-agonist — the GLP-1:glucagon activity ratio is intended to retain meaningful glucagon-receptor activation (for hepatic fat/lipid effects and energy expenditure) rather than being GLP-1-dominant like most incretin weight-loss drugs. Incorporates a lipidation/half-life-extension modification supporting once-weekly, no-titration dosing.

Signalling cascade. GLP-1R activation -> Gs -> adenylate cyclase -> cAMP/PKA in CNS appetite centers, gut, and pancreatic beta cells -> satiety, slowed gastric emptying, glucose-dependent insulin secretion. GCGR activation -> Gs -> cAMP in hepatocytes -> upregulated fatty-acid beta-oxidation and suppressed de novo lipogenesis, plus proposed increased whole-body energy expenditure and lipid clearance. The net hepatic-fat reduction exceeds what the observed weight loss alone would predict, consistent with a direct glucagon action on the liver; the direct hepatic and energy-expenditure steps are class/mechanistic (established in glucagon biology and preclinical models) rather than directly measured in the pemvidutide human trials.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection, once weekly. In trials given at fixed doses without the stepwise dose titration typically required for GLP-1 drugs. (Trial-descriptive only; not a dosing recommendation.)
Absorption / bioavailability
Subcutaneous depot absorption typical of lipidated therapeutic peptides; designed for sustained weekly exposure.
Half-life
Long-acting, consistent with once-weekly dosing (multi-day half-life); exact human t1/2 is not emphasized in the peer-reviewed primary trial abstracts reviewed here and should be confirmed from full trial reports.
Metabolism
As a peptide, cleared primarily by proteolytic/peptidase catabolism to amino acids rather than by hepatic CYP metabolism; general principles per therapeutic-peptide PK reviews.
Excretion
Peptide catabolism to amino acids; no dominant renal/hepatic small-molecule excretion pathway expected.
Notes
A dedicated Phase 1 drug-drug-interaction study (NCT04972396) assessed the effect of pemvidutide on the pharmacokinetics of metformin, warfarin, atorvastatin, digoxin, and a combined oral contraceptive (ethinylestradiol/levonorgestrel) in healthy volunteers — relevant because GLP-1-class delayed gastric emptying can alter co-administered oral drug absorption. Quantitative human PK parameters should be confirmed from the full trial reports rather than abstracts.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

In the Phase 1b/2a MASLD trial (12 weeks, NCT05006885), relative liver-fat reductions were 46.6%, 68.5%, and 57.1% at 1.2, 1.8, and 2.4 mg respectively vs 4.4% placebo — a non-monotonic pattern where 1.8 mg gave the maximal liver-fat, weight, ALT, and cT1 responses, and 2.4 mg did not add benefit (PMID 39002641). Over 24 weeks (extension, NCT05292911) the liver-fat reductions were 56.3%, 75.2%, and 76.4% vs 14.0% placebo, with ~6.2% body-weight loss (PMID 41113119). In Phase 2b MASH (IMPACT), both 1.2 mg and 1.8 mg met MASH resolution (58% and 52% vs 20% placebo), with the 1.8 mg not clearly superior to 1.2 mg on resolution (PMID 41237796). Bottom line: efficacy appears to plateau near the 1.8 mg range for liver endpoints — this is descriptive of the trials, NOT a dosing recommendation.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Semaglutide (GLP-1 mono-agonist)
    same: Both cause GLP-1-driven appetite suppression, weight loss, and GI side effects; both reduce liver fat
    different: Pemvidutide adds glucagon-receptor agonism, proposed to give larger liver-fat and lipid reductions per unit weight loss and a claimed more favorable fat-vs-lean loss ratio; however semaglutide has far larger long-term outcome and safety datasets and is approved, whereas pemvidutide is Phase 2 and its comparative advantages are not yet proven head-to-head
  • vs Tirzepatide (GLP-1/GIP dual agonist)
    same: Both are dual-agonist injectables producing strong metabolic and hepatic-fat benefits
    different: Tirzepatide's second target is GIP (not glucagon) and it produces larger raw weight loss; pemvidutide's glucagon arm is positioned as more directly hepatotropic/lipid-lowering and around liver-fat and lean-preservation rather than maximal weight loss; tirzepatide is approved with large trials, pemvidutide is Phase 2
  • vs Survodutide / mazdutide (other GLP-1/glucagon dual agonists)
    same: Same dual-agonist class and same glucagon-driven hepatic-fat rationale; all show MASLD/MASH promise
    different: Direct peers competing in the same GLP-1/glucagon space, differing in GLP-1:glucagon ratio, dosing, and which endpoints they have hit; pemvidutide is distinguished by its balanced-ratio positioning and lean-mass narrative, but cross-drug superiority is not established
  • vs Retatrutide (GLP-1/GIP/glucagon triple agonist)
    same: Shares the glucagon arm and strong hepatic-fat/weight effects
    different: Retatrutide adds GIP for a triple mechanism and larger weight loss; pemvidutide is a simpler two-receptor agent — the trade-off is efficacy magnitude vs a more targeted lipid/liver profile
  • vs Resmetirom (approved THR-beta agonist for MASH)
    same: Both target MASH/liver fat
    different: Resmetirom is a liver-selective thyroid-hormone-receptor-beta agonist (approved for MASH with fibrosis) and is not a weight-loss drug; pemvidutide additionally drives weight loss via a completely different (incretin/glucagon) mechanism, but is not approved

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Originated as ALT-801 at Altimmune (Gaithersburg, MD). Preclinical proof-of-concept in a diet-induced NASH mouse model (2022, PMID 35461369) reported it outperformed semaglutide and elafibranor on liver endpoints. Clinical development advanced through Phase 1 (including a DDI study, NCT04972396), a Phase 1b/2a MASLD trial and its extension (NCT05006885 / NCT05292911; PMID 39002641, PMID 41113119), and the Phase 2b IMPACT MASH biopsy trial (NCT05989711), whose 24-week results were published in The Lancet in 2025 (PMID 41237796): it MET the MASH-resolution primary endpoint but did NOT meet the fibrosis-improvement co-primary at 24 weeks, with 48-week data pending. An obesity program (MOMENTUM) and Phase 2 programs in alcohol use disorder (RECLAIM, NCT06987513) and alcohol-associated liver disease (RESTORE, NCT07009860) are ongoing. As of the sources reviewed here, pemvidutide is not approved by any regulator and has no Phase 3 or outcome data.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Does it actually improve/reverse liver fibrosis (scarring)? The 24-week Phase 2b fibrosis co-primary was not met — the 48-week and future data are decisive and currently unknown.
  • Is the 'preserves lean mass' claim real head-to-head? No peer-reviewed DXA-controlled comparison vs semaglutide/tirzepatide exists; the physique selling point is unproven.
  • Are pemvidutide's human lipid (LDL-C/triglyceride) effects as large as claimed? A peer-reviewed primary lipid outcome for pemvidutide has not been located here — current support is class-level and sponsor/congress-level.
  • Long-term cardiovascular safety of the glucagon arm — the heart-rate increase (shown for GLP-1+glucagon co-agonism generally) and effects on cardiac work over years are unknown; no cardiovascular-outcome trial.
  • Durability of weight and liver-fat benefit, and degree of regain after stopping.
  • Long-term glycemic safety — whether balanced glucagon agonism stays glucose-neutral over time, especially in type 2 diabetes.
  • Head-to-head efficacy vs survodutide, mazdutide, tirzepatide, and retatrutide — relative positioning is not established.
  • Full human pharmacokinetics, immunogenicity (anti-drug antibodies), and rare/serious adverse-event profile at Phase 3 scale.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction context only, not a protocol and not an endorsement of non-trial use — pemvidutide is unapproved and should only be taken inside a clinical trial. Parameters clinicians track in these trials, and that any responsible monitoring would emphasize: (1) Cardiovascular — resting heart rate and blood pressure, given the glucagon/GLP-1 heart-rate signal. (2) Glycemia — fasting glucose and HbA1c, because glucagon agonism can oppose glucose lowering, especially in diabetes. (3) Liver — ALT/AST and, where available, imaging (MRI-PDFF/cT1) and fibrosis markers (ELF, FibroScan/FAST) to track the intended hepatic effect. (4) Lipids — LDL-cholesterol, HDL, triglycerides. (5) Body composition — ideally DXA (not just scale weight) to watch lean-vs-fat loss, plus attention to dietary protein and resistance training to protect muscle. (6) GI tolerability and hydration/nutrition, since nausea/vomiting can drive under-eating and micronutrient gaps. (7) Because GLP-1-class gastric slowing can alter absorption of oral medications, co-administered drugs (e.g., oral contraceptives, narrow-therapeutic-index agents) warrant attention. Red flags to seek medical care for: persistent severe abdominal pain (pancreatitis concern with the incretin class), signs of gallbladder disease, sustained tachycardia, or intractable vomiting/dehydration. This lists what to watch — it deliberately gives no doses, no titration schedule, and no administration instructions. Any product obtained outside a trial is of unverified identity and purity and carries additional, unquantifiable risk.

Caveats

This is a pipeline drug — everything here is short-to-medium-term Phase 1/2 data, largely sponsor-run (Altimmune), with no approval, no Phase 3, and no long-term or cardiovascular-outcome evidence. Four honesty flags dominate: (1) The headline liver-fat and MASH-resolution numbers are real, verified against the primary papers, and impressive — but the Phase 2b IMPACT trial did NOT meet the fibrosis-improvement co-primary endpoint at 24 weeks (33%/36% vs 28% placebo, non-significant), and fibrosis is the endpoint that actually predicts hard liver outcomes; the 48-week data will help decide whether pemvidutide is truly disease-modifying. (2) The physique-relevant 'preserves lean mass' story is among the least-proven parts: pemvidutide is discussed at regulatory workshops as a candidate muscle-protective agent and the sponsor reports favorable body-composition ratios, but there is no peer-reviewed, DXA/head-to-head trial proving it spares lean mass better than semaglutide or tirzepatide — do not treat 'muscle-sparing' as fact. (3) The lipid (LDL-C/triglyceride) benefit is biologically plausible and reported for the dual-agonist class, but pemvidutide-specific human lipid magnitudes here rest on class reviews and sponsor/congress-level reporting, not on a peer-reviewed primary lipid outcome. (4) Glucagon agonism is a double-edged sword: it is thought to drive the liver-fat benefits but can raise resting heart rate (shown mechanistically for GLP-1+glucagon co-agonism in healthy-subject infusion studies), transiently nudge glucose/blood pressure, and its long-term cardiac and glycemic safety in this balanced ratio is not established. There are no dosing protocols here and no endorsement of non-trial use. This is a mechanism/evidence explainer, not medical advice — no one should self-source or self-administer an unapproved peptide.
