Ashwagandha

The "stress-dial" herb: it turns down your cortisol stress signal — the one adaptogen with real human trials behind the calm.

Human / approved 33 citations
Class
Adaptogenic herbal extract (botanical); active compounds are withanolide steroidal lactones (withaferin A, withanolide A) and sitoindosides
Primary target
The HPA axis (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal stress system) — net effect is lower circulating cortisol
Human status
REAL human RCT evidence — one of the better-evidenced supplements. The stress/cortisol/anxiety/sleep effects rest on numerous double-blind, placebo-controlled RCTs and several 2024-2026 systematic reviews and meta-analyses (e.g. PMID 39348746, 39083548, 40746175, 41740946). The cortisol drop is the standout finding. Testosterone and strength benefits are also human-tested (RCTs PMID 30854916, 26609282, 30463324; meta-analysis PMID 42356302) but are MODEST and population-dependent — this is not a steroid-equivalent T-booster. BE HONEST about two things: (1) molecular mechanisms (GABA-A agonism, Nrf2/NF-kB) are largely inferred from RODENT/in-vitro work, not directly demonstrated in human clinical data — the corpus MoA entry flags exactly this. (2) Many RCTs are small (n often 50-150), short (6-12 weeks), use proprietary branded extracts, and are industry-funded, which inflates effect sizes; some meta-analyses note high heterogeneity and that perceived stress improves less reliably than the cortisol biomarker (PMID 40746175). 'Longevity/anti-aging' framing is SPECULATIVE — extrapolated from rodent antioxidant/anti-inflammatory data, not from human longevity trials.

At a glance

  • Lowers cortisol: most consistent, best-replicated effect; ~20-30% reductions in morning serum cortisol in stressed adults (Evidence-human, meta-analyzed).
  • Reduces perceived stress and anxiety: significant improvements on PSS and HAM-A scales vs placebo in multiple RCTs and meta-analyses (Evidence-human).
  • Improves sleep quality and sleep onset, including in insomnia patients (Evidence-human).
  • Modest testosterone increase in men (especially stressed/aging/sub-fertile), with improved sperm parameters — real but smaller and less consistent than the cortisol effect (Evidence-human).
  • Small gains in muscle strength, size, recovery and VO2max with resistance training (Evidence-human, modest).
  • GABA-mimetic action at GABA-A receptors — the proposed calming/sedative mechanism (Evidence-rodent).
  • Antioxidant (Nrf2) and anti-inflammatory (NF-kB inhibition) cellular effects (Evidence-rodent / in-vitro).

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Withanolides get in and act on the brain's stress circuitry Established

    The active ingredients are a family of plant steroids called withanolides (the headline ones are withaferin A and withanolide A) plus related sitoindosides. Standardized root extracts are dosed by their withanolide content (e.g. ~21 mg/day glycosides up to ~300-600 mg extract). These molecules cross into the brain and act on the systems that govern the stress response, rather than on a single classic drug receptor. PMID 41356880; PMID 34254920

  2. It turns down the HPA axis, so less cortisol is released Evidence-human

    When you're stressed, the hypothalamus-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) chain fires and your adrenal glands pump out cortisol. Ashwagandha dampens this cascade. Across human trials, morning serum cortisol drops meaningfully versus placebo (pooled reductions on the order of 20-30% in stressed adults). This cortisol drop is the single most consistent, best-replicated biological effect. PMID 40746175; PMID 38140274; PMID 41740946

  3. It mimics GABA — the brain's main 'brake' — which calms anxiety Evidence-rodent

    Separate from cortisol, withanolides behave like GABA at GABA-A receptors, the same calming brake that alcohol and anti-anxiety meds nudge. This GABA-mimetic action is the proposed reason ashwagandha eases anxiety and improves sleep onset. Important caveat: the receptor-level evidence is mostly from rodent and cell studies; the downstream anxiety/sleep benefit is shown in humans. PMID 34254920; PMID 41356880

  4. Lower stress signaling translates into measurable calm and better sleep Evidence-human

    With cortisol down and GABA tone up, randomized placebo-controlled trials show real reductions in perceived stress and anxiety scores (e.g. PSS, HAM-A) and improvements in sleep quality and time to fall asleep. Meta-analyses of these RCTs confirm the anxiety/stress benefit, though trials are mostly small, short (6-12 weeks), and many are industry-funded. PMID 39348746; PMID 39083548; PMID 32818573

  5. In men, it nudges the testosterone axis upward — modestly Evidence-human

    Ashwagandha also touches the HPG (reproductive hormone) axis. In men — especially stressed, overweight, aging, or sub-fertile men — it produces a modest rise in testosterone and DHEA and improvements in sperm parameters. The effect is real in RCTs but smaller and less consistent than the cortisol effect, and it is NOT a steroid-like testosterone booster. Some of the rise may simply follow from lowering cortisol, which competes with testosterone. PMID 30854916; PMID 41249015; PMID 41740946

  6. It calms inflammation and oxidative stress (antioxidant / Nrf2 / NF-kB) Evidence-rodent

    Withanolides activate the Nrf2 antioxidant defense pathway and inhibit NF-kB, a master switch for inflammation. This is the proposed basis for 'cell-protective' and recovery claims. Be careful here: this arm is largely from rodent and test-tube work, so treat anti-inflammatory and longevity-type claims as biologically plausible but not proven in humans. PMID 41356880; PMID 38592845

  7. Knock-on effects: strength, recovery and endurance in training Evidence-human

    Because lower cortisol and higher testosterone favor an anabolic state, trials in resistance-trained adults report small gains in strength, muscle size, recovery and VO2max versus placebo. A 2026 three-level meta-analysis supports a modest exercise-performance benefit. These are secondary, downstream effects — useful, but smaller and more variable than the core stress effect. PMID 42356302; PMID 26609282; PMID 30463324

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Ashwagandha is a whole-plant EXTRACT, not a single molecule, so 'binding affinity' must be read per-constituent and most numbers are extract-level, not classic Ki/Kd. KEY DIRECT BINDING DATA (Evidence-in vitro): The single best mechanistic study (Candelario 2015, J Ethnopharmacol, PMID 26068424) used two-electrode voltage-clamp on native rat-brain GABA-A channels and human GABA-rho1 receptors expressed in Xenopus oocytes. Aqueous root extract acted as a GABA-A AGONIST with EC50 equivalent to ~4.7 mg/mL extract (Hill coefficient 1.6) and a PARTIAL-agonist efficacy (smaller currents than GABA itself); the current was BLOCKED by the GABA-A antagonist bicuculline, proving it acts at the GABA-A orthosteric/channel complex. It was a far more potent and near-full AGONIST at extrasynaptic GABA-rho1 (GABA-C) receptors — rho1 was ~27-fold MORE sensitive than GABA-A, and rho1 maximal currents were statistically indistinguishable from GABA's. Crucially, PURIFIED withaferin A and withanolide A did NOT activate either receptor — so the GABAergic action is carried by other (still unidentified) constituents, NOT the marquee withanolides. Withania root also contains essentially no free GABA, so the effect is true receptor agonism, not GABA contamination. OTHER REPORTED TARGETS (lower-certainty, mostly in vitro/rodent): withanolide A and withanolides act as Hsp90 client-protein disruptors and NF-kB pathway inhibitors (withaferin A binds IKK-beta and inhibits NF-kB — Evidence-in vitro); withanolides activate Nrf2/ARE antioxidant signalling (Evidence-rodent/in vitro); weak acetylcholinesterase inhibition and promotion of neurite/dendrite outgrowth attributed to withanolide A (Kuboyama/Tohda lineage, PMID 15956813, Evidence-in vitro/rodent). Physicochemistry of the lead cytotoxic withanolide withaferin A (ChEMBL CHEMBL517080): C28H38O6, MW 470.6, cLogP 3.35, 0 Lipinski violations, 2 H-bond donors / 6 acceptors, PSA 96 — a drug-like, membrane-permeant steroidal lactone. ChEMBL lists NO curated approved-drug mechanism-of-action entry for withaferin A (it is a natural-product probe, max clinical phase 1), so there is no validated single human target.

Signalling cascade. Net signalling story has two arms. ANTI-STRESS / HPA arm (the headline 'lowers cortisol' effect): Increased inhibitory GABAergic tone (via GABA-A and especially extrasynaptic GABA-rho1 agonism, Candelario 2015) dampens excitatory drive onto the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus, reducing corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) output; less CRH -> less pituitary ACTH -> less adrenal cortisol synthesis. This is plausible mechanistically and matches the human readout (morning serum/salivary cortisol falls), but the GABA->PVN->CRH chain is INFERRED in humans, not directly measured — labelled Evidence-rodent/in vitro for the molecular steps, Evidence-human only for the downstream cortisol drop. Parallel serotonergic modulation is suggested (meta-analysis found serotonin rose, PMID 41740946). ANTI-INFLAMMATORY / CYTOPROTECTIVE arm: withaferin A covalently/non-covalently inhibits IKK-beta, blocking IkB phosphorylation so NF-kB stays sequestered in the cytoplasm -> down-regulation of TNF-alpha, IL-6, iNOS, COX-2 (Evidence-in vitro); concurrently withanolides activate Nrf2, which translocates to the nucleus and drives antioxidant-response-element genes (HO-1, NQO1, glutathione synthesis) (Evidence-rodent). Withaferin A also binds and destabilises Hsp90, degrading client oncoproteins (basis of its anticancer interest, not its supplement use). The neuro-regenerative claims (withanolide A promoting axon/dendrite regrowth, mild AChE inhibition) sit on rodent/in-vitro data and are not established in humans.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsules, tablets, powder of root or root+leaf extract). The active matrix is a mixture of withanolides (withaferin A, withanolide A, 12-deoxy-withastramonolide) and glycosylated withanosides (withanoside IV/V); products are standardised to total withanolide % (commonly 1.5-10%). No injectable/transdermal human product.
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapid absorption. In a validated human PK study (Ramapalaniappan 2025, PMID 40748423, n=20 healthy adults, single dose, total withanolides by LC-MS/MS), plasma withanolides appeared within 15 min, confirming fast oral uptake; formulation strongly changes exposure (a 1.5%/125 mg formulation gave 2.1-fold higher AUC than a 5%/600 mg product and 1.3-fold higher than a 10%/500 mg product), showing bioavailability is extract- and matrix-dependent, not just dose-dependent. Rodent PK (Modi 2022, PMID 35268576, rats 500 mg/kg root extract): Cmax (ng/mL) withaferin A 124.4, 12-deoxy-withastramonolide 57.5, withanoside IV 13.8, withanolide A 7.3; Tmax very short (~0.25-0.75 h), i.e. minutes to <1 h. In silico ADMET predicted Lipinski-compliant oral absorption and BBB penetration; ex-vivo intestinal permeation was time-dependent. Mouse data (Patil 2013, PMID 23584079) showed withaferin A has ~1.5x the relative oral bioavailability of withanolide A. Absolute human bioavailability is NOT established — labelled Evidence-human (Cmax/Tmax) but absolute %F Unknown.
Half-life
Short. Human single-dose study tracked plasma to 24 h with most withanolide exposure in the first hours (Evidence-human, exact t1/2 per-analyte not cleanly resolved because of isobaric isomers). Rodent terminal t1/2 reported on the order of a few hours. Practical takeaway: parent withanolides are cleared within hours, so the durable clinical effects (cortisol lowering over weeks) reflect repeated dosing / downstream adaptation, not drug accumulation. CERTAINTY: human t1/2 is only approximately characterised — Contested/Unknown at the precise-number level.
Metabolism
Withanolides are steroidal lactones expected to undergo hepatic phase-I (CYP-mediated oxidation/hydroxylation) and phase-II (glucuronidation/sulfation) metabolism; glycosylated withanosides can be hydrolysed by gut/intestinal enzymes to their aglycone withanolides before/after absorption. Specific human CYP isoforms responsible have NOT been definitively mapped — Speculative/Contested at the enzyme level. There are signals of CYP modulation relevant to herb-drug interactions but no consensus enzyme list.
Excretion
Not well characterised in humans. Given rapid clearance and steroidal-lactone structure, biliary/fecal and renal elimination of conjugated metabolites are presumed. Quantitative human excretion balance: Unknown.
Notes
(1) The named 'cortisol' mechanism (GABAergic) is carried by constituents OTHER than withaferin A / withanolide A, yet products are standardised to those very withanolides — a real pharmacological mismatch. (2) Withanosides are effectively glycoside PRODRUGS of withanolides. (3) Predicted BBB penetration is consistent with the CNS/anxiolytic effects but is in-silico/rodent, not directly shown in humans. (4) Because exposure is heavily formulation-dependent, two products at the 'same mg' can differ ~2-fold in plasma withanolides — important for both efficacy and toxicity.

How it compares

  • vs Rhodiola rosea (rosavins/salidroside) — another classic adaptogen
    same: Both marketed as adaptogens that blunt the stress response and reduce fatigue/perceived stress; both act largely on the HPA/stress-axis with modest human RCT support.
    different: Rhodiola's proposed mechanism centres on monoamine modulation (serotonin/dopamine/norepinephrine) and is more 'activating'/stimulant-like, whereas ashwagandha is GABAergic and sedating/anxiolytic. Ashwagandha has more consistent cortisol-lowering and sleep/testosterone data; Rhodiola is favoured for fatigue/cognitive endurance. Rhodiola lacks ashwagandha's documented hepatotoxicity signal and thyroid-stimulating effect.
  • vs Benzodiazepines (e.g. diazepam) — pharmaceutical GABAergics
    same: Both increase GABA-A-mediated inhibitory tone, producing anxiolysis and sedation; ashwagandha extract's GABA-A current is bicuculline-blocked, confirming action at the GABA-A complex.
    different: Benzodiazepines are POSITIVE ALLOSTERIC MODULATORS (potentiate GABA, do not open the channel alone) with high potency, defined receptor subunits, tolerance, dependence and overdose risk; ashwagandha extract is a weak PARTIAL AGONIST (acts even without GABA, far lower potency, EC50 in mg/mL not nM) with no established dependence/withdrawal syndrome and a much milder effect. Ashwagandha also uniquely lowers cortisol and modulates thyroid/sex hormones — benzodiazepines do not.
  • vs L-theanine — amino-acid relaxant
    same: Both promote calm/anxiolysis without classic sedative-hypnotic dependence, both popular OTC stress supplements.
    different: L-theanine acts via glutamate-receptor antagonism, increased GABA/glycine and alpha-brainwave activity, with a fast/acute effect and a very clean safety profile (no hepatotoxicity, no hormonal effects). Ashwagandha works over WEEKS, measurably lowers cortisol and shifts thyroid/testosterone, and carries a real (if rare) liver-injury and thyroid-stimulation risk.
  • vs Ksm-66 vs Sensoril vs Shoden (the main commercial ashwagandha extracts) — same plant, different standardisation
    same: All are Withania somnifera extracts standardised to withanolides and used for stress/cortisol.
    different: They differ in plant part (root-only vs root+leaf), withaferin-A content (leaf-containing extracts have more cytotoxic withaferin A), and total-withanolide standardisation (e.g. Shoden ~35% withanolide glycosides dosed at low mg; KSM-66 ~5% root-only at higher mg). Bioavailability differs up to ~2-fold between products at nominally 'equal' doses (Ramapalaniappan 2025), so efficacy and toxicity are not interchangeable across brands.

History &amp; legal status

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera, 'Indian ginseng' / 'winter cherry') is a Rasayana (rejuvenative) herb used in Ayurvedic medicine for >2,500-3,000 years for vitality, sleep and stress. The species name 'somnifera' (sleep-inducing) reflects its traditional sedative use. Modern pharmacology began isolating withanolides (withaferin A, the first, in the 1960s). REGULATORY STATUS: In the USA it is sold as a DIETARY SUPPLEMENT under DSHEA — NOT FDA-approved as a drug, not premarket-evaluated for efficacy, and bound only by supplement (cGMP/labeling) rules; it is generally treated as having GRAS-like food/supplement status. In the EU it is a food supplement/botanical, but safety reviews are tightening. NOTABLE RESTRICTIONS: Following clusters of liver-injury reports, several national regulators have moved to restrict or warn — Denmark's food authority (DTU) advised against ashwagandha over concerns about liver, thyroid and reproductive/hormonal effects and effects in pregnancy; other Nordic/EU bodies have issued cautions. It is widely contraindicated in pregnancy (traditional abortifacient reputation and uterine effects). It is NOT a controlled/scheduled substance anywhere and is not banned by WADA (not a prohibited substance in sport). The supplement market is large and rapidly growing, which amplifies the public-health relevance of its idiosyncratic hepatotoxicity and thyroid signals.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Which constituent(s) actually carry the GABAergic / cortisol-lowering effect? Candelario 2015 showed it is NOT withaferin A or withanolide A — the active GABA-A/rho1 agonist molecule(s) in the root remain unidentified, so products standardised to withanolides may be standardising to the WRONG marker.
  • Is the GABA -> PVN/CRH -> ACTH -> cortisol chain real in humans? The cortisol drop is measured, but every intermediate molecular step is inferred from rodent/in-vitro data; no human study has shown the upstream CRH/ACTH changes directly.
  • What is the true human pharmacokinetic profile — absolute bioavailability, precise per-withanolide half-lives, the responsible CYP enzymes, and excretion route? Current human PK is single-dose and formulation-confounded.
  • What is the absolute incidence, dose threshold, and host risk factors (HLA genotype, pre-existing liver disease) for idiosyncratic hepatotoxicity? Cases are 'probable' by RUCAM but denominator and predictors are unknown.
  • Is there a genuine continuous dose-response, and what is the optimal/ceiling dose? Trials use only a few fixed doses with ~2-fold bioavailability differences between products, so the effect-vs-mg-withanolide curve is undefined.
  • Long-term (>3 month) safety, including sustained thyroid stimulation, hormonal shifts, autoimmune-disease interactions, and pregnancy outcomes — essentially unstudied in controlled human trials.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

HARM-REDUCTION MONITORING (no dosing advice). LIVER is the priority: because idiosyncratic hepatotoxicity is the most serious documented harm, watch for jaundice (yellow eyes/skin), dark urine, pale stools, unexplained itching (pruritus), right-upper-quadrant pain, nausea or unusual fatigue — and stop immediately and seek care if any appear. A baseline and periodic liver panel (ALT, AST, ALP, total bilirubin) is reasonable, especially with pre-existing liver disease, alcohol use, or other hepatotoxic supplements/drugs; a hepatocellular pattern (high ALT/AST relative to ALP, high 'R-value') is the reported signature. THYROID: because ashwagandha raises thyroid hormones, monitor TSH/free T4 (and T3) in anyone with thyroid disease or on levothyroxine — watch for hyperthyroid signs (palpitations, tremor, heat intolerance, weight loss, anxiety/insomnia paradoxically worsening). HORMONES: in men, testosterone and DHEA-S can rise; relevant if there is a hormone-sensitive condition. CNS/SEDATION: watch for excess drowsiness or additive sedation if combined with alcohol, benzodiazepines, sleep aids or other CNS depressants. SPECIAL POPULATIONS: avoid in pregnancy (uterine/abortifacient concerns) and use caution in autoimmune disease (theoretical immune stimulation) and with immunosuppressant or sedative medications. Track baseline cortisol/stress symptoms only as an efficacy readout, not a safety gate. Any new or worsening symptom on a herbal supplement warrants stopping and clinician review — these are signs to monitor, not thresholds to self-treat.
