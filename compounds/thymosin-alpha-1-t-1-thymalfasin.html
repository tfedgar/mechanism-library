Thymosin alpha-1

A natural 28-amino-acid thymus hormone that acts like a "tuning dial" for the immune system — it wakes up the alarm cells (dendritic cells) through innate sensors and pushes T-cell maturation, boosting antiviral/antifungal defense while calming runaway inflammation.

Human / approved 19 citations
Class
Endogenous thymic peptide hormone / immunomodulator (synthetic form = thymalfasin)
Primary target
Innate immune pattern-recognition receptors — chiefly Toll-like receptors (TLR2, TLR9) signaling through the MyD88 adaptor — on dendritic cells; this is the upstream switch that then shapes downstream T-cell (Th1) immunity
Human status
MIXED — partly Established (regulatory-approved in several countries), partly Evidence-human, partly preclinical. Approved as thymalfasin in ~30+ countries (notably China, parts of Asia/Europe) for chronic hepatitis B and as an immune adjuvant/vaccine enhancer in immunocompromised people; widely used in China for severe infection and cancer adjunct. Human RCT/meta-analytic signals exist for severe acute pancreatitis (meta-analysis PMID 40599771) and sepsis, and an RCT protocol is underway for post-aortic-dissection organ protection (PANDA II, PMID 40367062). It is NOT FDA-approved in the US. COVID-19 use was emergency/observational, not confirmed by rigorous Western RCTs. The detailed TLR9/MyD88/IRF7 antiviral mechanism is best demonstrated in mouse and in-vitro models; human mechanistic data are largely consistent but less complete.

At a glance

  • Boosts antiviral defense (type I interferon / IFN-γ) — basis for hepatitis B and CMV use
  • Enhances antifungal Th1 immunity (e.g. Aspergillus models)
  • Improves vaccine response in elderly / immunocompromised people (immunosenescence)
  • Increases thymic output of fresh naïve T cells; studied for age-related immune decline
  • Repolarizes macrophages (M2→M1) and lowers Tregs — explored as a cancer immunotherapy adjunct
  • Acts as an immune 'rheostat' — calms damaging inflammation in severe infection/pancreatitis (immunoregulatory, not purely stimulatory)
  • Anti-inflammatory/antioxidant signaling reported in aging and organ-protection contexts

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Engages innate sensors on dendritic cells via TLR/MyD88 Evidence-rodent

    Tα1 binds to/activates Toll-like receptors (notably TLR9 and TLR2) on dendritic cells — the immune system's 'alarm and antigen-presenting' cells. The signal runs through the MyD88 adaptor protein, the same wiring these cells normally use to detect microbes. PMID 14982877 (Blood 2004); PMID 17804687 (Int Immunol 2007)

  2. Drives dendritic-cell maturation and IL-12 output (p38 MAPK/NF-κB) Evidence-rodent

    Through that signal, Tα1 makes dendritic cells 'grow up' (mature) and pump out interleukin-12 (IL-12), using the p38 MAPK and NF-κB intracellular pathways. Mature DCs with IL-12 are the cells that instruct T cells. PMID 14982877 (Blood 2004)

  3. For viruses: activates the TLR9/MyD88/IRF7 interferon axis in plasmacytoid DCs Evidence-rodent

    In viral infection, Tα1 switches on plasmacytoid dendritic cells via TLR9→MyD88→IRF7, triggering type I interferon (IFN-α) and IFN-γ — the body's frontline antiviral signaling that limits viral replication. PMID 17804687 (Int Immunol 2007, murine CMV model)

  4. Pushes T-cell maturation and a Th1 (cell-killing) response Evidence-human

    Downstream of the activated dendritic cells, Tα1 promotes maturation and differentiation of T cells and biases them toward Th1 — the response that coordinates killing of virus-infected cells and fungi. As a thymic hormone it also increases thymic output of fresh naïve T cells. PMID 36871535 (Int Immunopharmacol 2023, review); PMID 41373628 (Int J Mol Sci 2025, review); PMID 39867848 (CVID naïve T-cell study)

  5. Modulates monocytes/macrophages — repolarizes 'pro-tumor' M2 toward M1 Evidence-rodent

    Tα1 reprograms tumor-associated macrophages away from the immune-suppressing 'M2' state toward an anti-tumor 'M1' state and reduces regulatory T cells in the tumor microenvironment, helping CD8+ T cells attack tumors (shown with oncolytic virus combos). PMID 39357524 (Cell Rep Med 2024)

  6. Acts as a two-way 'rheostat' — also restrains excess inflammation Evidence-human

    Rather than only ramping immunity up, Tα1 rebalances it: in over-inflamed states (severe infection, pancreatitis, post-surgery) it dampens harmful inflammation and promotes tolerance, which is why it's framed as an immune regulator, not a pure stimulant. PMID 17495242 (Ann N Y Acad Sci 2007); PMID 40599771 (severe acute pancreatitis meta-analysis 2025)

  7. Direct effects on individual immune-cell subsets (esp. CD8+ T cells) Evidence-in vitro

    Beyond dendritic cells, Tα1 directly changes the gene-expression/proliferation of purified human immune cells — with the strongest effect on activated CD8+ killer T cells — while barely changing tumor cells themselves, arguing its action is immune-cell-centric. PMID 40955371 (Onco Targets Ther 2025, human cell subsets)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Tα1 is a 28-amino-acid acetylated N-terminal peptide fragment of prothymosin alpha. CERTAINTY-CRITICAL POINT: unlike a classic drug, Tα1 has NO single validated high-affinity cell-surface receptor with a published Kd/Ki — there is no canonical 'Tα1 receptor' the way there is, say, an androgen receptor (Established that the receptor is undefined; King & Tuthill 2016 PMID 27450734). Instead it acts as a pleiotropic biological-response modifier whose best-characterized molecular entry points are pattern-recognition Toll-like receptors on dendritic cells: TLR9 (and TLR2) in myeloid/plasmacytoid DCs is the documented signaling route, with TLR7 implicated in the macrophage/tumor setting (Evidence-rodent/in vitro; Romani 2004 Blood PMID 14982877; Wei 2022 Cancer Res PMID 35364609). No agonist/antagonist Ki values exist because binding is not a simple single-site orthosteric interaction — affinity numbers should NOT be invented. Tα1 also partitions into membranes and is reported to bind phosphatidylserine on apoptotic cells (Evidence-rodent; PMID 35364609).

Signalling cascade. Documented cascade (mostly Evidence-rodent/in vitro): Tα1 engages TLR2/TLR9 on dendritic cells -> recruits the adaptor MyD88 -> activates p38 MAPK and NF-kappaB -> drives DC functional maturation and IL-12 production -> polarizes naive T cells toward a Th1 phenotype and promotes T-cell differentiation/thymic output (Romani 2004 Blood PMID 14982877; King & Tuthill 2016 PMID 27450734). In the tumor/macrophage arm, Tα1 binds phosphatidylserine on apoptotic cells, is internalized by macrophages, and triggers a lysosomal TLR7/MyD88 -> SHIP1 axis that dephosphorylates TBK1 and lowers efferocytosis-induced IL-10, reversing M2 (immunosuppressive) polarization toward an anti-tumor phenotype (Evidence-rodent; Wei 2022 Cancer Res PMID 35364609). Net pleiotropic effect: increases CD4+/CD8+ T cells, NK activity, IL-2/IFN-gamma and monocyte HLA-DR expression, while it is described as an immune 'rebalancer' rather than a pure stimulant (Evidence-human surrogate markers; Wu 2013 ETASS PMID 23327199 showed HLA-DR recovery). A newly described, separate signaling role: Tα1 upregulates MRAP via FoxO1 in thymic stromal cells, linked to thymic adipogenesis/involution (Evidence-rodent; PMID 41266306).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Parenteral only — subcutaneous injection is the clinical form (thymalfasin, e.g. Zadaxin). It is a peptide, so it is NOT orally bioavailable (would be digested); no oral/transdermal clinical formulation exists (Established; Ancell 2001 PMID 11381492).
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapidly absorbed after SC injection, reaching peak serum concentration within ~2 hours (Evidence-human; Ancell 2001 PMID 11381492). No formal absolute %bioavailability figure is established in the public literature — state as unknown rather than guessing.
Half-life
Serum half-life approximately 2 hours; blood levels return to baseline within 24 hours (Evidence-human; Ancell 2001 PMID 11381492). This short half-life is why clinical regimens dose repeatedly (e.g. twice weekly for hepatitis, or every 12 h in sepsis trials).
Metabolism
As a small 28-aa peptide it is cleared by ubiquitous proteolytic/peptidase degradation into amino acids rather than by cytochrome P450 hepatic metabolism; therefore essentially no CYP-mediated drug-drug interactions are expected (Established in principle for peptides; specific peptidase enzymes for Tα1 not individually mapped — Evidence-in vitro/inferred). Numbers beyond 'peptidase clearance' should not be invented.
Excretion
Degradation products (amino acids/small fragments) are handled renally; no intact-drug renal excretion data of note is published — treat exact route/percentages as not established.
Notes
No ester/prodrug form. Does not meaningfully cross the blood-brain barrier (large hydrophilic peptide) — its actions are peripheral/immune. No accumulation expected given rapid clearance. The biologic acts as an immune 'signal' that outlasts its plasma presence because downstream DC/T-cell programming persists after the peptide is gone.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human dose-response is only loosely characterized and largely indication-anchored rather than a clean dose-titration curve. HEPATITIS B (human): the registrational regimen is 1.6 mg (~900 micrograms/m2) SC twice weekly; a 26-week course produced complete virological response (HBV DNA + HBeAg clearance) in ~40.6% vs 9.4% untreated controls, and extending to 52 weeks did NOT improve response (26.5%) — i.e. longer duration was not better, arguing against a simple more-is-better relationship (Evidence-human RCT; Chien & Liaw 1998 PMID 9581695; Ancell 2001 PMID 11381492). SEPSIS (human): regimens have escalated to far higher frequency — ETASS used a loading then maintenance SC schedule and the definitive TESTS phase 3 used a fixed dose every 12 h for 7 days, yet TESTS found NO mortality benefit at that dose, so a higher-intensity regimen did not translate to efficacy (Evidence-human RCT; Wu 2025 TESTS PMID 39814420; Wu 2013 ETASS PMID 23327199). RODENT/IN VITRO: dose-dependent DC maturation, IL-12 induction and Th1 protection against Aspergillus are demonstrable across micromolar peptide concentrations, and macrophage M2-reversal is dose-related in tumor models (Evidence-rodent/in vitro; Romani 2004 PMID 14982877; Wei 2022 PMID 35364609). BOTTOM LINE: there is no validated human dose-response curve linking a specific microgram dose to a graded immune-marker or clinical-outcome magnitude; the most rigorous human data (TESTS) show that the standard immunomodulatory dose did not move 28-day mortality. NO dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs Thymopentin (TP-5, a synthetic 5-aa fragment of thymopoietin)
    same: Both are thymus-derived/thymus-mimetic peptide immunomodulators given parenterally, both promote T-cell maturation/differentiation, both have short half-lives and benign safety profiles
    different: Thymopentin derives from thymopoietin (a different parent protein than prothymosin alpha) and is an even shorter 5-residue peptide; Tα1's signaling is specifically TLR/MyD88-anchored on dendritic cells, whereas thymopentin's mechanism is less TLR-defined. Tα1 has far broader modern approvals and a much larger clinical trial base.
  • vs Thymosin beta-4 (Tβ4)
    same: Shares the 'thymosin' name and thymic-fraction origin; both are small bioactive peptides studied for tissue/immune benefit
    different: MECHANISTICALLY UNRELATED despite the name — Tβ4 is a G-actin-sequestering peptide whose main roles are cytoskeletal regulation, cell migration, angiogenesis and wound/tissue repair, NOT TLR-mediated immune activation. Tα1 (alpha family) is the immunomodulator; Tβ4 (beta family) is the actin-binding repair peptide. Different protein families entirely.
  • vs Interferon-alpha (IFN-alpha / IFN-alpha-2b)
    same: Both are injectable immune-acting agents used in chronic hepatitis B and historically combined or compared head-to-head; both ultimately aim to drive antiviral T-cell/innate immunity
    different: IFN-alpha binds a defined high-affinity cytokine receptor (IFNAR) and triggers a strong, well-characterized JAK-STAT antiviral program — but with substantial toxicity (flu-like illness, cytopenias, depression). Tα1 has no defined receptor, acts upstream via DC/TLR programming, and is markedly better tolerated, but with weaker/less consistent efficacy. In hepatitis B RCTs Tα1 produced a delayed, post-treatment 'accumulating' response unlike IFN's on-treatment effect.
  • vs Ulinastatin (frequent sepsis co-intervention)
    same: Repeatedly combined with Tα1 in sepsis immunomodulation trials and meta-analyses as a paired 'bi-immunomodulation' strategy
    different: Ulinastatin is a urinary trypsin inhibitor (anti-protease, anti-inflammatory) acting to DAMPEN excessive inflammation, the mechanistic opposite pole to Tα1's immune-RESTORING action — the rationale for pairing them is to correct both the hyper-inflammatory and immunosuppressed phases of sepsis. Much of the 'positive' sepsis meta-analytic signal is confounded by this combination rather than Tα1 alone.

History & legal status

"Origin: Tα1 was isolated by Allan Goldstein and colleagues in the 1970s from thymosin fraction 5 of calf thymus as the active component restoring immune function in thymectomized animals; it is a naturally occurring acetylated 28-amino-acid N-terminal fragment of prothymosin alpha (Established; PMID 17567941 Garaci historical overview; PMID 27450734). It was chemically synthesized as thymalfasin (brand Zadaxin, developed by SciClone Pharmaceuticals). Regulatory status: thymalfasin is APPROVED in ~30+ countries (notably China and parts of Asia, plus several countries in Latin America, the Middle East and Southern/Eastern Europe such as Italy) for chronic hepatitis B and as an immune enhancer / vaccine-response adjuvant in immunocompromised patients; in China it is also widely used off-label-by-Western-standards in cancer, severe sepsis and severe infection, and saw emergency use during SARS and COVID-19 (Established; PMID 36871535; PMID 27450734). It is NOT FDA-approved in the United States — it has been studied in US trials (e.g. hepatitis C, melanoma) but never reached US marketing approval, so in the US it exists only as an investigational/compounded/grey-market peptide. It is not a controlled substance and is not on standard anti-doping prohibited lists as a named agent, but its sale as a research/wellness peptide in countries where it is unapproved is unregulated. The pivotal phase 3 sepsis trial (TESTS, BMJ 2025) being NEGATIVE is a significant recent regulatory/clinical development tempering enthusiasm for the sepsis indication."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No validated high-affinity receptor or binding constant: the actual primary molecular target(s) and whether TLR2/TLR7/TLR9 engagement is direct or indirect (e.g. via membrane partitioning or chaperoned ligand presentation) remain unresolved — there is no Kd/Ki to cite.
  • Why the most rigorous human trial (TESTS phase 3, n=1106) was completely negative for sepsis mortality while the earlier ETASS and numerous Chinese meta-analyses were positive — publication bias, single-blind design, combination confounding (ulinastatin), or a genuinely absent effect?
  • The age/diabetes subgroup interactions in TESTS (possible harm in <60, possible benefit in diabetics) are unexplained and unconfirmed — real biological effect modifiers or statistical noise?
  • Whether any of the preclinical oncology mechanisms (TLR7/SHIP1 M2-reversal, 'cold-to-hot' tumor conversion, checkpoint-inhibitor synergy) translate to human cancer outcomes — essentially all current oncology mechanism data are rodent/in vitro.
  • Long-term safety of chronic/repeated dosing in healthy people using it as a wellness/performance peptide is essentially uncharacterized — trials studied finite courses in sick populations, not indefinite use in healthy adults.
  • Real-world product identity and purity: most non-prescription 'Tα1' peptide sold to consumers is unregulated, and actual content, sterility and contamination are unknown for any given vial.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT dosing guidance). Because Tα1 is an immune modulator, the sensible things to watch are: (1) Injection sites — redness, swelling, persistent nodules or signs of infection (non-sterile grey-market product is the bigger real-world risk than the peptide itself). (2) Liver function (ALT/AST) — especially relevant for anyone with hepatitis B/C, where an immune-mediated transaminase 'flare' can occur; a flare is on-target but should be physician-monitored, and unexplained ALT rises warrant evaluation. (3) Signs of immune over-activation or autoimmune flare — new or worsening inflammatory symptoms (joint pain, rashes, thyroid symptoms), particularly in anyone with a personal/family history of autoimmune disease. (4) Complete blood count with differential — to track lymphocyte/monocyte trends and rule out unexpected cytopenias if used long-term. (5) General constitutional symptoms — persistent fatigue, fevers, or malaise should prompt a pause and clinical review. (6) Anyone immunosuppressed, pregnant, on immunosuppressant drugs, with active autoimmune disease, or post-transplant should involve a physician before any use — the immune-stimulating mechanism is precisely the wrong direction for some of these states. Most important harm-reduction point: this is an unapproved peptide in many jurisdictions (including the US), so product authenticity, sterility and dose accuracy are unverified — that, not a known intrinsic toxicity, is the dominant practical risk."

Caveats

CERTAINTY & HUMAN-DATA GAP: The precise molecular mechanism (TLR9/MyD88/IRF7 → interferon; TLR2/p38/NF-κB → DC maturation/IL-12) rests largely on mouse and in-vitro work from one main research lineage (Romani/Garaci group, PMIDs 14982877, 17804687) — robust but not yet fully replicated mechanistically in humans. Tα1 is genuinely pleiotropic with NO single classical receptor, so 'primary target = TLR/MyD88 on dendritic cells' is the best-supported node, not an exclusive one. REGULATORY: Approved as thymalfasin in several countries (chronic hepatitis B; vaccine adjuvant in immunocompromised) and used broadly in China — but it is NOT FDA-approved in the US and is sold there as a research/'grey-market' peptide. Approval elsewhere does not equal proven efficacy for every indication YouTubers discuss. HYPE vs EVIDENCE: Human efficacy is strongest as an adjunct in specific clinical settings (HBV, severe infection/sepsis, severe acute pancreatitis meta-analysis PMID 40599771) and is still being tested (PANDA II RCT, PMID 40367062). COVID-19 use was emergency/observational and is NOT validated by rigorous Western RCTs. Cancer and anti-aging/'immune-boost' claims are mostly preclinical (rodent/in-vitro: PMIDs 39357524, 40955371) — do not present these as established human benefits. DO NOT CONFUSE with Thymosin β4 / TB-500, a structurally and functionally unrelated actin-binding tissue-repair peptide — different molecule, different mechanism. NOT MEDICAL ADVICE: This is a mechanistic/educational summary of published evidence, not dosing or treatment guidance.
