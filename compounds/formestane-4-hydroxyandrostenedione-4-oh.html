Formestane

The first clinically-used selective aromatase inhibitor — a steroidal "suicide" inactivator now living a second life as a gray-market estrogen blocker in the PED/prohormone world.

Limited human data 12 citations
Class
Steroidal (type-I) aromatase inhibitor / inactivator — an androstenedione substrate analog that irreversibly (mechanism-based, "suicide") inactivates the aromatase enzyme
Primary target
Aromatase (cytochrome P450 CYP19A1) — the enzyme that converts androgens (testosterone, androstenedione) into estrogens (estradiol, estrone)
Human status
Extensively studied in humans — but as a cancer drug, not as a men's estrogen-control agent. Formestane was a marketed pharmaceutical (Lentaron, Ciba-Geigy/Novartis) for postmenopausal ER-positive advanced breast cancer, with multiple Phase II/III trials establishing the injectable 250 mg every-2-weeks regimen, ~20-30% second-line objective response rates, and comparability to megestrol. It was later withdrawn from markets, superseded by the more potent, orally convenient third-generation AIs (anastrozole, letrozole) and the steroidal exemestane. It now has essentially no legitimate pharmaceutical supply and circulates in the prohormone/PED gray market — often as transdermal creams or unregulated injectables. There are NO rigorous controlled trials of formestane as an estrogen-control agent in men or athletes; that use is extrapolated from its class mechanism and from oncology-era endocrine data. WADA prohibits aromatase inhibitors in sport (class S4). Note: ChEMBL lists formestane at max_phase 2 with no approval flag recorded — a reflection of that database's curation rather than a claim that it was never marketed; its historical marketing as Lentaron and subsequent commercial (non-safety) withdrawal are well documented.

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Formestane is a look-alike of aromatase's natural substrate Established

    Formestane is 4-hydroxyandrostenedione — chemically androstenedione (a natural aromatase substrate) with an added hydroxyl at the 4-position. Because it mimics the real substrate, it slots into the active (substrate) site of aromatase (CYP19A1) instead of the normal androgen. Dowsett 1994 (PMID 7873460); Dowsett & Coombes 1994 (PMID 7949207)

  2. Aromatase turns formestane into its own poison (suicide inhibition) Established

    Aromatase does not just bind formestane — it begins to process it. During catalysis the enzyme converts formestane into a reactive intermediate that binds tightly and permanently to the enzyme, inactivating that enzyme molecule for good. This is called mechanism-based or 'suicide' inhibition, and it is why formestane is called an aromatase inactivator rather than a simple reversible blocker. Dowsett 1994 (PMID 7873460)

  3. Irreversible inactivation means a long-lasting, highly selective effect Established

    Because the enzyme is permanently disabled, the cell must synthesize brand-new aromatase protein to recover activity — so a dose keeps working long after the drug itself has cleared. Binding the substrate pocket (rather than the heme iron, as the non-steroidal AIs do) also makes it highly selective for aromatase, with no significant effect on other steroid-synthesizing pathways in patients. Dowsett 1994 (PMID 7873460); Dowsett & Coombes 1994 (PMID 7949207)

  4. Less aromatase means less estrogen made from androgens Evidence-human

    With aromatase disabled, the body's ability to convert androgens into estradiol and estrone drops. At the injectable dose used clinically (250 mg IM every 2 weeks), peripheral aromatase activity fell by roughly 85% and circulating estradiol by roughly 65% in postmenopausal women. This is the intended effect: lower systemic estrogen. Dowsett & Coombes 1994 (PMID 7949207); Dowsett 1994 (PMID 7873460)

  5. In estrogen-driven breast tumors, less estrogen means less growth signal Evidence-human

    In postmenopausal, estrogen-receptor-positive breast cancer, tumor growth depends on estrogen made locally and peripherally by aromatase. Cutting estrogen supply removes the growth stimulus, producing tumor shrinkage or stabilization in a subset of patients. This was formestane's approved therapeutic rationale. Wiseman & Goa 1996 (PMID 8894526); Pérez Carrión 1994 (PMID 7873457)

  6. In men and PED users, lower estradiol shifts the testosterone:estrogen balance Contested

    In males, estradiol comes largely from aromatizing testosterone. Suppressing aromatase lowers estradiol and can raise testosterone by relieving estrogen's negative feedback on the brain's LH/FSH signaling. This LH/FSH/testosterone-raising effect is well established for aromatase inhibitors as a CLASS in men, and is the rationale for formestane's off-label use to control estrogen during anabolic-steroid or prohormone use — but there are no controlled formestane-specific trials in men, so the male effect is extrapolated from the class, not measured for this drug. Class mechanism: Dowsett 1994 (PMID 7873460); off-label anti-estrogen use context in athletes: Piper 2012 (PMID 22354842)

  7. Formestane is not purely 'anti' — it and its metabolites carry weak androgenic activity Evidence-in vitro

    Formestane is a steroid, and both it and several of its urinary metabolites activate the androgen receptor in cell-based (yeast) reporter assays. Clinically this shows up as a dose-related fall in sex-hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) when the drug is given orally — a fingerprint of mild androgenic action — though overt androgenic side effects were not prominent at therapeutic injectable doses. In vitro androgenicity: Keiler 2018 yeast androgen/estrogen screen (PMID 29702199); clinical SHBG effect: Dowsett & Coombes 1994 (PMID 7949207)

  8. Formestane is also made by the body — a wrinkle for anti-doping Established

    In small amounts, humans produce formestane endogenously by hydroxylating androstenedione. Because of this, anti-doping labs cannot simply detect its presence; above a urinary threshold (WADA uses >100 ng/mL as presumptive of ingestion) they use carbon isotope ratio mass spectrometry (IRMS) and urinary metabolite ratios to prove an external source before declaring a violation. Piper 2012 (PMID 22354842); de la Torre 2019 (PMID 30904502); de la Torre 2014 (PMID 25516450)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Binds the androgen-substrate pocket of aromatase (CYP19A1), not the heme iron. As a 4-hydroxylated androstenedione analog it is recognized as a pseudo-substrate; the enzyme initiates catalysis and generates a reactive intermediate that irreversibly (mechanism-based, 'suicide') inactivates the enzyme. This substrate-site, type-I mechanism makes it highly selective for aromatase — in patients it produced no significant effects on steroid-synthetic pathways other than estrogen synthesis — and its action is long-lasting because recovery requires new enzyme synthesis. It additionally has weak direct androgen-receptor agonism (itself and metabolites, demonstrated in vitro).

Signalling cascade. Formestane → occupies aromatase substrate site → enzyme-catalyzed conversion to a reactive species → permanent enzyme inactivation → ↓ conversion of testosterone/androstenedione to estradiol/estrone → ↓ estrogen-receptor signaling (tumor growth arrest in ER+ breast cancer) and, in males, ↓ estrogen negative feedback → ↑ LH/FSH → ↑ endogenous testosterone (the latter an AI class effect, not measured for formestane specifically in men).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Clinically used as a depot intramuscular injection (250 mg every 2 weeks) because oral bioavailability is poor. Gray-market products also appear as transdermal creams and oral/sublingual preparations, which have different and less predictable kinetics.
Absorption / bioavailability
Oral administration is inefficient due to extensive first-pass metabolism, which drove the choice of an IM depot for the approved product. Transdermal and oral routes produce distinct metabolite excretion patterns (characterized in anti-doping metabolism studies).
Half-life
The parent compound clears relatively quickly, but the pharmacodynamic effect (aromatase inactivation) far outlasts the parent because the enzyme is permanently disabled — the depot IM schedule reflects duration of enzyme suppression rather than plasma half-life.
Metabolism
Hepatic; major routes are reduction/hydroxylation and glucuronidation. Key urinary metabolites include 4-hydroxyandrosterone and 4α-hydroxyepiandrosterone (4-OH-EA); the 4-OH-EA/4-OH-androsterone ratio differs by route (oral vs transdermal), a fact exploited in anti-doping testing.
Excretion
Renal, largely as glucuronide-conjugated hydroxylated metabolites. Endogenous background production exists, so absolute urinary presence is not proof of exogenous use.
Notes
de la Torre 2019 (PMID 30904502) characterizes human transdermal vs oral metabolism and route-diagnostic metabolite ratios; de la Torre 2014 (PMID 25516450) adds metabolite biomarkers; Piper 2012 (PMID 22354842) documents endogenous production and IRMS-based origin discrimination.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

In postmenopausal women, IM 250 mg every 2 weeks produced ~85% peripheral aromatase inhibition and ~65% estradiol suppression; a 500 mg every-2-weeks regimen was also studied, with some evidence of higher tumor response rates at the higher dose. Notably, aromatase suppression by formestane is incomplete relative to the more potent third-generation AIs (anastrozole, letrozole), which achieve deeper (>97-99%) aromatase inhibition — a major reason formestane was superseded. Oral dosing unmasks a dose-related fall in SHBG, reflecting its minor androgenic component. No validated male/PED dose-response data exist. (Dowsett & Coombes 1994, PMID 7949207; Wiseman & Goa 1996, PMID 8894526; dose-ranging endocrine/PK data: Dowsett 1989, PMID 2917360.)

How it compares

  • vs Exemestane (Aromasin)
    same: Both are steroidal, type-I, irreversible mechanism-based aromatase inactivators derived from the androgen substrate; both are the 'suicide inhibitor' branch of AIs and share weak intrinsic androgenic character.
    different: Exemestane is orally active, more potent, still on-market and pharma-grade, and is the modern steroidal successor. Formestane required injection (poor oral bioavailability), is less potent, and is withdrawn/gray-market. Exemestane is usually the drug people are actually reaching for when they want a 'steroidal AI.'
  • vs Anastrozole / Letrozole (non-steroidal, type-II AIs)
    same: All lower estradiol by inhibiting aromatase and are used (on- or off-label) to control estrogen.
    different: Anastrozole/letrozole are reversible, competitive, bind the heme iron (not the substrate site), are non-steroidal (no intrinsic androgenicity), orally potent, and achieve deeper (>97-99%) aromatase suppression than formestane's ~85%. They are the current standard; formestane is essentially obsolete pharmacologically.
  • vs Aminoglutethimide (first-generation AI)
    same: Both are older-generation aromatase inhibitors used historically in breast cancer.
    different: Aminoglutethimide was non-selective (also blocked other steroidogenic P450s, often requiring corticosteroid replacement) and poorly tolerated; formestane was the first SELECTIVE aromatase inhibitor with wide clinical availability — a meaningful selectivity advance in its day.
  • vs 4-Hydroxytestosterone (4-OHT, its 17β-hydroxy analog / an anabolic prohormone)
    same: Structurally close (formestane is the 17-keto; 4-OHT is the 17β-hydroxy form); both have aromatase-inhibiting and androgenic activity, produce overlapping urinary metabolites, and are prohibited in sport.
    different: 4-OHT is a more overtly anabolic/androgenic steroid marketed as a prohormone (and, per Keiler 2018, investigated in a breast-cancer trial); formestane is characterized primarily as the aromatase inactivator. Their urinary phase I/II metabolite patterns are very similar, which is exactly why anti-doping analysis must distinguish them.

History & legal status

Formestane (4-hydroxyandrostenedione, developed as CGP 32349 by Ciba-Geigy) was the first SELECTIVE aromatase inhibitor to reach wide clinical use, marketed as Lentaron from the early 1990s for postmenopausal ER-positive advanced breast cancer, typically after tamoxifen failure. It was historically important as proof-of-concept that selective aromatase inhibition was clinically effective and safe. It was superseded within about a decade by the third-generation AIs (anastrozole, letrozole — more potent and orally convenient) and the steroidal exemestane, and Lentaron was withdrawn from markets (a commercial/obsolescence withdrawal, not a safety withdrawal). ChEMBL lists it at max_phase 2 with no approval flag and withdrawn_flag false — a database-curation artifact rather than a claim about its real-world marketing history. It subsequently reappeared in the bodybuilding/prohormone gray market as an estrogen-control agent (creams, injectables) and is a recognized doping agent — WADA lists aromatase inhibitors (class S4) as prohibited at all times, and anti-doping labs developed dedicated IRMS and metabolite-ratio methods because formestane is also produced endogenously.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No controlled trials of formestane for estrogen control in men or PED users — dose, efficacy, and safety in that population are entirely extrapolated from the AI class.
  • Pharmacokinetics and real potency of gray-market transdermal/oral formestane products (vs the studied IM depot) are poorly characterized and product-dependent.
  • Long-term cardiovascular and bone consequences of formestane specifically (as opposed to the AI class) are essentially unquantified, especially in men — the only formestane bone data are short-term turnover markers that did not show clear bone loss.
  • The net clinical impact of its intrinsic (in vitro) androgenic activity at real-world doses is unclear — it complicates the assumption that it is a 'pure' estrogen blocker.
  • Whether formestane's ~85% aromatase-inhibition ceiling (vs >97% for modern AIs) meaningfully changes estrogen-control outcomes in non-cancer users is untested.
  • Purity, contamination, and dose accuracy of unregulated gray-market material are unknown by definition.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is what to WATCH, not a protocol. Because formestane lowers estradiol, the central risk is over-suppression: watch for symptoms of estrogen deficiency — new or worsening joint pain, low libido or erectile problems, low mood, hot flushes and sleep disturbance, and, over time, signs of bone loss. A clinician-ordered sensitive/LC-MS estradiol assay (the assay matters — standard immunoassays are inaccurate at the low estradiol levels seen in men) alongside testosterone, LH/FSH, and SHBG can help detect crashed estrogen and the drug's androgenic footprint. Baseline and periodic lipid panel, and with prolonged use bone mineral density (DEXA), are the relevant safety checks given estrogen's role in lipids and bone. Watch for injection-site reactions with injectables. Red flags warranting stopping and seeking medical care: persistent bone or joint pain, mood disturbance, sexual dysfunction, or any signs of virilization. Anyone competing in tested sport should know it is WADA-prohibited and detectable (via isotope-ratio testing) despite endogenous background. None of this is a recommendation to use a withdrawn, unregulated drug — it is harm-reduction context for someone who might encounter it.

Caveats

Read this as a monograph on how formestane works, not a how-to. Key honesty points: (1) The robust human evidence is in postmenopausal women with breast cancer; its popular use — estrogen control in men on anabolics/prohormones — has essentially no controlled human trials, so male dose-response, safety, and efficacy are extrapolated from the AI class, not measured for formestane. (2) It is a WITHDRAWN drug: gray-market material has unknown potency, purity, and dosing, and transdermal/oral products behave differently from the studied intramuscular depot. (3) Formestane is not a clean 'anti-estrogen' — it is a steroid with intrinsic weak androgenic activity (itself and its metabolites, shown in vitro), so its effects are not purely estrogen-lowering. (4) Aggressively crushing estradiol carries real harms (bone loss, adverse lipids, joint pain, mood, libido, and in men possibly worse cardiovascular and sexual outcomes) — these are class-level concerns for aromatase inhibitors; formestane-specific long-term human data on bone and cardiovascular endpoints are essentially absent. Estrogen is not a 'junk' hormone to be zeroed out. (5) It is endogenously produced, which matters for interpreting drug tests. This is educational/harm-reduction information, not medical advice, and contains no dosing protocol.
