The once-a-week off-switch for prolactin — a long-acting ergot that quiets the pituitary's milk hormone, but whose ergot backbone can also tug on heart-valve serotonin receptors.

Limited human dataFDA-approved 17 citations
Class
Ergot-derived (ergoline) dopamine D2 receptor agonist
Primary target
Dopamine D2 receptors on anterior-pituitary lactotroph cells — the "brake" that suppresses prolactin synthesis and secretion
Human status
Fully approved human drug with decades of clinical use. Cabergoline (brand Dostinex, and Cabaser for Parkinson's) is FDA/EMA-approved and is first-line treatment for hyperprolactinemia and prolactinomas, where it is more effective and better tolerated than bromocriptine. It is also used for Parkinson's disease (at much higher doses) in some countries, though ergot agonists have largely been displaced there by non-ergot agonists specifically because of the valve-disease risk. Its use as a prolactin-lowering ancillary in bodybuilding/PED and peptide (GHRP/GHRH) contexts is entirely off-label and self-directed; there are no controlled trials of cabergoline in that population.

At a glance

  • Lowers serum prolactin within hours and keeps it suppressed for about a week per dose
  • Normalizes prolactin in the majority of hyperprolactinemic patients and shrinks prolactin-secreting pituitary tumors
  • Restores libido, erectile and gonadal function when the problem is prolactin-driven
  • Reduces prolactin-driven gynecomastia (but NOT the progesterone-receptor arm of 19-nor steroid gyno)
  • Off-target liability: ergot backbone can drive fibrotic heart-valve disease at high cumulative exposure
  • Class dopaminergic effects: nausea, orthostatic hypotension, and impulse-control disorders

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps6 human-evidenced 1 preclinical 1 speculative

  1. Prolactin lives under a dopamine brake Established

    Unlike almost every other pituitary hormone, prolactin is controlled mainly by INHIBITION, not stimulation. Dopamine neurons in the hypothalamus (the tuberoinfundibular pathway) constantly drip dopamine onto the pituitary's prolactin-making cells (lactotrophs), holding prolactin down. Take the dopamine away and prolactin rises. Cabergoline works by impersonating dopamine at those cells' D2 receptors. PMID 11249538; PMID 3745407

  2. Cabergoline is a potent, D2-selective agonist that binds hard and lets go slowly Established

    Cabergoline is a synthetic ergoline with high affinity and strong selectivity for the D2 receptor. It binds tightly and dissociates very slowly, and it lingers in pituitary tissue — which is why a single dose keeps prolactin suppressed for days, not hours. That slow off-rate plus a long elimination half-life is the pharmacological basis for once- or twice-weekly dosing. (In cultured rat pituitary cells its IC50 for suppressing prolactin was ~34-fold lower than bromocriptine's.) PMID 11249538; PMID 12844325; PMID 3745407

  3. At the receptor: it switches off the cell's cAMP amplifier (Gi signaling) Established

    The D2 receptor is a Gi/Go-coupled receptor. When cabergoline activates it, the Gi protein shuts down adenylyl cyclase, so intracellular cAMP falls and PKA — the enzyme that helps drive prolactin gene activity and release — goes quiet. Less cAMP means less prolactin made and less released. This is canonical D2/lactotroph pharmacology. PMID 11249538; corpus ask_tutor synthesis over lactotroph Gi/cAMP/PKA physiology

  4. It also hyperpolarizes the cell and cuts off the calcium needed to secrete Established

    Beyond cAMP, D2 activation (via Gβγ) opens potassium channels (GIRK) so the lactotroph becomes electrically 'quieter' (hyperpolarized), and it reduces calcium entry through voltage-gated calcium channels. Since a rise in internal calcium is the trigger that dumps prolactin-filled vesicles out of the cell, choking off calcium directly blocks the actual act of secretion — the fast, minutes-to-hours part of the effect. This is textbook lactotroph electrophysiology rather than something measured for cabergoline specifically. corpus ask_tutor synthesis; canonical D2/GIRK/Ca2+ lactotroph electrophysiology

  5. Prolactin plummets within hours and stays down for a week Evidence-human

    In hyperprolactinemic patients, a single oral dose drops serum prolactin starting within about 3 hours, hits a ~50% fall at 2–5 days, and stays suppressed out to 7 days. With chronic weekly dosing in macroprolactinoma, mean prolactin fell ~93% and normalized in most patients — more effectively and with fewer side effects than the older drug bromocriptine. PMID 3745407; PMID 8964874

  6. Over time it can shrink prolactin-secreting tumors Evidence-human

    In actual prolactinomas, sustained D2 stimulation not only lowers hormone output but shrinks the tumor — tumor size fell in 11 of 15 patients on weekly cabergoline in one macroadenoma trial. Mechanistically this reflects reduced lactotroph size/activity and, with potent agonists, some programmed cell death (apoptosis) of lactotrophs. The apoptosis component is better characterized in rodent/in-vitro lactotroph work than in humans. (For a healthy PED user with drug-induced high prolactin there is no tumor — the point is simply the prolactin-lowering.) PMID 8964874; PMID 11249538

  7. Why it matters for PED users: it addresses the prolactin arm of '19-nor / deca dick' Speculative

    19-nortestosterone steroids (nandrolone/deca, trenbolone) and GH secretagogues (GHRP-2, GHRP-6, hexarelin, and to a lesser degree MK-677) can raise prolactin. High prolactin lowers libido and drives erectile dysfunction and prolactin-type gynecomastia. Cabergoline lowers prolactin via D2 and is the standard tool people reach for. Crucial caveat: with 19-nors, part of the sexual/gyno problem comes from the steroid's own progesterone-receptor activity, which an anti-prolactin drug does NOT fix — so cabergoline treats the prolactin arm, not the progestin arm. This PED framing is mechanistic extrapolation; there are no controlled cabergoline trials in PED users. corpus MoA cards 'Prolactin & dopamine', 'Progesterone receptor', 'ISSUE: Gynecomastia'; extrapolation from endocrine literature

  8. The catch: the same ergot backbone can grab heart-valve serotonin (5-HT2) receptors Evidence-in vitro

    Cabergoline is an ergot. Its bulky N6 chemical group lets it also act as an agonist at serotonin 5-HT2 receptors on heart-valve cells. Turning those on drives valve-cell proliferation and fibrosis (the same pathway blamed for fen-phen and carcinoid valve disease). Which subtype does the damage is contested: the fen-phen story points at 5-HT2B, but the study that directly dissected cabergoline found its valve-cell ERK1/2 and matrix-building effect was mediated by 5-HT2A. Either way this is an off-target liability of the drug's structure — separate from its prolactin benefit — and it is dose- and duration-dependent. The receptor-level mechanism is in-vitro; the resulting valve disease at high cumulative dose is human. PMID 21518772; PMID 10617681; PMID 17202454

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. High-affinity, D2-selective agonist. Cabergoline is a synthetic ergoline (ergot alkaloid derivative) with strong, selective agonism at dopamine D2 receptors (and D3), a slow receptor dissociation rate, and prolonged retention in pituitary tissue — together explaining its exceptionally long duration of prolactin suppression. Critically, its bulky N6 substituent also confers agonism at serotonin 5-HT2B and 5-HT2A receptors; replacing that N6 group with a simple methyl converts the 5-HT2 agonism to antagonism while keeping D2 agonism, directly linking the drug's structure to its valve-disease liability. Note the receptor subtype responsible for valve fibrosis is contested: the fen-phen literature emphasizes 5-HT2B, but in isolated valve interstitial cells cabergoline's ERK1/2 activation and matrix production were blocked by a 5-HT2A antagonist, implicating 5-HT2A for cabergoline specifically. It has weaker activity at adrenergic and other serotonin receptors that contributes to nausea and orthostatic hypotension.

Signalling cascade. On the pituitary lactotroph, D2 activation is Gi/Go-coupled: it inhibits adenylyl cyclase → lowers cAMP → lowers PKA activity (dampening prolactin gene activity and vesicle release), while Gβγ subunits open GIRK potassium channels (hyperpolarizing the cell) and reduce voltage-gated Ca2+ entry (removing the calcium trigger for exocytosis). Net result: rapid inhibition of prolactin secretion plus slower inhibition of prolactin synthesis, and with chronic exposure, lactotroph involution and (in rodent/in-vitro models) apoptosis, the basis of tumor shrinkage. On heart-valve interstitial cells, the OFF-TARGET 5-HT2 pathway is Gq-coupled: it drives inositol-phosphate hydrolysis, intracellular Ca2+ rise, and the MAPK/ERK1/2 mitogenic cascade, stimulating cell proliferation and extracellular-matrix (collagen, glycosaminoglycan) deposition — i.e., valve fibrosis.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral
Absorption / bioavailability
Peak plasma concentration reached ~2–3 hours after an oral dose; linear pharmacokinetics across the 0.5–7 mg range in healthy volunteers and parkinsonian patients. Absolute bioavailability is unknown (a significant first-pass effect occurs). Food does not meaningfully alter its pharmacokinetics.
Half-life
Long — elimination half-life estimated at roughly 63–109 hours from urinary data in healthy subjects. Combined with slow receptor dissociation and pituitary retention, this supports twice-weekly dosing for hyperprolactinemia and once-daily for Parkinson's.
Metabolism
Extensively metabolized by the liver, predominantly by hydrolysis of the acylurea bond of the urea moiety; cytochrome-P450 involvement is minimal (so CYP-based drug interactions are limited). Major metabolites do not contribute to the therapeutic effect. Less than 4% is excreted unchanged in urine.
Excretion
Primarily as hepatic metabolites; renal excretion of parent drug is minor (<4%). Mild-to-moderate renal or hepatic impairment did not significantly alter pharmacokinetics in study populations.
Notes
Moderate (~40%) plasma-protein binding, concentration-independent, so displacement interactions with other highly protein-bound drugs are unlikely. The dominant PK message: this is a very long-acting agent — effects (both benefit and any off-target valve exposure) accumulate over weeks, and cumulative dose over months-to-years is the relevant exposure metric for valve risk.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Two very different dose worlds, and conflating them is the central error. ENDOCRINE (prolactin) dosing is low — hyperprolactinemia/prolactinoma is typically controlled with small weekly totals, and even a single sub-milligram dose halves prolactin for days. PARKINSON'S dosing is far higher and daily, producing much larger cumulative exposure. The prolactin-lowering (D2) effect saturates at low doses — you do not need high doses to crush prolactin. The 5-HT2 valve effect, by contrast, is cumulative-dose- and duration-driven: in the Zanettini NEJM study, patients with clinically important regurgitation had received significantly higher mean cumulative cabergoline doses than those without. So the therapeutic logic is 'lowest effective dose for the shortest necessary time' — the prolactin benefit plateaus early while the valve risk climbs with total exposure. There is no established safe cumulative threshold, and isolated case reports of valvulopathy at low (endocrine) doses exist; but the weight of prospective and epidemiologic evidence suggests endocrine-level cumulative doses carry little detectable valve risk while Parkinson-level cumulative doses carry substantial risk.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Bromocriptine
    same: Both ergot-derived dopamine agonists that lower prolactin via D2.
    different: Cabergoline is more D2-selective, far longer-acting (twice-weekly vs 2–3×/day), more effective at normalizing prolactin and shrinking tumors, and better tolerated (less nausea). Bromocriptine has a shorter half-life and is the historical reference agent; it is generally second-line for hyperprolactinemia. Note bromocriptine is not valve-risk-free — it too has valvulopathy case reports, though the signal is weaker.
  • vs Pramipexole (non-ergot D2/D3 agonist)
    same: Both are dopamine agonists; both can lower prolactin and both carry impulse-control-disorder risk.
    different: Pramipexole is NON-ergot, so it lacks the 5-HT2 valve-fibrosis liability — a key safety advantage. It is not an approved prolactin drug and is shorter-acting; in PED circles it is sometimes used off-label for prolactin but its dopaminergic side-effect and ICD profile differ.
  • vs Aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole/exemestane)
    same: Both are ancillary drugs used to manage gynecomastia and sexual side effects on cycle.
    different: Completely different targets. AIs cut estradiol (aromatase/CYP19A1) and fix ESTROGEN-driven gyno. Cabergoline lowers PROLACTIN. For 19-nor 'deca/tren dick' the driver is prolactin PLUS progesterone-receptor activity, so an AI alone often fails and cabergoline addresses only the prolactin arm — they are complementary, not interchangeable.
  • vs Lisuride (8α-ergoline dopamine agonist)
    same: Both ergoline dopamine agonists that lower prolactin.
    different: Lisuride is a potent 5-HT2B ANTAGONIST rather than agonist, and no cardiac valvulopathy has been linked to it despite its ergot structure — a clean demonstration that valve risk tracks 5-HT2B agonism, not merely ergot backbone.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • GHRP-2 counteracts ↓ Established mechanism (GHRP-2 raises prolactin in humans; cabergoline lowers it); the specific pairing is extrapolation
    GHRP-2 is a non-selective secretagogue that produces a modest but real rise in prolactin (off-target lactotroph stimulation via the same Gq/PLC machinery). Cabergoline is a long-acting dopamine D2 agonist that impersonates dopamine on lactotrophs, shutting down cAMP/PKA- and calcium-driven prolactin secretion - the physiologic brake GHRP-2 nudges against. So cabergoline directly counteracts the prolactin arm of GHRP-2's spillover (relevant if libido drops or galactorrhea appears). GHRP-2 monograph step 6 & side_effect_mechanisms (prolactin rise, PMID 9285939); Cabergoline monograph (D2 agonist lowers prolactin; addresses prolactin-driven, not progesterone-driven, effects) Watch: Serum prolactin (confirm it is actually elevated first and that it normalizes); libido/erectile function. Cabergoline is not benign - watch for nausea and orthostatic dizziness; heart-valve concern is cumulative-exposure-related and low at endocrine exposure.
  • GHRP-6 counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    GHRP-6 is a GHS-R1a agonist whose receptor engagement in humans produces prolactin release (alongside ACTH/cortisol) in addition to GH, reflecting receptor expression beyond somatotrophs. Cabergoline lowers prolactin through pituitary D2 agonism, counteracting that GHRP-6-induced prolactin elevation — the same lactotroph endpoint pushed the opposite way. cabergoline.json mechanism_steps#7; ghrp-6 monograph side_effect 'Prolactin elevation' (PMID 9285939; PMID 15177700) Watch: Serum prolactin to verify the elevation and prevent over-suppression. GHRP-6 also raises cortisol/ACTH; cabergoline does not fix that. Monitor for cabergoline's dopaminergic AEs (dizziness on standing, impulse-control behaviors).
  • Hexarelin counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    Hexarelin is not a clean GH releaser — its GHS-R1a agonism in the pituitary transiently raises prolactin along with GH (shown in humans). Cabergoline is a dopamine D2 agonist that tonically suppresses prolactin at the lactotroph, so it directly counteracts hexarelin's prolactin bump. This is the same mechanism used to manage prolactin elevations from other sources. Hexarelin monograph (prolactin rise, PMID 10404825/11238504); corpus PMID 15177700 (hexarelin raises prolactin in humans); cabergoline monograph (D2-selective agonist lowers prolactin) Watch: Serum prolactin to confirm control; symptoms of high prolactin (low libido, erectile changes, galactorrhea). Note cabergoline itself needs monitoring for mood and, at higher cumulative exposure, cardiac valve effects.
  • MK-677 counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    MK-677 is an oral GHS-R1a agonist that is relatively GH-selective but still produces modest, usually transient prolactin (and small cortisol) increases via lactotroph/HPA spillover. Cabergoline lowers prolactin via pituitary D2 agonism, so it can counteract a symptomatic MK-677 prolactin rise — but the magnitude of MK-677's prolactin effect is smaller and less consistent than the injectable GHRPs (e.g. PRL +23% within the normal range; several studies found no chronic change), so the interaction is weaker and often clinically unnecessary. cabergoline.json mechanism_steps#7 + key_unknowns (MK-677 prolactin uncertain); mk-677 monograph side_effect 'Transient rise in cortisol/prolactin' (PMID 18981485; PMID 9467542) Watch: Serum prolactin to decide whether an MK-677 prolactin rise is real and symptomatic (galactorrhea, mood/libido change) before adding a dopamine agonist, and to avoid over-suppression. Because MK-677 also worsens insulin sensitivity and cabergoline has a mild glucose-lowering signal, fasting glucose/HbA1c monitoring is reasonable on the combination.
  • Nandrolone counteracts ↓ Established
    Nandrolone (a 19-nortestosterone) raises prolactin, partly through its intrinsic progestogenic activity, and high prolactin drives low libido, erectile dysfunction and prolactin-type gynecomastia ('deca dick'). Cabergoline works at the opposite end of the same axis: it is a D2 agonist on pituitary lactotrophs that suppresses prolactin synthesis and secretion. So cabergoline directly counteracts the prolactin ARM of nandrolone's sexual/breast side effects. It does NOT touch the separate progesterone-receptor arm of nandrolone, which needs a SERM, not a dopamine agonist. cabergoline.json mechanism_steps#7 (D2/prolactin, PMID 3745407); nandrolone monograph step 6 (PGR + prolactin, PMID 19941958); corpus MoA cards 'Prolactin & dopamine', 'Progesterone receptor' (ESTABLISHED for nandrolone) Watch: Serum prolactin (before and during, to confirm the problem is actually prolactin-driven and to avoid overshooting into abnormally LOW prolactin). If libido/ED fail to improve despite a normalized prolactin, the residual driver is likely nandrolone's progesterone-receptor activity, which cabergoline will not fix. Watch for cabergoline's own dopaminergic harms: orthostatic dizziness and impulse-control changes.
  • Trenbolone counteracts ↓ Contested
    Trenbolone is a non-aromatizing 19-nor that binds the progesterone receptor (more tightly than progesterone) and is reported to raise prolactin; elevated prolactin is thought to contribute to 'tren dick', low libido and gyno that estrogen blockers cannot fix.     Complementary but NON-overlapping pairing for 19-nor gynecomastia: the two share the clinical endpoint (gyno) and the diagnostic workup, but hit different mechanisms and neither substitutes for the other. Anastrozole lowers estradiol via aromatase (CYP19A1) inhibition and fixes ESTROGEN-driven gyno; cabergoline lowers PROLACTIN and fixes the prolactin arm of 19-nor gyno. Because 19-nor breast/sexual side effects are driven by prolactin PLUS progesterone-receptor activity, an aromatase inhibitor alone often fails and does NOT substitute for cabergoline — and neither drug addresses the progestin (PGR) arm. Avoid crashing estradiol with the AI (harms lipids, joints, libido, mood) while separately over-suppressing prolactin with cabergoline.

    A deliberately NON-interacting contrast. Because CJC-1295+ipamorelin does not meaningfully elevate prolactin, cabergoline's prolactin-lowering action has essentially no target to act on here — adding cabergoline would be redundant and only expose the user to cabergoline's own dopaminergic/ergot risks with no offsetting benefit. History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Cabergoline was developed by Farmitalia (later Pharmacia/Pfizer) and introduced in the 1990s (Dostinex for hyperprolactinemia; Cabaser for Parkinson's). It rapidly became first-line for hyperprolactinemia/prolactinomas, displacing bromocriptine on efficacy and tolerability. In 2007 the Zanettini NEJM study and companion reports established that ergot dopamine agonists (pergolide, cabergoline) are associated with fibrotic valvular heart disease at Parkinson's-level doses; pergolide was subsequently withdrawn from the US market (2007) and regulators added valve-monitoring requirements and dose caps for ergot agonists in Parkinson's. Crucially, later prospective and large database studies (Vroonen 2017, PMID 27709470; Trifiró 2012, PMID 22204718) showed the low endocrine doses used for prolactin do not carry a detectable valve signal, so cabergoline remains standard endocrine therapy while ergot agonists have largely fallen out of favor for Parkinson's in favor of non-ergot agents. Its use as a PED/peptide-cycle ancillary is entirely off-label and unstudied.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Whether the intermittent, self-directed dosing used by PED/peptide users produces enough cumulative exposure over years to cause valve fibrosis — no data exist in this population.
  • Whether there is a truly 'safe' cumulative-dose threshold for valvulopathy, or only a continuum of risk (low-dose case reports muddy the reassuring epidemiology).
  • Which serotonin receptor subtype (5-HT2B vs 5-HT2A) actually drives cabergoline's valve fibrosis — the mechanistic literature disagrees.
  • Long-term consequences of driving prolactin below normal in otherwise-healthy men (iatrogenic hypoprolactinemia) are poorly characterized.
  • The real-world incidence and reversibility of impulse-control disorders at low endocrine doses, given conflicting review vs prospective data, and who is most susceptible.
  • How much of the metabolic (glucose/insulin) benefit is prolactin-mediated versus central-dopaminergic, and whether it is clinically meaningful.
  • Whether MK-677 (an oral ghrelin-mimetic secretagogue) meaningfully raises prolactin in humans to a degree warranting cabergoline, versus the injectable GHRPs where prolactin elevation is better described.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring only — no dosing guidance. (1) HEART VALVES: because the drug is an ergot with 5-HT2 activity, anyone using it chronically or at higher cumulative exposure warrants a baseline transthoracic echocardiogram and periodic follow-up echoes, plus clinical vigilance for new breathlessness, exercise intolerance, ankle swelling, or a new heart murmur — these can signal valve regurgitation. (2) PROLACTIN: check serum prolactin to confirm the problem is actually prolactin-driven before and during use, and to avoid overshooting into abnormally low prolactin; if libido/ED does not improve despite normalized prolactin on a 19-nor, the driver is likely the steroid's progesterone-receptor activity, not prolactin (a different problem cabergoline won't fix). (3) PSYCHIATRIC: actively watch for impulse-control changes — new or worsening gambling, compulsive spending, hypersexuality, or repetitive behaviors — and for mood changes or psychosis; these warrant reducing or stopping the drug and getting help. (4) CARDIOVASCULAR/AUTONOMIC: watch for orthostatic dizziness or fainting, especially early. (5) GENERAL: nausea, headache, fatigue, and rare signs of pleural/retroperitoneal fibrosis (persistent cough, breathlessness, flank/back pain, leg swelling) should prompt evaluation. None of this substitutes for a physician; valve and psychiatric harms are the two that most justify professional oversight.

Caveats

Every claim here is cited to real human or mechanistic literature, but note the following honesty flags. (1) The prolactin-lowering mechanism and efficacy are Established/Evidence-human and rock-solid. (2) The valve-disease MECHANISM (5-HT2 agonism → valve-cell proliferation) is in-vitro/mechanistic, and the receptor subtype is genuinely contested: the fen-phen paradigm (PMID 10617681) implicates 5-HT2B, but the one study that directly dissected cabergoline (PMID 21518772) found its ERK1/2 and matrix-producing effect on valve cells was actually mediated by 5-HT2A, and concluded native 5-HT2A is 'not the preferential target in VHD in vivo' — so the exact receptor is not settled, even though cabergoline clearly is agonist at both. (3) The valve-disease RISK is strongly dose- and duration-dependent: the alarming valve findings (Zanettini NEJM, ~29% clinically important regurgitation on cabergoline) come from Parkinson's patients on much higher, chronic cumulative doses. At the far lower doses used for prolactin, a large prospective echo study (Vroonen 2017) found no clinically relevant valve changes, and a multi-country case-control study (Trifiró 2012) found no increased valve risk in hyperprolactinemic patients — though low-dose case reports of valvulopathy do exist. So 'cabergoline causes valve disease' is well-supported at high cumulative exposure and not clearly true at low endocrine exposure — dose is most of the story. (4) There is ZERO published evidence on cabergoline specifically in PED/GHRP users; PED claims are extrapolation from endocrine populations. (5) Impulse-control disorders are a real psychiatric harm; note the evidence is mixed — reviews report cases even at endocrine doses (PMID 36125673), but a small prospective study found no increased ICD signal with short-term cabergoline in prolactinoma (PMID 36861582). (6) This is not medical advice and contains no dosing guidance; the sections below describe monitoring only.
