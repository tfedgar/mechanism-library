Selank

Main target: No confirmed classical receptor. Human evidence is still limited.

A synthetic 7-amino-acid peptide built from the immune fragment tuftsin, marketed in Russia as a benzodiazepine-like anti-anxiety drug — it appears to gently nudge the brain's calming GABA system and protect the body's own "feel-okay" enkephalin peptides, without the sedation or dependence of Valium-type drugs. Most mechanism data are from rats and cells; human data exist but are small and almost entirely Russian.

Human status
NOT FDA-approved and not approved anywhere outside Russia. In Russia, Selank (intranasal drops) is a registered drug that reportedly completed phase III trials for generalized anxiety disorder. Human evidence is real but thin and almost entirely from Russian groups: small comparator/RCT-style trials vs benzodiazepines (medazepam, phenazepam) showing comparable anxiolytic effect with fewer side effects (PMID 18454096; 26356395, a randomized add-on trial; 25176261), plus a 52-person resting-state fMRI study showing amygdala-connectivity changes (PMID 32342318). There are no large independent Western trials, no robust long-term safety data, and no published human pharmacokinetics of note. Bodybuilding/peptide-forum use is off-label, unregulated research-chemical use. Mechanistic claims rest mostly on rodent and in-vitro work.

At a glance

  • Anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) — the core, best-documented effect; reported comparable to benzodiazepines in small human trials but without strong sedation, and it can blunt benzodiazepine withdrawal/side effects (PMID 26356395, 25176261, 28280289)
  • Nootropic / pro-cognitive and memory-protective in rodent models, including protection against alcohol-, toxin-, and protein-synthesis-blockade-induced memory loss (PMID 31625062, 18488898, 24605419)
  • Antidepressant-like and 'antiasthenic'/anti-fatigue signals in rodents and small human reports, via monoamine and dopamine modulation (PMID 18661785)
  • No major sedation, no clear physical dependence or withdrawal reported — contrasted with benzodiazepines (the main selling point); but this rests on limited data (PMID 30255741, 26356395)
  • Reduces opioid-withdrawal signs and interacts with the opioid system in rodents (PMID 36322304, 22550852, 17415472)
  • Reported immunomodulatory / interferon-inducing and antiviral-adjuvant effects (a tuftsin-derived property) — included by the manufacturer but weakly characterized mechanistically

Mechanism, step by step

7 steps2 human-evidenced 4 preclinical 1 speculative

  1. It's tuftsin with a tail — built to last Established

    Selank is a lab-made 7-amino-acid peptide. Its first four residues ARE tuftsin, a natural immune-signaling fragment the body cuts out of an antibody (IgG). Chemists bolted on a short Pro-Gly-Pro tail so it survives longer in the body and brain instead of being chewed up in minutes. So Selank starts life borrowing tuftsin's biology, then carries it into the nervous system. PMID 30255741; 18661785 (sequence Thr-Lys-Pro-Arg-Pro-Gly-Pro); corpus MoA-gen: Selank

  2. It boosts the brain's main 'calm-down' switch (GABA-A) — like a gentle benzodiazepine Evidence-rodent / Evidence-in vitro

    GABA is the brain's primary brake pedal; GABA-A receptors are the docks GABA plugs into to quiet neurons. In rat-brain membrane binding experiments, Selank acts as a POSITIVE ALLOSTERIC MODULATOR: it doesn't replace GABA, it makes GABA's own braking signal land harder — the same general trick benzodiazepines like Valium use, though Selank binds a different (only partly overlapping) spot and can actually blunt diazepam's effect. This is the leading explanation for why it calms anxiety. PMID 30255741 (radioligand [3H]GABA binding, positive allosteric modulation); 28361410 (Selank raises inhibitory currents in rat hippocampal neurons)

  3. It changes which GABA-system genes are switched on — but maybe not directly Evidence-rodent (in vivo) / Contested at the cellular level

    Beyond the moment-to-moment receptor effect, giving Selank to rats shifts the activity of dozens of genes that run GABA signaling (receptor subunits, transporters) in the frontal cortex — a slower, longer-lasting retuning. Important honesty check: in a neuroblastoma cell line, Selank ALONE changed nothing, and only altered gene activity when combined with GABA or olanzapine. So the gene effects in a living animal may be indirect — Selank tweaking how GABA itself acts rather than flipping genes on its own. PMID 26924987 (45 genes changed in rat cortex); 28293190 (NO direct mRNA effect in IMR-32 cells alone)

  4. It protects the body's natural 'feel-okay' peptides (enkephalins) Contested (enkephalin link is inferred/associative, not a proven direct target)

    Enkephalins are your own opioid-like molecules that ease pain and stress; enzymes called enkephalinases destroy them quickly. In human blood plasma, Selank blocked those enzymes (more potently than standard inhibitors), so enkephalins stick around longer. In people with generalized anxiety, enkephalins break down too fast — Selank counteracts exactly that. This opioid-system link is supported by the fact that the opioid-blocker naloxone cancels several of Selank's effects in animals. PMID 11550013 (inhibits enkephalin-degrading enzymes, IC50 ~15 µM); 18454096; 22550852, 17415472 (naloxone-reversible effects)

  5. It nudges dopamine and serotonin — the 'mood and drive' chemicals Evidence-rodent

    Selank isn't only about calming. In rodents it also lifts activity of the brain's monoamine systems — increasing dopamine synthesis and turnover and touching serotonin pathways. This is the proposed basis for the 'energizing'/anti-fatigue and mild antidepressant-like edge users describe, which sets it apart from sedating anti-anxiety drugs. It modulates rather than floods these systems. PMID 18661785 (activation of monoaminergic systems, dopamine synthesis/turnover); 17415472 (dopaminergic modulation, naloxone-sensitive)

  6. It raises BDNF — brain 'fertilizer' for memory and resilience Evidence-rodent

    BDNF is a growth factor that helps neurons survive, connect, and form memories; low BDNF is linked to stress and cognitive decline. In rats whose memory was damaged by long-term alcohol, Selank restored BDNF levels in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex and protected memory. This is the leading explanation for its nootropic (cognition-supporting) reputation — but it's a rodent finding, not shown in humans. PMID 31625062 (Selank normalizes BDNF in hippocampus/prefrontal cortex, rats)

  7. Net effect: dialing down the fear circuit Evidence-human (small) for the brain-imaging and clinical effect; mechanism-to-effect link is inferred

    Put together, these molecular nudges add up to quieter anxiety circuitry. In a human brain-imaging study of healthy volunteers, a single dose changed the connectivity between the amygdala (the brain's fear/threat hub) and nearby temporal cortex within minutes — consistent with the behavioral calming seen in animals and small clinical trials. The body-level result reported in trials: anxiety relief similar to benzodiazepines, but with less sedation and no clear dependence. PMID 32342318 (resting-state fMRI, amygdala connectivity, n=52); 26356395 (randomized clinical trial); 28280289 (rat anxiety models)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Selank is the synthetic heptapeptide Thr-Lys-Pro-Arg-Pro-Gly-Pro (TKPRPGP) — the immune tetrapeptide tuftsin (TKPR) elongated at the C-terminus with a Pro-Gly-Pro (PGP) cap added specifically to slow enzymatic breakdown. It has NO classical high-affinity receptor of its own; it is best described as a low-potency, indirect/allosteric modulator rather than a direct agonist or antagonist. (1) Enkephalin-degrading enzymes (the most quantified target): in human blood plasma Selank dose-dependently INHIBITS enzymatic hydrolysis of [Leu]-enkephalin with an IC50 of ~15 µM, and was more potent than the reference peptidase inhibitors bacitracin and puromycin (Evidence-human/in vitro; PMID 11550013). The relevant enzymes are aminopeptidases plus neprilysin-type enkephalinases; a companion tritium-label study showed the related peptide Semax inhibits plasma enkephalinases chiefly via action on aminopeptidases, and frames Selank in the same enzymatic context (Evidence-in vitro; PMID 16637290). Functionally this means Selank is an enkephalinase INHIBITOR that raises/prolongs endogenous enkephalin tone rather than binding opioid receptors itself. (2) GABA-A system: Selank behaves as a positive allosteric modulator of [3H]GABA binding to brain membranes in a subtype-selective, concentration-dependent way, and can blunt/alter the modulatory action of diazepam and olanzapine — implying its site partially overlaps but is not identical to the benzodiazepine site (Evidence-in vitro; PMID 30255741). It is explicitly NOT a benzodiazepine-site agonist. (3) Negative binding data that define selectivity: in rat brain membranes Selank did NOT displace the D2 ligand [3H]spiperone (EC50 >100 µM) nor the delta/mu-opioid ligand [3H]DADLE (EC50 >40 µM), confirming it does not bind dopamine or opioid receptors directly (Evidence-in vitro; PMID 17415472).

Signalling cascade. The proposed cascade is INDIRECT and multi-nodal, not a single receptor event. Node 1 (opioid/enkephalin arm): by inhibiting enkephalin-degrading aminopeptidases/neprilysin, Selank slows breakdown of endogenous Leu-/Met-enkephalin, raising enkephalin tone and prolonging its half-life — in GAD/neurasthenia patients the shortened leu-enkephalin half-life normalised on treatment (Evidence-human; PMID 18454096, 11550013). Downstream opioid-receptor signalling is functionally required: the anxiolytic/anti-dopaminergic effect is BLOCKED by the non-selective opioid antagonist naloxone, even though Selank itself does not bind the opioid receptor (Evidence-rodent; PMID 17415472, 22550852). Node 2 (GABAergic arm): Selank allosterically enhances GABA-A function (PMID 30255741) and, after systemic dosing, increases GABA-A binding-site density — i.p. Selank raised [3H]SR95531 (gabazine) binding in frontal cortex by ~38% (Evidence-rodent; PMID 29787664). It does NOT change GABA-receptor-subunit mRNA in neuroblastoma IMR-32 cells (PMID 28293190), but in vivo in rat frontal cortex it shifted expression of ~45 neurotransmission genes at 1 h (22 still changed at 3 h), so the GABA effect appears to be post-translational/allosteric plus a transcriptional ripple rather than direct receptor gene induction (Evidence-rodent; PMID 26924987). Node 3 (monoaminergic/neurotrophic ripple): Selank activates brain monoaminergic systems, dopamine synthesis/turnover and tyrosine-hydroxylase modulation (Evidence-rodent; PMID 18661785) and changes BDNF content in hippocampus/prefrontal cortex (Evidence-rodent; PMID 31625062). Node 4 (immune arm, from its tuftsin lineage): Selank shifts the Th1/Th2 cytokine balance and modulates IL-6 expression in patient blood cells (Evidence-human; PMID 18577961). Net effect: a 'soft' anxiolytic profile combining enkephalin-sparing and GABA-A potentiation without direct receptor occupancy.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Clinically used as an intranasal solution (0.15% Selank drops, a registered Russian formulation); experimental animal work uses intranasal (i.n.) and intraperitoneal (i.p.) routes. Oral/peptide-in-gut delivery is not the clinical route — like other peptides it is intended to bypass first-pass gut degradation, and intranasal delivery is chosen partly for nose-to-brain access (Evidence-human/rodent; PMID 18454096, 29787664).
Absorption / bioavailability
Quantitative human bioavailability is NOT established in the indexed literature — a genuine data gap. The PGP C-terminal cap is the explicit design feature that improves metabolic stability and net systemic/CNS exposure versus parent tuftsin. Route matters mechanistically: in mice, i.p. vs i.n. dosing produced DIFFERENT central receptor footprints (i.p. raised cortical GABA-A binding +38%; i.n. instead raised hippocampal NMDA-site [3H]MK-801 binding +23%), attributed to route-dependent pharmacokinetics/biotransformation (Evidence-rodent; PMID 29787664).
Half-life
Plasma half-life is very short (minutes-scale), characteristic of small linear peptides; precise human t1/2 numbers are not given in the indexed sources. The behavioural/anxiolytic effect, however, far outlasts plasma presence — clinical anxiolysis persisted up to ~1 week after the last dose (Evidence-human; PMID 25176261), and intranasal regulatory peptides of this family show CNS effects 20-24 h post-dose, indicating effect duration is decoupled from parent-peptide plasma half-life.
Metabolism
Degraded by plasma and tissue peptidases. A tritium-label biodegradation study identified the major products of Selank (TKPRPGP) hydrolysis in blood plasma as the pentapeptide TKPRP, tripeptide TKP, and dipeptides RP and GP — i.e. sequential cleavage by aminopeptidases and prolyl/dipeptidyl peptidases. Some fragments (notably PGP-type glyprolines) are themselves bioactive, so metabolism partly generates active species rather than purely inactivating the drug (Evidence-rodent/in vitro; PMID 16637290).
Excretion
Not characterised in the indexed literature. As a small peptide it is expected to be cleared by peptidase hydrolysis to constituent amino acids/short fragments with renal handling of fragments; no quantitative renal/biliary excretion data were found — flag as unknown.
Notes
Not an ester or classic prodrug, but functionally pro-drug-like: the PGP extension is a stability cap, and degradation yields active glyproline fragments. BBB/nose-to-brain penetration is inferred from central receptor changes and behavioural effects after i.n. dosing rather than from direct human brain-concentration measurement (Evidence-rodent; PMID 29787664, 16637290).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human dose-response is essentially UNQUANTIFIED at the molecular level — the registered clinical product is a fixed-concentration intranasal solution and trials report clinical-scale outcomes, not mg-per-effect curves, so a true human dose-response relationship is not established (state explicitly). What IS documented: RODENT i.p. dosing shows a bell-shaped/non-monotonic anxiolytic and antidepressant profile. Low doses (100-300 µg/kg) gave the anxiolytic/antidepressant effect (e.g. reduced immobility in BALB/c mice), while high doses (600-2000 µg/kg) lost or reversed the antidepressant effect — a clear inverted-U (Evidence-rodent; PMID 18661785). The standard rodent 'anxiolytic dose' across the Zakusov-institute studies is 0.3 mg/kg i.p., which reduced morphine-withdrawal index by 39.6% (vs 49.3% for diazepam 2 mg/kg) and produced cognitive-stimulant/BDNF effects at the same dose (Evidence-rodent; PMID 36322304, 31625062). In the apomorphine/dopamine model the anti-dopaminergic effect spanned 0.01-10 mg/kg i.p. and was naloxone-reversible (Evidence-rodent; PMID 17415472). Enzyme-level dose-response is the cleanest number: enkephalinase inhibition IC50 ~15 µM in human plasma (Evidence-human/in vitro; PMID 11550013), and GABA-A allosteric modulation is concentration-dependent and subtype-selective in vitro (PMID 30255741). Bottom line for the audience: animal effects are real but non-linear (more is NOT better), and there is no validated human mg-to-effect curve.

How it compares

  • vs Tuftsin (parent peptide, TKPR)
    same: Selank is the direct synthetic analog — same N-terminal tuftsin core, shares immunomodulatory (Th1/Th2, phagocyte-activating) lineage
    different: Selank adds a C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro cap that confers protease resistance and a CNS anxiolytic/nootropic profile that native tuftsin (rapidly degraded, mainly an immune peptide) lacks (PMID 28745220, 16637290)
  • vs Semax (ACTH(4-10) analog, also a Myasoedov-institute regulatory peptide)
    same: Both are short intranasal Russian 'regulatory peptides' with PGP-type stabilising motifs, both inhibit enkephalinases, both have nootropic/neurotrophic (BDNF) effects and were studied with the same tritium-PK methods
    different: Semax is an ACTH/melanocortin-derived nootropic/neuroprotective agent (stroke, cognition); Selank is primarily an ANXIOLYTIC built on tuftsin with a stronger GABA-A/enkephalin anxiolytic axis (PMID 16637290, 18661785)
  • vs Benzodiazepines (medazepam, phenazepam, diazepam)
    same: Comparable anxiolytic efficacy in head-to-head human trials and rodent models; both ultimately enhance GABA-A inhibitory tone
    different: Benzodiazepines are direct positive allosteric agonists at the benzodiazepine site causing sedation, amnesia, tolerance and a withdrawal syndrome; Selank potentiates GABA-A at a DIFFERENT, non-benzodiazepine site (it can even blunt diazepam's modulation), adds enkephalinase inhibition, and in trials showed efficacy without sedation/dependence/withdrawal and even reduced benzodiazepine side effects when combined (PMID 30255741, 25176261, 26356395, 28280289)
  • vs Phenibut (β-phenyl-GABA, a GABA-B agonist sold in the same grey-market supplement space)
    same: Both are non-benzodiazepine GABA-system anxiolytics marketed to US consumers as 'dietary supplements' despite being foreign prescription/research agents
    different: Phenibut is a GABA-B receptor agonist with clear dose-dependent sedation, tolerance, dependence and a documented withdrawal syndrome (rising poison-control calls); Selank works via GABA-A allosteric modulation + enkephalinase inhibition with no demonstrated dependence/withdrawal — a much milder abuse-liability profile (PMID 34396551)

History & legal status

Selank was developed in Russia by the Institute of Molecular Genetics (Russian Academy of Sciences) in collaboration with the Zakusov Institute of Pharmacology and the Mental Health Research Center, as a stabilised synthetic analog of the endogenous immunopeptide tuftsin (sequence TKPRPGP). It completed Phase III clinical testing in Russia and is registered/approved THERE as a prescription intranasal anxiolytic (0.15% nasal drops) for generalised anxiety disorder and neurasthenia (Evidence-human; PMID 18661785, 18454096). It has NO FDA approval and is not an approved drug in the US, EU, UK, Canada or Australia — there is no Western regulatory dossier. In the US it circulates as a grey-market 'research chemical'/'dietary supplement', a status flagged as problematic in the pharmacology literature, which groups it with phenibut as a poorly-studied Russian GABAergic agent 'inexplicably sold to US consumers as dietary supplements' (PMID 34396551). It is not a scheduled/controlled substance in the US, but selling it for human consumption is not lawful as an unapproved drug. Essentially all efficacy/safety data are single-country (Russian), often open-label and small-n.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Human pharmacokinetics are essentially unquantified: no published human bioavailability, intranasal absorption fraction, plasma/CNS concentration, or terminal half-life — the effect clearly outlasts the parent peptide but the human PK that explains this is unknown.
  • Why does anxiolysis persist up to ~1 week after dosing when the peptide is cleared in minutes? The molecular basis of the long-duration effect (gene-expression changes, BDNF, active fragments, receptor density shifts) is hypothesised but not nailed down.
  • The true primary target is unsettled: enkephalinase inhibition (IC50 15 µM), GABA-A allosteric modulation, and monoaminergic/BDNF effects all occur — which dominates the human anxiolytic effect, and at what concentration each is reached in human brain, is unknown.
  • No high-quality randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled Western trial exists; all human data are small, mostly open-label, single-country (Russian) studies with potential publication/sponsor bias.
  • Long-term safety, dependence/abuse liability and chronic immune (Th1/Th2, IL-6) consequences are uncharacterised in humans — the immunomodulatory tuftsin lineage raises questions that no long-term study answers.
  • Route-dependent CNS effects (i.p. -> GABA-A up; i.n. -> NMDA-site up in mice) suggest different brain targets engage depending on delivery — the human relevance of this divergence is unknown.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a protocol, no dosing): Because human safety data are thin and the product is unapproved/grey-market, the practical signals to watch are mostly clinical rather than lab-based. (1) Mental-state signs: watch for paradoxical activation, restlessness, irritability or sleep disruption (the documented psychostimulant/monoaminergic component and inverted-U dose-response mean 'more' can flip the effect) — and for failure of anxiety to improve, which should prompt stopping rather than escalating. (2) Blood pressure: the only documented cardiovascular signal is a transient drop in BP / change in cerebral blood flow at high parenteral animal doses; anyone with hypotension or on antihypertensives should monitor BP and watch for lightheadedness. (3) Nasal/local tolerance: with intranasal use, watch for nasal irritation, congestion or epistaxis. (4) Immune signals: given the tuftsin-derived Th1/Th2 and IL-6 effects, watch for any unusual inflammatory or infection pattern; if extensive baseline and periodic CBC with differential is reasonable, though no specific abnormality is established. (5) Interaction watch: effects run through naloxone-sensitive opioid signalling and through GABA-A — be alert to additive sedation/CNS effects if combined with other GABAergic agents (benzodiazepines, phenibut, alcohol) or opioids, and to loss of effect with opioid antagonists. (6) Source/identity risk: because it is a research-chemical-market peptide, purity/identity verification (third-party analysis) is the single most important 'monitoring' step — contamination and mislabeling are realistic harms that no blood test will catch. Anyone using it for a clinical anxiety condition should be under a clinician's care rather than self-treating.

Caveats

SOURCE/COUNTRY BIAS: Essentially all Selank research comes from a small cluster of Russian institutions (notably the Institute of Molecular Genetics RAS and the Mental Health Research Center), often the same authors/developers (Myasoedov, Andreeva, Kost, et al.). There is almost no independent Western replication — a major limitation for trusting both efficacy and mechanism. HUMAN-DATA GAP: Mechanism is built largely on rodent and in-vitro work; human trials are small, partly non-blinded, and some only in Russian-language journals. No large modern RCTs, no robust long-term safety data, no real human PK. CONFLICTING EVIDENCE on the GABA story: the cleanest cell-line experiment (PMID 28293190) found NO direct effect of Selank alone on GABA-system gene expression — the GABA-A 'benzodiazepine-like' claim rests mainly on radioligand binding and electrophysiology, not a clean identified binding site, and the exact target site is unknown. MULTI-TARGET / FUZZY MECHANISM: Selank has no single proven receptor; effects are spread across GABA, opioid/enkephalin, monoamine, BDNF, and immune/interferon systems, so 'mechanism' is a network of plausible nudges, not one clean lock-and-key. ROUTE MATTERS: intranasal vs injected Selank produced different receptor effects in mice (GABA vs NMDA binding shifts) (PMID 29787664) — dose and route likely change what it does. REGULATORY/QUALITY: not approved outside Russia; material sold to consumers is unregulated research-chemical grade with no purity/identity guarantees. NOT MEDICAL ADVICE — this explains how the compound is thought to work, it does not establish that it is safe or effective for any person.
