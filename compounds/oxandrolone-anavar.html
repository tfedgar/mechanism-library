Oxandrolone

A mild, orally active "designer" version of DHT — it switches on the muscle-building androgen receptor without converting to estrogen, but the chemistry that lets it survive being swallowed is the same chemistry that strains your liver and crushes your good cholesterol.

Human / approved 50 citations
Class
Synthetic anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS); a 17alpha-alkylated, DHT-derived (5alpha-reduced) oral androgen
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR) — a ligand-activated, DNA-binding transcription factor in the nuclear-receptor family (ChEMBL target CHEMBL1871)
Human status
FDA-approved drug (Schedule III controlled substance in the US), max clinical phase 4 (ChEMBL/DailyMed). Approved/used in humans for weight gain and to offset protein catabolism after severe trauma, surgery, infection, and in conditions causing involuntary weight loss; extensively studied in severe burns (multiple RCTs and systematic reviews/meta-analyses, e.g. PMID 11303139, 26506070, 31504621, 41023744), HIV-associated wasting (PMID 12405647), and used off-label in Turner syndrome and constitutional growth delay. The mechanistic claims here rest on direct human evidence (muscle protein synthesis, lipid, HPG, hepatic outcomes), not just rodent work. Non-medical / performance-enhancement use is off-label and not an approved indication.

At a glance

  • Increases skeletal muscle protein synthesis and lean body mass via the androgen receptor (human RCT evidence; PMID 10443664, 11303139)
  • Does NOT aromatize to estrogen — low water retention, no estrogenic gyno from the drug itself (it is DHT-derived; PMID 15025546)
  • Already 5alpha-reduced (DHT-based), so it is not a substrate for further 5alpha-reductase conversion — finasteride-type drugs do not blunt it
  • High oral bioavailability because the 17alpha-methyl group resists hepatic first-pass breakdown (PMID 15025546)
  • Lowers HDL ('good') cholesterol and alters hepatic lipid/ketone metabolism (PMID 18797410)
  • Can cause liver enzyme elevations / hepatotoxicity, intrinsic to all 17alpha-alkylated orals though oxandrolone is comparatively mild (PMID 30545697)
  • Suppresses the HPG axis and endogenous testosterone production despite its 'mild' reputation (PMID 8319371)
  • Favorable myotrophic-to-androgenic ratio — relatively more anabolic than virilizing vs testosterone, which is why it was historically used in women and children (PMID 27535042, 15025546)
  • Not meaningfully progestogenic — its effects run through the androgen receptor, not the progesterone receptor

Mechanism, step by step

  1. The 17alpha-methyl group lets it survive being swallowed Established

    Plain testosterone taken by mouth is almost entirely destroyed by the liver before it reaches the bloodstream (first-pass metabolism). Oxandrolone has a methyl group bolted onto the 17-alpha position, which blocks that breakdown — so it is one of the few androgens that actually works as an oral pill. This same 17alpha-alkylation is also the feature that makes it hard on the liver. PMID 15025546 (Orr & Fiatarone Singh, Drugs 2004); PMID 16932191 (Plast Reconstr Surg DATA review 2006)

  2. It is a DHT relative that cannot turn into estrogen Established

    Oxandrolone is built from dihydrotestosterone (DHT), the already-5alpha-reduced androgen, and its A-ring is chemically altered (an oxygen replaces a carbon). Because of this structure the aromatase enzyme cannot act on it, so it does not convert to estrogen. That is why classic estrogenic side effects like water retention and gynecomastia are not expected from oxandrolone itself. PMID 15025546 (Orr & Fiatarone Singh, Drugs 2004); PMID 27535042 (Wu & Kovac, Curr Urol Rep 2016)

  3. It binds the androgen receptor and acts as a genomic switch Established

    Inside the cell oxandrolone binds the androgen receptor, a protein that normally waits for testosterone or DHT. Once bound, the receptor changes shape, releases its chaperone proteins, moves into the nucleus, pairs up, and docks onto specific DNA sequences (androgen response elements). There it turns target genes on or off — the androgen receptor literally works as a hormone-controlled gene switch. This is the same core machinery testosterone uses. ChEMBL CHEMBL1200436 / CHEMBL1871 (AR agonist, DailyMed); PMID 37717994 (Vitamins and Hormones, 'The androgen receptor' 2023)

  4. Switched-on genes ramp up muscle protein synthesis Evidence-human

    In skeletal muscle, the genes the androgen receptor activates drive the cell to build more contractile protein (including myosin) and shift the body into net protein gain. In controlled human studies oxandrolone increased net muscle protein synthesis and the efficiency with which muscle reuses amino acids — this is the direct anabolic effect, not just appetite or water. PMID 10443664 (Sheffield-Moore, JCEM 1999); PMID 10662711 (Sheffield-Moore, AJP-Endo 2000); PMID 16263771 (Balagopal, AJP-Endo 2006, myosin/gene expression)

  5. Its androgen signaling shuts down your own testosterone (HPG suppression) Evidence-human

    The brain reads oxandrolone as an androgen, so the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal feedback loop dials down its own signals (LH (FSH was unchanged in that study)) and the testes make less testosterone. In boys given oxandrolone, gonadotropin and testosterone responses were blunted while on treatment. So even a 'mild' oral androgen still suppresses natural production — it is not free of the shutdown that other steroids cause. PMID 8319371 (Malhotra, Clin Endocrinol 1993, GH and gonadal axes); PMID 33121403 (Abed, rat fertility model — Evidence-rodent supporting)

  6. It crushes HDL and reshapes liver lipid handling Evidence-human

    Oral 17alpha-alkylated androgens like oxandrolone strongly lower HDL ('good') cholesterol and change how the liver processes fats. In men, oxandrolone increased hepatic ketogenesis and lowered HDL — a marker of the liver's altered lipid metabolism. This unfavorable cholesterol shift is one of the most consistent and clinically meaningful effects of the drug. PMID 18797410 (Vega, J Investig Med 2008, hepatic ketogenesis + HDL); PMID 15025546 (Orr & Fiatarone Singh review)

  7. The same liver-stable chemistry strains the liver Evidence-human

    Because the 17alpha-methyl group makes oxandrolone resist liver breakdown, the liver is exposed to it heavily and can show stress — most commonly a rise in liver enzymes (transaminitis). In burn patients on oxandrolone, transaminase elevations were a measurable, dose-related event. Oxandrolone is considered milder than many oral steroids here, but the liver-burden mechanism is real and intrinsic to all 17alpha-alkylated orals. PMID 30545697 (Kiracofe, Burns 2019, oxandrolone-induced transaminitis); PMID 16932191 (DATA review)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Target = androgen receptor (AR; ChEMBL target CHEMBL1871). Oxandrolone (ChEMBL1200436) is a DIRECT AR AGONIST, max clinical phase 4 (ChEMBL mechanism mec_id 1327, action_type AGONIST, direct_interaction=true, disease_efficacy=true; refs DailyMed setid f622616e-4c11-4149-bf00-5ea5ce97800b). It is a 2-oxa, 17a-methyl analog of DHT (dihydrotestosterone), so it is a LOW-AFFINITY AR ligand relative to DHT/mibolerone/methyltrienolone: PMID 10077001 (Mol Endocrinol 1999) classes oxandrolone with fluoxymesterone as a 'lower affinity anabolic steroid' that requires markedly higher concentrations than DHT/T (which act at 0.1-1 nM) to induce the AR N-terminal/C-terminal (N/C) interaction characteristic of agonists. Non-aromatizable (cannot be converted to estradiol). The classic anabolic:androgenic dissociation rationale (5a-reductase concentration differences between muscle and androgenic tissue) is laid out in PMID 19861292. NOTE: I did NOT find a corpus/DB source giving a numeric Ki/RBA for oxandrolone specifically — the relative-affinity claim must be stated qualitatively (Evidence-in vitro), not with an invented number.

Signalling cascade. Genomic AR pathway: cytoplasmic AR–HSP complex → ligand binding → HSP dissociation, AR N/C intramolecular interaction (PMID 10077001) → nuclear translocation → dimerization → binding androgen-response elements (AREs) → coactivator recruitment → transcription of anabolic genes. PMID 18801930 (Mol Endocrinol 2008) contrasts steroidal androgen vs SARM genomic AND nongenomic (Src/MAPK) signaling. Muscle-specific transcriptional response to oxandrolone in humans documented in burned children: PMID 12616128 (Ann Surg 2003, gene-expression patterns in skeletal muscle) and PMID 12796576 (Wolf 2003, gene-expression changes + net protein balance). Broader AR biology / anabolic-androgenic dissociation mechanism review: PMID 41898445 (Int J Mol Sci) and PMID 37717994 (Vitamins & Hormones, 'The androgen receptor').

Hepatic. Major human metabolite 17-epi-oxandrolone (PMID 33477515, UHPLC-MS/MS urine screen). Comparative phase I/II metabolism characterized in horse (equine liver microsomes/S9): PMID 34378336. Hepatic effects on lipase systems: PMID 1128607. Rodent hepatic ultrastructure: PMID 1127354.

Renal/urinary (parent + 17-epi-oxandrolone); primary human data in PMID 4729902 (Karim 1973). Urinary detection windows: PMID 33477515, PMID 35947101.
Notes
Non-esterified, non-prodrug (active as administered), non-aromatizable, 2-oxa-DHT backbone. Possible drug-interaction signal: oxandrolone may suppress SHBG synthesis (anecdotal/clinical, MESO-Rx + Wasserman 2007 referenced in corpus) — flag as Evidence-human/Contested. No CYP-specific clearance source located this run; metabolism described at the metabolite level, not enzyme level — a genuine gap to label.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

HUMAN dose-response for cosmetic/PED hypertrophy is essentially ABSENT from the literature (Evidence gap — state explicitly). Therapeutic dose-response exists in three settings: (1) BURNS — fixed ~10 mg twice daily (20 mg/day) increased net muscle protein balance, lean body mass and altered muscle gene expression vs placebo (PMID 12796576 Wolf 2003; PMID 14636753 Demling, LBM gain maintained after discontinuation; PMID 11164665 weight restoration not age-dependent). Pediatric burns long-term ~0.1 mg/kg/day combined with propranolol (PMID 30048322 Herndon 2018 yearlong; PMID 29040226; PMID 17130281 + exercise). (2) TURNER SYNDROME — dose-ranging RCT of 0.03 vs 0.06 mg/kg/day on top of GH (PMID 20061421 Menke 2010, JCEM; PMID 31862699 Gault 2021 UK RCT final height) showed modest height gain that was DOSE-related but higher dose increased virilization/adverse signals. (3) HIV WASTING — 20 mg/day and higher in PMID 16540931 (Grunfeld 2006) produced dose-related LBM/weight gain but also dose-related HDL suppression and transaminitis. Across settings the pattern is: anabolic effect rises with dose but hepatic/lipid toxicity rises in parallel. Species note: rodent data (e.g. PMID 33121403 infertility, PMID 31018913 anti-inflammatory/organ-protective in burn mice) are mechanistic, not directly dose-translatable to human hypertrophy.

How it compares

  • vs Oxymetholone (Anadrol)
    same: Both oral 17a-alkylated AAS used historically for wasting/anemia; both hepatotoxic; both AR agonists (oxymetholone ChEMBL1200585, AGONIST, phase 4)
    different: Oxymetholone is far more androgenic/estrogenically-active (water retention, gynecomastia) and more hepatotoxic; oxandrolone is non-aromatizable with a milder side-effect profile. NOTE: the corpus's top 'HIV wasting' RCTs (PMID 12646793, 12815555-RETRACTED) are OXYMETHOLONE, not oxandrolone — must not be conflated.
  • vs Testosterone / DHT
    same: All AR agonists driving the same genomic anabolic program
    different: Oxandrolone is a low-affinity 2-oxa-DHT analog that is orally active, non-aromatizable, and 17a-alkylated (hepatotoxic); T/DHT bind AR with high affinity (0.1-1 nM, PMID 10077001), T aromatizes to estradiol, native T is not orally bioavailable. Head-to-head vs GH in burns: PMID 10323605.
  • vs Nandrolone (19-nortestosterone)
    same: Both AAS with favorable anabolic:androgenic dissociation used clinically for wasting
    different: Nandrolone is injectable (esterified), 19-nor not 17a-alkyl, undergoes 5a-reduction to a LESS potent metabolite in androgenic tissue (basis of its dissociation, PMID 19861292); oxandrolone is oral, DHT-derived, hepatotoxic. Joint review: PMID 27535042.
  • vs Stanozolol
    same: Both oral 17a-alkylated, non-aromatizable DHT-class AAS with hepatotoxic potential and high anabolic:androgenic ratio
    different: Distinct A-ring chemistry (stanozolol = pyrazole-fused; oxandrolone = 2-oxa). Spectroscopically contrasted in PMID 29278383.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Anadrol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Both are oral 17-alpha-alkylated DHT-derived AAS that lower HDL and both carry hepatotoxic potential (oxandrolone comparatively mild). Combined, they add first-pass hepatic burden and compound the HDL-crash/atherogenic lipid shift - the same class mechanism as with harsher orals, just with a milder partner. The additive lipid and liver signal still applies. Anadrol monograph side_effect_mechanisms (HDL crash via hepatic lipase; 17aa hepatotoxicity); oxandrolone monograph key_effects (lowers HDL, hepatotoxicity though comparatively mild); MoA hepatic_lipase_lipids + hepatotoxicity_17aa_orals Watch: Full lipid panel (HDL, LDL/ApoB) for the additive crash; ALT/AST/GGT and bilirubin for liver strain. Blood pressure as a CV-risk driver.
  • Masteron compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Oxandrolone and Masteron are both non-aromatizing, DHT-type (already 5-alpha-reduced) androgens that lower HDL and raise LDL. Because neither generates HDL-protective estrogen, their dyslipidemic effects add together, producing a more atherogenic lipid profile than either alone. This is a lipid/cardiovascular compounding, independent of oxandrolone's separate (comparatively mild) 17a-alkyl hepatic concern. Masteron monograph side_effect_mechanisms (HDL); Oxandrolone monograph (non-aromatizing, lowers HDL, already 5a-reduced) Watch: HDL-C, LDL-C and triglycerides (additive atherogenic shift); blood pressure; periodic liver enzymes given oxandrolone's mild hepatotoxicity.

History &amp; legal status

"Developed by Searle (G.D. Searle/Pfizer) in the early 1960s; foundational human disposition study published 1973 (PMID 4729902). Early therapeutic use for growth retardation (PMID 14299401, 1965) and Turner-syndrome short stature. Withdrawn from the US market in 1989, then reintroduced by BTG/Savient as Oxandrin (FDA-approved) in 1995 for adjunctive therapy to promote weight gain after weight loss (surgery, trauma, infection), to offset corticosteroid-induced catabolism, and for relief of bone pain in osteoporosis; also studied/used off-label in HIV wasting, severe burns, alcoholic hepatitis, Duchenne, Fanconi anemia (PMID 24019220), and constitutional growth delay. US DEA Schedule III controlled substance (Anabolic Steroids Control Act 1990); WADA-prohibited in sport at all times. Widely used illicitly under the brand name Anavar. Regulatory/clinical status synthesized in review PMID 35832568 (2022 reappraisal in burns) and PMID 15025546 (Orr & Fiatarone Singh, Drugs 2004). Synthesizer should verify exact FDA approval-year wording against a primary regulatory source before publishing the 1989/1995 dates."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No human dose-response data exist for cosmetic/physique (PED) hypertrophy — all human dosing evidence is therapeutic (burns, Turner, HIV); the effect size at bodybuilding doses and the dose at which hepatotoxicity becomes dangerous are uncharacterized.
  • Quantitative AR binding affinity (Ki/RBA) for oxandrolone is not well established in the located literature — it is described only qualitatively as 'low affinity' (PMID 10077001); the molecular basis of its anabolic:androgenic dissociation is still debated (PMID 41898445).
  • The enzymatic clearance route (specific CYP/UGT isoforms) is not defined in human data located this run — metabolism is described at the metabolite level (17-epi-oxandrolone) rather than enzyme level.
  • Long-term cardiovascular outcome data (not just surrogate HDL changes) in chronic non-medical users are absent.
  • Reversibility/time-course of HPG-axis and fertility suppression after oxandrolone specifically (vs class data) is not directly quantified in humans.
  • Whether the SHBG-suppression / free-androgen interaction is clinically meaningful at typical doses remains Contested (primarily forum- and small-study-derived).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (no dosing guidance). LIVER: baseline + periodic ALT/AST, bilirubin, ALP; transaminitis is the most common documented oxandrolone lab abnormality (PMID 30545697) — watch for RUQ pain, jaundice, dark urine. LIPIDS: fasting lipid panel with attention to HDL-C (expect suppression) and total/LDL; oxandrolone raises hepatic lipase activity (PMID 1128607) — track as a cardiovascular surrogate. ENDOCRINE: total + free testosterone, LH, FSH, SHBG, estradiol to detect HPG-axis suppression and altered free-hormone handling; in users wanting fertility, a semen analysis. HEMATOLOGIC/HEMOSTATIC: hematocrit/hemoglobin (erythrocytosis is a class effect) and awareness of altered hemostasis (PMID 16432848). BLOOD PRESSURE and weight (fluid/anabolic gain). PEDIATRIC/FEMALE users: monitor for virilization (voice change, hirsutism, clitoromegaly) and, in children, bone-age/growth-velocity advancement (PMID 20061421, 24776783). Any signs of liver dysfunction, marked HDL drop, or virilization are reasons to seek medical review and reassess use."

Caveats

Certainty labels: the AR-agonist genomic mechanism, oral activity via 17alpha-alkylation, and non-aromatizing DHT-derived chemistry are Established. The anabolic muscle effect, HDL-lowering/hepatic lipid shift, liver-enzyme elevations, and HPG suppression are Evidence-human (clinical studies, though several are small or in special populations like burns/boys rather than healthy athletes). Magnitude caveats: oxandrolone's reputation as 'mild' is relative — it has a favorable anabolic-to-androgenic ratio and is comparatively liver-sparing among oral 17aa steroids, but it STILL suppresses endogenous testosterone and STILL lowers HDL, so 'mild' does not mean 'side-effect-free.' Much of the strongest mechanistic human data comes from catabolic/illness populations (burns, HIV, elderly), where baseline physiology differs from a healthy trained lifter; effect sizes in healthy young athletes are less rigorously quantified. This is mechanistic education only — no dosing, cycling, or how-to guidance is provided, and androgen use carries cardiovascular, hepatic, fertility, and (in women) virilization risks.
