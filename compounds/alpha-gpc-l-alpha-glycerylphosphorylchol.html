Alpha-GPC

A choline "delivery truck" that reliably raises blood choline and, in rodents, brain acetylcholine — with small, inconsistent human signals on explosive power and focus that fall well short of the marketing.

Human / approved 11 citations
Class
Cholinergic precursor / choline-containing phospholipid; nootropic and putative ergogenic aid (prescription drug for cognitive impairment/dementia in parts of Europe/Asia; OTC dietary supplement in the US)
Primary target
No receptor of its own
Human status
Dual status. In much of Europe and Asia (e.g., Italy as Gliatilin, South Korea as choline alfoscerate) it is an approved PRESCRIPTION drug for cognitive impairment/dementia, with marketing authorization dating to ~1985. In the US it is sold as an OTC dietary supplement and a common pre-workout/nootropic ingredient — not FDA-approved to treat any disease. Human evidence: (1) choline delivery is proven in people (Marcus 2017, PMID 29042830); (2) cognition — a meta-analysis (Sagaro 2023, PMID 36683513: cognition MD 1.72 with donepezil; MD 3.50 vs placebo/comparator) and an MCI RCT (Jeon 2024, PMID 39300341: ADAS-cog improved by 2.34 points vs placebo over 12 weeks) show modest symptomatic benefit, strongest as an add-on to donepezil, in impaired older adults — NOT demonstrated in healthy young people; (3) sports performance — a handful of small RCTs (Bellar 2015 PMID 26582972, n=13; Marcus 2017 PMID 29042830, n=48; Gage 2021 PMID 34769925, n=14, multi-ingredient) show inconsistent acute effects on explosive power/rate of force development, several run or funded within the supplement industry, with dedicated trials still emerging (NCT06690619, Lindenwood University / NNB Nutrition, completed June 2024, results not yet posted; NCT07267845, Nanjing Nutrabuilding / Atlantia, recruiting, ~2026 completion).

At a glance

  • Reliably raises blood free choline in humans (well-established delivery effect)
  • Raises brain acetylcholine and evoked ACh release in rodents (mechanistic backbone; not directly measured in human brain)
  • Small, inconsistent acute boosts to explosive lower-body force / jump power in some short RCTs; upper-body and isometric strength usually not significantly changed
  • Modest cognitive benefit as an add-on (esp. with donepezil) in dementia/MCI populations; unproven for cognition in healthy young users
  • Reported transient acute growth-hormone rise around exercise (contested, small, unclear relevance; no dedicated human RCT published yet)
  • Generally well tolerated in trials; main honest uncertainty is a theoretical long-term cardiovascular/TMAO question that human data have NOT confirmed

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Oral dose is cleaved in the gut to free choline + glycerophosphate Established

    Alpha-GPC is not absorbed intact as a signaling molecule. In gut mucosal cells it is split into free choline and glycerophosphate. Because it is ~41% choline by weight and water-soluble, it is an efficient way to raise circulating choline. Lopez 1991 PMID 1662399; Che 2025 review PMID 40036805; Li 2025 review PMID 40556032

  2. Blood free choline rises measurably in humans Evidence-human

    In a human RCT, a single oral dose raised serum free choline by ~132% (500 mg) and ~59% (250 mg) versus placebo within 1-2 hours. This confirms the delivery step actually happens in people, not just rodents. Marcus 2017 PMID 29042830

  3. Choline reaches the brain and can feed acetylcholine synthesis Established

    Choline is the rate-limiting precursor for acetylcholine and crosses the blood-brain barrier. Alpha-GPC's choline load is proposed to enlarge the neuronal precursor pool available to choline acetyltransferase (ChAT). BBB transport of choline is well established; that supplemental alpha-GPC actually raises the human brain precursor pool is inferred, and the direct 'more ACh made' evidence is strongest in rodents. Che 2025 review PMID 40036805; Traini 2013 review PMID 24156263; Li 2025 review PMID 40556032

  4. Brain acetylcholine levels and evoked release increase (rodent) Evidence-rodent

    In rats, oral alpha-GPC partially reversed scopolamine-induced drops in acetylcholine in hippocampus and cortex (not striatum) and increased potassium-evoked ACh release from hippocampal slices; chronic dosing also partially restored M1 muscarinic receptor expression in aged rat hippocampus. This is the mechanistic backbone — but it is animal/ex-vivo data, not measured directly in human brain. Lopez 1991 PMID 1662399; Amenta 1994 PMID 7845062

  5. Enhanced cholinergic tone at the neuromuscular junction is the proposed 'power' mechanism Speculative

    The pre-workout claim is that more available acetylcholine sharpens central drive and neuromuscular transmission, improving motor-unit recruitment and rate of force development. This is a plausible bridge from step 4 to force output, but it is inferred, not directly demonstrated in human muscle. Bellar 2015 PMID 26582972; Gage 2021 PMID 34769925

  6. Acute growth-hormone spike is reported but small and transient Contested

    Reviews report a short-lived rise in growth hormone after a large acute dose around exercise. GH is often invoked to explain 'power' effects, but the rise is transient, its performance relevance is unclear, and it is not consistently reproduced; no dedicated, adequately powered human RCT isolating this effect is yet published. Che 2025 review PMID 40036805; NCT06690619 (Alpha-GPC, GH & muscle power RCT, completed 2024, results not yet posted)

  7. Glycerophosphate and phosphatidylcholine synthesis (secondary/structural role) Evidence-rodent

    The other half of the molecule (glycerophosphate) plus choline feed phosphatidylcholine, a major cell-membrane phospholipid, via the CDP-choline (Kennedy) pathway. This membrane-precursor role is invoked for the long-term neuroprotective/cognitive claims rather than the acute focus effect, and rests mainly on rodent and mechanistic data. Traini 2013 review PMID 24156263; Che 2025 review PMID 40036805

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Alpha-GPC has no intrinsic receptor pharmacology — it is a substrate/precursor, not a ligand. Its activity is entirely mediated by the free choline (and glycerophosphate) released after cleavage. Choline then supports two pathways: (1) acetylcholine synthesis via choline acetyltransferase (ChAT) in cholinergic neurons, and (2) phosphatidylcholine synthesis via the CDP-choline (Kennedy) pathway for membranes.

Signalling cascade. Oral alpha-GPC -> gut mucosal cleavage -> free choline + glycerophosphate -> systemic choline rise (human RCT: +132% at 500 mg) -> BBB choline transport -> enlarged neuronal choline pool (inferred in humans) -> increased ACh synthesis and, in rodents, increased evoked ACh release in hippocampus/cortex -> enhanced cholinergic neurotransmission -> proposed downstream effects on attention (nootropic) and neuromuscular drive/rate of force development (ergogenic).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsule/powder as supplement; also available as an injectable/oral prescription drug for cognitive impairment in some countries).
Absorption / bioavailability
High water solubility and ~41% choline content make it an efficient oral choline source; absorbed as free choline after gut cleavage rather than intact. Human data confirm a rapid, dose-dependent rise in serum free choline (peaking ~1-2 h).
Half-life
Behavioral/pharmacodynamic effects in the rodent amnesia model were long-lasting (up to ~30 h), attributed to its pharmacokinetics; precise human plasma half-life of the intact molecule is not well characterized because it is metabolized to choline.
Metabolism
Cleaved presystemically in gut mucosa to free choline + glycerophosphate. Choline is used for ACh and phosphatidylcholine synthesis; a fraction is oxidized to betaine (methyl donor) and can be metabolized by gut microbiota/liver toward TMA/TMAO.
Excretion
As choline metabolites (betaine, TMAO) renally; glycerophosphate enters normal phospholipid/glycerol metabolism.
Notes
Because the active species is choline, PK is effectively choline PK downstream of a reliable delivery vehicle.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Two distinct regimens appear in the literature; these are described for context only and are NOT a protocol. ACUTE / ergogenic: single or few-day dosing in the low-hundreds-of-mg range in sports RCTs; Marcus 2017 found the 250 mg arm (not 500 mg) gave the largest jump-power improvement, i.e. no clean dose-response and possibly a plateau/inverted signal, while the 500 mg arm showed significantly depressed TSH. Bellar 2015 used 600 mg/day for 6 days and saw a lower-body force effect. NCT06690619 tested 350 mg vs 700 mg vs placebo. CHRONIC / cognitive: pharmaceutical dementia and MCI RCTs use higher daily doses for weeks to months (Jeon 2024 used 600 mg/day x 12 weeks). Overall the human dose-response is not well defined and is not monotonic in the performance data — a reason to be skeptical of precise 'optimal dose' marketing claims. NO dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs CDP-choline (citicoline)
    same: Both are choline-donor nootropics that raise systemic choline and feed ACh + membrane phospholipid synthesis; both used for cognition.
    different: Citicoline also yields cytidine/uridine (nucleotide precursors) and has a larger cognitive/stroke trial base. Alpha-GPC has higher choline-by-weight (~41%) and the specific pre-workout/power reputation. Head-to-head performance data are lacking.
  • vs Choline bitartrate / plain choline salts
    same: All raise blood choline and can support ACh synthesis.
    different: Alpha-GPC delivers more choline per gram and is regarded as more efficient at raising the brain-available pool; cheaper choline salts are less bioavailable to brain and more likely to cause fishy body odor / GI effects at high doses.
  • vs Huperzine A / donepezil (acetylcholinesterase inhibitors)
    same: Both aim to raise cholinergic tone.
    different: AChE inhibitors BLOCK acetylcholine breakdown (increase ACh residence time) rather than supplying precursor. Alpha-GPC is often combined WITH donepezil in dementia trials — precursor + reduced breakdown — and the add-on combination is where its cognitive evidence is strongest (Sagaro 2023).
  • vs Caffeine (as a pre-workout comparator)
    same: Both marketed for focus + performance.
    different: In Marcus 2017 the caffeine arm was a direct comparator; caffeine has a far larger, more consistent ergogenic/CNS evidence base. Alpha-GPC's acute performance effect is smaller and less reliable than caffeine's.

History & legal status

Alpha-GPC (choline alfoscerate) was developed as a cognition enhancer and received marketing authorization around 1985. It became a prescription medicine for cognitive impairment/dementia and acute cerebrovascular disease in several European and Asian countries (e.g., Italy as Gliatilin; widely prescribed in South Korea). ChEMBL lists it as CHEMBL6067974 with ATC code N07AX02 (other CNS/psychoanaleptic drugs) and a maximum clinical phase of 3. Approval status is inconsistent across countries and its dementia efficacy remains debated. In the United States it was never approved as a drug; it is marketed as a dietary supplement and is one of the most common choline sources in nootropic and pre-workout formulations. Regulatory attention persists in Korea over both the breadth of its approved cognitive indications and the theoretical stroke-risk question.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether the small acute power/force signals are real and reproducible in adequately powered, industry-independent RCTs, or artifacts of small samples and selective endpoints.
  • Whether alpha-GPC does anything for cognition or focus in HEALTHY young adults (the actual pre-workout audience) — current cognitive evidence is in impaired/older populations.
  • The true magnitude, mechanism, and performance relevance (if any) of the acute growth-hormone response — no dedicated human RCT results are yet published.
  • Whether chronic high-dose use raises TMAO enough to affect cardiovascular/stroke risk in humans — the signal is theoretical and a large cohort points the opposite way.
  • Optimal dose and whether the dose-response is non-monotonic (Marcus 2017 hinted the lower dose worked better for jump power).
  • Direct measurement of brain acetylcholine changes in living humans after supplementation.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

General harm-reduction, not a protocol. Watch for cholinergic side effects — headache, nausea/GI upset, dizziness, sweating, low mood/irritability — and reduce or stop if they appear. Be cautious combining with acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, huperzine A) or other cholinergics, as effects can stack. People with a history of depression, thyroid disease (given the acute TSH signal seen in one trial), or cardiovascular disease should discuss use with a clinician and be aware that long-term high-dose choline loading carries an unresolved (currently unconfirmed) TMAO/cardiovascular question. Anyone using a prescription version for a cognitive condition should be monitored by the prescribing physician rather than self-managing. As with all supplements, third-party-tested products reduce the risk of mislabeled dose or contaminants. This entry does not provide dosing instructions.

Caveats

The 'focus + power' pre-workout story is mechanistically plausible but thinly evidenced in humans. The delivery step (blood choline up) is solid; the downstream 'more brain acetylcholine -> more force' chain is proven mainly in rodents and inferred in people. Sports RCTs are small (n=13-48), short (6-7 days or single-dose), often crossover with modest effect sizes, and several have supplement-industry involvement — publication and funding bias are real concerns. Within those trials the wins are selective: lower-body/jump peak force or velocity sometimes improves while upper-body and isometric strength do not, a red flag for cherry-picked endpoints. Cognitive benefits are in impaired/older populations, not healthy young lifters; do not generalize. The growth-hormone 'anabolic' framing is oversold: any rise is small and transient and no dedicated human RCT isolating it is yet published. One theoretical safety question — choline can feed hepatic/gut production of TMAO, a molecule associated in other contexts with atherosclerosis — remains UNCONFIRMED for alpha-GPC in humans; notably a large Korean cohort (Kim 2025, PMID 40155153, n=508,107 MCI patients) found alpha-GPC users had LOWER risk of conversion to Alzheimer's (HR 0.899) and vascular dementia (HR 0.832), with reduced stroke risk specifically among users who did not progress to dementia — cutting against the harm hypothesis, though as an observational study it is subject to confounding by indication.
