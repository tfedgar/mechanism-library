Fisetin

A plant flavonoid that may act as a "senolytic" — clearing worn-out zombie cells in mice, but with the human payoff still unproven.

Rodent / preclinical 19 citations
Class
Dietary flavonol (polyphenol); investigational senotherapeutic (senolytic / senomorphic)
Primary target
No single clean receptor
Human status
INVESTIGATIONAL / SUPPLEMENT — not an approved drug for any aging indication. Sold over-the-counter as a flavonoid supplement. The strongest efficacy data (senolysis, lifespan extension) are in mice and cell culture, and the lifespan claim already FAILED to replicate in the gold-standard NIA Interventions Testing Program (PMID 38041783). Multiple human trials are underway or completed (ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT03675724/AFFIRM-LITE-type frailty studies, NCT05595499 and NCT06113016 in breast cancer survivors, NCT06399809 in peripheral arterial disease, NCT04733534 in childhood-cancer-survivor frailty, NCT05593588 senolytics in immunodeficiency-related ILD, NCT04313634 completed in healthy adults, NCT05416515 carpal tunnel) — but these are predominantly Phase 1/2, focused on safety, pharmacokinetics, and senescence biomarkers, NOT on proven clinical aging benefit. One published controlled human trial (Gulf War Illness, PMID 33802381) found fisetin no better than placebo. Two small 2026 RCTs in obese men paired with exercise reported anti-inflammatory/metabolic improvements (PMID 42218768, PMID 41683255), but these test exercise+supplement combinations, are small, and are not aging-outcome trials. Bottom line for the audience: the senolytic story is real science, but the human payoff is still a hypothesis being tested — do not present mouse lifespan data as if it applies to people.

At a glance

  • Senolytic: selectively triggers apoptosis in senescent ('zombie') cells in vitro and in mice (Evidence-rodent/in vitro)
  • Reduces senescence markers across multiple tissues after intermittent dosing in mice (Evidence-rodent)
  • Anti-inflammatory: suppresses NF-kB and lowers IL-6 / TNF-alpha in lab and some human exercise-supplement data (Evidence-rodent; weak Evidence-human)
  • Antioxidant: raises SOD1/catalase, lowers reactive oxygen species (Evidence-rodent/in vitro)
  • Extended median and maximum lifespan in aged/progeroid mice in the original report (Evidence-rodent) — NOT replicated in the rigorous NIA ITP program (Contested)
  • Anti-cancer signaling in cell/animal models via apoptosis, anti-angiogenesis, signaling modulation (Evidence-in vitro/rodent)
  • Human healthspan/lifespan benefit: NOT demonstrated — all human trials to date are small, early-phase, or biomarker/safety-focused (human-data gap)
  • Early human safety signal appears acceptable in short studies, but efficacy on aging endpoints is unproven (Evidence-human, safety only)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Senescent cells accumulate with age and poison their neighbors Established

    As you age, damaged cells stop dividing but refuse to die — 'zombie' (senescent) cells. They pump out an inflammatory cocktail called the SASP (the senescence-associated secretory phenotype: IL-6, TNF-alpha, etc.) that drives chronic low-grade inflammation and age-related tissue dysfunction. Clearing these cells in animals improves multiple age-related conditions. This is the disease model fisetin is meant to attack. PMID 40563501 (Biomolecules 2025 review); PMID 36128715 (Open Biol 2022 review)

  2. Fisetin was screened as the most potent senolytic flavonoid Evidence-rodent

    In the landmark 2018 study, researchers screened 10 flavonoids on senescent mouse and human fibroblasts. Fisetin was the strongest at selectively killing senescent cells while sparing healthy ones. This is the paper essentially every 'fisetin senolytic' claim traces back to — and it is rodent + cell-culture work, not a human trial. PMID 30279143 (Yousefzadeh et al., EBioMedicine 2018)

  3. It works by knocking out senescent cells' survival 'force field' Evidence-in vitro

    Senescent cells stay alive by over-activating pro-survival pathways (SCAPs — senescent cell anti-apoptotic pathways), including PI3K/AKT and BCL-2-family signaling. Senolytics like fisetin transiently disable these survival circuits, so the senescent cell trips its own apoptosis (self-destruct) switch. Healthy cells, not dependent on those circuits, survive. This is the proposed mechanistic core of senolysis. PMID 36128715 (Open Biol 2022 mechanistic review); PMID 40563501 (Biomolecules 2025)

  4. 'Hit-and-run' dosing reduced senescence markers and extended lifespan IN MICE Evidence-rodent

    In aged and progeroid (fast-aging) mice, intermittent fisetin lowered senescence markers across multiple tissues, reduced age-related pathology, and extended median and maximum lifespan. 'Hit-and-run' means you don't need it constantly — you clear the zombie cells and the benefit persists. Crucially, this lifespan result is in mice, and the dose/exposure does not map cleanly onto a human supplement. PMID 30279143 (Yousefzadeh et al., EBioMedicine 2018)

  5. Separate from killing cells, it dials down inflammation and oxidative stress Evidence-rodent

    Beyond senolysis, fisetin acts as a 'senomorphic' and general anti-inflammatory: it suppresses NF-kB (a master inflammation switch), boosts antioxidant defenses (SOD1, catalase, lower ROS), and modulates inflammatory/apoptotic and growth-factor signaling. This broad polyphenol activity is well documented in cells and animals and explains its anti-inflammatory and anti-cancer signals in lab models. PMID 41683217 (Nutrients 2026 review); PMID 36558146 (Molecules 2022 review)

  6. The big caveat: a rigorous lifespan replication FAILED, and human aging benefit is unproven Contested

    The NIA Interventions Testing Program (the gold-standard, multi-site mouse lifespan program) tested fisetin in genetically diverse mice and found NO significant lifespan extension in either sex at the doses tested. Meanwhile every human trial so far is small/early-phase, mostly testing safety and senescence biomarkers — there is NO published evidence fisetin extends human healthspan or lifespan. One controlled human trial in Gulf War Illness found fisetin no better than placebo on symptoms. Treat 'fisetin extends lifespan' as a rodent finding that has already partly failed to replicate. PMID 38041783 (Harrison et al., GeroScience 2024, ITP negative); PMID 33802381 (RCT, IJERPH 2021, null in GWI)

  7. Low oral bioavailability is the practical wildcard Established

    Fisetin is poorly water-soluble and rapidly metabolized, so the blood levels that look so effective in a petri dish are hard to reach by swallowing a pill. This is why human translation is uncertain regardless of the rodent data, and why much research focuses on improved-delivery formulations. PMID 36558146 (Molecules 2022 review); PMID 41683217 (Nutrients 2026 review)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Fisetin (3,3',4',7-tetrahydroxyflavone; C15H10O6, MW 286.24; ChEMBL CHEMBL31574) is a polyphenolic flavonol, NOT a single-receptor ligand. It is a promiscuous, low-micromolar multi-target enzyme inhibitor with NO defined high-affinity orthosteric receptor. Measured in-vitro affinities (ChEMBL-curated IC50, Established for the isolated-enzyme assay only): acetylcholinesterase IC50 ~12 nM; CDK5/p25 IC50 570 nM; CDK1/cyclinB 790 nM; CDK6 850 nM; GSK-3 420 nM; casein kinase 2 (CK2) IC50 ~350 nM; ALOX12 (12-lipoxygenase) 950 nM; PKM2 900 nM; RSK3/RPS6KA2 ~891 nM; mitochondrial NADH-oxidase ~15 nM and succinoxidase ~45 nM; rat lipid-peroxidation IC50 ~200 nM (antioxidant). Action type where annotated = INHIBITOR (negative modulator). Key point for the senolytic story: these nM kinase IC50s are cell-free; the concentrations needed for senolysis in intact senescent cells are 10s of micromolar (Evidence-in vitro), so fisetin's selectivity for senescent cells is functional (they are 'primed' for apoptosis), not high-affinity target binding. ChEMBL lists NO approved mechanism-of-action record (get_mechanism returned 0 entries) — it is a natural product at max clinical phase 2. Drug-likeness: 0 Lipinski violations, QED 0.51, ALogP 2.28, PSA 111 A2 (borderline for CNS but fisetin does cross the BBB in rodents).

Signalling cascade. The accepted senolytic mechanism (Evidence-human tissue ex vivo + Evidence-rodent): senescent cells survive by upregulating 'senescent-cell anti-apoptotic pathways' (SCAPs) — PI3K/AKT, BCL-2/BCL-xL/BCL-W, p53/FOXO4/p21, HIF-1alpha, and ephrin-dependent survival signaling (Zhu/Kirkland framework, PMID 26711051, 32686219). Fisetin transiently disables several of these nodes, tipping primed senescent cells past their apoptotic threshold while sparing normal cells — a 'hit-and-run' kinetic (senescent cells take weeks to re-accumulate, so intermittent dosing works; PMID 30279143). Convergent downstream signalling fisetin engages (Evidence-rodent/in vitro): (1) inhibition of PI3K/AKT and consequent de-repression of pro-apoptotic BAD/BAX, shifting the BCL-2:BAX balance toward mitochondrial outer-membrane permeabilization, cytochrome-c release and caspase-3/9 activation; (2) suppression of NF-kB transcriptional activity, lowering the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) cytokines (IL-6, IL-8, IL-1beta) — this is the SENOMORPHIC arm, distinct from senolysis; (3) activation of SIRT1 and modulation of mTOR/autophagy, and induction of NRF2 antioxidant response (raising SOD1/catalase, lowering ROS). Fisetin is also a direct radical scavenger and metal chelator (3-OH/4-keto and catechol B-ring). Net effect described in the pivotal paper: reduced p16INK4a/SA-beta-gal+ cell burden across multiple tissues, reduced SASP, restored tissue homeostasis.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (dietary/supplement capsule) is the human/translational route; rodent PK studies used i.v., i.p. and p.o. Found in strawberries (highest, ~160 ug/g), apples, persimmons, onions, grapes, wine.
Absorption / bioavailability
Poor and the defining limitation. Absolute oral bioavailability in mouse = 7.8% at 100 mg/kg and 31.7% at 200 mg/kg (non-linear, saturable first-pass; Evidence-rodent, PMID 27810278). Low aqueous solubility + high lipophilicity + extensive first-pass phase-II conjugation drive this. Numerous nanoformulations (PLGA/HPbetaCD, lipid carriers, liposomes) raise Cmax and AUC severalfold in rodents (Evidence-rodent, PMID 28156161, 30119198). Human absolute bioavailability is UNKNOWN — no published human absolute-BA study; dedicated human PK trials are only now running (e.g. NCT06796374 FISEKIN-1, NCT06431932).
Half-life
Short and biphasic. Mouse after 223 mg/kg i.p.: Cmax 2.5 ug/mL at 15 min; distribution half-life ~0.09 h (~5 min) and terminal half-life ~3.1 h (Evidence-rodent, PMID 21840301). This rapid clearance is part of why intermittent 'hit-and-run' dosing is used. Human half-life UNKNOWN.
Metabolism
Extensive phase-II conjugation (glucuronidation by UGTs, sulfation by SULTs) plus rapid 3'-O-methylation by COMT to GERALDOL (3,4',7-trihydroxy-3'-methoxyflavone), an ACTIVE metabolite that circulates at higher concentration/AUC than parent fisetin and is MORE cytotoxic/antiangiogenic in vitro (Evidence-rodent, PMID 21840301, 27810278). In rat plasma the free:glucuronide:sulfate AUC ratio is ~1:6:21 (sulfates dominate; PMID 29862816). Fisetin is itself a potent inhibitor of SULT1A1 and modulates CYP1A1/1A2/3A4 and induces phase-II detox enzymes (UGT, GST, quinone reductase) — basis for potential herb-drug interactions (Evidence-in vitro/human enzymes, PMID 16289744).
Excretion
Significant biliary excretion, predominantly as sulfate conjugates, mediated by P-glycoprotein (Evidence-rodent, rat: sulfate biliary excretion rate ~823% of plasma AUC; P-gp dependent; PMID 29862816). Enterohepatic recycling of conjugates is plausible. Renal contribution of conjugates also occurs.
Notes
BBB-penetrant in rodents (neuroprotective effects in vivo). No clinical prodrug/ester is approved; bioavailability-enhancement is the central pharmaceutical problem. Because most circulating drug is conjugated/methylated, plasma 'fisetin' levels understate total active material (geraldol + deconjugatable pools). P-gp and SULT/UGT/COMT involvement flag interaction and inter-individual-variability risk (e.g. COMT and SULT1A1 polymorphisms could alter exposure — Speculative for clinical effect).

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Dose-response is well characterized in MICE and essentially UNKNOWN (no validated efficacy dose-response) in HUMANS. MOUSE (Evidence-rodent, pivotal PMID 30279143): fisetin was the most potent of 10 flavonoids screened for senolysis; in progeroid (Ercc1-/Delta) and aged wild-type mice, oral/dietary fisetin reduced senescence markers (p16, SA-beta-gal, SASP) across fat, kidney, brain and other tissues, reduced age-related pathology, and EXTENDED median and maximum lifespan even when started in late life (~85% of murine lifespan). Effective dosing was intermittent/acute (e.g. ~100 mg/kg oral by gavage or ~500 ppm in diet in related work) consistent with 'hit-and-run.' CELL (Evidence-in vitro): senolysis in murine and human fibroblasts occurs in the low-to-mid micromolar range (~tens of uM), far above kinase IC50s — selectivity arises because senescent cells are apoptosis-primed. HUMAN: dose-response for senescent-cell clearance or clinical benefit is NOT established. The most relevant human senolytic dose-response signal comes from the analogous D+Q trial (PMID 38956196): the overall postmenopausal cohort showed NO change in bone-resorption marker CTx, but an exploratory subgroup with the HIGHEST baseline senescent-cell burden (top tertile T-cell p16 mRNA) showed responses (P1NP +34%, CTx -11% at 2 wk; radius BMD +2.7% at 20 wk) — i.e. clinical response may depend on baseline senescent-cell burden rather than a simple mg dose. For fisetin specifically, ~20 ongoing/completed phase-2 trials (frailty, osteoarthritis, PAD, COVID-19, etc.) use intermittent high-dose oral regimens, but published efficacy dose-response data are still pending. Bottom line: extrapolating the mg/kg mouse doses to humans is NOT validated, and the human therapeutic window/effective dose is a genuine open question.

How it compares

  • vs Quercetin (and the Dasatinib+Quercetin / D+Q combination)
    same: Both are flavonol senolytics acting on SCAP/anti-apoptotic networks (PI3K/AKT, BCL-2 family) with intermittent hit-and-run dosing; both natural products; quercetin shares fisetin's poor oral bioavailability and heavy phase-II conjugation.
    different: Fisetin was MORE potent than quercetin as a single-agent senolytic in the EBioMedicine screen (PMID 30279143). Quercetin is usually paired with the prescription tyrosine-kinase inhibitor dasatinib (D+Q) to broaden cell-type coverage; fisetin is studied largely as monotherapy. The strongest human senolytic RCT to date used D+Q, not fisetin.
  • vs Navitoclax (ABT-263)
    same: Both are senolytic by tipping primed senescent cells into apoptosis via the BCL-2-family axis.
    different: Navitoclax is a high-affinity, designed BH3-mimetic (direct nanomolar BCL-2/BCL-xL/BCL-W inhibitor; PMID 26711051) — far more potent and specific than fisetin, but causes dose-limiting thrombocytopenia (on-target BCL-xL in platelets). Fisetin is a low-micromolar, promiscuous, dietary compound with a benign safety profile but unproven human efficacy and poor bioavailability.
  • vs Geraldol (3'-O-methyl-fisetin, fisetin's own active metabolite)
    same: Same flavone scaffold; shares antioxidant/antiangiogenic/cytotoxic activities; geraldol is generated in vivo from fisetin.
    different: Geraldol circulates at higher concentration/AUC than fisetin after dosing and is MORE cytotoxic and antiangiogenic in vitro (PMID 21840301), meaning some of fisetin's in-vivo activity is actually mediated by this metabolite — a key reason plasma 'fisetin' underestimates active exposure.
  • vs Resveratrol / kaempferol / apigenin (other polyphenol senotherapeutics)
    same: All polyphenols converging on SIRT1 activation, NF-kB inhibition and autophagy/SASP modulation (senomorphic), with antioxidant ROS-lowering effects (PMID 40005954).
    different: Fisetin is among the few with demonstrated bona-fide SENOLYTIC (cell-killing) potency in vivo plus lifespan extension, whereas resveratrol/apigenin are predominantly senomorphic (SASP-suppressing) rather than senolytic; potencies and target spectra differ.

History &amp; legal status

"Fisetin is a naturally occurring plant flavonol, first isolated in the 19th century from the smoke tree (Cotinus coggygria / Rhus cotinus) and historically used as a yellow textile dye (C.I. Natural Brown 1 / C.I. 75620; synonyms 5-desoxyquercetin, NSC-407010). It is a normal dietary constituent (strawberries, apples, persimmons, onions, grapes). REGULATORY STATUS: It is NOT an approved drug anywhere — ChEMBL lists max clinical phase 2 with no approval and no defined mechanism-of-action record. In the US it is sold as a dietary supplement (DSHEA framework; not FDA-evaluated for efficacy, not GRAS-affirmed as a drug). It is NOT scheduled or banned and is not on WADA's prohibited list. Its modern scientific prominence dates to ~2017-2018 when the Mayo Clinic/Scripps/Minnesota groups (Kirkland, Niedernhofer, Robbins) identified it as the most potent flavonoid senolytic and showed lifespan extension in mice (PMID 30279143). Since then it has entered ~34 registered clinical trials (ClinicalTrials.gov) across frailty (AFFIRM/AFFIRM-LITE NCT03430037/NCT03675724), osteoarthritis, peripheral artery disease, childhood-cancer-survivor frailty, COVID-19, sepsis, CKD, Alzheimer's, and bone/skeletal health — overwhelmingly phase-2, many led by Mayo Clinic. As of 2026 no senolytic (fisetin included) has regulatory approval for an aging or age-related indication; use outside trials is considered premature by the field (PMID 32686219)."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Human efficacy and dose-response: no completed fisetin trial has yet established that oral fisetin clears senescent cells or improves a hard clinical endpoint in humans; the effective human dose, schedule, and whether benefit (as with D+Q) requires high baseline senescent-cell burden are all unresolved.
  • Human pharmacokinetics: absolute human oral bioavailability, half-life, tissue distribution, and the in-vivo contribution of the active metabolite geraldol in humans are essentially unmeasured — dedicated human PK trials (e.g. FISEKIN-1 NCT06796374) are only now running.
  • Bioavailability problem: whether nanoformulations/prodrugs translate the rodent AUC gains into meaningful human exposure without altering the safety profile is unknown.
  • Selectivity basis: the precise molecular reason fisetin kills senescent (but not normal) cells — which SCAP nodes dominate in which human cell types — is incompletely mapped; cell-type-restricted senolysis means one tissue's senescent cells may be resistant.
  • Long-term safety of repeated senolysis: chronic or repeated clearing of senescent cells could theoretically impair wound healing/tissue repair, tumor suppression, or fibrosis resolution; long-term human safety data do not exist.
  • Herb-drug interactions in vivo: fisetin inhibits SULT1A1, several CYPs and engages P-glycoprotein in vitro, but the clinical magnitude of interactions with common medications (anticoagulants, CYP3A4/P-gp substrates) has not been quantified.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (NOT a protocol, NOT dosing advice). Because fisetin is being used as a senolytic supplement off-label, sensible things to watch: (1) Bleeding/clotting — given antiplatelet potential and flavonol class effects, watch for easy bruising or bleeding, especially with concurrent anticoagulants/antiplatelets/NSAIDs; baseline awareness of CBC/platelets is reasonable. (2) Liver and kidney function — fisetin is heavily hepatically conjugated and biliary-excreted; LFTs (ALT/AST/ALP/bilirubin) and renal panel (creatinine/eGFR) are sensible to track, particularly in those with hepatic/biliary disease since P-gp-mediated biliary excretion dominates. (3) Drug-interaction surveillance — if on CYP3A4 or P-glycoprotein substrate drugs (and SULT1A1-handled drugs), watch for altered drug effect/levels. (4) GI tolerance — nausea/loose stool at high intake. (5) For the senolytic rationale specifically, research settings track senescence/inflammation biomarkers (e.g. circulating SASP cytokines IL-6/IL-8/TNF-alpha, CRP, and T-cell p16INK4a/CDKN2A mRNA as a senescent-burden readout) and, for skeletal endpoints, bone turnover markers (CTx, P1NP) — these are how trials judge response and would be the rational things to follow, not a substitute for clinical care. (6) Endocrine/reproductive caution in those with hormone-sensitive conditions given preclinical hormonal modulation. Anyone considering fisetin should do so under medical supervision and ideally within a registered trial, since human efficacy and long-term safety are unestablished."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE. (1) The flagship 'fisetin extends lifespan' claim is from ONE 2018 mouse/cell study (PMID 30279143); the gold-standard NIA Interventions Testing Program tested it in genetically diverse mice and found NO significant lifespan extension in either sex (PMID 38041783). When two rodent results disagree, the rigorous multi-site replication is the one to trust. (2) NO published human trial shows fisetin extends healthspan or lifespan — current trials (ClinicalTrials.gov, mostly Phase 1/2) are testing safety, pharmacokinetics, and senescence biomarkers, not proven clinical benefit. (3) The one published controlled human efficacy trial (Gulf War Illness, PMID 33802381) was NULL. (4) Recent small 2026 RCTs (PMID 42218768, PMID 41683255) test fisetin ON TOP of exercise in obese men and are about inflammation/metabolism, not aging — easy to misread as 'fisetin works in humans.' (5) Poor oral bioavailability means the doses that look magic in a dish may not be achievable from a capsule (PMID 36558146). (6) Forum/biohacker framing of fisetin as a proven anti-aging senolytic outruns the evidence — accurate framing is: promising rodent/in-vitro senolytic with an unproven, actively-being-tested human story. (7) NO androgen-receptor or anabolic activity — fisetin is unrelated to AAS/PED mechanisms; do not conflate. (8) This is mechanism education, not medical or dosing advice; supplement quality and interactions are unregulated. (9) Most aging/senolytic reviews and the foundational study come from a small set of overlapping labs (Mayo/Scripps/Minnesota) — note the concentrated authorship when weighing enthusiasm.
