N-acetylcysteine

The workhorse glutathione precursor: a proven antidote for paracetamol liver injury, repurposed by the fitness world as generic oxidative-stress and organ support with far thinner evidence for that use.

Human / approved 18 citations
Class
Glutathione (GSH) precursor / thiol antioxidant / mucolytic; acetylated form of the amino acid L-cysteine
Primary target
Rate-limiting cysteine donor for de novo glutathione synthesis (feeding the glutamate-cysteine ligase step); secondarily a direct free-thiol radical scavenger and a disulphide-reducing agent
Human status
Established, long-marketed drug in humans. NAC is an approved medicine worldwide: intravenous/oral acetylcysteine is the standard antidote for paracetamol (acetaminophen) poisoning and oral NAC is a licensed mucolytic; it is also sold OTC as a dietary supplement in many countries. Decades of human use and a broad safety record. However, evidence quality is highly indication-dependent: rock-solid for paracetamol overdose (PMID 519312) and reasonable but expert-consensus tier for chronic bronchitis/COPD exacerbations (PMID 42158130); genuinely negative for a headline claim relevant to this audience - a 5177-patient RCT (PRESERVE, NCT01467466; PMID 29130810) found oral NAC did NOT prevent contrast-associated kidney injury or death. For the specific fitness use here - protecting liver/kidney during anabolic-steroid cycles - there are NO human outcome trials; support is mechanistic plus rodent (e.g. NAC partially blunting nandrolone-induced liver injury/fibrosis in rats, PMID 41242401) and extrapolation from its glutathione biology.

At a glance

  • Proven antidote for paracetamol (acetaminophen) overdose - prevents severe liver injury and death when started early (ideally within ~8 h; efficacy falls off after 8-10 h and is ineffective after ~15 h)
  • Replenishes cysteine and glutathione, raising the cell's main endogenous antioxidant capacity
  • Mucolytic: breaks disulphide bonds in mucus, thinning airway secretions (its original licensed use)
  • Modestly lowers oxidative-stress and inflammation markers (TBARS, IL-6) and reduces exercise-induced muscle soreness and lactate in pooled human data - small, heterogeneous effects
  • Expert-consensus-supported adjunct in COPD/chronic bronchitis for reducing exacerbations at 600 mg twice daily (NECTAR respiratory consensus)
  • Widely used in the fitness/PED community as 'liver and kidney support' on cycle - but this specific indication is extrapolated, not proven by any human outcome trial
  • Does NOT prevent contrast-associated kidney injury (large PRESERVE RCT was negative)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. NAC is deacetylated to cysteine, the scarce building block for glutathione Established

    Swallowed NAC is largely deacetylated (in gut wall and liver) to L-cysteine. Cysteine is the rate-limiting ingredient for making glutathione (GSH) - the cell's main built-in antioxidant - because the other two amino acids (glutamate, glycine) are usually plentiful. By topping up cysteine, NAC lets cells build more GSH when they are running low. This is the core, textbook reason NAC 'works'. PMID 42158130; PMID 40867647

  2. Cysteine feeds glutamate-cysteine ligase (GCL), the rate-limiting GSH-synthesis enzyme Evidence-in vitro

    The first and slowest step of glutathione synthesis is run by the enzyme glutamate-cysteine ligase (GCL, also called gamma-GCS). Its speed is capped by how much cysteine is around. Supplying cysteine via NAC pushes this step, raising GSH. In liver-cell (HepG2) and neuron-like (PC-12) models, blocking GSH synthesis with BSO makes cells vulnerable to oxidant/DNA damage, while adding NAC restores GSH and protects them - direct in-vitro evidence that this is the pathway NAC acts through. PMID 16996296; PMID 24896367

  3. NAC also liberates protein-bound cysteine already circulating in blood Evidence-human

    Beyond acting as a precursor itself, NAC's free thiol group swaps with disulphide bonds that tether cysteine to plasma proteins (like albumin). In human plasma incubated ex vivo, clinically relevant NAC concentrations sharply increased free (unbound) cysteine - protein-bound cysteine fell from ~85% bound down toward ~0% at high NAC. So part of NAC's benefit may be unlocking a reservoir of cysteine the body already has, not just adding new material. PMID 23018672

  4. Restored glutathione detoxifies the reactive paracetamol metabolite NAPQI (the antidote mechanism) Established

    This is NAC's best-proven, life-saving action. A paracetamol (acetaminophen) overdose overwhelms normal metabolism and generates a highly reactive, toxic byproduct called NAPQI. NAPQI is normally mopped up by glutathione; in overdose GSH is depleted and NAPQI binds and kills liver cells. NAC restocks cysteine/GSH (and provides thiols directly), letting the liver neutralise NAPQI. Given early enough it prevents severe liver damage. PMID 519312; PMID 7039926

  5. The free thiol directly scavenges radicals and reduces disulphides (a modest, GSH-independent effect) Evidence-in vitro

    NAC's sulfhydryl (-SH) group can react directly with some reactive oxygen/nitrogen species and can break disulphide bonds - which is also why it is a mucolytic (it splits the disulphide crosslinks that make airway mucus thick and sticky). But as a matter of thiol chemistry NAC is a relatively weak direct scavenger at achievable blood levels; most of its antioxidant punch is believed to come indirectly, through glutathione, rather than from mopping up radicals molecule-for-molecule. PMID 2029805; PMID 42158130

  6. Higher glutathione and lower oxidative load can dampen redox-driven inflammatory signalling Evidence-human

    By raising GSH and lowering oxidative stress, NAC can indirectly quiet redox-sensitive signalling (e.g. NF-kB-linked inflammation). In pooled human exercise studies NAC modestly lowered markers like IL-6 and lipid-peroxidation (TBARS) and reduced muscle soreness and lactate - but effect sizes were small and heterogeneous, and hard outcomes (like consistent performance gains) were not reliably improved. PMID 39632267; PMID 37299425

  7. In the brain, NAC-derived cystine drives the cystine-glutamate antiporter (system xc-) - relevant to psychiatry, not organ support Contested

    NAC is a cystine pro-drug for a glial transporter called system xc- (xCT/SLC7A11), which imports cystine and exports glutamate, modulating glutamate tone. This is the proposed basis for NAC trials in addiction, OCD and mood, and rat data show NAC can reduce nicotine self-administration and cue-induced seeking. But the pathway is contested: in a mouse model, NAC's antidepressant-like effect did NOT require system xc-, so the mechanism is not settled. This is largely rodent/mechanistic and is a different mechanism from the liver/kidney 'organ support' rationale. PMID 22903390; PMID 28882088

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. NAC has no classic receptor. It is a small acetylated thiol amino-acid derivative whose 'target' is metabolic: it is deacetylated to L-cysteine, the rate-limiting substrate for glutamate-cysteine ligase (GCL/gamma-GCS), the first and rate-limiting enzyme of glutathione synthesis. Its free sulfhydryl (-SH) group additionally participates in thiol-disulphide exchange (liberating protein-bound cysteine; breaking mucus disulphide crosslinks) and in direct, relatively weak, radical scavenging.

Signalling cascade. NAC -> (deacetylation, gut/liver) -> L-cysteine -> GCL combines cysteine + glutamate -> gamma-glutamylcysteine -> glutathione synthetase adds glycine -> GSH. GSH then serves as cofactor for glutathione peroxidases/transferases, conjugating reactive electrophiles (e.g. NAPQI from paracetamol) and reducing peroxides. Parallel branch: NAC-derived cystine feeds system xc- (xCT/SLC7A11) in glia, exchanging for extracellular glutamate (CNS-relevant, contested).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsules/effervescent, and as a supplement) and intravenous (antidote/hospital). Also inhaled/nebulised as a mucolytic.
Absorption / bioavailability
Oral bioavailability of intact reduced NAC is LOW owing to extensive gut-wall and first-pass metabolism (much is incorporated into cysteine/protein pools before reaching plasma as intact NAC); older literature commonly cites single-digit percentages, but figures vary widely by assay (intact NAC vs total including cysteine) and are not pinned down by the human PK review cited here. What the cited human PK data do show: after a 200-400 mg oral dose, peak plasma NAC of ~0.35-4 mg/L is reached ~1-2 h later (PMID 2029805). A controlled crossover study in cats measured oral bioavailability at ~19% (PMID 23363356), illustrating species differences rather than the human value.
Half-life
Terminal half-life of reduced NAC ~6.25 h (oral, human). Intravenous multi-compartment kinetics differ; animal PK (e.g. cats show elimination t1/2 ~0.78 h IV / ~1.34 h oral and ~19% oral bioavailability) underscore species differences.
Metabolism
Rapidly deacetylated to cysteine and incorporated into glutathione and plasma/protein thiol pools; largely 'consumed' as a substrate rather than cleared as parent drug. Little intact NAC metabolism is characterised beyond this.
Excretion
Renal clearance ~0.19-0.21 L/h/kg; ~70% of total clearance is non-renal (metabolic incorporation). Major urinary excretory product is inorganic sulphate.
Notes
Protein binding rises to ~50% by ~4 h post-dose. Activated charcoal can adsorb oral NAC (relevant in overdose co-management). Whether NAC crosses the blood-brain barrier/placenta is not established in the cited PK review.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Deliberately NON-prescriptive here (harm-reduction). Conceptually: (a) Paracetamol-overdose antidote regimens are weight-based hospital protocols (the classic three-bag IV vs a simplified two-bag; a meta-analysis found the two-bag regimen non-inferior for hepatotoxicity with markedly fewer anaphylactoid reactions - PMID 40013897) and must be physician-directed. (b) The respiratory/mucolytic evidence base clusters around 600 mg twice daily for COPD exacerbation reduction (expert consensus, PMID 42158130). (c) For the antioxidant/'organ support' use there is no validated dose-response - human exercise studies used a wide range with small, inconsistent effects. Higher is not clearly better and may risk reductive stress / blunted adaptation. A suspected paracetamol overdose is a medical emergency requiring hospital assessment and timed nomogram-guided treatment, not self-management with OTC NAC.

How it compares

  • vs TUDCA (the other half of the organ-support stack)
    same: Both marketed to lifters as on-cycle organ protection; both have real, narrow, evidence-backed clinical roles outside bodybuilding.
    different: Different problem and mechanism: NAC targets OXIDATIVE stress by restocking glutathione (and is the paracetamol antidote); TUDCA is a bile acid targeting CHOLESTATIC stress (bile flow, ER stress, hepatocyte membrane protection), relevant to oral-17aa-steroid-driven cholestasis. They are complementary, not interchangeable - and neither has RCT proof for steroid-cycle organ protection.
  • vs Oral glutathione (GSH) supplements
    same: Both aim to raise the glutathione antioxidant system.
    different: Oral GSH is thought to be poorly and unreliably absorbed intact; NAC instead supplies the rate-limiting precursor (cysteine) that lets cells synthesise GSH internally, which is generally considered the more reliable route to raise intracellular GSH.
  • vs Methionine (alternative paracetamol antidote)
    same: Both are sulphur-amino-acid antidotes that can restore glutathione in paracetamol overdose.
    different: NAC became the treatment of choice for reliability, IV availability and safety; the Cochrane review notes NAC's superiority to methionine is not firmly proven, but NAC is the clinical standard.
  • vs Vitamins C/E (classic antioxidant supplements)
    same: Share the antioxidant premise and the same potential downside - blunting exercise adaptation at high doses (best demonstrated for C/E).
    different: NAC works upstream (precursor to the endogenous GSH system) rather than acting only as a direct sacrificial scavenger; NAC also has the unique mucolytic/antidote roles vitamins lack.

History & legal status

NAC was introduced in the 1960s as an inhaled/oral mucolytic (splitting mucus disulphide bonds). In the 1970s Prescott and colleagues established IV NAC as the antidote for paracetamol poisoning (BMJ 1979, PMID 519312), and it has since been on the WHO Essential Medicines list for that indication. It is a prescription antidote (IV) and, in many countries, an OTC mucolytic and a widely sold dietary supplement. Regulatory status of the supplement form has been contested (notably a period of FDA scrutiny in the US over whether NAC can be sold as a dietary supplement, given its prior approval as a drug), but it remains broadly available. Its adoption into fitness/PED 'organ support' stacks is off-label/extrapolated consumer use, not a regulated indication.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No human outcome trials show NAC prevents anabolic-steroid- or SARM-induced liver/kidney injury on cycle - the central claim for this audience rests on mechanism plus rodent data (e.g. PMID 41242401).
  • Whether NAC's antioxidant dosing blunts hypertrophy/strength/endurance adaptations in trained lifters (NAC-specific human data are sparse; concern extrapolated from vitamin C/E work).
  • Optimal (or even beneficial) dose for antioxidant/'support' use is undefined; the therapeutic-vs-counterproductive threshold is unknown.
  • Long-term safety of chronic high-dose supplemental NAC in otherwise healthy young users is not well characterised.
  • Real oral bioavailability and intracellular GSH-raising efficacy vary by assay and baseline GSH status - benefit may be largest only when glutathione is genuinely depleted, and minimal in replete, healthy individuals.
  • Whether the CNS/glutamatergic (system xc-) effects are clinically real is contested (PMID 28882088) and, in any case, separate from the organ-support rationale.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

What to watch (NOT a protocol, and not medical advice): Because NAC is being used as insurance for organ strain, the meaningful monitoring is of the ORGANS, not the supplement. Track a liver panel (ALT, AST, GGT, bilirubin - note ALT/AST can also rise transiently from hard training and from steroids independent of true injury) and renal function (serum creatinine, eGFR, cystatin C if available) with baseline and periodic bloodwork rather than assuming NAC is protecting you. For NAC itself, watch for GI intolerance (nausea/diarrhoea) and, with any IV/high-dose exposure, anaphylactoid symptoms (flushing, rash, wheeze, drop in blood pressure) - stop and seek care if these occur. Critical safety flag: NAC is NOT a substitute for emergency care in a real paracetamol overdose, which is time-critical and must be hospital-managed. If organ markers trend abnormal, the correct response is to address the underlying drug exposure and get clinical evaluation - not to escalate the supplement.

Caveats

Honest framing for this audience: (1) NAC's proven miracle is as a paracetamol antidote - do not assume that translates into general 'liver protection' against steroid-, alcohol-, or supplement-induced strain; that leap is unproven in humans. (2) The most audience-relevant rigorous test of NAC for organ protection (contrast-associated kidney injury, PRESERVE, n=5177) was NEGATIVE (oral NAC vs placebo, OR 1.02, 95% CI 0.78-1.33) - a cautionary data point against over-trusting the 'kidney support' story. (3) Antioxidant supplements can be a double-edged sword: exercise-induced reactive oxygen species are partly beneficial signals that drive training adaptation and mitochondrial biogenesis, and high-dose antioxidants (demonstrated mainly with vitamins C/E; the same redox logic plausibly extends to NAC but NAC-specific human proof is limited) can blunt some of those adaptations - so 'more antioxidant' is not automatically better for someone training hard (PMID 25889822). (4) Human antioxidant/performance effects that do exist are small and inconsistent (PMID 39632267; PMID 37299425). (5) Much of the 'exciting' NAC mechanism literature (system xc-, neuroprotection, anti-fibrotic) is rodent/in-vitro and does not establish the claimed on-cycle organ benefits in humans. (6) NAC does not license or excuse toxic drug use; it is at best partial insurance with unproven benefit in that setting, and monitoring bloodwork matters more than any supplement.
