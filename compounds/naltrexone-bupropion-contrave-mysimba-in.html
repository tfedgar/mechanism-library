Naltrexone/Bupropion

Main target: Hypothalamic arcuate-nucleus POMC. Backed by human data.

EvidenceHuman evidence
ApprovalFDA-approved
Safety⚠ Boxed warning

A two-drug combo that pushes on the brain's appetite "gas pedal" (bupropion) while cutting the brake that normally stalls weight loss (naltrexone) — modest, real weight loss with a real side-effect and cardiovascular-safety story.

Strong human dataFDA-approved 10 citations
Class
Fixed-dose oral combination: naltrexone (opioid receptor antagonist) + bupropion (norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitor, NDRI / aminoketone)
Primary target
Hypothalamic arcuate-nucleus POMC (pro-opiomelanocortin) neurons and the downstream melanocortin (MC3R/MC4R) pathway, plus mesolimbic dopamine reward circuitry
Human status
FDA-approved (September 2014) and EMA-approved (as Mysimba, 2015) for chronic weight management in adults with obesity (BMI >=30) or overweight (BMI >=27) plus a weight-related comorbidity, as an adjunct to a reduced-calorie diet and increased physical activity. Backed by four pivotal phase 3 RCTs (COR-I, COR-II, COR-BMOD, COR-Diabetes; ~4,500 patients total). Now typically a second- or third-line oral option: its ~6% weight loss is modest next to GLP-1/GIP agonists (semaglutide ~11-15%, tirzepatide ~15-21%). A dedicated cardiovascular outcomes trial (LIGHT; NCT01601704, ~8,910 randomized) was terminated early in 2015 after the sponsor publicly released confidential 25% interim data, so long-term cardiovascular benefit/safety was never definitively established (the interim data did not show excess major adverse CV events, but the trial could not confirm the prespecified non-inferiority margin).

At a glance

  • Weight loss of ~6.1-6.4% of body weight at 56 weeks (placebo-subtracted ~4.1-5.2%); ~48-51% of participants (NB32) achieve >=5% loss vs ~17% placebo (Established, PMID 20673995, PMID 23408728)
  • Reduced hunger, appetite, and improved self-reported control of eating (Evidence-human, PMID 23408728)
  • Improvements in cardiometabolic markers: waist circumference, triglycerides, HDL, and, in type 2 diabetes, HbA1c/glycemic control (Evidence-human, PMID 24144653)
  • A small transient rise in blood pressure and heart rate (~1.5 mmHg early, then ~1 mmHg below baseline) — the drug blunts the larger BP fall normally expected from weight loss rather than lowering BP as much (Evidence-human, PMID 20673995)
  • Nausea is the dominant, dose-limiting adverse effect (~30% vs ~5% placebo; up to ~42% in the diabetes trial), usually mild-moderate and transient (Established, PMID 20673995, PMID 24144653)
  • Possible reduction in alcohol/smoking-related craving (bupropion is an approved smoking-cessation drug; naltrexone treats alcohol use disorder) — plausible ancillary effect asserted in narrative reviews, not an obesity endpoint and not demonstrated in the obesity trials (Speculative, PMID 39762910)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps1 human 2 established 3 rodent / preclinical 1 contested / speculative

  1. Bupropion turns up the POMC 'satiety' neurons Evidence-rodent

    Bupropion is an NDRI — it blocks reuptake of dopamine and norepinephrine, raising their levels. In the hypothalamic arcuate nucleus this is proposed to stimulate POMC neurons to fire. In mouse brain-slice electrophysiology, bupropion increased POMC neuron activity. POMC neurons are the brain's appetite-suppressing satiety cells. PMID 18997675

  2. Firing POMC neurons release alpha-MSH, which activates MC4R to cut appetite Established

    When POMC neurons fire they release the peptide alpha-MSH (alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone), which activates melanocortin-4 receptors (MC4R) in downstream hypothalamic centers. MC4R activation is one of the body's core 'eat less' signals — humans with MC4R mutations develop severe (monogenic) obesity, confirming this pathway's central role. Note: this is the general melanocortin biology; it is not a direct action of either drug (neither binds MC4R). PMID 30929417

  3. The catch: POMC neurons brake themselves with beta-endorphin Evidence-rodent

    POMC neurons also make beta-endorphin, an opioid peptide. beta-endorphin loops back onto mu-opioid receptors on the same POMC neurons and shuts them off — a built-in negative-feedback 'brake' (autoinhibition). This is proposed to be why single appetite drugs tend to hit a weight-loss plateau. This autoinhibition loop is demonstrated mainly in rodent electrophysiology. PMID 18997675

  4. Naltrexone releases the brake by blocking mu-opioid autoinhibition Evidence-rodent

    Naltrexone is an opioid-receptor antagonist. By blocking the mu-opioid receptors that beta-endorphin uses, it is proposed to remove the POMC self-braking loop. On its own naltrexone does little for weight, but paired with bupropion it is proposed to let POMC stimulation keep going instead of plateauing. In mice, bupropion+naltrexone stimulated POMC neurons and cut food intake more than either drug alone — an effect the authors described as consistent with synergism. PMID 18997675

  5. Net effect in the proof-of-concept human study: weight loss without an early plateau Evidence-human

    The combination produced a larger, more sustained drop in weight than either agent alone in a small phase-2 human study. Combination completers diverged from naltrexone-alone and placebo by week 16 and from bupropion-alone by week 24 — i.e., it kept working past the point where monotherapy stalled. This is an observed clinical trajectory in a small proof-of-concept trial; it is consistent with, but does not by itself prove, the rodent autoinhibition mechanism in humans. PMID 18997675

  6. Second proposed arm: dampening food reward in the mesolimbic dopamine system Contested

    Beyond the hypothalamic hunger circuit, the combo is proposed to act on reward/craving pathways: bupropion's dopaminergic action plus naltrexone's opioid blockade may reduce the rewarding pull of highly palatable food (the same opioid-reward logic behind naltrexone's use in alcohol craving). Reviews frame NB as acting on both hypothalamic (homeostatic) and mesolimbic (reward-based) systems, but the human reward-eating contribution is inferred and not robustly demonstrated. PMID 41947825

  7. Downstream: reduced energy intake drives modest weight loss Established

    The behavioral output is reduced calorie intake and improved 'control of eating,' producing weight loss of roughly 5-6.4% of body weight over ~56 weeks in the pivotal trials (placebo-subtracted ~4-5%). This is real but modest — well below GLP-1 agonists like semaglutide or tirzepatide. PMID 20673995

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Naltrexone: competitive antagonist at mu- (highest affinity), kappa-, and delta-opioid receptors; centrally it blocks beta-endorphin-mediated mu-opioid autoinhibition of POMC neurons. Bupropion: relatively weak but functionally relevant inhibitor of dopamine (DAT) and norepinephrine (NET) reuptake transporters (an NDRI/aminoketone); also a non-competitive nicotinic acetylcholine receptor antagonist. Neither is a direct MC4R ligand — the melanocortin effect is indirect via POMC neuron activation.

Signalling cascade. Bupropion raises synaptic DA/NE -> proposed excitation of arcuate POMC neurons -> alpha-MSH release -> MC3R/MC4R activation -> reduced appetite. In parallel, POMC-derived beta-endorphin normally activates mu-opioid receptors on POMC neurons to inhibit them (autoinhibition); naltrexone blocks this brake, proposed to allow sustained POMC firing. A second proposed circuit: combined dopaminergic + opioid-blocking action reduces mesolimbic reward signaling to palatable food.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral, fixed-dose sustained-release (SR) bilayer tablet (naltrexone HCl 8 mg + bupropion HCl 90 mg per tablet); the SR formulation smooths peak concentrations to reduce nausea and seizure risk. (No dosing/titration schedule is provided here.)
Absorption / bioavailability
Bupropion is well absorbed orally but undergoes extensive first-pass metabolism (absolute bioavailability not fully established). Naltrexone is rapidly absorbed with high first-pass extraction; systemic naltrexone bioavailability is low and variable.
Half-life
Bupropion parent terminal half-life is on the order of half a day, with hydroxybupropion longer (~20+ h); naltrexone parent ~4 h and 6-beta-naltrexol ~13 h. Steady state is reached within days.
Metabolism
Bupropion is metabolized mainly by CYP2B6 to the active metabolite hydroxybupropion (which contributes substantially to activity), plus threohydrobupropion/erythrohydrobupropion. Bupropion and hydroxybupropion are inhibitors of CYP2D6 — a clinically important interaction (raises levels of CYP2D6 substrates). Naltrexone is metabolized (non-CYP, via dihydrodiol dehydrogenase) to the active metabolite 6-beta-naltrexol.
Excretion
Predominantly renal excretion of metabolites for both drugs. Dose reduction is advised in renal or hepatic impairment; use is generally not recommended in end-stage renal disease or significant hepatic impairment.
Notes
Contraindicated with chronic opioid therapy or acute opioid withdrawal, uncontrolled hypertension, seizure disorder or conditions lowering the seizure threshold, bulimia/anorexia nervosa, abrupt discontinuation of alcohol/benzodiazepines/antiepileptics, MAO inhibitor use, and pregnancy. CYP2D6 and CYP2B6 interactions are the main PK cautions.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Efficacy is dose-related for both components. In COR-I the higher naltrexone dose (NB32: naltrexone 32 mg/day) produced greater weight loss (-6.1%) than the lower dose (NB16: -5.0%), both beating placebo (-1.3%); the 32 mg naltrexone regimen became the approved maintenance dose. Bupropion's dose is capped (360 mg/day) partly because seizure risk rises with bupropion dose. Nausea and other GI/CNS adverse effects are also dose-related, which is why the product is titrated up gradually rather than started at full dose. There is a practical efficacy ceiling — the combination does not approach GLP-1-class weight loss regardless of adherence.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Semaglutide / GLP-1 receptor agonists
    same: Both are centrally-acting anti-obesity drugs that reduce appetite; both are thought to act partly on hypothalamic/POMC and reward circuits.
    different: Semaglutide produces roughly double-plus the weight loss (~11-15% vs ~6%), is injectable (oral forms emerging), tends to lower BP, and has proven CV benefit — NB is oral, modest, raises BP slightly, and has unproven CV outcomes.
  • vs Tirzepatide (GIP/GLP-1)
    same: Central appetite suppression, chronic weight-management indication.
    different: Tirzepatide achieves ~15-21% weight loss, far exceeding NB; NB's relative advantages are oral dosing and an oral-drug price point.
  • vs Phentermine/topiramate (Qsymia)
    same: Oral fixed-dose central AOM combining two repurposed drugs; both titrated to limit adverse effects.
    different: Phentermine/topiramate generally gives more weight loss (~8-10%) but topiramate is teratogenic and both carry CV/psychiatric cautions; NB is preferred where topiramate is unsuitable (e.g., preconception).
  • vs Bupropion alone or naltrexone alone
    same: Same molecular actors.
    different: Monotherapy plateaus — bupropion alone gives limited sustained weight loss and naltrexone alone gives almost none; the combination's rationale is that naltrexone releases the POMC autoinhibition brake so bupropion's effect is sustained (the core, rodent-derived hypothesis).
  • vs Low-dose naltrexone (LDN) hype
    same: Both involve naltrexone.
    different: LDN (~1.5-4.5 mg) for pain/inflammation/'metabolism' is a separate, largely unproven use; Contrave uses naltrexone 32 mg/day paired with bupropion and is FDA-approved specifically for weight — do not conflate the two.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Developed by Orexigen Therapeutics on Frank Greenway and colleagues' rational-design hypothesis (Pennington Biomedical): pair a putative POMC stimulator (bupropion) with an opioid antagonist (naltrexone) to defeat the beta-endorphin autoinhibition plateau (Obesity 2009). The phase 3 CONTRAVE Obesity Research (COR) program (COR-I 2010, COR-BMOD 2011, COR-II and COR-Diabetes 2013) supported efficacy. The FDA initially issued a Complete Response Letter (2011) requesting cardiovascular safety data; the LIGHT CV outcomes trial (NCT01601704) was launched to satisfy this. FDA approved Contrave in September 2014; EMA approved it as Mysimba in 2015. LIGHT was terminated early in 2015 after the sponsor publicly released confidential 25% interim data, undermining the trial's ability to definitively establish CV outcomes (Nissen et al., JAMA 2016). Marketing rights later passed through Takeda, Valeant/Bausch, and Currax Pharmaceuticals.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • How much of the human clinical effect is the elegant POMC-autoinhibition-release mechanism versus simpler additive appetite/reward effects — the autoinhibition data are largely rodent electrophysiology.
  • Long-term (multi-year) cardiovascular safety and any CV benefit: never definitively established because LIGHT was terminated early.
  • Real-world durability and adherence — high early discontinuation (mainly nausea) limits effectiveness outside trials; long-term weight-maintenance data are limited.
  • Head-to-head comparative effectiveness and combinability with GLP-1/GIP agonists beyond post-hoc analyses.
  • The magnitude of the reward/craving (mesolimbic) contribution vs the homeostatic/hypothalamic contribution in humans — the reward arm is inferred, not directly demonstrated.
  • Whether any ancillary craving benefits (alcohol, smoking, binge/reward eating) translate to clinically meaningful outcomes in obesity populations.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

What to watch (harm-reduction, not a dosing guide): (1) Blood pressure and heart rate — NB can raise both; monitor especially early and in anyone with hypertension; uncontrolled hypertension is a contraindication. (2) Seizure risk — screen out and avoid in seizure disorders, bulimia/anorexia, and abrupt alcohol/benzodiazepine/antiepileptic withdrawal; seek care for any seizure. (3) Opioid status — do NOT use with any opioid (prescription or otherwise); it blocks opioid analgesia and can precipitate withdrawal; flag before any surgery or pain management. (4) Neuropsychiatric — boxed warning for suicidal thoughts/behavior (bupropion/antidepressant class); monitor mood, especially early and in younger patients. (5) Nausea/GI tolerability — the main reason people stop. (6) Liver — consider LFTs if there is a clinical concern; avoid in significant hepatic impairment. (7) Pregnancy — contraindicated; weight loss is not a pregnancy goal. (8) Drug interactions — CYP2D6 substrates (bupropion inhibits CYP2D6) and MAO inhibitors (contraindicated). Reassess benefit: if <5% weight loss after ~4 months of maintenance dosing, it is generally considered a non-responder situation.

Caveats

The elegant 'block the POMC self-braking loop' mechanism rests largely on mouse brain-slice electrophysiology (Greenway 2009); the precise contribution of each arm in the human brain — and how much of the clinical effect is hypothalamic vs mesolimbic reward vs simple bupropion/naltrexone additive effects — is not directly proven in humans. Efficacy is modest and highly dependent on continued use plus lifestyle change; stopping the drug typically causes weight regain. Both components carry independent baggage: bupropion has a dose-related seizure risk and a boxed warning for suicidal thoughts/behavior (antidepressant class effect); naltrexone is contraindicated with any opioid use (blocks opioid analgesia and can precipitate withdrawal). The combination raises blood pressure/heart rate slightly rather than lowering them, and the LIGHT cardiovascular outcomes trial was halted early, leaving hard CV outcomes unresolved. This entry is an evidence summary, not medical advice; it deliberately gives no dosing/titration protocol.
