RAD-150

Main target: Androgen receptor. Human evidence is still limited.

Works in humans?Limited human data
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A benzoate ester of the SARM RAD-140 sold as a "longer-acting next-gen" research chemical — with essentially zero direct scientific data of its own. Everything credible about it is inherited from its parent RAD-140, and even that stops at preclinical plus a single small Phase 1 oncology trial.

Limited human data 12 citations
Class
Non-steroidal selective androgen receptor modulator (SARM), supplied as a benzoate ester (a putative prodrug/depot form) of RAD-140 (testolone/vosilasarm)
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR) — tissue-selective agonist
Human status
No approval anywhere and no human clinical data on RAD-150 itself. There are zero PubMed records and zero ClinicalTrials.gov entries for RAD-150 / TLB-150 as a substance (apparent search hits like a 1975 'RAD 150' haloalkylamine code and '150 rad' radiation-dose strings are unrelated false positives). The parent RAD-140 (vosilasarm) reached only a single small Phase 1 first-in-human oncology study in postmenopausal ER+/HER2- (AR+) metastatic breast cancer (NCT03088527, Radius Health, sponsor of record Stemline Therapeutics; 22 patients enrolled, completed) and was never approved for any indication, including muscle-wasting. That trial found a human half-life of ~44.7 h, AR engagement (SHBG fell in all evaluable patients, PSA rose in most), and frequent liver-enzyme elevations. RAD-150 is sold purely as a gray-market 'research chemical.' SARMs are banned in sport by WADA and are not lawful dietary-supplement ingredients; the FDA has warned against SARM-containing products. Everything positive said about RAD-150 is extrapolation from RAD-140 plus vendor marketing — not evidence about RAD-150.

At a glance

  • Intended: increased lean muscle mass and strength via tissue-selective AR agonism (shown for parent RAD-140 in animals; anabolic endpoint never demonstrated in humans, and never for RAD-150)
  • Intended by marketing: 'longer-acting' / smoother release from the benzoate ester — an unverified claim with no PK data for RAD-150
  • Suppression of endogenous testosterone / HPT-axis shutdown (expected class effect of any effective AR agonist; consistent with SHBG suppression in the RAD-140 Phase 1 trial)
  • Hepatotoxicity — AST/ALT/bilirubin elevations were the commonest adverse events in the RAD-140 Phase 1 trial, and overt cholestatic drug-induced liver injury is documented in RAD-140 case reports
  • Cardiac injury — myocarditis, myopericarditis and heart failure reported with parent RAD-140
  • Virilization in women (voice deepening, acne, hair changes) — reported in a user anecdote for 'RAD-150' specifically, consistent with androgenic action but not formal evidence
  • Product-identity risk: 'RAD-150' capsules/liquids are unregulated and frequently mislabeled or contain a different substance than stated

Mechanism, step by step

6 steps2 human-evidenced 2 preclinical 2 speculative

  1. RAD-150 is an ester 'pro-form' — it must be cleaved to RAD-140 to do anything Speculative

    RAD-150 is RAD-140 with a benzoate group attached (marketed under the code 'TLB-150 benzoate'). By direct analogy to esterified anabolic steroids, the ester is a delivery modification: esterases in blood and tissue are presumed to hydrolyze the benzoate off, releasing the active molecule, which is the actual receptor ligand. NOTE: this specific cleavage has never been published for RAD-150 — it is inferred from how androgen esters behave in general, and ChEMBL does not classify the parent RAD-140 as a prodrug. General ester-prodrug principle: PMID 24910615 (PDE7B/esterases hydrolyze androgen esters such as testosterone enanthate to the active parent); AAS review (17-OH esterification prolongs half-life, then esterases cleave the ester to release parent) — corpus PMC OA textbook chunk. No RAD-150-specific data.

  2. The released RAD-140 binds the androgen receptor as a non-steroidal ligand Established

    Once liberated, RAD-140 acts like the parent SARM: it is a potent, orally active non-steroidal compound that binds the androgen receptor's ligand-binding domain. This is the only step in the whole chain that rests on real, published data — and it is about RAD-140, not RAD-150. PMID 24900290 (Miller et al., discovery/characterization of RAD140 as a potent oral non-steroidal SARM); PMID 30825507 (AR binding confirmed in vitro + in silico). ChEMBL CHEMBL1672635 (RAD-140/vosilasarm; C20H16ClN5O2, MW 393.8, 0 Ro5 violations).

  3. AR activation is tissue-selective: strong in muscle/bone, weaker in prostate Evidence-rodent

    SARMs like RAD-140 are designed to switch on the androgen receptor strongly in anabolic tissues (muscle, bone) while acting as a weak or partial agonist in reproductive tissue such as prostate. This selectivity is the entire marketing premise ('gains without the prostate/androgenic penalty'), but it is a matter of degree, not a clean on/off — reproductive-tissue effects are reduced, not absent. Selectivity is characterized preclinically; note that in the human Phase 1 trial PSA rose in most patients, showing prostate-axis engagement is not absent in people. PMID 24900290 (preclinical anabolic selectivity, Hershberger/primate models); PMID 30825507 (tissue-selective AR agonism characterized in vitro); PMID 34565686 (human PSA/SHBG changes indicating AR engagement).

  4. Activated AR drives a gene program that builds muscle and bone Evidence-rodent

    Ligand-bound AR translocates to the nucleus and turns on androgen-responsive genes that increase muscle protein synthesis and bone anabolism. This is the mechanism behind the 'lean-mass' effect people are chasing. Demonstrated for the parent compound in animals; on-treatment human tumor biopsies confirmed AR engagement, but an anabolic muscle endpoint was never measured for RAD-140 in humans, and never at all for RAD-150. PMID 24900290 (anabolic action on muscle/bone in preclinical models); PMID 34565686 (AR engagement confirmed on human biopsy, oncology context).

  5. The benzoate ester is claimed to slow release and extend half-life — unverified for this molecule Speculative

    The whole selling point of RAD-150 is 'longer-acting.' In steroid pharmacology, adding a carboxylic-acid ester slows absorption/clearance and lengthens half-life (e.g., testosterone enanthate's half-life is ~4.2 days vs. hours for unesterified testosterone). Vendors extrapolate this to RAD-150. But no pharmacokinetic study of RAD-150 exists, and RAD-140 lacks the free 17-hydroxyl that classic 17-OH steroid esters use, so it is not even certain the benzoate behaves like a conventional depot ester here. For reference, unesterified RAD-140 already has a fairly long human half-life of ~44.7 h. Ester to longer half-life principle: corpus AAS textbook review (enanthate ~4.2 d, propionate ~1.0 d); PMID 24910615; PMID 21383644 (PDE7B genotype affects testosterone-ester bioavailability). Parent RAD-140 human half-life 44.7 h: PMID 34565686. No RAD-150-specific PK data exist in PubMed/ClinicalTrials.gov.

  6. Off-target AR activation in heart, liver and the HPT axis produces the harms Evidence-human

    The same receptor engagement that builds muscle also hits the liver, heart and the hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular axis. In the RAD-140 Phase 1 trial, AST/ALT/bilirubin elevations were the most common adverse events; published RAD-140 case reports document overt cholestatic liver injury, myocarditis/myopericarditis and heart failure; suppression of the body's own testosterone is expected from any effective AR agonist and is consistent with the SHBG suppression seen in the trial. A longer-acting ester form would, if anything, prolong these exposures — not reduce them. Trial-level hepatotoxicity + AR-engagement (SHBG): PMID 34565686. Liver case reports: PMID 36561105, PMID 39328701. Cardiac case reports: PMID 35233331, PMID 39157568, PMID 38864168. (All human data are for RAD-140; no RAD-150-specific reports exist.)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. The pharmacophore is RAD-140 (vosilasarm), a non-steroidal oxadiazole-containing AR ligand (ChEMBL CHEMBL1672635; C20H16ClN5O2; MW ~393.8; QED ~0.67; 0 Rule-of-Five violations). It binds the androgen receptor ligand-binding domain as a tissue-selective agonist — strong agonism in anabolic tissue (muscle, bone), weak/partial agonism in reproductive tissue. Oral activity is established from preclinical work and the human Phase 1 trial (which used once-daily oral dosing), not from ChEMBL (which lists the compound's oral flag as false). RAD-150 is this molecule esterified with benzoate ('TLB-150 benzoate'); the ester is presumed pharmacologically inert until hydrolyzed to RAD-140.

Signalling cascade. RAD-150 (ester) -> presumed esterase-mediated hydrolysis to RAD-140 (inferred, never shown for this compound) -> RAD-140 binds AR LBD -> receptor activation and nuclear translocation -> transcription of androgen-responsive genes -> muscle protein synthesis / bone anabolism; concurrent AR engagement in liver, heart and hypothalamus/pituitary produces off-target and feedback-suppressive effects.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsules or liquid) as sold for RAD-150; no validated route or formulation exists for RAD-150. Parent RAD-140 was dosed orally once daily in its Phase 1 trial.
Absorption / bioavailability
Unknown for RAD-150. Parent RAD-140 is orally active (PMID 24900290; oral QD dosing in PMID 34565686). Whether the benzoate ester survives first-pass and is absorbed intact, or is cleaved presystemically, is unstudied.
Half-life
Unknown for RAD-150. The 'longer half-life' selling point is extrapolated from steroid-ester behavior (e.g., testosterone enanthate ~4.2 d vs. hours for unesterified testosterone), NOT measured for this compound. The parent RAD-140 has a measured human half-life of ~44.7 h (about 1.9 days) from the Phase 1 trial (PMID 34565686), which already supports once-daily dosing without any ester.
Metabolism
Presumed esterase/hydrolase cleavage of benzoate to RAD-140, then hepatic metabolism of RAD-140. Hydrolytic enzymes such as PDE7B/carboxylesterases mediate analogous androgen-ester activation (PMID 24910615; PMID 21383644). None of this is confirmed for RAD-150.
Excretion
Not characterized for RAD-150; RAD-140 and its metabolites are detectable in urine by anti-doping LC-MS/MS methods.
Notes
Every RAD-150 PK statement here is inference or vendor claim. There is no PK study, no Cmax/Tmax/AUC, and no bioequivalence data for RAD-150 in the literature or trial registries. The only real human PK numbers belong to unesterified RAD-140 (PMID 34565686).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

No dose-response data exist for RAD-150 in humans or animals — none can be responsibly stated, and no dosing guidance is given. For the parent RAD-140, preclinical anabolic activity was dose-dependent in animal models (PMID 24900290), and the Phase 1 oncology study explored escalating oral doses of 50, 100 and 150 mg once daily with a maximum tolerated dose of 100 mg/day, limited chiefly by liver-enzyme elevations (PMID 34565686). Harm appears achievable at self-administered 'recreational' exposures: published RAD-140 liver-injury and cardiac cases occurred after weeks-to-months of use at unregulated doses. A key hazard of an ester 'depot' form is that if the longer-acting claim is true, an adverse reaction cannot be quickly reversed by stopping, because drug keeps releasing.

How it compares

  • vs RAD-140 (testolone/vosilasarm, the parent)
    same: Same active pharmacophore and AR-agonist mechanism once the ester is cleaved; same expected harms.
    different: RAD-150 adds a benzoate ester marketed as longer-acting; unlike RAD-140 (which has preclinical data plus one human Phase 1 trial) it has literally no data of its own, so it is strictly less well-characterized, not 'improved.' And RAD-140 already has a ~44.7 h human half-life without any ester.
  • vs Testosterone esters (enanthate/cypionate)
    same: Ester strategy borrowed from AAS to slow release and extend half-life; esterases regenerate the active molecule.
    different: Classic AAS esterify a 17-beta-hydroxyl; RAD-140 lacks that group, so the benzoate is attached elsewhere and depot behavior is not guaranteed. Testosterone esters are pharma-grade and studied; RAD-150 is neither.
  • vs Other 'ester-ized' gray-market SARMs (e.g., ostarine acetate)
    same: Same gray-market pattern: take a known SARM, add an ester, market as next-gen, ship with near-zero PK/safety data.
    different: Each behaves differently and none is well-studied; sharing the 'ester' label does not confer shared, verified pharmacology.

History & legal status

RAD-140 was created at Radius Health (Miller et al.; PMID 24900290) as an anabolic/muscle-wasting and later oncology candidate; it advanced to a single Phase 1 breast-cancer trial (NCT03088527; results PMID 34565686) under the name vosilasarm and was never approved. SARMs as a class are WADA-prohibited in sport and are not legal dietary-supplement ingredients; the U.S. FDA has issued warnings about bodybuilding products containing SARMs. RAD-150 / 'TLB-150 benzoate' emerged (circa 2023-2025) purely as a gray-market research-chemical spin-off — an ester analog created and sold outside any regulatory or clinical framework. It has no INN of its own, no trial registry entry, and no regulator has evaluated it.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether RAD-150 is actually hydrolyzed to RAD-140 in humans, and how efficiently.
  • Its real pharmacokinetics — is it genuinely longer-acting than RAD-140's ~44.7 h, and by how much? (no RAD-150 PK data exist).
  • Whether the benzoate placement even permits classic depot behavior given RAD-140's structure (no free 17-OH).
  • Potency, dose-response and therapeutic-vs-toxic margin in humans.
  • Whether the ester changes the toxicity profile (e.g., prolonged liver/cardiac exposure) vs RAD-140.
  • Long-term effects, reversibility of HPT-axis suppression, and fertility outcomes.
  • What is actually in gray-market 'RAD-150' products (identity, purity, contaminants).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Educational harm-reduction only — not an endorsement to use, and no dosing, cycling, or PCT guidance is provided. If someone has taken a product sold as RAD-150, clinically relevant things to MONITOR (with a clinician) include: liver function (ALT, AST, ALP, bilirubin), given that transaminase/bilirubin elevations were the commonest adverse events in the parent-drug trial and biopsy-proven cholestatic injury is documented — watch for jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, itching, or right-upper-quadrant pain; cardiac symptoms (chest pain, breathlessness, palpitations, exertional intolerance, swelling) given reported myocarditis/heart failure, with a low threshold for ECG/troponin/echo evaluation; a hormonal panel (total/free testosterone, LH, FSH, estradiol; SHBG fell in the trial) to detect HPT-axis suppression, plus lipids and hematocrit; and in women, signs of virilization (voice change, acne, hair-pattern change), some of which may not fully reverse. Because an ester 'depot' cannot be quickly cleared if the longer-acting claim holds, any adverse reaction warrants prompt medical attention rather than 'waiting it out.'

Caveats

This is an emerging/near-data-free compound. Treat the entire entry as evidence-by-analogy: RAD-150 has NO published mechanistic, pharmacokinetic, animal, or human data of its own (PubMed and ClinicalTrials.gov return nothing for it as a substance). The mechanism chain is built from (a) established RAD-140 pharmacology, including one small human Phase 1 trial, and (b) the general principle that androgen esters are hydrolyzed to an active parent — neither of which has been demonstrated for RAD-150 specifically. The 'longer-acting/next-gen' claim is a marketing assertion with zero supporting PK data, and is chemically uncertain because RAD-140 does not carry the free 17-hydroxyl that classic steroid depot esters exploit (and ChEMBL does not flag RAD-140 as a prodrug). Human harm data (liver-enzyme elevations in-trial; overt liver injury, myocarditis and heart failure in case reports) come from RAD-140; a longer-acting ester would plausibly prolong rather than reduce these exposures. Products sold as 'RAD-150' are unregulated and, per analytical studies of the SARM market, frequently mislabeled, under/over-dosed, or contain a different compound entirely — so what is in the bottle may not even be this molecule. This entry is educational mechanism information and explicitly not a dosing protocol or endorsement. No dosing, titration, or 'cycle' guidance is provided by design.
