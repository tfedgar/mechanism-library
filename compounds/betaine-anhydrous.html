Betaine anhydrous

Main target: Dual. Human evidence is still limited.

EvidenceLimited human data
ApprovalFDA-approved

A cheap, food-derived osmolyte that doubles as a methyl donor — with real (if modest) RCT signals for strength and power, and a legitimate role as a homocysteine-lowering orphan drug.

Limited human dataFDA-approved 12 citations
Class
Methyl donor / organic osmolyte (trimethylglycine); FDA/EMA-approved metabolic agent for homocystinuria (ATC A16AA06)
Primary target
Dual: (1) intracellular organic osmolyte driving cell hydration/volume; (2) methyl-group substrate for betaine-homocysteine S-methyltransferase (BHMT), remethylating homocysteine to methionine and thereby feeding S-adenosylmethionine (SAMe)
Human status
Extensively studied in humans and an approved drug. Betaine anhydrous (Cystadane, FDA 1996; EMA Cystadane/Amversio) is approved for homocystinurias, with decades of clinical use and a formal post-authorization safety study (Mütze 2022, PMID 35358327). Separately, its ergogenic use rests on multiple small (n~12-30) double-blind RCTs in resistance-trained men and athletes, showing modest and endpoint-inconsistent gains in power, force and training volume at roughly 2-2.5 g/day. Its homocysteine-lowering effect is robustly demonstrated in placebo-controlled human trials. It is not a novel or pipeline compound — the open questions are about the SIZE and MECHANISM of the fitness benefit, not whether betaine reaches and acts in humans.

At a glance

  • Reliably lowers fasting plasma homocysteine in a dose-dependent manner (best-proven human effect)
  • Small, inconsistent improvements in muscular power and isometric force in short resistance-training RCTs
  • Modest increase in bench-press training volume / reps-to-failure (~6.5%) at ~2.5 g/day
  • FDA/EMA-approved (as Cystadane) to lower homocysteine in inborn homocystinurias
  • Common creatine adjunct in pre-workouts (rationale = methyl-donor support for creatine synthesis; additive benefit unproven)
  • May modestly raise TOTAL cholesterol at higher supplemental doses (>=4 g/day; meta-analysis signal — no significant effect on LDL, HDL or triglycerides)
  • Generally well tolerated; main issues are GI upset and, at high doses, a fishy body/breath odor

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps1 human 4 established 2 contested / speculative

  1. Betaine is trimethylglycine — a zwitterionic small molecule Established

    Betaine anhydrous is glycine carrying three methyl groups on its nitrogen (trimethylglycine, C5H12NO2+, MW 118.16). ChEMBL catalogs it as CHEMBL95889, an orally available natural product with zero Lipinski (rule-of-five) violations and max clinical phase 4 (approved). Its two chemical 'jobs' both flow from this structure: the charged, water-attracting zwitterion makes it an osmolyte, and the three N-methyl groups make it a methyl donor. ChEMBL CHEMBL95889 (max_phase 4, first approval 1996)

  2. As an osmolyte, betaine accumulates inside cells and pulls water in Speculative

    Cells take betaine up via osmolyte transporters (e.g. BGT-1) and hold it to balance osmotic stress without disrupting proteins (a 'compatible solute' / chemical-chaperone osmolyte). In muscle this is PROPOSED to increase cell water content / cell swelling, a state classically linked to a pro-anabolic, anti-catabolic signal. The osmolyte role is well established in general cell biology; the specific claim that oral supplemental doses meaningfully swell HUMAN muscle cells during training is an inference — it has not been directly measured in humans and rests on no primary human study. Mechanistic rationale; no primary human muscle-cell measurement identified

  3. As a methyl donor, betaine remethylates homocysteine to methionine via BHMT Established

    The enzyme betaine-homocysteine S-methyltransferase (BHMT, EC 2.1.1.5), a zinc metalloenzyme mainly in liver and kidney, transfers one methyl group from betaine to homocysteine, producing methionine and dimethylglycine. This is an alternative to the folate/B12 remethylation route. It is the mechanism behind betaine's approved medical use and behind measurable drops in plasma homocysteine in healthy people. Olthof 2003 PMID 14652361; Morris 2016 PMID 27778219

  4. More methionine feeds the SAMe methylation cycle Established

    Methionine made by BHMT is converted to S-adenosylmethionine (SAMe), the body's universal methyl donor for DNA, proteins, creatine synthesis (the guanidinoacetate methylation step) and phospholipids. Topping up methionine can support SAMe availability and one-carbon metabolism. This is established biochemistry; the further inference that it drives training adaptation is theoretical. Morris 2016 PMID 27778219

  5. Betaine lowers plasma homocysteine in a dose-dependent way (proven human effect) Established

    In a placebo-controlled trial in healthy adults, low-dose betaine (~1.5, 3, 6 g/day) produced immediate and sustained dose-dependent reductions in fasting and post-methionine-load plasma homocysteine (12%, 15%, 20% lower fasting at 6 wk vs placebo). This is the single most robust, reproducible human effect of betaine and the clearest downstream readout of the BHMT/methyl-donor mechanism. Olthof 2003 PMID 14652361

  6. Ergogenic signal: improved power and force in short RCTs (~2.5 g/day) Evidence-human

    In a double-blind crossover RCT (n=12), 14 days of betaine increased bench-throw power and isometric bench/squat force versus placebo, most clearly in smaller upper-body muscles, though it did not increase repetitions. A separate crossover RCT at 2.5 g/day found ~6.5% more total bench-press reps/volume load but no change in peak power or force. The effect is real but small, inconsistent across endpoints, and the mechanism (osmolyte cell-swelling vs. creatine/methylation support vs. endocrine) is not settled. Lee 2010 PMID 20642826; Trepanowski 2011 PMID 22080324

  7. Proposed anabolic-endocrine and creatine-adjunct effects are plausible but unsettled Contested

    Betaine has been proposed to raise the testosterone:cortisol ratio and IGF-1 and to support endogenous creatine synthesis (creatine's guanidinoacetate step needs a SAMe methyl group), which is why it appears in creatine-containing pre-workouts. In one youth-soccer cohort betaine raised testosterone and the T:cortisol ratio (but not IGF-1); a separate BFR study found higher post-exercise IGF-1 with betaine. These signals come largely from a single research group or single studies, are small, and inconsistent across endpoints, and betaine has NOT been shown to raise muscle creatine or to add measurably on top of co-ingested creatine in humans. Nobari 2021 PMID 33663545 (T:cortisol); Machek 2022 PMID 36501070 (IGF-1)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Not a receptor ligand. Two mechanisms: (1) an intracellular organic osmolyte/'compatible solute' that accumulates in cells (via osmolyte transporters such as BGT-1) to buffer osmotic stress and act as a chemical chaperone; (2) the methyl-donor cosubstrate for betaine-homocysteine S-methyltransferase (BHMT, EC 2.1.1.5), a zinc metalloenzyme in liver/kidney that transfers one of betaine's three N-methyl groups to homocysteine.

Signalling cascade. BHMT: betaine + homocysteine -> dimethylglycine + methionine. Methionine -> S-adenosylmethionine (SAMe, universal methyl donor) -> methylation reactions (incl. guanidinoacetate -> creatine) -> S-adenosylhomocysteine -> homocysteine, closing the cycle. In parallel, betaine osmotically hydrates cells, a state associated with pro-anabolic/anti-catabolic signaling.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral; highly water-soluble zwitterion.
Absorption / bioavailability
Well absorbed orally; also obtained from diet (wheat bran, spinach, beets — 'beta' from Beta vulgaris/beetroot). Endogenously produced from choline oxidation.
Half-life
Plasma betaine rises within hours of dosing; homocysteine-lowering is seen acutely and sustained with daily dosing. Precise elimination half-life is not a primary determinant of use given daily dosing.
Metabolism
Acts as a methyl donor via BHMT (liver/kidney), yielding dimethylglycine (further demethylated to sarcosine then glycine) and methionine. Not dependent on hepatic CYP metabolism.
Excretion
Renal handling of betaine and its demethylated products; betaine is also reabsorbed/retained as an osmolyte in the kidney medulla.
Notes
Because it is a normal dietary and endogenous metabolite, 'baseline' betaine varies with diet (choline/betaine intake) and renal function.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Homocysteine lowering is dose-dependent and begins at low intakes (~1.5-3 g/day lowers fasting homocysteine in healthy adults; ~6 g/day lowers it more). The ergogenic literature clusters around ~2-2.5 g/day (Trepanowski used 2.5 g/day; Lee used twice-daily dosing; Nobari used 2 g/day over a season; the Machek BFR study used 6 g/day). There is no established dose-response for strength/power — 2-2.5 g/day is a convention inherited from the early RCTs, not an optimized dose. The cholesterol meta-analysis signal specifically applies to trials at >=4 g/day. In approved homocystinuria treatment, doses are far higher and titrated to plasma homocysteine — a medical use that should not be extrapolated to fitness. No dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Creatine monohydrate
    same: Both are cheap, food-derived, involve one-carbon/creatine-adjacent metabolism, and appear together in pre-workouts.
    different: Creatine has large, replicated evidence for strength/power/lean mass and a validated mechanism (phosphocreatine energy buffering); betaine's ergogenic evidence is small, inconsistent, and mechanistically unsettled. Betaine has not been shown to add on top of creatine.
  • vs Beta-alanine
    same: Both are common pre-workout ingredients marketed for performance; both have modest, endpoint-specific effects.
    different: Beta-alanine has a well-defined mechanism (raises muscle carnosine -> H+ buffering) and better-characterized dose-response; betaine's performance mechanism (osmolyte vs methyl-donor) remains ambiguous.
  • vs Choline / folic acid (other homocysteine-lowering nutrients)
    same: All feed one-carbon metabolism and can lower homocysteine; choline is betaine's metabolic precursor.
    different: Folate/B12 lower homocysteine via the alternative methionine-synthase route; betaine works via BHMT and is the go-to when the folate route is impaired. Lowering homocysteine has not translated to cardiovascular event reduction for any of them.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Betaine (trimethylglycine) was first isolated from sugar beet (Beta vulgaris), giving it its name. Betaine anhydrous (Cystadane) was approved by the FDA in 1996 and by the EMA (Cystadane; also Amversio) as an orphan drug for the treatment of homocystinuria (CBS deficiency, MTHFR deficiency, and cobalamin cofactor metabolism defects), where it lowers dangerously elevated homocysteine (ATC A16AA06; ChEMBL CHEMBL95889, max_phase 4, first approval 1996). A formal post-authorization safety study (Mütze 2022, PMID 35358327) supported its long-term safety in that population. Separately, betaine anhydrous is sold as a dietary supplement and is a common ingredient in pre-workout/creatine stacks. Its supplement use is regulatorily distinct from and far less evidenced than its orphan-drug indication.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Whether oral betaine actually increases human muscle cell volume/hydration at supplemental doses, or whether cell-swelling is only a general osmolyte rationale never measured in human muscle.
  • The true effect size and durability of the strength/power benefit beyond small, short, industry-adjacent RCTs.
  • Whether betaine adds any measurable benefit on top of creatine, despite being marketed as a creatine adjunct.
  • Whether the testosterone:cortisol / IGF-1 signals (largely from one research group's youth-athlete cohort) are real, reproducible outside that setting, and mechanistically causal for any performance change.
  • The clinical meaning of the total-cholesterol-raising signal at >=4 g/day over the long term.
  • Whether homocysteine lowering by betaine confers any cardiovascular benefit — early endothelial-function (FMD) and event data are unsupportive.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is educational, not a protocol. For general supplement users: watch for GI upset and, at higher doses, a fishy body odor (both dose-related and reversible). Given the meta-analytic signal that betaine can modestly raise TOTAL cholesterol at >=4 g/day, anyone using it chronically at higher doses — especially with existing dyslipidemia or cardiovascular risk — should be aware a lipid panel is the relevant thing to track with a clinician. In the medical (homocystinuria) setting, treatment is monitored via plasma homocysteine and methionine under clinician oversight because excessive hypermethioninemia (rarely, cerebral edema) is the key risk. People with kidney disease or on multiple methyl-donor supplements should involve a clinician. No dosing or 'how to run it' guidance is given here; consult a healthcare professional before starting.

Caveats

Betaine is a genuine established compound, but honesty about the fitness signal matters: the ergogenic RCTs are small, short (often 2 weeks), industry-adjacent (several used Danisco/DuPont betaine and share authors/suppliers), and inconsistent — some endpoints improve while others in the same study do not, and effects cluster in upper-body/small muscles. The 'cell-swelling drives hypertrophy' story is a well-established osmolyte principle in general cell biology but has NOT been directly shown to swell human muscle cells at supplemental doses; it is a mechanistic inference, not a measured human finding. Endocrine (testosterone/IGF-1) and creatine-adjunct claims are the weakest links — the T:cortisol signal comes from a single research group's youth-athlete cohort (a puberty-confounded setting), IGF-1 findings are mixed, and betaine has not been shown to add measurably on top of creatine. A meta-analysis of trials at >=4 g/day found betaine modestly raised TOTAL cholesterol (by ~0.34 mmol/L) with no significant change in LDL, HDL or triglycerides — so the methyl-donor 'more is better' intuition is not free of tradeoffs, but the lipid signal is narrower than a whole-panel worsening. This is mechanism education, not a protocol or medical advice.
