Mazdutide

A weekly "twin-key" peptide that hits both the GLP-1 brake (less hunger) and the glucagon throttle (more calorie burn) — China's first-approved dual agonist, with up to ~16-18% body-weight loss in phase 3.

Limited human data 19 citations
Class
GLP-1 receptor / glucagon receptor (GLP-1R/GCGR) dual agonist; a long-acting, once-weekly injectable peptide (an engineered oxyntomodulin analogue; also known as IBI362 / LY3305677)
Primary target
Two G-protein-coupled receptors at once: the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) and the glucagon receptor (GCGR)
Human status
APPROVED (China), not a research-only peptide — an important distinction for a peptide audience. Mazdutide (brand Xinermei, Innovent Biologics + Eli Lilly) was approved in China in June 2025 for long-term weight management in adults (BMI ≥28, or ≥24 with a weight-related comorbidity), and in September 2025 for glycemic control in type 2 diabetes. Approval rests on real phase 3 human trials: GLORY-1 (NEJM 2025, n=610) and GLORY-2 (JAMA 2026, n=461) for obesity, plus phase 3 T2D trials (Nature 2026). It is NOT yet FDA/EMA-approved (data so far are predominantly in Chinese adults), and head-to-head trials vs semaglutide (DREAMS-3) and MASLD/sleep-apnea/alcohol-use-disorder studies are ongoing. So: the appetite (GLP-1) and glycemic effects are solidly human-proven; the glucagon-driven 'extra calorie burn' and liver-fat benefits are mechanistically promising but lean heavily on rodent data with human confirmation still maturing.

At a glance

  • Substantial weight loss: ~14% at 48 wks on 6 mg (GLORY-1, NEJM) and ~16.7% at 60 wks on 9 mg (GLORY-2, JAMA) in Chinese adults with obesity, vs minimal change on placebo
  • Blood-sugar control in type 2 diabetes: HbA1c reductions up to ~2% vs placebo (phase 3, Nature), with the glucagon arm's sugar-raising tendency offset by the GLP-1 arm
  • Broad cardiometabolic improvements: better lipids, blood pressure, and waist circumference reported alongside weight loss
  • Liver fat reduction / MASLD potential — the standout theoretical advantage of adding glucagon vs pure GLP-1 drugs (rodent + early human signals; confirmatory trials ongoing)
  • Main side effects are GI: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, reduced appetite — mostly mild-to-moderate, concentrated during dose escalation; low discontinuation rates in trials (~1.5-2.9%)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. One peptide, two receptors (built from oxyntomodulin) Established

    Mazdutide is a synthetic peptide modeled on oxyntomodulin, a natural gut hormone that already talks to BOTH the GLP-1 and glucagon receptors. Engineers fused it to a carrier and tweaked it so it survives in the body for about a week (once-weekly shot) and leans harder on GLP-1R than GCGR. So a single molecule pushes two metabolic levers at the same time. Shirley 2025, Drugs (First Approval), PMID 41028652; Neff 2025, Diabetes Obes Metab, PMID 41025406

  2. GLP-1 arm: turns down appetite in the brain Established

    The GLP-1R half acts on appetite centers in the brain (hypothalamus/hindbrain) and on the gut. It makes you feel full sooner, dials down hunger and food 'noise,' and slows stomach emptying. This is the same lever semaglutide pulls — the best-understood, human-proven part of the mechanism. Result: you eat less. Son 2026, Endocr Rev, PMID 41054801; Zhu 2026, Nature (phase 3 T2D), PMID 41407859

  3. GLP-1 arm: helps control blood sugar Evidence-human

    The GLP-1R also tells the pancreas to release insulin when glucose is high and to ease off glucagon's sugar-raising signal in a glucose-dependent way. This is what lets the drug ADD a glucagon agonist (which on its own would tend to raise blood sugar) without losing glycemic control. In the phase 3 diabetes trial, HbA1c dropped up to ~2% versus placebo. Zhu 2026, Nature, PMID 41407859; Guo 2026, Nature (vs dulaglutide), PMID 41407860

  4. Glucagon arm: speeds up calorie burn (energy expenditure) Evidence-rodent

    The glucagon-receptor half is the differentiator versus pure GLP-1 drugs. Activating GCGR is thought to raise energy expenditure — the body burns more calories at rest — adding to the 'eat less' effect. Importantly, this 'burn more' piece is robust in rodents but only partly confirmed in humans; direct human energy-expenditure data for mazdutide specifically are still thin. Treat the size of this contribution as not yet nailed down. Elmendorf 2026, Pharmacol Res (IUPHAR review), PMID 41478576; Neff 2025, PMID 41025406

  5. Glucagon arm: pulls fat out of the liver Evidence-rodent

    Glucagon acts strongly on the liver, where its receptor is concentrated: it pushes the liver to break down and oxidize fat (lipolysis + mitochondrial fat-burning) and to handle lipids better. This is why dual GLP-1/glucagon agonists look especially promising for fatty liver disease (MASLD). Rodent and early human/imaging data point this way; mazdutide is in dedicated MASLD trials, results pending. Neff 2025, Diabetes Obes Metab, PMID 41025406; Elmendorf 2026, Pharmacol Res, PMID 41478576

  6. Net effect: the two arms add up Evidence-human

    Combine 'eat less' (GLP-1) with 'burn more + cleaner liver' (glucagon) and you get larger weight loss plus metabolic improvements (blood sugar, lipids, blood pressure, liver fat). The GLP-1R bias keeps glucagon's downside (higher glucose) in check. The trade-off is GLP-1-class side effects — mostly nausea, vomiting and diarrhea, usually mild-to-moderate and worst during dose escalation. (Phase-2: up to ~14% body-weight loss at 6 mg over 48 weeks.) Ji 2025, NEJM (GLORY-1 phase 3), PMID 40421736; Gao 2026, JAMA (GLORY-2 phase 3), PMID 42251595

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Mazdutide (IBI362 / LY3305677) is a once-weekly synthetic 39-amino-acid peptide based on the human OXYNTOMODULIN scaffold — the natural gut hormone that, like all peptides in this family (glucagon, GLP-1, GIP, secretin), shares sequence homology and engages class B (secretin-family) G-protein-coupled receptors [Established; PMID 41025406, 40081498]. It is a DUAL AGONIST: a full/near-full agonist at both the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) and the glucagon receptor (GCGR), licensed from Eli Lilly's oxyntomodulin-analog program [Established; PMID 36247927, 40421736]. It carries a fatty-acid (lipid) chain attached to the peptide backbone; this acyl group binds circulating ALBUMIN, which is what slows clearance and gives the ~once-weekly half-life (the same engineering principle used in semaglutide and liraglutide) [Established mechanism for the class; mazdutide-specific half-life ~8 days confirmed PMID 40832785]. CRITICAL CERTAINTY GAP: the exact equilibrium binding affinities (Ki) and functional potencies (cAMP EC50) of mazdutide at human GLP-1R vs GCGR, and the precise potency RATIO between the two receptors, are held in Innovent/Lilly internal pharmacology and are NOT quantified in the peer-reviewed literature retrieved here. The drug is consistently described as 'GLP-1R-biased' — i.e. relatively more GLP-1R than GCGR activity — but published EC50 numbers for mazdutide specifically were not found [Contested/under-reported; no verifiable EC50 source found in this run]. The general principle that the GLP-1R:GCGR potency ratio determines the efficacy/safety balance (more GCGR = more weight/liver-fat effect but more risk of hyperglycemia and tachycardia) is established from analog-series work [Evidence-rodent; PMID 38518602].

Signalling cascade. Both target receptors are Gs-coupled class B GPCRs. (1) GLP-1R ARM — In pancreatic beta cells, agonist binding activates Gs -> adenylyl cyclase -> rises in cAMP -> protein kinase A (PKA) and Epac2 activation -> closure of K_ATP channels, membrane depolarization, Ca2+ influx, and GLUCOSE-DEPENDENT insulin exocytosis (glucose-dependence is why hypoglycemia risk is low on its own). The same pathway suppresses alpha-cell glucagon secretion at high glucose and is trophic to beta cells. In the brain (hypothalamic arcuate nucleus, area postrema, NTS), GLP-1R activation drives satiety/reduced appetite and slows gastric emptying via vagal and central circuits — the dominant driver of food-intake reduction and the GI side effects [Established; PMID 41312047, 39568409, 40081498]. (2) GCGR ARM — In hepatocytes, glucagon-receptor Gs/cAMP/PKA signalling stimulates lipolysis and mitochondrial fatty-acid (fat) OXIDATION, increases energy expenditure, promotes hepatic amino-acid catabolism (lowering circulating gluconeogenic amino acids), and reduces intrahepatic fat — the basis for the liver-fat and added weight-loss benefit beyond pure GLP-1 agonism [Established/Evidence-human; PMID 41025406; biomarker confirmation — fasting gluconeogenic amino acids fell and ketone (beta-hydroxybutyrate) rose >900-1200% in humans on 16 mg, PMID 40832785]. The 'arms add up' concept: GLP-1R cuts calorie INTAKE (appetite) while GCGR raises energy OUTPUT and burns liver fat, so the two effects are complementary on body weight; GCGR's tendency to raise glucose is offset by the GLP-1R insulinotropic arm [Established; PMID 41025406, 40421736].

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection, once weekly. No oral or other form is approved or in late development [Established; PMID 40421736, 40832785].
Absorption / bioavailability
Absorbed from subcutaneous depot; as a large acylated peptide it is not orally bioavailable (would be digested), which is why it is injected. Exposure was dose-proportional/moderately variable across the 16 mg high-dose study; steady weekly dosing is supported by the long half-life [Evidence-human; PMID 40832785].
Half-life
Approximately 8 DAYS (terminal half-life of the 16 mg dose by non-compartmental analysis), which directly supports the once-weekly subcutaneous interval. Doses are uptitrated stepwise over weeks (e.g., 3->6 mg, or escalations toward 9/10/16 mg) to limit GI side effects [Evidence-human; PMID 40832785; titration PMID 36247927].
Metabolism
As a peptide it is NOT metabolized by cytochrome P450 enzymes. Like other acylated therapeutic peptides (semaglutide class), it is expected to be cleared by proteolytic degradation into amino acids and small fragments throughout the body; specific peptidase pathways for mazdutide were not detailed in the retrieved sources [Established for class / Evidence-in vitro by analogy; no mazdutide-specific enzymology source found].
Excretion
Peptide catabolites and amino acids are recycled/excreted via normal protein handling (no intact-drug renal/biliary excretion expected for an acylated peptide). Mazdutide-specific mass-balance excretion data were not found in the retrieved literature [class inference; no mazdutide-specific source].
Notes
The fatty-acid side chain is NOT a prodrug ester — it is a permanent albumin-binding lipid that extends half-life. Albumin binding also keeps the large peptide in circulation and limits free drug. Because it is a large peptide, central nervous system effects (appetite, satiety) are thought to act mainly via circumventricular/hindbrain regions that lack a tight blood-brain barrier rather than broad BBB penetration, consistent with the GLP-1R class [Established for class; PMID 40081498]. No clinically significant CYP-mediated drug-drug interactions are expected, though it slows gastric emptying which can alter absorption of co-administered oral drugs (a class effect) [Evidence-human; gastric-emptying delay confirmed via acetaminophen test PMID 40832785].

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human dose-response is well characterized for WEIGHT and GLYCEMIA across phase 1-3 (all in Chinese/East-Asian or US populations); the curve is clearly dose-dependent. WEIGHT (non-diabetic obesity/overweight): phase 2 at 24 wk gave -6.7% (3 mg), -10.4% (4.5 mg), -11.3% (6 mg) vs +1.0% placebo [PMID 38092790]; phase 3 GLORY-1 at 48 wk gave -11.0% (4 mg) and -14.0% (6 mg) vs +0.3% placebo, with ~50% of the 6 mg arm losing >=15% [PMID 40421736]; high-dose phase 1 pushed to 16 mg gave ~-20 to -21% at 20 wk (n=24), the largest mazdutide weight effect reported, with 67-75% achieving >=15% [Evidence-human; PMID 40832785]. GLYCEMIA (type 2 diabetes): phase 2 HbA1c fell ~-1.4 to -1.7% at doses up to 6 mg [PMID 37943529]; phase 3 monotherapy gave HbA1c -1.57% (4 mg) and -2.15% (6 mg) vs -0.14% placebo at 24 wk [PMID 41407859]; vs dulaglutide 1.5 mg, both 4 and 6 mg were non-inferior/superior on HbA1c and clearly superior on weight [PMID 41407860]. GCGR-ARM target engagement is dose-dependent in humans: at 16 mg, fasting ketones rose ~900-1200%, endogenous glucagon fell 91-97%, and gluconeogenic amino acids dropped — direct evidence the glucagon-receptor arm scales with dose [Evidence-human; PMID 40832785]. RODENT analog work shows that as the GCGR component is increased relative to GLP-1R, weight-loss and liver-fat benefit increase but so does the safety signal (hyperglycemia, tachycardia) — defining the therapeutic window [Evidence-rodent; PMID 38518602]. UNKNOWN: a formal exposure-response (concentration vs effect) model and the maximally tolerated long-term dose in large populations are not established; almost all efficacy data are East-Asian, so dose-response in other ancestries and at >6 mg long-term is uncertain.

How it compares

  • vs Cotadutide (MEDI0382, AstraZeneca) — GLP-1R/GCGR dual agonist
    same: Same dual-receptor target class (GLP-1R + GCGR), oxyntomodulin-derived, peptide, weight + glucose + liver-fat benefit; pooled with mazdutide in meta-analyses.
    different: Cotadutide is DAILY (shorter half-life) vs mazdutide's WEEKLY dosing; different receptor-potency balance; cotadutide development de-emphasized while mazdutide advanced to phase 3 and approval pathway. [PMID 38740695, 41025406]
  • vs Survodutide (BI 456906, Boehringer/Zealand) — GLP-1R/GCGR dual agonist
    same: Same dual GLP-1R/GCGR mechanism, weekly peptide, strong weight loss and MASH/liver-fat program.
    different: Different molecule and receptor-potency ratio; survodutide has a prominent MASH (liver fibrosis) program; both are direct competitors in the GCGR-based class, compared in network meta-analyses. [PMID 41787737, 41025406]
  • vs Tirzepatide (GLP-1R/GIP dual agonist) and semaglutide (GLP-1R mono)
    same: Overlapping GLP-1R arm: glucose-dependent insulin secretion, appetite suppression, gastric slowing, similar GI side-effect profile; all weekly injectables for weight/diabetes.
    different: Mazdutide's SECOND receptor is GLUCAGON (energy expenditure + liver-fat oxidation), whereas tirzepatide's second receptor is GIP and semaglutide has none. This gives mazdutide a distinct energy-OUTPUT mechanism (ketones up, glucagon-driven fat burn) but adds glucagon-specific signals (heart-rate rise, theoretical hyperglycemia if mis-balanced). In DREAMS-2 mazdutide beat dulaglutide; head-to-head vs semaglutide (DREAMS-3) is ongoing. [PMID 41407860, 41260459, 40081498]
  • vs Retatrutide (GLP-1R/GIP/GCGR TRIPLE agonist)
    same: Shares both the GLP-1R and GCGR arms with mazdutide; same Lilly-derived peptide lineage and glucagon-energy-expenditure concept.
    different: Retatrutide adds a third receptor (GIP) on top of GLP-1+glucagon, generally producing larger weight loss in early trials; mazdutide is the two-receptor (GLP-1+GCGR) member of the same family. [PMID 41025406, 39305981]

History & legal status

"Originated at Eli Lilly as an oxyntomodulin-analog GLP-1R/GCGR dual agonist (LY3305677); licensed to Innovent Biologics (China) and developed as IBI362, now INN 'mazdutide' (USAN/-dutide stem = oxyntomodulin analogs, USAN 2021). ChEMBL: CHEMBL5095358, max clinical phase recorded as 3 (a SEQ/protein entity, no small-molecule structure deposited). Clinical program (GLORY for obesity, DREAMS for diabetes) ran phase 1b (NCT04440345), phase 2 obesity (NCT04904913) and T2D, phase 3 GLORY-1 obesity (NCT05607680), phase 3 T2D vs placebo and vs dulaglutide (DREAMS-2), with DREAMS-3 (vs semaglutide, NCT design published 2025, PMID 41260459) ongoing, plus a US high-dose phase 1 to 16 mg (NCT05623839). Regulatory status: developed primarily for the Chinese market via Innovent; as of the retrieved literature (through 2026) it is at/near approval in China for weight management and type 2 diabetes (NMPA pathway) — exact approval dates were not confirmed in the peer-reviewed sources retrieved here [history Established; precise current approval/marketing status should be re-verified against a regulatory source — not confirmed in this run]. It is NOT FDA- or EMA-approved in the retrieved record. No bans noted; it is a prescription investigational/approved biologic, not a scheduled/controlled substance, and is being marketed in fitness/PED circles as a research peptide outside any approved supply chain — that grey-market use is unregulated and unverified for purity."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Exact human receptor pharmacology: published Ki/EC50 values and the precise GLP-1R:GCGR potency ratio for mazdutide are not in the peer-reviewed literature — the 'GLP-1R-biased' label is qualitative, so the true degree of glucagon engagement at clinical doses is not publicly quantified.
  • Long-term cardiovascular safety of the persistent heart-rate increase (~13-15 bpm, ~92% of high-dose users): no cardiovascular-outcomes trial data; whether chronic glucagon-driven tachycardia translates to adverse CV events is unknown.
  • Generalizability beyond East-Asian populations: nearly all phase 2/3 efficacy and safety data are from Chinese adults; dose-response, tolerability and effect size in other ancestries and the long-term safety at high doses (9-16 mg) are not established.
  • Hepatic safety signal: the transient ALT elevations at high dose vs the net liver-fat-LOWERING benefit are not reconciled — predictors of who gets enzyme rises, and long-term liver outcomes (MASH/fibrosis endpoints), remain open.
  • Durability and rebound: weight regain after stopping (the 16 mg study showed partial regain in the 8-week off-drug window), and whether GCGR co-agonism preserves lean mass better than GLP-1 mono-agonists, are unresolved.
  • Mechanistic contribution split: how much of the weight loss comes from the GLP-1 (appetite/intake) arm vs the glucagon (energy-expenditure/fat-oxidation) arm in humans is inferred from biomarkers (ketones, amino acids) but not directly partitioned; the magnitude of true human energy-expenditure increase is uncertain.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT dosing guidance). Resting HEART RATE and blood pressure: pulse rises in the large majority of users (mean ~13-15 bpm at high dose) — track resting HR and report palpitations or sustained tachycardia. LIVER enzymes (ALT/AST): transient elevations occurred at high dose, including one >3x ULN that needed a dose hold — baseline and periodic LFTs are reasonable, and new right-upper-quadrant pain, jaundice or dark urine warrant urgent evaluation. GLYCEMIA: fasting glucose and HbA1c (net lowering expected, but watch for hypoglycemia symptoms especially if combined with insulin/sulfonylureas, and conversely watch for any unexpected glucose rise from the glucagon arm). KETONES: beta-hydroxybutyrate can rise dramatically (~900-1200% at 16 mg) — be aware of this, distinguish benign nutritional/pharmacologic ketosis from ketoacidosis (ketones + high glucose + illness is an emergency). GI tolerability: nausea/vomiting/diarrhea are the main reasons people stop; persistent vomiting risks dehydration and gallstone formation with rapid weight loss — watch for severe abdominal pain (gallbladder, rare pancreatitis). WEIGHT and lean mass: track rate of loss and, ideally, body composition. HYDRATION/electrolytes during heavy GI side effects. GREY-MARKET CAUTION: 'research peptide' mazdutide is not quality-controlled — purity, dose accuracy and sterility are unverified, which compounds every risk above. Any of these monitoring red flags should prompt review with a clinician, not self-management."

Caveats

DO NOT overstate the glucagon 'fat-burning' story to a fitness audience: the energy-expenditure and liver-fat benefits of GCGR agonism are well-demonstrated in rodents but only partially confirmed in humans, and the precise human contribution for mazdutide specifically is not yet quantified (IUPHAR review PMID 41478576; Neff PMID 41025406 both flag this gap explicitly). 2) Efficacy numbers (~14% on 6 mg, ~16.7% on 9 mg) come almost entirely from Chinese-adult populations; generalizability to other populations and long-term durability are not yet established, and like all incretin drugs weight tends to regain after stopping. 3) It is GLP-1R-biased by design — too much glucagon activation would raise blood glucose and heart rate; the safety of chronic GCGR activation in humans is still being characterized. 4) Side effects are real and dose-dependent: in GLORY-2, vomiting (53%), nausea (47%) and diarrhea (39%) were far more common than placebo. 5) This is a prescription drug approved abroad, NOT a grey-market research peptide; 'peptide vendor' mazdutide is unregulated and not equivalent to the studied product. This package is educational, not medical advice. 6) Glucagon-class agents warrant monitoring for heart rate and, theoretically, are studied carefully in anyone with cardiovascular concerns.
