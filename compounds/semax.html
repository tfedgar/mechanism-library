Semax

A Russian-made synthetic mini-fragment of the stress hormone ACTH that, stripped of its hormonal punch, nudges the brain to make more of its own growth factors (BDNF/NGF) — marketed as a nootropic and used clinically in Russia for stroke.

Rodent / preclinical 32 citations
Class
Synthetic neuropeptide; melanocortin (ACTH(4-10)) analogue / heptapeptide nootropic-neuroprotective agent (Met-Glu-His-Phe-Pro-Gly-Pro)
Primary target
No single classical receptor confirmed
Human status
Approved and used in RUSSIA only (since the 1990s) as a prescription intranasal peptide for ischemic stroke, transient ischemic attack, cognitive/optic-nerve disorders — NOT approved by the FDA or EMA, and sold elsewhere as a grey-market 'research peptide'/nootropic. Human evidence is thin and low-quality by modern standards: mostly small, often non-blinded Russian stroke trials from the originating group (e.g. PMID 11517472, 15792140, 29798983) plus a couple of small healthy-volunteer fMRI studies (PMID 30225715, 32342318) showing the drug reaches and affects the brain. There are essentially NO large, independent, placebo-controlled, double-blind trials, and the cornerstone mechanistic data (BDNF/NGF/TrkB, neuroprotection) come almost entirely from rodent and in-vitro work by a small set of labs. Treat human efficacy claims — especially for 'nootropic' use in healthy people — as unproven.

At a glance

  • Pro-cognitive / nootropic: improved memory, attention and learning (robust in rodents; suggestive in small human studies)
  • Neuroprotective in stroke/ischemia models — reduces infarct damage, oxidative stress and harmful inflammation (rodent + Russian clinical use)
  • Raises brain BDNF/NGF and TrkB signaling — the mechanistic core (rodent)
  • Anti-stress / anxiolytic and mild analgesic, partly via prolonged enkephalin signaling (in vitro + rodent)
  • Modulates dopamine/serotonin and GABA/glycine neurotransmission (rodent/in vitro)
  • In healthy human volunteers, alters resting-state brain network (default mode network) topology on fMRI — a measurable CNS effect, NOT proof of cognitive benefit

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It's a designed fragment of a stress hormone — minus the hormone job Established

    Semax is a lab-made 7-amino-acid peptide copied from the 4-10 region of ACTH (the pituitary hormone that drives cortisol release), with an added Pro-Gly-Pro 'tail' to make it last longer in the body. Crucially, this fragment keeps the brain/behavioral (neurotropic) activity of ACTH while lacking the hormonal action — it does NOT stimulate the adrenal glands to pump out cortisol. The Pro-Gly-Pro tail protects it from blood enzymes, which is why a nasal spray can work. PMID 16996037; PMID 9173745; PMID 1851003 / PMID 8392718 (degradation/stability)

  2. It sticks to specific spots in the brain Evidence-rodent

    Semax doesn't just float around randomly. Radiolabeled-binding experiments in rat basal forebrain show specific, reversible, calcium-dependent binding sites with nanomolar affinity (KD ~2.4 nM) — meaning there is a real molecular target it docks onto, even though the exact receptor protein hasn't been cleanly identified. This is the honest gap: we know it binds something specific, we don't fully know what. PMID 16635254

  3. It makes the brain produce more of its own growth factors (BDNF and NGF) Evidence-rodent

    This is the headline mechanism behind the 'nootropic' reputation. A single dose of Semax raises BDNF protein and ramps up the genes for BDNF and NGF (neurotrophins — the brain's fertilizer for neurons) in the hippocampus, cortex and basal forebrain. BDNF/NGF support neuron survival, synapse strengthening and learning. The effect is real but modest (~1.4-fold BDNF protein) and shown in rats. PMID 16996037; PMID 16635254; PMID 19662538; PMID 17353092

  4. More growth factor switches on the TrkB receptor (the 'plasticity' switch) Evidence-rodent

    BDNF works by binding its receptor, TrkB. Semax raises TrkB activation (tyrosine phosphorylation) and TrkB gene expression — so it's not just making more BDNF, it's turning up the receiver that lets BDNF strengthen connections between neurons. This BDNF→TrkB axis is the plausible molecular route from 'peptide' to 'better memory/attention.' PMID 16996037; PMID 19633950

  5. It blocks the enzymes that destroy your natural feel-good peptides Evidence-in vitro

    Semax inhibits enkephalin-degrading enzymes in human serum (IC50 ~10 µM) — the enzymes that chew up enkephalins (your endogenous opioid 'feel-good/anti-pain' peptides). By slowing their breakdown, Semax can prolong enkephalin signaling, a proposed route for its anti-stress, anti-anxiety and mild pain-dampening effects. Notably this was shown using human serum. PMID 11443939

  6. It nudges dopamine, serotonin, GABA and glycine systems Evidence-rodent

    Beyond growth factors, Semax tweaks the brain's classic signaling chemicals: it raises serotonin/dopamine turnover (5-HIAA up ~25% in striatum) in rodents, and in isolated neurons it boosts GABA-A currents (calming) while shrinking glycine currents. These broad neuromodulatory effects likely contribute to its mood/attention and neuroprotective profile, but the picture is diffuse rather than one clean lever. PMID 16362768 (monoamines); PMID 29577196 (GABA/glycine, in vitro)

  7. In stroke/injury, it flips on protective gene programs Evidence-rodent

    In rat brain-ischemia (stroke) models, genome-wide studies show Semax shifts the expression of immune-response, vascular/VEGF, and anti-apoptotic genes — calming damaging inflammation, supporting blood-vessel function and reducing nitric-oxide-driven oxidative damage. This transcriptome-level 'damage-control' reprogramming is the proposed basis for its use in Russian stroke therapy. PMID 24661604; PMID 28255762; PMID 22772900; PMID 11245825 (nitric oxide); PMID 32580520

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Semax is the heptapeptide Met-Glu-His-Phe-Pro-Gly-Pro (MEHFPGP), built from the ACTH(4-7) tetrapeptide message sequence (Met-Glu-His-Phe) fused to a C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro (PGP) protective tripeptide. CRITICAL CAVEAT ON THE 'TARGET': despite the framing as a 'BDNF/NGF/TrkB' agent, Semax has NO identified high-affinity cell-surface receptor. It does NOT directly agonize the canonical melanocortin receptors MC1-5R, and no measured Ki/EC50 at MC3R/MC4R has been published — Established that the specific brain binding site is UNKNOWN (Contested/Speculative as to identity). It is best described as a melanocortin-derived neuropeptide whose ACTH(4-7) 'message' fragment carries the classic melanocortin behavioural/neurotrophic activity while lacking the C-terminal residues needed for corticosteroidogenic (adrenal MC2R) activity, so it has NO ACTH-like steroidogenic effect (Established). DO NOT trust any single tool that names a discrete receptor (e.g., a corpus tutor pass spuriously named GHSR1a/the ghrelin receptor — that is an artifact, not Semax data). Three concrete, MEASURED molecular interactions ARE documented: (1) High-affinity Cu(II) chelation — the N-terminal Met-Glu-His ATCUN-like motif binds copper(II), forming [CuLH-2]2- with 4N planar coordination predominant above pH 5; this is a genuine measured affinity (sub-nanomolar-range conditional stability typical of ATCUN motifs) and underlies redox-silencing/anti-amyloid effects (Evidence-in vitro, PMID 25310602, 35080861). (2) Inhibition of plasma/brain enkephalin-degrading enzymes — Semax potently inhibits aminopeptidase activity of plasma enkephalinases, slowing breakdown of endogenous enkephalins (Evidence-in vitro/rodent, PMID 16637290). (3) A 2025 spinal-cord-injury study identified the μ-opioid receptor (gene Oprm1) as a Semax target by network pharmacology + molecular docking (NOT a measured binding affinity — computational/indirect), linking it downstream to USP18 and FTO deubiquitination (Evidence-rodent/in silico, PMID 40692165).

Signalling cascade. The dominant, best-replicated downstream signature is up-regulation of the neurotrophin system rather than direct receptor agonism. In intact rat hippocampus a single dose (50 µg/kg) produces a ~1.4-fold rise in BDNF protein, ~1.6-fold increase in TrkB tyrosine phosphorylation, ~3-fold increase in exon-III (activity-dependent) BDNF mRNA and ~2-fold increase in TrkB mRNA (Evidence-rodent, PMID 16996037). Mechanistically this is INDIRECT and transcriptional: Semax (and even its PGP metabolite) raise transcription of Bdnf, Ngf, Nt-3 and their receptors TrkA/TrkB/TrkC, with Semax acting selectively in ischemic cortex (PMID 19633950). Elevated BDNF then engages TrkB → autophosphorylation → PLCγ/PI3K-Akt and Ras-MAPK/ERK → CREB-driven pro-survival and synaptic-plasticity gene programs (mechanism inferred from canonical TrkB signalling; Established for the pathway, Evidence-rodent for Semax engaging it). Parallel arms: (a) monoaminergic modulation — Semax raises striatal serotonin turnover (extracellular 5-HIAA up to ~180% over 1-4 h) and potentiates amphetamine-evoked dopamine release without changing basal dopamine (PMID 16362768); (b) anti-inflammatory/immunomodulatory transcriptional reprogramming after ischemia — suppression of Il1a/Il1b/Il6/Tnfa induction and shifting the IL-10/TNF balance toward anti-inflammatory (PMID 34097675, 10358912); (c) anti-oxidant/anti-excitotoxic effects partly via copper redox-silencing and reduced NO/lipid-peroxidation after ischemia.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Clinically used as 0.1% or 1% intranasal drops/spray (the Russian registered product); experimentally also intraperitoneal and intracerebroventricular. Intranasal is the human route and exploits direct nose-to-brain transport, partly bypassing first-pass metabolism. No oral form — as a small peptide it would be digested.
Absorption / bioavailability
No rigorous human oral or systemic bioavailability figures are published (Evidence gap). Intranasal delivery achieves CNS exposure sufficient for behavioural effects at microgram/kg doses; intranasal is reported MORE potent than intraperitoneal for the nootropic (learning) effect in rats, but FAILS to reproduce the analgesic effect that IP dosing gives — implying route-dependent access to different brain structures (Evidence-rodent, PMID 21268834). Quantitative absolute bioavailability: UNKNOWN.
Half-life
Parent peptide half-life is very short — on the order of a few minutes in blood/tissue (typical for a naked hepta-peptide subject to peptidases); the C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro was deliberately added specifically to slow degradation, and the therapeutic effect FAR outlasts the parent (clinically described as 20-24 h of activity after a single intranasal 0.015-0.050 mg/kg dose; Evidence-human/rodent, PMID 9173745). This long pharmacodynamic tail despite a short pharmacokinetic half-life is the central PK paradox: effects are mediated by a transcriptional cascade and by active metabolites, not by sustained parent levels.
Metabolism
Degraded by aminopeptidases (N-terminal trimming) and other peptidases. In the presence of nerve cells the major biodegradation products are the pentapeptide HFPGP and the tripeptide PGP (Pro-Gly-Pro) (Evidence-in vitro, PMID 16637290). PGP is itself biologically active and reproduces part of Semax's neurotrophin-transcription effect (PMID 19633950) — so Semax behaves partly as a pro-drug delivering PGP. The C-terminal PGP motif resists prolyl peptidases, explaining the prolongation. Semax also INHIBITS plasma enkephalinase/aminopeptidase activity, which both prolongs its own and endogenous enkephalin lifetime (PMID 16637290).
Excretion
Not formally characterized in humans. As a small peptide, expected to be cleared by peptidase hydrolysis to constituent amino acids plus renal filtration of fragments; no quantitative renal/biliary excretion or mass-balance ADME study exists (Evidence gap).
Notes
PGP C-terminal extension = stabilizing/pro-drug motif (the design innovation). Nose-to-brain delivery is the key route for CNS effects. No human PK/PD modelling, no Cmax/Tmax/AUC, no metabolizing-CYP data (it is peptidase- not CYP-metabolized, so classic CYP drug-interaction risk is low). The dissociation between minutes-long parent half-life and ~24 h effect should be communicated clearly to a lay audience.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Dose-response is reasonably mapped in RODENTS and only crudely in HUMANS. RODENT: classic nootropic/neurotrophic window is roughly 5-50 µg/kg; the canonical BDNF/TrkB induction study used a single 50 µg/kg intranasal dose (~1.4-fold BDNF protein, ~3-fold exon-III BDNF mRNA) (PMID 16996037). Neuroprotective/antihypoxic and anti-NO effects in ischemia models reported at 100-150 µg/kg (PMID 10358912, 11245825). Monoamine effects (serotonin turnover, amphetamine potentiation) at ~0.15 mg/kg IP (PMID 16362768). Nootropic vs analgesic effects follow DIFFERENT dose-response curves and different optimal routes — intranasal favours cognition, IP is needed for analgesia (PMID 21268834), indicating non-monotonic, structure/region-specific dosing rather than a single linear curve. HUMAN: dose-response is essentially UNKNOWN in any modern controlled sense. The Russian literature reports cognitive/attention benefit after single intranasal 0.015-0.050 mg/kg (PMID 9173745); the stroke trials used FAR higher daily doses — 12 mg/day for moderate and 18 mg/day for severe ischemic stroke over 5-10 day courses (PMID 11517472), i.e. a several-hundred-fold higher daily exposure than the nootropic microdose. No placebo-controlled human dose-titration with validated cognitive endpoints exists, so any 'optimal human nootropic dose' claim is unsupported. State plainly: human dose-response = poorly characterized / no Western RCT dose-finding.

How it compares

  • vs Selank (TKPRPGP)
    same: Sister Russian regulatory-peptide drug from the same Institute of Molecular Genetics design program; both are short peptides stabilized by a C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro motif, both intranasal, both modulate BDNF/neurotrophin expression and monoamines, both register on PubMed almost exclusively via Russian groups.
    different: Selank is derived from the immunopeptide tuftsin (not ACTH) and is positioned as an ANXIOLYTIC (GABA/serotonin, benzodiazepine-receptor-adjacent effects) rather than a nootropic/neuroprotectant; Semax is the cognition/stroke agent. Their biodegradation pathways were characterized together (PMID 16637290).
  • vs ACTH(4-10) / native ACTH and α-MSH (melanocortins)
    same: Semax IS the ACTH(4-7) message-sequence core; shares the classic melanocortin neurotrophic/behavioural (memory, attention, neuroprotection) actions historically ascribed to ACTH/MSH fragments.
    different: Semax LACKS the residues required for adrenal MC2R-mediated steroidogenesis, so unlike full ACTH it does NOT raise cortisol — a deliberate dissociation of the 'neurotropic' from the 'hormonal' melanocortin activity. Native ACTH(4-10) is also far more rapidly degraded; the PGP tail gives Semax its prolonged action.
  • vs Cerebrolysin (and other neurotrophic 'nootropics')
    same: Both are marketed (in Russia/post-Soviet and some other markets) as neuroprotective/neurotrophic agents for stroke and cognitive impairment, and both have been shown to exert trophic effects on PC12 cells (PMID 12124658); mechanism converges on neurotrophin signalling.
    different: Cerebrolysin is a porcine-brain-derived peptide/amino-acid mixture (heterogeneous biologic), whereas Semax is a single defined synthetic heptapeptide; Semax additionally has the distinct copper-chelation and enkephalinase-inhibition properties Cerebrolysin lacks.
  • vs Semax NA derivatives / N-acetyl-Semax-amidate (research analogs)
    same: Share the core MEHFPGP pharmacophore and the BDNF/neurotrophic intent; developed to probe structure-activity and stability.
    different: N-terminal acetylation changes copper(II)/zinc(II) coordination and biological properties (PMID 27586814) — i.e., blocking the N-terminus abolishes the ATCUN copper-binding arm, dissociating the metal-chelation activity from the neurotrophic activity and showing those are mechanistically separable.

History & legal status

"Semax was developed in the 1980s-1990s at the Institute of Molecular Genetics of the Russian (formerly USSR) Academy of Sciences together with Moscow State University (key figures: Ashmarin, Myasoedov, Kamensky), as a deliberately engineered, non-steroidogenic ACTH(4-10) analog stabilized with a C-terminal Pro-Gly-Pro (PMID 9173745 reviews '15 years' of design). It is REGISTERED and approved by the Russian Ministry of Health and is in routine clinical use in Russia and some post-Soviet states — indications include acute ischemic stroke, transient ischemic attack/cerebrovascular insufficiency, cognitive/attention disorders, and optic nerve disease (PMID 11517472, 15792140, 10741256). It has NEVER been approved by the FDA or EMA, has not undergone Western-standard randomized controlled trials, and essentially all human efficacy data originate from Russian groups/journals with limited independent replication (a point raised even in user communities). Legal status in the US/EU: not an approved drug; sold in the grey 'research chemical'/peptide-nootropic market (often as 'not for human consumption'), so quality, identity and sterility are unregulated. It is not a controlled substance in most jurisdictions but its sale for human use is generally unauthorized."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • What is the actual molecular target / receptor? No high-affinity binding site is identified; it does not directly agonize known melanocortin receptors, the 2025 μ-opioid (Oprm1) link is computational, and at least one corpus tool spuriously invented a ghrelin-receptor target — the receptor question is genuinely open.
  • How does a peptide with a minutes-long parent half-life produce ~24 h of effect? The relative contributions of the active PGP metabolite, enkephalinase inhibition, and a self-sustaining transcriptional (BDNF/CREB) cascade are not quantitatively resolved.
  • Is there any reproducible human cognitive benefit under blinded, placebo-controlled, Western-standard conditions? All positive human data are non-randomized and single-country; independent RCTs are absent.
  • What is the real human pharmacokinetic/ADME profile (Cmax, Tmax, AUC, intranasal bioavailability, CNS penetration, excretion)? Essentially uncharacterized.
  • Does chronic use perturb systemic copper/trace-metal homeostasis or immune function at behaviorally relevant doses, given the documented Cu(II) chelation and cytokine modulation?
  • Are there developmental, pregnancy, or very-long-term safety risks? Rodent neonatal data show durable behavioural reprogramming, and human data in these populations are nonexistent.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a protocol, no dosing): Because Semax sold for human use is an unregulated grey-market peptide, product identity/purity/sterility is the first real risk — prefer third-party-tested material and watch for signs of contaminated injectables/nasal product (local infection, unusual reactions). Track and log: (1) Neuro/psychiatric signs — overstimulation, anxiety, restlessness, sleep disruption, headache, or paradoxical mood changes (plausible from monoaminergic enhancement); stop and reassess if these appear. (2) Nasal/local tolerance — irritation, congestion, epistaxis with intranasal use. (3) Because of the documented copper-chelation and immune-modulating mechanisms, anyone using it chronically should have baseline and periodic general bloodwork reviewed by a clinician — CBC (immune/inflammatory status) and, given the chelation mechanism, serum copper and ceruloplasmin are reasonable to watch for any trend toward copper depletion (mechanism-driven precaution, not an established clinical problem). (4) General safety labs (CMP, including renal and liver panels) are sensible for any ongoing experimental peptide. (5) Special caution / avoid without medical oversight in pregnancy, in children/adolescents (rodent neurodevelopmental signal), and alongside serotonergic or dopaminergic/stimulant drugs (potential additive monoaminergic effects). None of this substitutes for evaluation by a qualified clinician; report new neurological symptoms promptly, especially since the people most drawn to a 'stroke/neuroprotection' peptide may have underlying cerebrovascular risk."

Caveats

MAJOR HUMAN-DATA GAP: the mechanism story (BDNF/NGF up, TrkB on, neuroprotection) is overwhelmingly rodent/in-vitro; do not present it as proven in humans. PUBLICATION/SOURCE BIAS: most positive data come from the Russian institutes that invented and commercialize Semax — limited independent replication and few English-language blinded RCTs. UNKNOWN RECEPTOR: it binds a specific brain site but the actual receptor is not identified — claims that it 'activates melanocortin receptor X' are oversimplified/unverified. EFFECT SIZE IS MODEST: BDNF rise is ~1.4-fold, not dramatic. fMRI changes in healthy people show a brain effect, NOT a proven cognitive or clinical benefit. GREY-MARKET PRODUCT: outside Russia it is an unregulated research peptide — purity, dose and contamination are real concerns, and long-term safety in healthy users is essentially unstudied. Forum/YouTube hype about 'instant focus' and 'neurogenesis' far outruns the controlled human evidence. This is educational mechanism content, not medical advice.
