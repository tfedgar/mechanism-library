A pregnancy hormone that doubles as a "fake LH" — it switches on the testes' testosterone factory directly, bypassing the brain.

Limited human data 17 citations
Class
Gonadotropin (glycoprotein hormone); luteinizing-hormone-receptor (LH/CG receptor) agonist
Primary target
The LH/choriogonadotropin receptor (LHCGR), a Gs-coupled GPCR on the surface of testicular Leydig cells
Human status
Approved human drug with decades of clinical use. Established in men for hypogonadotropic hypogonadism and fertility (often combined with FSH), and used to preserve spermatogenesis and testicular size during testosterone replacement, since exogenous testosterone shuts down the brain's LH/FSH output and collapses intratesticular testosterone. Human evidence supports the testosterone-raising and fertility-preserving effects (PMID 23260550, 36682942, 39025301, 33085606, 25337850); the upstream signaling mechanism (LHCGR→cAMP→StAR→testosterone) is established largely from molecular and rodent Leydig-cell work (PMID 29566165, 11191081, 17412805).

At a glance

  • Raises testosterone by stimulating the testes directly, independent of the brain's LH signal.
  • Sharply raises intratesticular (inside-the-testis) testosterone, which is what spermatogenesis actually depends on — far higher locally than blood levels.
  • Maintains or restores testicular size and sperm production in men whose own LH is suppressed (e.g., during exogenous testosterone use) or deficient (hypogonadotropic hypogonadism).
  • Keeps Leydig cells functional/trophic via ERK signaling rather than letting them go dormant.
  • Secondarily raises estradiol through aromatization of the extra testosterone.

Mechanism, step by step

8 steps7 human-evidenced 1 preclinical

  1. hCG impersonates LH Established

    hCG and luteinizing hormone (LH) are sister glycoprotein hormones that bind the SAME receptor (LHCGR) on Leydig cells, so injected hCG acts like a long-lasting copy of the body's own LH signal — but it comes from outside, so it works even when the brain has stopped sending LH. PMID 17412805; PMID 29566165

  2. It binds the LH/CG receptor on Leydig cells Established

    hCG docks onto the LHCGR, a G-protein-coupled receptor sitting on the surface of the Leydig cells inside the testes — the cells whose job is to make testosterone. PMID 29566165; PMID 40332028

  3. The receptor fires up cAMP inside the cell Established

    Activated LHCGR couples to the Gs protein and raises the second messenger cAMP, which turns on protein kinase A — this is the main 'go' signal that drives testosterone production. PMID 29566165; PMID 33706963

  4. Cholesterol is shuttled into the mitochondria (the rate-limiting step) Established

    The cAMP/PKA signal switches on StAR (steroidogenic acute regulatory protein), which ferries cholesterol — the raw material — into the mitochondria; this transport step, not any later enzyme, sets the overall speed of testosterone production. PMID 11191081; PMID 29566165; PMID 33706963

  5. Cholesterol is converted step-by-step into testosterone Established

    Inside the mitochondria the enzyme CYP11A1 makes the first cut of cholesterol into pregnenolone, and a chain of enzymes (including 3β-HSD and 17β-HSD) finishes the job, producing testosterone that is released into the blood and, crucially, into the testis itself. PMID 29566165; PMID 33706963

  6. It also acts as a trophic / growth signal for Leydig cells Evidence-rodent

    Beyond cAMP, LHCGR activation switches on the ERK1/2 kinase cascade, which makes Leydig cells proliferate and stay healthy — part of why hCG keeps the testosterone-making machinery alive rather than just squeezing out a one-off burst. PMID 17412805

  7. At high/sustained doses the receptor desensitizes Evidence-human

    Like other GPCRs, the LHCGR can be down-regulated and 'dampened' via beta-arrestin and biased signaling when over-stimulated, so chronic high hCG can blunt the very response it is meant to drive — a reason steady physiological signaling beats hammering the receptor. PMID 29999287

  8. Some of the testosterone is converted to estradiol Evidence-human

    Because hCG drives Leydig cells hard, it raises testosterone AND its downstream product estradiol (testosterone is aromatized to estrogen), which is why strong hCG stimulation can also push estrogen up. PMID 36682942

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. hCG is a ~36–40 kDa heterodimeric glycoprotein: a common alpha subunit (shared with LH, FSH, TSH) non-covalently paired with a hormone-specific beta subunit. Its defining structural feature is a 24–31 amino-acid C-terminal peptide (CTP) on the beta subunit carrying up to four O-linked sialic-acid-rich glycans — absent on LH — which is the main reason hCG circulates far longer than LH (see PK). TARGET: the LH/CG receptor (LHCGR), a class A (rhodopsin-like) GPCR on Leydig cells (testis) and theca/granulosa/luteal cells (ovary). hCG and LH are FULL AGONISTS at the same receptor — there is no separate 'hCG receptor.' AFFINITY/SELECTIVITY (Established, but exact numbers vary by assay): hCG binds LHCGR with roughly nanomolar affinity and, because of its slow off-rate and long half-life, produces more sustained receptor occupancy than an equivalent pulse of LH. hCG is highly selective for LHCGR but, because the alpha subunit and receptor ectodomains across the glycoprotein-hormone family are homologous, hCG has weak partial cross-reactivity at the TSH receptor — clinically relevant only at the very high hCG concentrations of pregnancy/trophoblastic disease (gestational thyrotoxicosis), not at andrology doses (Evidence-human, mechanistic).

Signalling cascade. LHCGR is a large GPCR with a leucine-rich-repeat ectodomain that clamps the hormone, plus a hinge region and a 7-transmembrane bundle. Cryo-EM of full-length glycoprotein-hormone-receptor signalling complexes (Duan et al., Nature 2021) showed that hormone binding to the ectodomain repositions a conserved 'p10'/hinge segment that pushes against the top of the transmembrane bundle, triggering the canonical class-A activation rearrangement and opening an intracellular cavity for the G protein. PRIMARY PATHWAY (Established): activated LHCGR couples to Gs -> adenylyl cyclase -> rises in cAMP -> activation of protein kinase A (PKA). PKA drives steroidogenesis at two key points: (1) it phosphorylates and acutely activates StAR (steroidogenic acute regulatory protein), which shuttles cholesterol from the outer to inner mitochondrial membrane — the true rate-limiting step; and (2) it up-regulates the steroidogenic enzymes CYP11A1 (cholesterol side-chain cleavage, cholesterol -> pregnenolone), 3-beta-HSD, CYP17A1 (17-alpha-hydroxylase/17,20-lyase), and 17-beta-HSD, converting pregnenolone through to testosterone. SECONDARY PATHWAYS (Evidence-human/rodent): at higher occupancy LHCGR also engages Gq/11 -> phospholipase C -> IP3/DAG -> intracellular Ca2+ and PKC, and recruits beta-arrestin, which both desensitizes the receptor (internalization) and seeds longer-term gene-expression/ proliferation signals (ERK/MAPK). In the OVARY the same cAMP/PKA cascade in granulosa/luteal cells drives progesterone and estradiol output and, importantly, induction of VEGF — the link to ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Parenteral only — intramuscular or subcutaneous injection. Forms: urinary-derived hCG (uhCG, extracted from pregnancy urine) and recombinant choriogonadotropin alfa (rhCG). Oral/intranasal routes are nonviable: it is a large, glycosylated protein that would be digested and cannot cross the gut. Dosed in International Units (IU) for uhCG; rhCG by mass (62.5 µg ≈ 1500 IU activity per Handelsman 2024).
Absorption / bioavailability
Absorbed slowly from the injection depot. SC and IM give broadly comparable systemic exposure and testosterone responses in men; SC is commonly used clinically and is the basis of self-injection pens. Peak serum hCG typically occurs ~6–24 h after injection, with the downstream serum testosterone peak lagging by roughly 48–96 h because steroidogenic machinery must be up-regulated and StAR-mediated cholesterol transport ramped up (Evidence-human: Smals 1979, peak T at ~72 h; Handelsman 2024).
Half-life
Long and bi-phasic. In men, pooled terminal half-life ~5.8 days on multi-dosing (Handelsman 2024, PMID 38446525); single-dose terminal t½ for recombinant choriogonadotropin alfa ~32 h and for the newer choriogonadotropin beta ~45–47 h (Kyhl 2021, PMID 33982429). The long half-life vs LH (LH t½ ~1–2 h) is due almost entirely to the heavily sialylated beta-CTP glycans, which resist hepatic galactose/asialoglycoprotein-receptor clearance and slow renal filtration. This is why a single hCG injection raises testosterone for 4–6 days (Smals 1979) whereas an LH pulse lasts minutes-to-hours.
Metabolism
Catabolized as a protein/glycoprotein rather than by cytochrome-P450 drug metabolism. Cleared by (1) receptor-mediated uptake and lysosomal degradation after binding LHCGR, and (2) hepatic clearance governed by its terminal sialic-acid/galactose glycan caps (desialylation exposes galactose -> hepatic asialoglycoprotein receptor uptake). The intact dimer, free subunits, and a characteristic urinary fragment (beta-core fragment) are produced during degradation. No clinically important CYP interactions.
Excretion
Renal. Intact hCG and its beta-core fragment are filtered and appear in urine — the basis of urine hCG pregnancy tests and of anti-doping detection of exogenous hCG in men. Because clearance is partly renal, severe renal impairment can prolong exposure.
Notes
hCG is NOT esterified and has no prodrug form — the CTP glycopeptide is the natural 'slow-release' feature engineered into the molecule by evolution (and copied into long-acting biologics). It does NOT cross the blood–brain barrier in any meaningful amount; its CNS-attributed effects (e.g. mood, libido) are secondary to the testosterone/estradiol it raises. Urinary vs recombinant products are not formally bioequivalent on PK but give comparable testosterone responses at matched activity (Handelsman 2024).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

HUMAN (men) — the dose-response is unusually well characterized for the testosterone-maintenance use case. PIVOTAL RCT (Coviello 2005, PMID 15713727): in 29 normal men made gonadotropin-suppressed with testosterone enanthate, intratesticular testosterone (ITT) fell 94% (from ~1234 to ~72 nmol/L) when no hCG was given; adding hCG every other day restored ITT dose-dependently and LINEARLY — 125 IU left ITT ~25% below baseline, 250 IU ~7% below baseline, and 500 IU pushed ITT ~26% ABOVE baseline. This established that low, sub-fertility doses suffice to keep intratesticular testosterone in range. SINGLE DOSE (Smals 1979, PMID 447810): one 1500 IU IM injection raised serum testosterone in normal men with a peak at ~72 h sustained for 96–120 h; giving the same dose daily for 3 days added essentially nothing within the first 48 h — i.e., the Leydig cell is already maximally driven and there is no benefit to stacking doses inside ~48 h. DESENSITIZATION CEILING: above the maintenance range the response saturates and then falls — repeated high doses down-regulate the LHCGR and deplete steroidogenic enzyme (see side effects), so 'more hCG' does not mean linearly 'more testosterone' beyond a point. RODENT: a single high-dose hCG bolus (e.g. 25 IU IV in rats) causes a testosterone surge then a 48-h desensitization trough with ~97% loss of CYP17 protein yet only ~62% loss of testosterone output, because the enzyme becomes more catalytically efficient (Kühn-Velten 1997, PMID 9150706). KNOWN GAP: there is NO good human dose-response defining the threshold for restoring/maintaining SPERMATOGENESIS (vs merely ITT), and no controlled dose-response in the AAS-recovery setting that dominates real-world use — those protocols are extrapolated, not established. (Per system rules, no dosing protocol is given here; figures are reported only to convey the shape of the dose-response.)

How it compares

  • vs Luteinizing hormone (LH) / recombinant LH (lutropin alfa)
    same: Same receptor (LHCGR), same Gs-cAMP-PKA-StAR steroidogenic cascade, both full agonists driving Leydig-cell testosterone.
    different: hCG has the extra sialylated beta-CTP, giving a half-life of days vs LH's 1–2 hours, so one hCG shot mimics many days of LH; LH is pulsatile and physiological, hCG gives sustained, non-pulsatile stimulation, which is more prone to desensitization and to a higher estradiol/testosterone ratio.
  • vs GnRH agonists/antagonists (e.g. triptorelin; or clomiphene/enclomiphene as SERMs)
    same: All ultimately aim to maintain testicular function/testosterone in the context of HPG-axis suppression.
    different: hCG acts DOWNSTREAM at the testis (works even when the pituitary is shut down or absent), whereas clomiphene/GnRH approaches act UPSTREAM and require an intact, responsive pituitary–testis axis. hCG bypasses the brain entirely; SERMs/GnRH modulators raise the body's own LH/FSH.
  • vs FSH / human menopausal gonadotropin (hMG) / recombinant FSH
    same: Glycoprotein gonadotropins sharing the same alpha subunit; often co-administered with hCG to restore fertility.
    different: FSH acts on Sertoli cells (FSH receptor) to support spermatogenesis directly; hCG acts on Leydig cells for testosterone. hCG alone restores ITT/testosterone but FSH is usually needed alongside it to optimize sperm production in gonadotropin-deficient men.
  • vs Exogenous testosterone (TRT)
    same: Both raise serum testosterone and treat hypogonadal symptoms.
    different: TRT suppresses the axis and shrinks the testes by removing LH drive; hCG REPLACES the LH signal, so it preserves testicular size, intratesticular testosterone and fertility (Coviello 2005). But hCG depends on functioning Leydig cells and produces a higher estradiol response; TRT works regardless of testicular capacity.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Anastrozole counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    hCG is an LH-receptor (LHCGR) agonist on Leydig cells that directly stimulates intratesticular steroidogenesis, raising both intratesticular/circulating testosterone AND estradiol because the freshly made androgen is aromatized in the testis and periphery. So hCG characteristically drives E2 up (water retention, gynecomastia risk) even without exogenous testosterone. Anastrozole sits one step downstream at CYP19A1 and blocks the androgen-to-estrogen conversion, counteracting the hCG-driven estrogen rise while leaving the LHCGR-mediated testosterone production intact. Different targets (GPCR vs enzyme) converging on the same E2 output with opposite sign. human-chorionic-gonadotropin-hcg.json (LHCGR agonist; key_effects 'secondarily raises estradiol through aromatization'); anastrozole.json mechanism_steps 1-4; corpus MoA Aromatase Watch: Estradiol (sensitive assay) plus total testosterone; watch for hCG-driven E2 elevation and, conversely, for AI over-suppressing E2 below the physiologic range. Track symptoms of both excess (breast tenderness, bloat) and deficiency (joint pain, libido loss).
  • Andarine counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    Andarine suppresses LH, the signal that tells testicular Leydig cells to make testosterone, causing testicular atrophy. hCG is an LH mimic that binds LHCGR directly on the testis, bypassing the suppressed pituitary to maintain intratesticular testosterone and testicular size. It counteracts the downstream consequence of andarine's central suppression but does nothing to reverse the central LH/FSH shutdown itself. andarine-s-4.json (HPG suppression, testicular atrophy); human-chorionic-gonadotropin-hcg.json (LHCGR agonist on Leydig cells, raises intratesticular T + E2) Watch: Total testosterone and estradiol (hCG raises intratesticular T and E2) plus testicular size; LH/FSH stay low because the pituitary signal is not restored. Harm-reduction context only, no dosing.
  • Boldenone undecylenate counteracts ↓ Established
    Boldenone suppresses the HPG axis (lowers LH/FSH, intratesticular testosterone, spermatogenesis) via AR negative feedback. hCG acts directly on testicular LHCGR (an LH-receptor agonist) to restore intratesticular testosterone and testis volume, counteracting that suppression downstream. Trade-off: hCG stimulation also raises intratesticular aromatase activity, so estradiol can rise even while EQ tends to read low. EQ monograph side_effect_mechanisms (HPG suppression, PMID 33126548); hCG monograph (LHCGR agonist raises intratesticular T + E2 via aromatization) Watch: Testis size/symptoms; LH/FSH (will stay suppressed — hCG bypasses the pituitary rather than restarting it); and estradiol (hCG can raise E2). Watch E2 climb when weighing any AI decision.
  • DHB counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    DHB is a potent androgen that triggers hypothalamic-pituitary negative feedback, shutting down LH/FSH and therefore endogenous and intratesticular testosterone, causing testicular atrophy; and because DHB cannot aromatize, it also removes the estradiol that endogenous testosterone would have supplied. hCG mimics LH at the testicular LHCGR to directly stimulate intratesticular testosterone (and, via aromatization of that testosterone, some estradiol), partially counteracting DHB's suppression of testicular function and the estrogen deficit inherent to a non-aromatizing compound. This mitigates DHB's testicular/HPTA effect; it does not restart the shut-down axis (LH/FSH stay suppressed by androgen feedback). hCG monograph (LHCGR agonist, raises intratesticular T and, via aromatization, E2); DHB HPTA suppression (DHB monograph side_effect_mechanisms HPG entry, PMID 16621347) Watch: Testicular size/consistency, LH/FSH (will stay suppressed by androgen feedback), total and free testosterone, and estradiol; watch for over-stimulation and rising estradiol once intratesticular testosterone is restored.
  • Exemestane counteracts ↓ Established
    hCG elevates estradiol by pushing testosterone through aromatase; exemestane is a steroidal, irreversible (mechanism-based, 'suicide') aromatase inactivator that covalently disables the CYP19A1 enzyme it binds. It counteracts the hCG-driven E2 rise via the same target as the other AIs, differing in being an irreversible inactivator rather than a competitive inhibitor, so its effect outlasts a given exposure. PMID 32445446 (hCG-stimulated aromatase/estradiol); exemestane monograph (irreversible CYP19A1 inactivator) Watch: Estradiol (avoid over-suppression), testosterone/DHT, lipids, and estrogen-deficiency symptoms; because inactivation is irreversible, watch for lag in E2 recovery.
  • Letrozole counteracts ↓ Established
    hCG acts as a long-lasting LH mimic on Leydig-cell LHCGR, driving up intratesticular testosterone AND estradiol — because the extra androgen it stimulates is aromatized to estrogen, hCG characteristically raises the E2-to-testosterone ratio and can cause water retention and gynecomastia. Letrozole blocks that aromatization at CYP19A1, opposing the hCG-driven estrogen rise while leaving hCG's testicular-stimulating (fertility/testis-size) effect intact. human-chorionic-gonadotropin-hcg.json side_effect step 1 & step 8 (PMID 38446525); letrozole.json step 1; Corpus Aromatase MoA card Watch: Sensitive serum estradiol during hCG exposure; watch for high-E2 gyno/bloat signs balanced against AI over-suppression (joint/libido/mood). LH can read artificially because hCG cross-reacts on some assays — track total/free testosterone rather than relying on LH.
  • Nandrolone counteracts ↓ Established
    Nandrolone drives negative feedback on the hypothalamus/pituitary, shutting down LH/FSH and therefore intratesticular testosterone and spermatogenesis; recovery is slow because of the long decanoate depot. hCG bypasses the suppressed pituitary by acting directly as an LH analog on testicular LHCGR, restoring intratesticular testosterone and testicular size. It thus counteracts the downstream gonadal-shutdown/atrophy limb of nandrolone's HPG suppression — though it does not restore the pituitary signal itself, nor address the progestin/prolactin symptoms, and it only works if Leydig cells are still responsive. Nandrolone monograph step 7 and side_effect_mechanisms (HPG suppression, PPAAH); hCG monograph steps 1-5 (LHCGR agonist restoring intratesticular testosterone) and comparisons vs exogenous testosterone (preserves testicular size/ITT) Watch: LH/FSH (stay low while suppressed), total/free testosterone, testicular size and, if fertility matters, semen parameters; estradiol (hCG raises intratesticular aromatization and the estradiol-to-testosterone ratio, adding estrogen); hematocrit (androgen-driven, via the testosterone hCG produces).
  • Testosterone counteracts ↓ Established
    Exogenous testosterone suppresses the brain's LH/FSH output, which collapses intratesticular testosterone (ITT) and shuts down spermatogenesis, shrinking the testes and causing azoospermia in a large fraction of men on TRT. hCG is a direct LHCGR agonist on Leydig cells, so it replaces the missing LH signal at the testis and restores/maintains ITT and testicular size even while the pituitary is suppressed. This is the core reason hCG is co-used with TRT: it counteracts the specific fertility/atrophy penalty testosterone imposes, without needing an intact HPG axis. Note the two also COMPOUND total androgen load — both raise serum testosterone and, downstream, estradiol, DHT, and hematocrit. PMID 23260550 (concomitant hCG preserves spermatogenesis on TRT); hCG monograph comparisons/key_effects; testosterone-trt monograph (axis suppression, testicular atrophy) Watch: Total/free testosterone (over-shoot when both are on board), estradiol (hCG raises the E2/T ratio on top of TRT's aromatization), hematocrit/hemoglobin (combined androgen erythrocytosis, thrombotic risk), and semen analysis / testicular size if fertility preservation is the goal.
  • Finasteride opposes Established
    hCG raises not only testosterone and estradiol but also dihydrotestosterone (DHT), because some of the extra Leydig-cell testosterone is 5-alpha-reduced (human hCG-stimulation studies show plasma DHT rising alongside T and E2). Finasteride blocks 5-alpha-reductase type 2, cutting circulating DHT by roughly 65-70%, so it opposes the DHT component of hCG's output — blunting androgenic scalp/prostate/skin effects. It does NOT reduce the testosterone or estradiol that hCG raises. PMID 1264608 and PMID 2943108 (hCG raises DHT alongside T and E2 in men); finasteride monograph (SRD5A2 inhibition, ~65-70% DHT reduction) Watch: DHT and androgenic symptoms (scalp shedding, acne, prostate/urinary changes); note estradiol and testosterone are unaffected by finasteride, so hCG's estrogen/gynecomastia risk persists and must be tracked separately.
  • Methyltestosterone opposes Established
    hCG is an LHCGR agonist that stimulates testicular Leydig cells to raise intratesticular testosterone and maintain testicular function, whereas methyltestosterone as an AR agonist drives negative feedback that suppresses hypothalamic GnRH and pituitary LH/FSH, shutting down the endogenous axis. The exogenous androgen works against hCG's purpose of preserving testicular output; the two pull the HPG axis in opposite directions. Morales 1994 methyltestosterone suppressed LH/SHBG PMID 8072077; methyltestosterone monograph side_effect_mechanisms (HPG-axis suppression); hCG monograph (LHCGR agonist) Watch: LH/FSH, total and free testosterone, testicular size/symptoms; estradiol (hCG also raises intratesticular aromatization). Interpret suppressed gonadotropins as expected axis shutdown.
  • Clomiphene citrate redundant Established
    Both aim to raise testosterone and preserve testicular function/fertility, but at OPPOSITE ends of the HPG axis. Clomiphene is a SERM that blocks estrogen negative feedback at the hypothalamus, raising the body's own LH and FSH — it therefore REQUIRES an intact, responsive pituitary and testis. hCG works downstream, mimicking LH directly at the Leydig-cell LHCGR, so it works even when the pituitary is suppressed or absent (e.g., on TRT). The overlapping endpoint (drive Leydig cells, raise T, keep testes active) makes them partly redundant; stacking both drives Leydig cells from two directions (endogenous LH plus an LH-mimic), compounding testosterone and, via aromatization, estradiol — to which clomiphene's own mixed-estrogenic isomer adds. hCG monograph comparisons (downstream LHCGR vs upstream SERM, intact-axis requirement); clomiphene monograph (raises LH/FSH and endogenous T, preserves ITT/testes) Watch: Testosterone (additive over-shoot), estradiol (both can raise it — gynecomastia/mood/fluid), LH/FSH (to gauge whether central stimulation is even working), and hematocrit.
  • Enclomiphene redundant Evidence-human
    Both aim to raise endogenous/testicular testosterone, but they enter the LH→Leydig pathway at DIFFERENT points via non-overlapping molecular targets — so they are largely redundant rather than sharing a target. Enclomiphene works at the TOP of the axis (blocks hypothalamic estrogen receptors → more endogenous LH/FSH → drives the testis physiologically). hCG BYPASSES the brain, acting directly as an LH-receptor (LHCGR) agonist on Leydig cells. They converge on the same downstream effector (Leydig-cell / intratesticular testosterone) by different routes — partly redundant, partly complementary. Because hCG raises intratesticular T and E2 and, through feedback, suppresses the pituitary's own LH output, it can blunt or work against enclomiphene's central 'restart' logic if the goal is axis self-sufficiency. Enclomiphene monograph (mechanism_steps 3-5, central drive); hCG monograph (LHCGR agonist on Leydig cells, downstream of the brain; raises E2); Clomiphene monograph (comparison: hCG acts downstream vs SERM upstream); corpus MoA card 'HPG axis' Watch: Total/free testosterone and estradiol (both can rise, E2 especially with hCG-driven aromatization). Track LH/FSH — hCG feedback suppresses endogenous LH, so a FALLING LH/FSH signals the pituitary drive enclomiphene is meant to build is being overridden. Hematocrit/hemoglobin on a full blood count; semen analysis and testicular exam if fertility is the goal.

History & legal status

"hCG was identified in the 1920s as the pregnancy hormone behind the original Aschheim–Zondek and 'rabbit/frog' pregnancy tests; it is produced by placental syncytiotrophoblast and is the analyte in modern urine pregnancy tests. Therapeutically, urinary-derived hCG (purified from pooled pregnancy urine) has been used for decades to induce ovulation, treat male hypogonadotropic hypogonadism/cryptorchidism, and trigger final oocyte maturation in assisted reproduction. Recombinant choriogonadotropin alfa (e.g. Ovidrelle/Ovitrelle) was approved around 2000, dosed by mass; newer recombinant variants (choriogonadotropin beta) are in development with longer half-lives (Kyhl 2021). REGULATORY/LEGAL STATUS: hCG is an approved prescription drug (FDA, EMA and others) for those fertility/endocrine indications. In the US the FDA forced removal of OTC 'homeopathic hCG' weight-loss products and requires a boxed warning that hCG has NOT been shown to aid weight loss. In SPORT, hCG is banned in males by the World Anti-Doping Agency (it raises endogenous testosterone and is used to restart the axis after steroid use); it is detectable in male urine via the same beta-core fragment used in pregnancy testing. Off-label/illicit use to preserve fertility and 'restart' the HPG axis during or after anabolic-steroid use is widespread but outside its approved labeling."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • The human intratesticular-testosterone THRESHOLD required to maintain or restore spermatogenesis is still unknown — Coviello defined the ITT dose-response but explicitly could not say what ITT level is needed for sperm production.
  • No controlled human dose-response exists for the real-world AAS-recovery / 'HPG-axis restart' use case; protocols there are extrapolated from fertility data, not validated.
  • Whether long-term or repeated high-dose hCG causes durable Leydig-cell desensitization/dysfunction in humans (vs the reversible rodent CYP17 depletion) is not well characterized.
  • The degree to which hCG raises the intratesticular estradiol/testosterone ratio — and whether that independently impairs spermatogenesis or drives gynecomastia risk more than equivalent androgen exposure — is incompletely quantified in humans.
  • Optimal hCG-versus-LH (and hCG+FSH) regimens for fertility restoration, and whether the newer long-half-life recombinant variants offer real clinical advantage over urinary hCG, remain open.
  • Quantitative human risk thresholds for hCG-driven vascular/VEGF effects (OHSS in women; any vascular contribution in men) at andrology vs ART doses are not precisely mapped.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (no dosing guidance). Baseline and periodic bloodwork that clinicians typically follow on hCG: total and free testosterone (response and over-shoot), ESTRADIOL (hCG raises the estradiol/testosterone ratio — watch for gynecomastia, nipple tenderness, fluid retention), and a full blood count / HEMATOCRIT and hemoglobin (erythrocytosis raises thrombotic risk). Track DHT if androgenic effects (acne, oily skin, scalp shedding) appear. In men using hCG for fertility, semen analysis and sometimes LH/FSH and INSL3 are followed to gauge testicular function. Thyroid function (TSH/free T4) is worth a baseline given the TSH-receptor cross-reactivity at high concentrations, though clinically relevant thyroid stimulation is unlikely at andrology doses. WARNING SIGNS to act on: new or worsening breast tissue/tenderness; marked testicular pain or swelling; signs of high hematocrit (headaches, flushing, visual changes); and — chiefly in women undergoing ovulation induction/ART — abdominal distension, rapid weight gain, nausea, reduced urine output or shortness of breath, which can signal OHSS and are a medical emergency. Renal impairment can prolong hCG exposure, so kidney function is relevant context. Anti-doping note: exogenous hCG is detectable in male urine and is a banned substance in sport."

Caveats

Direction and target are well established (hCG = LH-receptor agonist → more testosterone), but several details carry lighter certainty. The ERK1/2 trophic/proliferation pathway (step 6) is mainly rodent/cell-line data, not direct human proof. Receptor desensitization with high/chronic dosing (step 7) is a recognized GPCR phenomenon for the LHCGR but the dose-response in men is not cleanly quantified — magnitude is uncertain. Note hCG works ONLY if Leydig cells are present and responsive (primary/testicular failure won't respond); it does NOT replace FSH, so for full sperm production FSH is often needed alongside it (PMID 8263139, 36682942).
