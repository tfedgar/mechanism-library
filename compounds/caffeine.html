Caffeine

The most-used and best-evidenced ergogenic aid in sport — a non-selective adenosine antagonist that raises CNS drive, blunts perceived effort, and modestly boosts endurance, with smaller benefits for strength and power.

Human / approved 10 citations
Class
Methylxanthine / CNS stimulant (trimethylxanthine)
Primary target
Non-selective antagonism of adenosine A1 and A2A receptors, which disinhibits CNS excitatory drive and downstream catecholamine signalling; at supraphysiologic concentrations it also potentiates sarcoplasmic-reticulum calcium release and inhibits phosphodiesterase
Human status
Ubiquitous and extensively studied in humans. Caffeine is an approved drug (ChEMBL CHEMBL113, max phase 4; caffeine citrate — Cafcit/Peyona — is used clinically for apnoea of prematurity, and caffeine is a common analgesic adjuvant) and the most-researched ergogenic aid in sport. The ISSN position stand (Guest et al., 2021) rates it an A-tier ergogenic, effective at ~3-6 mg/kg body mass, with the most consistent moderate-to-large benefit for aerobic endurance and smaller benefits for strength, power and anaerobic tasks. It was removed from the WADA prohibited list in 2004 and is now on the WADA Monitoring Program only, so it is legal in competition. The evidence base is large but predominantly young men; data in women, older adults, and for chronic training adaptation are thinner.

At a glance

  • Small-to-moderate improvement in aerobic/endurance performance (the most consistent and largest benefit)
  • Smaller but real improvements in muscular strength, power, jumping, sprinting and muscular endurance
  • Increased alertness, vigilance and attention; reduced perception of effort and fatigue
  • Partial preservation of performance under sleep deprivation
  • Modest thermogenesis and lipolysis; backbone of the ephedrine-caffeine (ECA) fat-loss stack
  • Dose-dependent side effects: anxiety/jitteriness, tachycardia, small blood-pressure rise, insomnia, GI upset
  • Tolerance to subjective effects and a withdrawal syndrome (headache, fatigue) on abrupt cessation

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Blocks adenosine A1 and A2A receptors Established

    At the concentrations reached from a normal dietary or ergogenic dose, caffeine's dominant action is competitive, non-selective antagonism of adenosine A1 and A2A receptors. Because adenosine normally acts as an inhibitory 'brake' that accumulates with metabolic activity and promotes sleep pressure, blocking these receptors releases that brake. ChEMBL curates caffeine (CHEMBL113) as an adenosine-receptor antagonist (direct interaction), and knockout-mouse work shows A2A blockade is necessary for its stimulant effect. ChEMBL CHEMBL113; PMID 15081797; PMID 28646346

  2. A2A blockade drives arousal and (in humans) lowers perceived effort Evidence-human

    The mechanistic evidence that the wakefulness/arousal effect specifically REQUIRES A2A receptors comes from knockout MICE: animals lacking A2A are not stimulated by caffeine regardless of A1 status, while A1-knockout mice are actually more stimulated (rodent evidence). The translation to humans is the well-replicated finding that caffeine reduces the perception of effort and pain during exercise and increases vigilance — a central action many researchers consider the core of its ergogenic effect. The receptor-requirement mechanism is rodent-anchored; the lowered-perceived-effort outcome is human. PMID 15081797; PMID 33388079

  3. Disinhibition increases central neural drive and catecholamine tone Evidence-human

    By removing adenosine's dampening of excitatory neurotransmission, caffeine raises central neural drive and sympathetic outflow, which raises circulating catecholamines (adrenaline/noradrenaline). This is the plausible bridge between the receptor event and whole-body effects like alertness, heart rate, and energy-substrate mobilisation. Caffeine does raise catecholamines in humans, but the magnitude is modest and variable; the mechanistic synthesis here rests substantially on review-level sources rather than a single exercise catecholamine trial. PMID 16840650; PMID 33388079

  4. Sensitises skeletal-muscle calcium release (only at high concentrations) Established

    Caffeine potentiates calcium-induced calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum via the ryanodine receptor (RyR1), which can enhance excitation-contraction coupling. This is genuine, well-characterised pharmacology, but it requires caffeine concentrations far above those achieved by ergogenic oral doses, so it is generally NOT thought to explain performance benefits in humans — it is the mechanism behind caffeine contracture in muscle physiology and its interaction with malignant-hyperthermia-susceptible RyR1. PMID 19789379

  5. Inhibits phosphodiesterase and raises cyclic AMP (mainly at high doses / in the ECA stack) Evidence-human

    Caffeine inhibits phosphodiesterase (PDE), slowing breakdown of cyclic AMP and prolonging catecholamine/beta-adrenergic signalling — the basis of the classic synergy in the ephedrine-caffeine (EC/ECA) stack, where ephedrine drives catecholamine release and caffeine sustains the cAMP signal. Meaningful PDE inhibition needs concentrations above typical dietary exposure, so as a stand-alone ergogenic mechanism at normal doses its contribution is minor and contested; its clearest human relevance is the thermogenic ECA combination. PMID 16840650

  6. Net effect: modest, reliable improvement across many exercise tasks Established

    The integrated result of central disinhibition (plus small peripheral contributions) is a small-to-moderate improvement in aerobic endurance and smaller benefits in strength, power, muscular endurance, sprinting and jumping. An umbrella review of 11 systematic reviews (21 meta-analyses) found caffeine ergogenic across this range with GRADE-moderate certainty overall (some outcomes low/very-low), and effects are generally larger for aerobic than anaerobic tasks. PMID 30926628; PMID 33388079; PMID 29527137

  7. Response is modified by genetics and habituation Evidence-human

    Individual response varies with genes affecting metabolism (CYP1A2) and receptor signalling (ADORA2A). The ADORA2A 1976T>C variant (rs5751876) is linked to caffeine-potentiated startle and anxiety (notably in women), and habitual intake blunts some subjective effects — meaning the same dose helps some people, does nothing for others, and makes a minority anxious or jittery. The genotype-anxiety link is human; how much genotype explains the ERGOGENIC response specifically is less certain. PMID 22012471; PMID 33388079

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Competitive, non-selective antagonist at adenosine A1 and A2A receptors (also A2B, A3) at physiologically relevant concentrations. ChEMBL curates CHEMBL113 as an adenosine-receptor antagonist with direct interaction. In knockout MICE, the stimulant/arousal effect specifically requires A2A (A2A-knockout mice are not stimulated by caffeine, whereas A1-knockout mice are hyper-responsive), showing A1 modulates but does not mediate the arousal effect — this receptor-requirement evidence is rodent. Secondary, higher-concentration actions: phosphodiesterase inhibition (raises cAMP) and sensitisation of RyR1-mediated sarcoplasmic-reticulum calcium release.

Signalling cascade. Adenosine A1/A2A blockade -> loss of the inhibitory 'adenosine brake' on excitatory neurotransmission -> increased CNS neural drive and arousal + increased sympathetic outflow -> modest rise in circulating catecholamines and cAMP-mediated signalling -> lowered perceived exertion, increased alertness, substrate mobilisation, small thermogenesis. High-concentration RyR1/PDE effects add peripheral muscle and metabolic actions but are minor at ergogenic oral doses.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsule, coffee, gum, gels, energy drinks); anhydrous caffeine and gum are absorbed faster than coffee.
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapid and near-complete absorption; oral bioavailability approximately 100%. Peak plasma concentration typically ~30-120 min after ingestion (faster for gum/anhydrous, slower with food or coffee). Most exercise studies dose ~60 min pre-exercise.
Half-life
Roughly 3-7 hours in healthy non-smoking adults, but highly variable. Markedly shortened in smokers (CYP1A2 induction) and substantially prolonged by oral contraceptives, pregnancy (can exceed ~15 h in late pregnancy) and liver disease.
Metabolism
Almost entirely hepatic via cytochrome P450 CYP1A2 (~90-95% of clearance), producing the principal active metabolite paraxanthine plus theobromine and theophylline. CYP1A2 activity varies widely between people and is induced by smoking and inhibited by oral contraceptives, some SSRIs, fluvoxamine and quinolone antibiotics.
Excretion
Renal excretion of metabolites; <5% of parent caffeine excreted unchanged. Not meaningfully cleared unchanged by the kidney.
Notes
Grzegorzewski et al. systematically analysed caffeine PK across 141 publications and highlighted large inter-individual variability driven by smoking, oral contraceptives, drug interactions and disease — which is why a fixed mg/kg dose produces very different plasma exposure between people (PMID 35280254).

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

ISSN position stands conclude caffeine is reliably ergogenic at ~3-6 mg/kg body mass, with the minimal effective dose possibly as low as ~2 mg/kg. Higher doses (>=9 mg/kg) do NOT add performance benefit and increase side effects (anxiety, tachycardia, GI distress, insomnia). Effect size is dose-related up to a plateau and is larger for aerobic endurance than for strength/power. In the Grgic strength/power meta-analysis the pooled benefit was small (strength SMD 0.20, 95% CI 0.03-0.36; power SMD 0.17, 95% CI 0.00-0.34), with the clearer effect on upper-body strength. This is a research summary of what the literature reports about effective ranges — NOT a recommendation of how to dose or 'run' caffeine.

How it compares

  • vs Ephedrine (in the ECA stack)
    same: Both raise sympathetic/catecholamine tone and support thermogenesis and fat oxidation; combined they act synergistically (ephedrine drives catecholamine release, caffeine sustains cAMP via PDE inhibition).
    different: Ephedrine directly promotes catecholamine release and has a far worse cardiovascular/adverse-event and regulatory profile; caffeine's ergogenic action is mainly central adenosine antagonism, not catecholamine release.
  • vs Theophylline / theobromine (other methylxanthines)
    same: Same chemical class; also adenosine antagonists and PDE inhibitors; theophylline and theobromine are caffeine metabolites.
    different: Theophylline has a much narrower therapeutic window and stronger bronchodilator/cardiac effects; caffeine is the most CNS-selective and best-tolerated of the group at common doses.
  • vs Modafinil (wakefulness agent)
    same: Both promote wakefulness/vigilance and are used against sleep deprivation.
    different: Modafinil acts largely through dopaminergic/other wake-promoting circuits rather than adenosine-receptor antagonism, is prescription-only, and is a WADA-prohibited stimulant, whereas caffeine is permitted in competition.
  • vs Coffee (as delivery vehicle)
    same: Coffee delivers the same caffeine molecule and shows ergogenic effects.
    different: Coffee's absorption can be slower and dose less precise, and it contains other bioactive compounds; earlier guidance suggested anhydrous caffeine may be more reliably ergogenic than coffee, though newer work argues coffee is broadly comparable.

History &amp; legal status

Caffeine is the world's most widely consumed psychoactive substance, first isolated from coffee in the early 19th century. It has long-standing clinical uses (apnoea of prematurity, via caffeine citrate; analgesic adjuvant). In sport it was formerly on the WADA/IOC prohibited list with a urinary threshold until it was removed in 2004; since then it has been on the WADA Monitoring Program only, making it legal in competition. It is the A-tier ergogenic in the ISSN position stands and the backbone of pre-workout supplements. The ephedrine-caffeine (ECA) fat-loss stack drove its use in weight-loss products until ephedra/ephedrine's cardiovascular risks led to regulatory restriction of the ephedrine component (not caffeine). Regulatory bodies broadly cite ~400 mg/day as a moderate intake not associated with adverse effects in healthy non-pregnant adults.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether caffeine's acute ergogenic benefits translate into any enhancement of CHRONIC skeletal-muscle remodelling / hypertrophy or long-term training adaptation — currently unresolved, and explicitly flagged as an open question in the recent literature.
  • How much of the real-world individual response is explained by CYP1A2 and ADORA2A genotype versus habitual intake, expectation/placebo, and sleep state.
  • The true magnitude and practical relevance of habituation: whether regular users lose most of the ergogenic benefit or mainly the subjective 'buzz'.
  • Generalisability beyond young men — effects in women (including across the menstrual cycle), older adults and diverse athletes are under-studied.
  • Optimal timing and delivery form (gum, mouth rinse, gels) relative to capsule dosing for specific sports.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction guidance on what to WATCH, not a dosing protocol. Track resting heart rate and blood pressure, especially in anyone with hypertension, arrhythmia, or palpitations — caffeine produces a small but real pressor/tachycardic effect. Watch for anxiety, tremor, jitteriness and panic-like symptoms (higher risk in ADORA2A 'risk' genotype carriers and those with anxiety disorders). Protect sleep: because caffeine blocks the adenosine sleep-pressure signal with a multi-hour and highly variable half-life, late-day intake can measurably degrade sleep quality; smokers clear it faster and users of oral contraceptives or pregnant people much slower. Note drug interactions via CYP1A2 (fluvoxamine, quinolone antibiotics, oral contraceptives can raise caffeine levels; smoking lowers them) and additive effects with theophylline. Be alert for intoxication signs at high total daily intake (from stacked pre-workouts/energy drinks): agitation, vomiting, marked tachycardia/arrhythmia, seizures. Anticipate a withdrawal headache/fatigue syndrome on abrupt cessation. Pregnancy, uncontrolled cardiovascular disease, and significant anxiety disorders warrant particular caution.

Caveats

Benefits are small-to-moderate and heavily individual — genetics (CYP1A2 metabolism, ADORA2A signalling) and habitual intake mean the same dose helps some people, does nothing for others, and makes a minority anxious or jittery. The two 'peripheral' mechanisms most often cited in marketing (RyR1 calcium sensitisation and phosphodiesterase inhibition) require concentrations well above those from real ergogenic doses, so the human benefit is overwhelmingly CENTRAL (adenosine antagonism, lowered perceived effort), not a direct 'harder muscle contraction' effect. A specific evidence caveat: the finding that A2A receptors are strictly REQUIRED for the stimulant effect comes from knockout mice, not humans — human data support the ergogenic/perceived-effort outcome, not the receptor-requirement mechanism directly. Most performance studies are acute and in young men; caffeine has NOT been shown to enhance chronic muscle hypertrophy or long-term training adaptation (this remains explicitly unresolved). High doses (~9 mg/kg) add side effects without adding benefit. The ephedrine-caffeine (ECA) stack works but ephedrine's cardiovascular/adverse-event profile is why that combination was restricted; nothing here is a dosing protocol. Caffeine is not benign for everyone: those with arrhythmia, uncontrolled hypertension, anxiety disorders, pregnancy, or on interacting drugs (CYP1A2 inhibitors, theophylline) should be cautious.
