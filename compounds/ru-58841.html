RU-58841

A topical androgen-receptor blocker designed to fight pattern hair loss at the scalp without shutting down androgens body-wide — supported by animal, primate and human-tissue-graft models, but never tested in a published, registered human clinical trial.

Limited human data 13 citations
Class
Nonsteroidal antiandrogen (topical androgen-receptor antagonist); aryl-hydantoin class, structurally related to nilutamide/flutamide
Primary target
Competitive antagonist at the androgen receptor (AR)
Human status
Not approved anywhere and never marketed. Originally developed at Centre de Recherches Roussel Uclaf (France) in the early 1990s as a topical antiandrogen for acne, androgenetic alopecia and hirsutism, and later carried forward under Hoechst Marion Roussel / ProStrakan as PSK-3841 / HMR-3841. Clinical development was ultimately discontinued and no Phase III efficacy/safety data were ever published; a search of ClinicalTrials.gov returns no registered RU-58841/PSK-3841/HMR-3841 trial. Direct human evidence is limited to ex-vivo human balding scalp grafts maintained on mice (PMID 9415227); the remaining efficacy data are rodent, non-human primate and in-vitro. Today it circulates as an unapproved 'research chemical' used off-label by the online hair-loss community, without pharmaceutical-grade quality control, without an established human dose, and without a formal human safety dataset. Community reports (e.g., r/tressless) describe both perceived benefit and side effects, but these are uncontrolled anecdotes, not evidence, and should not be weighed as such.

At a glance

  • Blocks DHT/testosterone at the androgen receptor locally in scalp skin — the driver of pattern hair loss
  • Increased hair-growth rate and follicle recycling in human balding scalp grafts (on nude mice) and increased hair density in the stumptailed macaque model of pattern baldness
  • Potent, reversible shrinkage of androgen-dependent sebaceous glands (relevant to acne/oiliness) in rodent models
  • Engineered for local action: at effective topical doses in animals, no measurable change in serum testosterone/DHT or prostate weight was seen
  • Does NOT inhibit 5-alpha-reductase — mechanistically distinct from finasteride/dutasteride (it blocks the receptor rather than blocking DHT synthesis), so it does not lower systemic DHT levels
  • NOT proven in humans: efficacy and safety rest on animal, primate and ex-vivo human-graft data; there is no published, registered controlled human trial establishing regrowth or a human safety profile

Mechanism, step by step

  1. DHT signaling through the AR drives follicle miniaturization in AGA Established

    In androgenetic alopecia, DHT (produced from testosterone by 5-alpha-reductase) binds the androgen receptor inside scalp dermal papilla cells and switches on a program that progressively shrinks genetically susceptible follicles, shortening each growth cycle until hairs become thin and vellus. This DHT-AR axis in dermal papilla cells is the disease target RU-58841 is meant to interrupt. Supporting evidence includes work showing testosterone/DHT inhibits hair-follicle epithelial growth specifically via dermal papilla cells from balding scalp, an effect that RU-58841 can antagonize. PMID 8977424; PMID 30811143

  2. RU-58841 competitively occupies the androgen receptor Evidence-in vitro

    RU-58841 binds the AR ligand-binding pocket with high affinity and competes with DHT/testosterone. In human prostate PC3 cells transfected with wild-type AR and an androgen-responsive reporter, RU-58841 competitively suppressed DHT-driven AR transcriptional activity, with potency comparable to hydroxyflutamide (the active metabolite of flutamide) and to bicalutamide. It also binds hamster prostate and flank-organ AR with high affinity. PMID 9798729; PMID 8136306

  3. It behaves as an antagonist, not an androgen mimic, in these assays Evidence-in vitro

    On its own, RU-58841 did not activate wild-type AR in PC3 cells at the concentrations tested; it only blocked DHT activation. So in these systems it acts as an androgen blocker rather than an androgen mimic — removing the follicle-shrinking signal rather than adding one. (See the caveat step: this 'pure antagonist' behavior is context-dependent.) PMID 9798729

  4. Blocking AR increases hair growth from human balding follicles (ex-vivo graft model) Evidence-human

    When human balding scalp grafts were maintained on testosterone-conditioned nude mice, topical 1% RU-58841 in ethanol produced a significantly higher linear hair-growth rate (P<0.04) and more follicles re-entering a growth cycle (8 of 29 active follicles vs 2 of 28 in vehicle controls) versus ethanol vehicle over 6 months of monitoring. This is human tissue, but grafted onto mice — it is ex-vivo human-tissue evidence, not an in-vivo human clinical trial. PMID 9415227

  5. Increases hair density in a non-human primate model of pattern baldness Evidence-rodent

    In stumptailed macaques — which spontaneously develop androgen-dependent frontal balding after puberty — topical RU-58841 produced a marked increase in hair density, thickness and length, with no systemic effects detected in that study. This shows the mechanism can translate to visible regrowth in an intact primate scalp, but it is animal (non-human primate) evidence, not human. PMID 9798729; PMID 8977424

  6. The same AR blockade shrinks sebaceous glands (an androgen-dependent tissue) Evidence-rodent

    Topical RU-58841 reduced sebaceous gland size by ~60% in hamster ear skin and produced castrate-level sebaceous regression in male fuzzy rats — confirming it functionally counteracts endogenous androgens in skin. In fuzzy rats it shrank sebaceous glands like castration WITHOUT reducing prostate weight or changing serum testosterone/DHT, consistent with the intended local-only action in that model. PMID 7625751; PMID 9449168

  7. Designed to self-limit systemically via fast clearance and low active-metabolite formation Evidence-rodent

    In rats, RU-58841 is cleared relatively quickly (plasma elimination half-life on the order of ~1 hour after IV dosing) and, unlike its sister compound RU-56187, converts only minimally (~1%) to the long-lived, systemically antiandrogenic N-desalkyl metabolite RU-56279 (RU-56187 converts ~77%). This low metabolite formation is the proposed pharmacological basis for its 'local strong, systemic weak' profile. All of this is rodent data. PMID 7947350

  8. Caveat: like other AR antagonists, it can act as an agonist in the right cellular context Evidence-in vitro

    In human prostate cancer DU145 cells, RU-58841 — like hydroxyflutamide, bicalutamide and cyproterone acetate — promoted interaction between the AR and the coactivator ARA70 and then ENHANCED AR transcriptional activity, an agonist-like effect. This is the type of mechanism implicated in 'antiandrogen withdrawal' in prostate cancer. Whether the coactivator environment of scalp follicles ever permits this is unknown, but it is a genuine mechanistic asterisk on the 'pure antagonist' label. PMID 9636157

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. High-affinity competitive antagonist at the androgen receptor ligand-binding domain. It binds hamster prostate and flank-organ AR with high affinity and, in cell reporter systems (PC3 cells transfected with wild-type AR + an androgen-responsive reporter), competitively suppresses DHT-driven AR transcription with potency comparable to hydroxyflutamide and bicalutamide. It is an aryl-hydantoin structurally related to nilutamide/flutamide. Chemical name in the literature: 4-[3-(4-hydroxybutyl)-4,4-dimethyl-2,5-dioxo-1-imidazolidinyl]-2-(trifluoromethyl)benzonitrile.

Signalling cascade. AR blockade prevents ligand (DHT/T)-driven AR transcriptional activation of androgen-response genes in dermal papilla and sebaceous cells. Downstream, this is proposed to halt the pro-miniaturization program in susceptible follicles (permitting recycling/regrowth, as seen in animal and human-graft models) and to reduce androgen-driven sebaceous gland growth. It does NOT inhibit 5-alpha-reductase, so it does not reduce DHT synthesis — it competes with DHT at the receptor.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Topical (scalp/skin), designed for cutaneous, largely local delivery.
Absorption / bioavailability
In rat models, skin penetration occurs substantially via the sebaceous-gland/follicular pathway: scar skin lacking sebaceous glands absorbed 3-4x less RU-58841 than normal skin, and liposomal carriers concentrated the drug in sebaceous glands, whereas an alcoholic (ethanol) solution favored stratum corneum/epidermal localization. Human transdermal/systemic absorption at real-world doses and vehicles is not characterized in the published literature.
Half-life
Short parent half-life — on the order of ~1 hour after IV dosing in rats. The RU-56279 metabolite is eliminated much more slowly (the theoretical concern for systemic carryover), but little of it forms from RU-58841.
Metabolism
Metabolized in skin, including by human keratinocytes, fibroblasts and occipital-scalp dermal papilla cells — the myristate prodrug RU-58841-myristate is converted locally to active RU-58841. Systemically, RU-58841 forms the N-desalkyl metabolite RU-56279, which is itself antiandrogenic; in rats only ~1% of RU-58841 converts to RU-56279 (versus ~77% for the sister compound RU-56187), which is the proposed mechanistic basis for its low systemic activity.
Excretion
Not well quantified in the accessible human literature; plasma clearance is rapid in rodents. Human excretion/mass-balance data are not published.
Notes
All quantitative PK (half-life, metabolite fraction, absorption route) is rodent or ex-vivo. Human PK — the parameter that actually determines systemic safety in users — has not been published.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Animal data show a topical dose-response: in the hamster flank organ, RU-58841 produced dose-dependent regression at doses as low as ~1 microgram/animal topically, while systemic (deep sex-organ) effects and effects on testosterone appeared only at much higher exposures (up to 100 micrograms/animal topically showed no effect on testosterone or deep sex organs), supporting a local-vs-systemic therapeutic window in that model. In hamster ear sebaceous glands, ~60% maximal reduction occurred at ~10 micrograms/day, plateaued (4 vs 12 weeks similar) and was fully reversible within ~4 weeks of stopping. The human graft study used a 1% ethanol solution. There is NO validated human dose-response curve, and no human data defining the dose at which systemic AR blockade would begin — a key safety gap. (No dosing protocol is provided here.)

How it compares

  • vs Finasteride / dutasteride (5-alpha-reductase inhibitors)
    same: Both ultimately aim to reduce DHT-driven follicle miniaturization in AGA
    different: RU-58841 BLOCKS the receptor and does NOT lower systemic DHT; finasteride/dutasteride BLOCK DHT synthesis and lower systemic DHT. Finasteride has large approved human RCT efficacy/safety data and a documented (if debated) systemic side-effect profile; RU-58841's selling point is avoiding systemic 5-AR effects, but it has no comparable human safety dataset.
  • vs Topical finasteride
    same: Both are topical strategies intended to limit systemic exposure while treating scalp AGA
    different: Topical finasteride still inhibits 5-AR and measurably lowers systemic DHT/plasma finasteride in human PK studies (only partially 'local') and has human trial data including a Phase III. RU-58841's mechanism is receptor blockade, and its systemic sparing is shown mainly in animals, not confirmed in published human PK, with no registered human trial.
  • vs Clascoterone (cortexolone 17α-propionate)
    same: Both are topical AR antagonists acting locally in dermal papilla / sebaceous cells, and clascoterone has been shown to be an AR antagonist in dermal papilla cells in vitro
    different: Clascoterone completed formal clinical development, is FDA-approved (for acne) and has published human trial data; RU-58841 has NO approval and no published, registered controlled human efficacy/safety trial. Clascoterone is a manufactured pharmaceutical; RU-58841 is a gray-market research chemical.
  • vs Bicalutamide / flutamide / enzalutamide (systemic AR antagonists)
    same: Same broad class (nonsteroidal AR antagonists) and share the mechanistic possibility of coactivator-dependent agonism
    different: Those are oral systemic agents intended for body-wide AR blockade (prostate cancer; flutamide/bicalutamide also used off-label for female pattern hair loss). RU-58841 was engineered to self-limit systemically so it could be applied to the scalp without those systemic antiandrogen effects — a design goal demonstrated in animals, not confirmed in humans.

History &amp; legal status

Discovered at Centre de Recherches Roussel Uclaf (France) in the early 1990s as a topical antiandrogen for acne, androgenetic alopecia and hirsutism (first characterized by Battmann et al., 1994). Development later proceeded under Hoechst Marion Roussel and ProStrakan as PSK-3841 / HMR-3841. Clinical development was ultimately discontinued; no Phase III efficacy/safety results were published, the compound never gained regulatory approval anywhere, and ClinicalTrials.gov shows no registered trial under its names. Since roughly the 2010s it has circulated as an unapproved 'research chemical' widely used off-label in the online hair-loss community — sold without pharmaceutical manufacturing standards, without an approved label, and without human safety oversight.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Human systemic absorption and blood levels at real-world scalp doses and vehicles (especially with penetration enhancers) — the parameter that determines whether 'no systemic effect' holds in people
  • Whether RU-58841 measurably suppresses the HPTA or causes systemic antiandrogen effects (libido, fertility, gynecomastia) in humans with chronic use
  • Long-term efficacy and safety in humans — no controlled or registered trial has measured hair outcomes or adverse events over time
  • Whether the in-vitro ARA70-driven agonist conversion is relevant in scalp follicles (could theoretically blunt efficacy or cause paradoxical effects)
  • Reproductive/teratogenic risk in humans (mechanistically expected for an antiandrogen but unstudied for this molecule)
  • Real-world product identity, purity and contaminant profile of gray-market material
  • Optimal or safe formulation — most human-relevant data used a 1% ethanol solution, whereas community formulations (varied solvents, the myristate prodrug, nanoparticle carriers) are largely uncharacterized in humans

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction information, not an endorsement or a protocol. Because RU-58841 is unapproved and its human systemic exposure is unquantified, anyone exposed should treat 'no systemic effect' as unproven. Reasonable things to WATCH FOR / monitor: (1) Local skin — irritation, redness, itching or dermatitis at the application site; stop if it develops. (2) Systemic antiandrogen signs — changes in libido, erectile function, mood or energy, and breast tenderness/swelling (possible gynecomastia); these warrant stopping and seeking medical review. (3) Bloodwork with a clinician if used — a clinician can order baseline and follow-up total/free testosterone, DHT, LH/FSH, estradiol, and an age-appropriate general panel to detect any unexpected systemic effect. (4) Absolute avoidance of exposure in anyone pregnant or who could become pregnant, and preventing transfer to others (partners, children) from treated skin, given antiandrogen teratogenic potential. (5) Source/product caution — unverified gray-market purity and identity are themselves hazards. No dosing regimen is provided; any decision about use should involve a qualified clinician.

Caveats

The central caveat: the 'topical finasteride alternative with no systemic effects' framing is a mechanistic hypothesis validated in animals, not a proven human fact. The 'no systemic activity' claim rests on rodent pharmacokinetics/pharmacology (fast clearance, ~1% conversion to the long-lived active metabolite RU-56279) and animal endpoints (unchanged serum androgens, unchanged prostate weight) — it has NOT been quantified with human blood-level or hormone data at real-world doses, vehicles (many users use strong penetration enhancers), and application areas. AR antagonists, including RU-58841, can behave as agonists in certain coactivator contexts (ARA70/DU145 data), which is a genuine mechanistic asterisk. Because it is a receptor blocker, it does NOT lower systemic DHT (unlike finasteride) — so it is not mechanistically interchangeable with 5-alpha-reductase inhibitors. Gray-market supply means unknown purity, identity and contamination. There is no controlled long-term human safety, teratogenicity, or reproductive dataset; as an antiandrogen it is a plausible reproductive/developmental hazard and exposure during pregnancy (including transfer from treated skin) should be avoided. Nothing here is a dosing recommendation or protocol.
