Beetroot juice / dietary inorganic nitrate

Main target: Entero-salivary nitrate-nitrite-NO pathway. Human evidence is still limited.

EvidenceLimited human data
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

The NOS-independent way to raise nitric oxide: swallow nitrate, let your mouth bacteria and low-oxygen tissue turn it into NO, and do the same work on less oxygen.

Limited human data 12 citations
Class
Nitrate-nitrite-NO precursor (dietary/inorganic nitrate ergogenic; A-tier for endurance in ISSN/AIS-style ergogenic classifications)
Primary target
Entero-salivary nitrate-nitrite-NO pathway: oral commensal bacteria reduce swallowed NO3- to nitrite (NO2-), which is further reduced to nitric oxide (NO) in acidic/hypoxic tissue
Human status
Established, extensively studied dietary ergogenic in humans, not a drug. Inorganic nitrate from beetroot is a normal food constituent (also from leafy greens); beetroot juice 'shots' are widely sold as sports supplements and are commonly ranked A-tier in ISSN/AIS-style ergogenic classifications for endurance. Human evidence is large: a 2022 systematic review/meta-analysis (Silva 2022, PMID 35580578) pooled 123 RCTs (1705 participants) and found a small but significant overall performance benefit (SMD 0.101, 95% CI 0.051-0.151, I2=0%), strongest for 2-10 min efforts, from beetroot juice or a high-nitrate diet — while nitrate salts were NOT significantly effective (P=0.629). Blood-pressure lowering is confirmed in a phase-appropriate double-blind RCT in hypertensive/pre-hypertensive patients (Kapil 2015, PMID 25421976; NCT01405898, n=68): 24-h ambulatory BP fell 7.7/5.2 mmHg with endothelial function improved ~20% and arterial stiffness reduced. The main honesty caveats: the ergogenic effect shrinks in elite/highly-trained endurance athletes, is inconsistent for pure strength/resistance and for reducing O2 cost in some recreational cohorts (Esen 2022, PMID 35889796 found improved intermittent-running performance but no drop in O2 cost), and depends entirely on an intact oral microbiome.

At a glance

  • Reduces the O2 cost of submaximal exercise (~1-5% lower VO2 for the same workload) - the core 'exercise economy' effect, though not observed in every recreational cohort
  • Extends time-to-exhaustion / time-to-task-failure in high-intensity constant-load exercise (e.g. Wylie 2013: +12-14% at effective doses)
  • Small but real improvement in short/middle-duration performance (meta-analytic SMD ~0.10), largest for efforts lasting ~2-10 minutes
  • Lowers resting and ambulatory blood pressure (dose-dependent; 24-h ambulatory BP fell 7.7/5.2 mmHg in hypertensive patients), improves endothelial function and reduces arterial stiffness
  • Increases NO bioavailability via a NOS-independent route that works best in hypoxia and acidosis - the mechanistic complement to citrulline/arginine
  • May aid high-force/sprint and breath-hold/apnea performance, with weaker (breath-hold: human but niche) or rodent-leaning (fast-twitch force) evidence
  • Effect is attenuated or absent in highly-trained elite endurance athletes and can be abolished by antibacterial mouthwash

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps6 human / established 1 rodent / preclinical 1 contested / speculative

  1. You swallow nitrate; a substantial fraction is concentrated back into saliva Established

    Beetroot juice delivers a big bolus of inorganic nitrate (NO3-). After absorption, a substantial fraction of circulating nitrate is actively taken up by the salivary glands and secreted back into the mouth (classically estimated at roughly a quarter in the Lundberg/Weitzberg physiology literature). This 'entero-salivary recirculation' is the setup step that makes the whole pathway work — nitrate has to come back to the tongue to be activated. (Note: the ~25% figure is standard physiology from tracer studies, not a number stated by the Kapil 2010 paper, which confirms the entero-salivary circulation mechanism qualitatively.) PMID 20585108 (Kapil 2010, Hypertension) — confirms nitrite is raised via bioconversion in the entero-salivary circulation (abstract only; the specific ~25% fraction is textbook tracer-study physiology, not quoted by this paper)

  2. Mouth bacteria reduce nitrate to nitrite Established

    Humans can't efficiently reduce nitrate on their own. Nitrate-reducing commensal bacteria living on the back of the tongue convert salivary NO3- to nitrite (NO2-), which is then swallowed and absorbed, raising plasma nitrite. This bacterial step is essential — Govoni 2008 showed a chlorhexidine antibacterial mouthwash abolished salivary nitrite conversion and markedly attenuated the plasma-nitrite rise in 7 volunteers. PMID 18793740 (Govoni 2008, Nitric Oxide) — abstract confirms mouthwash abolished salivary nitrite conversion and attenuated plasma nitrite; 'critically dependent on nitrate reduction in the oral cavity by commensal bacteria'

  3. Nitrite is reduced to NO where oxygen is low and tissue is acidic Established

    Unlike the classic L-arginine/NOS route (which needs oxygen), nitrite is converted to NO by deoxygenated haemoglobin/myoglobin, xanthine oxidoreductase and low pH. This means NO is generated preferentially in exactly the tissue that is working hardest and most oxygen-starved — a NOS-independent, hypoxia-favoured source of NO. This is the key mechanistic contrast with citrulline/arginine. (This nitrite-reductase biochemistry is established physiology; the cited Jones 2013 review frames the downstream exercise mechanisms tentatively.) PMID 23765348 (Jones 2013, review) — abstract states effect is 'presumably linked to bioconversion of ingested nitrate into nitrite and thence to nitric oxide' (review, abstract only)

  4. Plasma nitrite rises dose-dependently and blood pressure falls Established

    More nitrate in equals more plasma nitrite, up to a point, and the rise in nitrite tracks a fall in blood pressure via NO-mediated vasodilation and raised cyclic GMP. In hypertensive/pre-hypertensive patients, 250 mL beetroot juice daily for 4 weeks lowered 24-h ambulatory BP by 7.7/5.2 mmHg and improved endothelial function (~20%) and arterial stiffness — a real, durable human effect with no tachyphylaxis over the study. PMID 25421976 (Kapil 2015, Hypertension; NCT01405898) — abstract confirms n=68, 24-h ambulatory BP reduced 7.7/5.2 mmHg, endothelial function +~20%, arterial stiffness reduced (abstract only)

  5. Working muscle does the same job on less oxygen Evidence-human

    The signature ergogenic effect: after nitrate, the steady-state oxygen uptake (VO2) for a fixed submaximal workload drops by roughly 1-5%. In the foundational human trial (Bailey 2009), dietary nitrate cut the O2 cost of low-intensity cycling and extended time-to-exhaustion in high-intensity exercise. This 'improved exercise economy' is reproducible in recreationally-to-moderately trained people but is not universal across cohorts. PMID 19661447 (Bailey 2009, J Appl Physiol) — foundational demonstration of reduced O2 cost and enhanced high-intensity tolerance

  6. Part of the economy gain may come from more efficient mitochondria Contested

    One proposed reason muscle needs less O2: nitrate improves mitochondrial coupling. In a small double-blind human crossover trial, nitrate raised the P/O ratio (ATP made per oxygen consumed) in skeletal-muscle mitochondria and lowered proton leak, and the P/O improvement correlated with the drop in whole-body O2 cost. This is a single small human study that has not been consistently replicated — plausible but not settled. PMID 21284982 (Larsen 2011, Cell Metab) — abstract confirms improved P/O ratio, reduced state-4 respiration, and correlation with reduced O2 cost (small human crossover, abstract only; not consistently replicated)

  7. Nitrate improves calcium handling and force in fast-twitch fibres (rodent) Evidence-rodent

    A separate mechanism for 'pump'/power effects: in mice, dietary nitrate raised tetanic intracellular calcium and contractile force specifically in fast-twitch (not slow-twitch) muscle, with increased calsequestrin-1 and dihydropyridine-receptor expression. This may explain reports of improved high-force/sprint performance — but it is a rodent finding and has not been cleanly confirmed in human muscle. PMID 22687611 (Hernandez 2012, J Physiol) — abstract confirms increased fast-twitch tetanic [Ca2+], force, CASQ1 and DHPR, no change in slow-twitch; C57bl/6 mice (rodent only)

  8. Better breath-hold/apnea performance under hypoxia; broader O2-delivery effects less directly shown Evidence-human

    Where oxygen is limiting, nitrate's benefits are clearest. Engan 2012 showed acute nitrate lengthened breath-hold (dry static apnea) duration in trained apnea divers, attributed to reduced metabolic cost. The broader idea that NO from nitrite preferentially vasodilates hypoxic tissue and improves muscle O2 delivery is plausible review-level physiology but is not directly demonstrated by this apnea trial. Effects are generally clearest in hypoxia/altitude and smaller in normoxia. PMID 22588047 (Engan 2012, Respir Physiol Neurobiol) — abstract confirms increased apneic duration in 12 trained apnea divers, 'may increase apneic duration by reducing metabolic costs' (does NOT measure muscle O2 delivery directly)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. No classical receptor. The 'target' is a redox pathway: inorganic nitrate (NO3-) is an inert anion until reduced. Bioactivation requires (a) commensal oral bacterial nitrate reductases to make nitrite (NO2-), then (b) tissue nitrite reductases - deoxyhaemoglobin, deoxymyoglobin, xanthine oxidoreductase, and non-enzymatic acidic disproportionation - to liberate nitric oxide (NO). NO then acts through soluble guanylate cyclase / cGMP (vasodilation) and via nitrosation/redox effects on mitochondrial proteins and muscle Ca2+-handling proteins.

Signalling cascade. NO3- (diet) -> salivary recirculation -> oral bacteria -> NO2- -> tissue reduction (favoured by hypoxia/acidosis) -> NO -> [sGC/cGMP -> vasodilation & O2 delivery] + [improved mitochondrial P/O coupling, contested] + [enhanced fast-twitch Ca2+ handling, rodent] -> lower O2 cost of exercise, greater tolerance, lower blood pressure. Crucially NOS-independent, so it complements (does not duplicate) L-arginine/L-citrulline.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (beetroot juice concentrate 'shots', whole beetroot, high-nitrate leafy greens, or nitrate salts).
Absorption / bioavailability
Nitrate is rapidly and near-completely absorbed in the upper GI tract; oral bioavailability of nitrate itself is high (~100% is the commonly cited figure). The functional bottleneck is bacterial conversion to nitrite, not absorption. Plasma nitrate peaks ~1-2 h; the downstream plasma nitrite peaks later, ~2-3 h post-ingestion (Wylie 2013), the basis for pre-exercise timing.
Half-life
Plasma nitrate half-life is on the order of several hours; plasma nitrite is much shorter-lived and is the bioactive intermediate. Daily dosing sustains the O2-cost effect for at least 15 days provided the same nitrate dose is taken daily (Jones 2013 review).
Metabolism
A substantial fraction of absorbed nitrate is actively re-secreted into saliva (entero-salivary circulation; classically estimated ~25% from tracer studies, though not quantified in the cited Kapil 2010 paper) and reduced to nitrite by tongue bacteria; nitrite is then swallowed and further reduced to NO in blood and tissue. Endogenous mammalian enzymes do not efficiently perform the nitrate->nitrite step, hence the obligatory microbial role.
Excretion
Renal; the majority of an ingested nitrate load is excreted in urine over ~24-48 h. Plasma nitrite elevation is transient (hours); nitrate elevation lasts longer.
Notes
Peak plasma nitrite ~2-3 h after ingestion (Wylie 2013, PMID 23640589) underlies the standard pre-exercise timing window. Antibacterial mouthwash markedly attenuates the plasma-nitrite rise (PMID 18793740).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Dose-dependent up to a plateau. Wylie 2013 (PMID 23640589, n=10) gave 4.2, 8.4 and 16.8 mmol nitrate (70/140/280 mL concentrated beetroot juice): 70 mL (4.2 mmol) did not alter physiological responses to exercise; 140 mL (8.4 mmol) and 280 mL (16.8 mmol) reduced submaximal VO2 (by ~1.7% and ~3.0%) and extended time-to-task-failure (by ~14% and ~12%), with NO additional benefit from 16.8 vs 8.4 mmol - i.e. a plateau near ~8 mmol. The 2022 meta-analysis (PMID 35580578) reported the most effective acute dose as 5-14.9 mmol taken >=150 min before exercise, and for multi-day protocols an optimal 5-9.9 mmol/day, with benefit greatest for efforts lasting 2-10 min and in more hypoxic conditions. (No specific product/serving protocol is given here by design - this is mechanism and evidence description, not a how-to.)

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs L-citrulline / L-arginine
    same: Both aim to raise NO and improve blood flow / 'pump' and endurance economy.
    different: Citrulline/arginine feed the OXYGEN-DEPENDENT NOS enzyme pathway; nitrate feeds the NOS-INDEPENDENT nitrite->NO pathway that works best in hypoxia/acidosis. Nitrate has the stronger direct evidence for reducing O2 cost of exercise and for lowering blood pressure; the two are mechanistically complementary rather than interchangeable.
  • vs Beetroot juice vs nitrate salts (NaNO3/KNO3)
    same: Both deliver inorganic nitrate and raise plasma nitrite.
    different: Meta-analytic data show whole beetroot juice is reliably ergogenic whereas nitrate salts were NOT significantly effective (PMID 35580578), implying betalains/polyphenols in beetroot may contribute beyond nitrate alone; salts also carry a sodium/potassium load.
  • vs Organic nitrates (nitroglycerin/GTN, isosorbide - drugs)
    same:
    different: Pharmaceutical organic nitrates release NO directly and largely non-enzymatically for angina, cause classic nitrate tolerance and headaches, and differ in kinetics; dietary inorganic nitrate is a slower, microbiome-dependent, food-based NO source without classic nitrate tolerance.
  • vs Caffeine (A-tier ergogenic)
    same:
    different: Caffeine acts largely centrally (adenosine antagonism, lower perceived exertion) with broader performance effects; nitrate acts peripherally on O2 economy/vasculature. Effects appear non-overlapping and are often stacked.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Inorganic nitrate is a natural food constituent (beetroot, spinach, rocket, celery). The modern sports story began when Larsen and colleagues (Karolinska) showed nitrate lowered the O2 cost of exercise, followed by Bailey 2009 (Exeter) formalizing the ergogenic effect and the Jones/Vanhatalo group building the field. Kapil/Ahluwalia (Barts/Queen Mary) established the blood-pressure application (NCT01405898). It is not a regulated drug: beetroot juice is sold as a food/supplement (e.g. concentrated 'shots'). It is NOT on the WADA prohibited list and is permitted in sport. WHO/EFSA set an Acceptable Daily Intake for nitrate as a food additive (~3.7 mg/kg/day); typical beetroot 'shot' doses can exceed this acutely - but the food-additive ADI was set around processed-food nitrite/nitrosamine concerns, not vegetable nitrate, and is widely regarded as not directly applicable to whole-vegetable sources. (Historical/regulatory framing is general-knowledge context, not tied to a single cited trial.)

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Whether elite endurance athletes get any meaningful ergogenic benefit, or whether their high baseline NO/economy leaves no headroom.
  • The true contribution of the mitochondrial-efficiency mechanism in humans - Larsen 2011 (PMID 21284982) has not been consistently replicated, and some groups find no P/O change.
  • Whether the rodent fast-twitch Ca2+/force mechanism (PMID 22687611) translates to human strength/sprint performance.
  • How much of beetroot's benefit is nitrate vs betalains/polyphenols, given nitrate salts underperformed whole juice in meta-analysis (PMID 35580578).
  • Long-term safety of repeated high-dose nitrate loading, and the real-world relevance (if any) of endogenous nitrosamine concerns for vegetable-source nitrate.
  • Optimal individualization given large inter-individual variability in oral microbiome and thus in nitrite conversion.
  • Interaction with training adaptation - whether chronic use blunts or supports endurance adaptations over a season.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

What to watch (harm-reduction, not a protocol): (1) Blood pressure - the BP-lowering effect is real (24-h ambulatory ~7.7/5.2 mmHg in patients); anyone on antihypertensives, organic nitrates, or PDE5 inhibitors, or with low baseline BP, should be aware of possible additive hypotension and monitor BP. (2) Beeturia - red urine/stool is harmless; knowing it can happen avoids a false 'blood in urine' scare. (3) Oral hygiene practices - antibacterial/chlorhexidine mouthwash, tongue-scraping, or recent antibiotics can silently abolish the benefit; if there is 'no effect,' suspect the oral microbiome. (4) GI tolerance to concentrated juice. (5) Renal considerations - nitrate is renally cleared, and the key hypertension RCT excluded eGFR<50; those with significant renal impairment should be cautious with large loads. (6) Remember that plasma nitrite peaks ~2-3 h after ingestion and the ergogenic response plateaus around ~8 mmol in dose-response work - a reason not to keep escalating intake in search of more effect. Consult a clinician before use if hypertensive-medicated, pregnant, or renally impaired.

Caveats

Honest limits: (1) The whole pathway is abolished by antibacterial (chlorhexidine) mouthwash — no oral bacteria, no plasma-nitrite rise (PMID 18793740, n=7). (2) Benefit is attenuated or absent in elite endurance athletes, who already have high NO/economy; most positive data come from recreational-to-moderately-trained people (PMID 35580578). (3) Effects on maximal strength and resistance-training performance are inconsistent and weaker than the endurance/economy data. (4) The mitochondrial-efficiency mechanism rests largely on one small human study and has not been consistently replicated (PMID 21284982); the Ca2+/force mechanism is rodent (PMID 22687611). (5) Dose-response plateaus: in Wylie 2013 there was no additional benefit from 16.8 vs 8.4 mmol nitrate (PMID 23640589). (6) Nitrate salts (e.g. sodium/potassium nitrate) were NOT significantly ergogenic in the 2022 meta-analysis whereas whole beetroot juice was, suggesting non-nitrate beetroot compounds (betalains, polyphenols) may contribute (PMID 35580578). (7) The economy mechanism is not obligatory: at least one RCT found improved intermittent-running performance with no reduction in O2 cost (PMID 35889796). (8) Beeturia (red urine/stool) is harmless but alarming to the uninitiated. This monograph describes mechanism and evidence, not a dosing protocol.
