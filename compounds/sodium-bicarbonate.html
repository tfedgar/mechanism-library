Sodium bicarbonate

Main target: Raises blood bicarbonate. Human evidence is still limited.

Works in humans?Limited human data
Approved?FDA-approved

The cheapest, most proven "buffer" in the supplement aisle: baking soda that borrows minutes of tolerance for all-out efforts by mopping up acid in the blood, not the muscle itself.

Limited human dataFDA-approved 9 citations
Class
Extracellular (blood) buffering agent / alkalizing salt; ISSN A-tier ergogenic aid
Primary target
Raises blood bicarbonate (HCO3-) and blood pH, steepening the H+ and lactate concentration gradient across the muscle membrane so protons and lactate leave the working muscle faster, delaying the fall in muscle pH that limits high-intensity effort
Human status
Established, human-proven ergogenic aid with decades of RCTs and multiple meta-analyses in trained and recreational athletes of both sexes. The ISSN position stand (2021, PMID 34503527) rates it as effective for high-intensity efforts of ~30 s-12 min. Effect sizes are small and context-dependent: a 2025 GRADE-rated meta-analysis of continuous running (PMID 41416636) found only negligible, non-significant benefit in a mixed-sex population (SMD 0.18, 95% CI -0.01 to 0.36, p=0.06, moderate certainty) after adjusting for GI dropouts and publication bias, though a small significant effect (SMD 0.40, 95% CI 0.18-0.63) persisted in male-only studies. Bottom line: real but modest, better established in males and in short, glycolytically-demanding tasks, and partly placebo-mediated. It is also a routine over-the-counter/medical substance (antacid, treatment of metabolic acidosis), so its safety profile at these acute doses is well characterized.

At a glance

  • Improves performance in high-intensity efforts lasting ~30 s to ~12 min (cycling, running, swimming, rowing, repeated sprints)
  • Ergogenic in combat sports (boxing, judo, karate, taekwondo, wrestling) and other muscular-endurance tasks
  • Works in single-bout and multiple-bout/intermittent exercise, and in both men and women
  • Typical magnitude is small (a few percent) and partly attributable to placebo/expectancy; can shrink to negligible in some mixed-sex continuous-endurance settings
  • Most common downside is GI distress: bloating, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain — which can itself hurt performance
  • May be additive when stacked with beta-alanine or creatine; unclear whether additive with caffeine or nitrate
  • Cheap, legal, and permitted by WADA (not on the prohibited list)

Mechanism, step by step

8 steps7 human-evidenced 1 preclinical

  1. You swallow a base, not a stimulant Established

    Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3, baking soda) is an alkaline salt. Once absorbed from the gut it dissociates into sodium and bicarbonate ions and adds bicarbonate to the blood. It does not act on a receptor or enzyme like a drug — it works purely by chemistry, shifting the acid-base balance of the fluid outside your cells. PMID 34503527

  2. Blood bicarbonate and pH rise (metabolic alkalosis) Established

    A standard 0.3 g/kg dose reliably raises blood bicarbonate by roughly 5-8 mmol/L and nudges blood pH upward, producing a mild, temporary metabolic alkalosis. This is the necessary precondition for any performance effect — no rise in blood buffering, no benefit. Peak bicarbonate is typically reached 60-180 min after ingestion, and the timing shifts with capsule size and formulation. PMID 33681279

  3. A steeper gradient pulls acid OUT of the muscle Established

    During all-out exercise, working muscle produces H+ (protons) and lactate. Because the blood outside the muscle is now more alkaline and bicarbonate-rich, the chemical gradient across the muscle membrane is steeper. This is the core idea: bicarbonate does not enter the muscle cell — it makes the outside a better 'sink,' so H+ and lactate flow out faster. PMID 34503527

  4. Efflux runs through lactate/proton co-transporters (MCTs) Evidence-human

    Protons and lactate leave muscle largely via monocarboxylate transporters (MCT1 and MCT4) in the muscle membrane, which co-transport one lactate with one H+ and are driven by the concentration gradient. A larger outward gradient — the mechanistic consequence of raising blood bicarbonate — increases the driving force for this efflux. The transporter biology is well established; that oral bicarbonate meaningfully accelerates flux through it in vivo is a reasonable inference rather than something measured directly in each performance trial. PMID 22012699

  5. Muscle pH falls a little more slowly, delaying fatigue Evidence-human

    By speeding removal of H+, bicarbonate helps blunt or delay the drop in muscle pH during repeated or sustained maximal effort. Rising intramuscular acidity is one contributor to fatigue (it interferes with the contractile and energy-producing machinery), so slowing that fall can extend the time you can hold high power. Note the muscle pH effect is modest and not always detectable — the consistent, measurable change is in the blood compartment. PMID 34503527

  6. Net result: more work before you hit the wall Established

    In tasks limited by acid accumulation — think 30 seconds to about 12 minutes of near-maximal effort — the delayed acidosis translates into small but real gains: a bit more total work, an extra sprint, a slightly faster time trial, or more repetitions to failure. The effect is on the order of a few percent, meaningful in competition but easily lost in day-to-day variability, and in some settings (e.g. a mixed-sex continuous-running meta-analysis) it shrinks to negligible after adjusting for GI dropouts. PMID 34503527

  7. A repeatedly-cited caveat: part of the benefit is placebo/expectancy Established

    The ISSN position stand explicitly concludes that a portion of bicarbonate's ergogenic effect appears to be placebo-driven — expecting a benefit contributes to it. This does not negate the physiology, but it means the 'pure' physiological effect is smaller than uncontrolled or unblinded results suggest. PMID 34503527

  8. Speculative bonus: it may amplify training adaptation Evidence-rodent

    Taking bicarbonate before a single high-intensity interval session augmented the exercise-induced rise in PGC-1a mRNA (a master switch for mitochondrial biogenesis) in human muscle (~7-fold vs ~5-fold), and chronic use enhanced adaptations in rats. This raises the hypothesis that habitual pre-training use could improve adaptations over time — but the human data are limited to acute molecular signals, not proven long-term outcomes; the long-term adaptation claim rests on rodent chronic-training data. PMID 26384407

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No receptor or enzyme target. Sodium bicarbonate is a simple inorganic salt that acts as a physiological buffer: HCO3- + H+ <-> H2CO3 <-> H2O + CO2. Its 'target' is the extracellular acid-base equilibrium, not a protein. In the gut, bicarbonate reacts with gastric acid to release CO2 (the source of belching/bloating); absorbed bicarbonate raises the plasma HCO3- reservoir.

Signalling cascade. Raised plasma HCO3-/pH -> increased extracellular buffering capacity and a steeper transmembrane H+ and lactate gradient -> greater driving force for H+/lactate efflux from working muscle via monocarboxylate transporters (MCT1/MCT4) and Na+/H+ exchange -> attenuated fall in intramuscular pH during maximal exercise -> reduced pH-related impairment of glycolytic enzymes and contractile function -> delayed fatigue. A parallel, speculative branch: pre-exercise alkalosis augments post-exercise PGC-1a signalling, hypothetically enhancing mitochondrial adaptation to training.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (aqueous solution, gelatine capsules, enteric-coated capsules, mini-tablets, or bicarbonate-rich mineral water). Enteric coating and smaller capsules/particles alter release site and speed.
Absorption / bioavailability
Bicarbonate is absorbed from the GI tract; the pharmacodynamic readout is the rise in blood [HCO3-]. A 0.3 g/kg dose reliably raises blood bicarbonate ~5-8 mmol/L. Peak alkalosis is not fixed at a single time — it depends on formulation and capsule size.
Half-life
Not a classical half-life; the ergogenic 'window' of elevated blood bicarbonate typically spans roughly 60-180+ min post-ingestion, which is why timing to peak alkalosis matters for competition.
Metabolism
Not metabolized in the drug sense; participates in the bicarbonate buffer system. Excess bicarbonate load is handled by respiratory compensation (CO2 exhalation) and renal bicarbonate handling.
Excretion
Renal excretion of excess bicarbonate and sodium; pulmonary elimination of CO2. Alkalosis is transient (hours).
Notes
Time-to-peak (Tmax) of blood bicarbonate is formulation-dependent: in a controlled comparison, smaller capsules (size 3) reached peak ~94 min vs ~121 min (size 000) and ~141 min (size 0) for larger capsules, and had a shorter lag before bicarbonate began rising (~13 vs ~28 min), without changing GI symptom scores in that study (PMID 33681279). This supports 'individualized time-to-peak' strategies over a fixed 60-min rule.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

The ISSN summarizes single-dose data as: 0.2 g/kg is about the minimum effective dose; 0.3 g/kg is the optimal single dose; and higher doses (0.4-0.5 g/kg) do NOT add benefit but DO increase the incidence and severity of GI side effects — a flat-to-inverted top end of the dose-response curve driven by tolerability, not efficacy. Multi-day 'loading' protocols (0.4-0.5 g/kg/day split across meals for 3-7 days) are also effective and may reduce competition-day GI risk. Effect magnitude is small regardless of dose; pushing the dose up mainly buys side effects, not performance. This is a description of the published dose-response science, not a personal usage protocol. (Source: PMID 34503527.)

How it compares

  • vs Beta-alanine (via carnosine)
    same: Both are buffers that help high-intensity, glycolytic efforts, and the two may be additive when stacked (co-supplementation gave the largest beta-alanine effect size in one meta-analysis, ES 0.43).
    different: Beta-alanine raises INTRACELLULAR (muscle) buffering by building carnosine over weeks of loading and has no acute GI-gas issue; bicarbonate raises EXTRACELLULAR (blood) buffering acutely on the day but causes GI distress. Bicarbonate acts within hours; beta-alanine is a chronic strategy. (PMID 27797728; PMID 34503527)
  • vs Caffeine
    same: Both are legal, WADA-permitted ergogenics used before high-intensity efforts.
    different: Caffeine is a CNS stimulant (adenosine antagonism) affecting perceived exertion and arousal; bicarbonate is a metabolic buffer with no stimulant action. Whether stacking bicarbonate with caffeine is additive is unclear per ISSN. (PMID 34503527)
  • vs Sodium citrate
    same: Another oral alkalizing salt that raises blood bicarbonate/pH and can improve high-intensity performance.
    different: Citrate is often considered to cause somewhat different GI distress but generally similar or slightly weaker ergogenic effect; head-to-head data are limited. Bicarbonate has the larger and more established evidence base. (PMID 34503527)
  • vs Creatine
    same: Both are A-tier, legal ergogenics; the ISSN notes bicarbonate may be additive with creatine.
    different: Creatine works via the phosphocreatine energy system (short, powerful bursts and repeated sprints) and requires loading; bicarbonate works via acid buffering for slightly longer glycolytic efforts. Different mechanism, different time-domain sweet spot; in competitive swimmers, creatine showed a significant effect whereas bicarbonate did not reach significance across pooled distances. (PMID 34503527; PMID 40205676)

History & legal status

Sodium bicarbonate is an old, ubiquitous compound — baking soda — long used medically as an antacid and to treat metabolic acidosis. Its ergogenic use dates to classic exercise-physiology work from the 1970s-1980s onward, maturing into a large RCT literature and multiple meta-analyses. In 2021 the International Society of Sports Nutrition issued a formal position stand endorsing it for high-intensity exercise (PMID 34503527). It is NOT prohibited by WADA and carries no anti-doping restriction. It is inexpensive and available over the counter, which is a large part of its appeal versus proprietary buffers.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • The true 'pure' physiological effect size once placebo/expectancy is removed — ISSN says part of the benefit is placebo, but the exact fraction is unquantified.
  • Why the mixed-sex continuous-running meta-analysis found negligible benefit while male-only and intermittent-exercise data show clearer effects — is this a real sex difference, a body-mass/dosing artifact, or GI-dropout selection? (Meta-regression flagged male sex and higher body mass as predictors of benefit.)
  • Whether habitual pre-training use genuinely enhances long-term training adaptation in humans (only acute human molecular signals and rodent chronic data exist).
  • Optimal individualized timing: whether routinely measuring each athlete's time-to-peak blood bicarbonate outperforms a fixed pre-exercise window in real competition.
  • Best-tolerated delivery (enteric-coated capsules, mini-tablets, hydrogel) for minimizing GI distress without sacrificing the alkalosis — formulations are still being compared.
  • Reliability of benefit in longer (>12 min) continuous events, where several trials (e.g., 40 km cycling time trials) show inconsistent results.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is a harm-reduction / what-to-watch list, not a protocol. (1) GI tolerance: trial any use in training well before competition — establish personal tolerance and never debut it on race day, because GI distress can erase the benefit. Watch for bloating, nausea, vomiting, cramping, diarrhoea. (2) Sodium/cardiovascular/renal status: the sodium load is meaningful — anyone with hypertension, heart failure, kidney disease, or on sodium/fluid-restricted regimens should treat this as medically significant and consult a clinician; it is not a casual supplement for those groups. (3) Concurrent medications/conditions: alkalinization can affect the excretion of some drugs and is inadvisable in existing metabolic alkalosis, significant vomiting, or diuretic use — flag to a physician. (4) Timing awareness: because peak blood bicarbonate varies with formulation and capsule size, mistimed intake may mean no ergogenic window when it's needed. (5) Expectation-setting: benefits are small and partly placebo — monitor for overreliance and for masking of inadequate training or pacing. If GI symptoms are severe or persistent, or if there is chest discomfort, marked swelling, confusion, or muscle twitching (possible signs of electrolyte/acid-base disturbance), stop and seek medical care.

Caveats

GI distress is the dominant real-world limiter: in a 2025 meta-analysis GI symptoms occurred in ~29.5% of bicarbonate trials vs ~2.6% placebo, and GI-related dropouts were higher with bicarbonate (8.7% vs 1.6%) — a mistimed or poorly tolerated dose can worsen performance rather than help it (PMID 41416636). The muscle-pH effect is smaller and less consistent than the reliable blood-bicarbonate change, so the mechanism is best described as extracellular buffering, not 'buffering the muscle.' The ISSN itself attributes part of the benefit to placebo (PMID 34503527). Evidence is skewed toward males: only ~20% of the literature includes women (and only ~7.4% of studies report women-only group data), though what exists supports an ergogenic effect in women too and does not justify sex-specific dosing (PMID 33487131). This is a high sodium load — a concern for anyone with hypertension, heart failure, or renal impairment, and it can interact with sodium/fluid balance; this monograph describes mechanism and monitoring, not a dosing protocol. Individual response (both to performance and to side effects) varies widely, so tolerance must be established in training, never trialed for the first time on competition day.
