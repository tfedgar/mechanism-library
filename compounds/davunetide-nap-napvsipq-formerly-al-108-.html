Davunetide

An 8-amino-acid fragment of a brain-development protein that stabilizes microtubules and curbs tau pathology in the lab — but failed its one large human trial (in PSP), and is now being re-aimed at a rare genetic disorder.

Limited human data 10 citations
Class
ADNP-derived neuroprotective peptide (microtubule-stabilizing / tau-modulating); intranasal (AL-108) or IV (AL-208) octapeptide
Primary target
Microtubule cytoskeleton — NAP recruits tau and end-binding (EB1/EB3) proteins onto microtubules to stabilize them, preserving axonal transport; it is NOT a receptor agonist
Human status
Investigational; NOT approved for any indication (ChEMBL CHEMBL2103826, max_phase 2). Three completed Phase 2 programs under Allon Therapeutics: MCI/prodromal-AD (NCT00422981, AL-108 intranasal, n=144, 12 weeks — reported by the sponsor/discoverers as positive on memory measures, but no results were ever posted to ClinicalTrials.gov and it was never confirmed in a pivotal AD trial), schizophrenia (NCT-registered, n=63, AL-108, 12 weeks — positive on the functional UPSA [p=0.048] but NEGATIVE on the primary MCCB cognition battery [p=0.45]; PMID 22169248), and a CABG-associated MCI IV study (NCT00404014, AL-208, n=234 — a single-dose safety/PK study that did not advance). The pivotal test was the PSP Phase 2/3 trial (NCT01110720 / AL-108-231, n=313, 30 mg intranasal twice daily for 52 weeks): it was clearly NEGATIVE — no difference from placebo on the co-primary PSPRS (p=0.41) or SEADL (p=0.92) endpoints (PMID 24873720, Lancet Neurol 2014), which effectively halted the original PSP/AD program and contributed to Allon's decline. The compound has since been renamed CP201 and, as of the most recent reviews, is PLANNED (not yet completed) for clinical trials in ADNP syndrome, leveraging the preferential 3R-tau interaction hypothesis (PMID 33329371). Human evidence for any longevity, cognitive-enhancement, or 'neuroprotective' use in healthy adults is essentially absent.

At a glance

  • Preclinical: microtubule stabilization, reduced tau hyperphosphorylation, restored axonal transport, neurite/synapse growth (robust across in vitro and rodent models)
  • MCI (prodromal AD) Phase 2 (NCT00422981, n=144): sponsor-reported signal on memory measures over 12 weeks with intranasal AL-108 — no results posted to ClinicalTrials.gov, never independently confirmed
  • Schizophrenia Phase 2 (n=63): improved functional capacity (UPSA, p=0.048) but NO significant benefit on the primary MCCB cognitive battery (p=0.45)
  • PSP Phase 2/3 (NCT01110720, n=313 — the one large, well-powered trial): NO benefit vs placebo on either co-primary endpoint (PSPRS p=0.41, SEADL p=0.92) — the drug failed
  • Consistently well tolerated; main drug-attributable effects are local nasal (epistaxis, rhinorrhoea, nasal discomfort)
  • No approved indication anywhere; renamed CP201 and planned (not yet completed) for trials in ADNP syndrome, a rare 3R-tau-relevant neurodevelopmental disorder

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Derived from ADNP, a protein essential for brain formation Evidence-rodent

    Davunetide is the smallest active 8-amino-acid snippet (NAPVSIPQ) of activity-dependent neuroprotective protein (ADNP). Full ADNP knockout is lethal in mice; a 50% reduction (haploinsufficiency) produces tau pathology, cognitive deficits and neurodegeneration. NAP partially compensates for ADNP deficiency — the drug is essentially a 'reductionist' stand-in for one arm of ADNP biology. PMID 21902667 (Gozes, Curr Pharm Des 2011); PMID 33453943 (Prog Mol Biol Transl Sci 2021)

  2. Binds the microtubule system and recruits tau + EB proteins Evidence-in vitro

    NAP interacts with tubulin/microtubules and works by recruiting tau and microtubule end-binding proteins (EB1/EB3) onto microtubules. Blocking tau's binding sites on microtubules abolishes NAP's effect, and EB/tau expression is required for its activity — so NAP is not acting alone, it needs an intact microtubule-associated protein machinery. PMID 30865715 (Ivashko-Pachima, PLoS One 2019); PMID 33453943 (ADNP-EB interactions)

  3. Stabilizes microtubules and shifts the tubulin tyrosination cycle Evidence-in vitro

    In cultured neurons and glia, NAP increases the pool of dynamic (tyrosinated) microtubules invading the growth cone, expands microtubule network area, and raises the neurite/differentiation marker tubulin-beta3. This is the cellular signature of a microtubule stabilizer that favors productive, dynamic microtubule growth rather than collapse. PMID 23272107 (Oz, PLoS One 2012)

  4. Reduces tau hyperphosphorylation and tau pathology (preclinical) Evidence-rodent

    By keeping tau engaged on stable microtubules, NAP lowers the pool of free, aggregation-prone tau. In animal tauopathy models (from Drosophila to mammals, including ADNP-haploinsufficient mice and STOP/MAP6-deficient models), NAP reduced tau hyperphosphorylation and tau/MAP6 pathology. This is the rationale for testing it in human tauopathies. PMID 21902667; PMID 24210139 (Magen & Gozes, Neuropeptides 2013)

  5. Protects axonal transport and neuronal morphology Evidence-rodent

    Because microtubules are the rails for axonal transport, stabilizing them preserves cargo movement, synapse formation (increased synaptophysin) and neurite outgrowth (increased MAP2). In microtubule-deficiency models this axonal-transport protection tracked with motor and cognitive protection. PMID 24210139; PMID 21902667

  6. Preferentially engages 3-repeat (3R) tau — a possible explanation for its clinical pattern Contested

    In rat-brain and cell assays, NAP interacts preferentially with the dynamic 3R tau isoform over 4R tau. The authors argue this may explain why it showed signals in mixed 3R/4R tauopathy (prodromal Alzheimer's / MCI) but failed in progressive supranuclear palsy, a predominantly 4R tauopathy. This is a post-hoc mechanistic hypothesis from the discovering group, not a prospectively tested one. PMID 30865715 (Ivashko-Pachima, PLoS One 2019); PMID 33453943

  7. Delivered intranasally to reach the brain Evidence-human

    NAP is a small, water-soluble peptide given intranasally (AL-108) or intravenously (AL-208). Intranasal delivery is used to get past the blood-brain barrier; reviews report favorable brain bioavailability and safety, though quantitative human CNS exposure data are limited and mostly from sponsor/animal work. PMID 40185278 (Galushkin & Gozes, Adv Drug Deliv Rev 2025)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Not a classical receptor ligand. Davunetide (NAPVSIPQ) binds the microtubule system: it interacts with tubulin and, critically, recruits the microtubule-associated protein tau and end-binding proteins EB1/EB3 to microtubules. Its activity depends on an intact tau/EB machinery — obstructing tau-binding sites on microtubules abolishes the effect. The SIP motif within NAP is shared with ADNP's EB-binding domain. In rat-brain and cell assays it shows preferential interaction with the dynamic 3-repeat (3R) tau isoform over 4R tau.

Signalling cascade. Microtubule binding + tau/EB recruitment -> microtubule stabilization and a shift toward dynamic (tyrosinated) microtubules -> reduced pool of free/hyperphosphorylated tau and reduced tau/MAP6(STOP) pathology -> preserved axonal transport, neurite outgrowth (MAP2) and synaptogenesis (synaptophysin) -> neuroprotection and cognitive/motor protection in tauopathy and microtubule-deficiency models. No monoaminergic, cholinergic, or hormonal receptor activity is invoked. Note: this cascade is established preclinically (in vitro + rodent) and did NOT translate into efficacy in the one large human tauopathy trial (PSP).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Intranasal (AL-108, the lead clinical formulation) or intravenous (AL-208, used in the CABG-MCI study).
Absorption / bioavailability
Intranasal delivery is used specifically to achieve CNS exposure; reviews report favorable brain bioavailability for the small, hydrophilic peptide. Rigorous quantitative human nose-to-brain PK is limited and largely sponsor/preclinical.
Half-life
Short plasma exposure expected for a small peptide; precise human t1/2 not robustly published. Clinical dosing in trials was twice daily (historical trial fact, not guidance).
Metabolism
As an L-amino-acid octapeptide it is subject to peptidase degradation (the basis for developing protease-resistant D-amino-acid analogs such as D-NAP). Detailed human metabolic mapping is not publicly established.
Excretion
Not well characterized in the public literature for the peptide in humans.
Notes
ChEMBL (CHEMBL2103826): molecular formula C36H60N10O12, MW ~824.9, HELM NAPVSIPQ, molecule_type Protein/peptide, max_phase 2. High polarity (PSA ~356, HBA 12, HBD 10, ALogP ~-4.3, 3 Ro5 violations, QED ~0.05) — i.e., not an oral small-molecule; route engineering (intranasal) is central to its design.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

No validated dose-response for any approved use (there is none). In the schizophrenia trial two intranasal doses were compared (5 mg vs 30 mg); notably the LOWER 5 mg dose showed the larger functional (UPSA) effect size (d~0.74 vs ~0.48) and the larger MCCB effect size (d~0.34 vs ~0.21), i.e., no clean monotonic dose-response and a hint of an inverted-U — though neither MCCB arm reached significance. The PSP trial used 30 mg intranasal twice daily for 52 weeks and showed no benefit. Because there is no established therapeutic window in humans and no data in healthy people, dose-response for any fitness/longevity use is entirely unknown. No dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs Other microtubule stabilizers (e.g., epothilone D / taxane-like agents tested in tauopathy)
    same: Shared goal of stabilizing microtubules and protecting axonal transport in tau-driven neurodegeneration
    different: Davunetide is a short endogenous peptide fragment acting via tau/EB recruitment (needs intact MAP machinery), not a direct taxane-site tubulin binder; it is intranasal and very well tolerated, whereas cytotoxic tubulin binders carry systemic/peripheral-neuropathy risk.
  • vs Anti-tau antibodies / tau-directed disease-modifiers in PSP (e.g., tilavonemab, gosuranemab)
    same: Both target tauopathy in the same PSP population; both large PSP programs ultimately failed on clinical endpoints
    different: Davunetide acts upstream on the microtubule/tau equilibrium rather than clearing extracellular tau; its failure helped establish that PSP is a feasible-but-hard trial setting (a point the trial authors explicitly made).
  • vs Cerebrolysin / peptide 'neurotrophic' mixtures used in the nootropic scene
    same: Marketed under a 'neuroprotective peptide' banner to overlapping audiences
    different: Davunetide is a single defined 8-mer with a specific microtubule mechanism and real (if negative) Phase 3-grade human data; most nootropic peptide claims lack any comparable controlled trial.

History & legal status

Discovered by Illana Gozes and colleagues at Tel Aviv University as the smallest active fragment (NAPVSIPQ) of ADNP. Developed by Allon Therapeutics (Canada) as AL-108 (intranasal) and AL-208 (IV); given the USAN 'davunetide' (2008, -netide peptide stem, per ChEMBL). Small Phase 2 signals in amnestic MCI and in schizophrenia (a functional measure only) led to a pivotal Phase 2/3 trial in progressive supranuclear palsy (AL-108-231, NCT01110720). That 313-patient trial, reported in 2014, was clearly negative on both co-primary endpoints; the failure effectively ended the original program and Allon's independent development. The molecule was subsequently renamed CP201, and reviews describe planned (not yet completed) development toward ADNP syndrome (a rare 3R-tau-relevant neurodevelopmental disorder), reflecting the preferential-3R-tau hypothesis. It has never received marketing approval for any indication in any jurisdiction.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether the 3R-vs-4R tau selectivity hypothesis is real and clinically actionable, or a post-hoc rationalization of the PSP failure — needs independent replication.
  • Whether the MCI/schizophrenia signals reflect true efficacy or small-trial noise; neither was confirmed in a pivotal study, and the MCI trial has no publicly posted results.
  • Quantitative human nose-to-brain pharmacokinetics and actual CNS target engagement at given intranasal doses.
  • Any efficacy or safety in healthy adults or in a fitness/longevity/cognitive-enhancement context — there is essentially no such data.
  • Long-term (multi-year) safety beyond the ~52-week PSP exposure.
  • Whether CP201 will show benefit in ADNP syndrome (trials for that indication are planned but not yet completed/reported).
  • How much of the favorable narrative depends on the originating group's own work versus independent confirmation.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For harm-reduction context only (this is an unapproved investigational drug with no established use outside trials, so the primary 'monitoring' advice is do not self-experiment). If someone were exposed via a study or gray-market intranasal product, the plausible signals to watch are LOCAL and nasal: recurrent nosebleeds (epistaxis), persistent runny nose, and nasal discomfort/irritation or crusting — the only consistently drug-attributable effects in trials. Any olfactory change, sinus pain, or signs of nasal mucosal damage warrant stopping and an ENT evaluation. Because product identity, purity, sterility, and dose of any non-pharmaceutical peptide are unverifiable, injection or intranasal use of research-grade material carries infection and contaminant risks that outweigh any speculative benefit. No routine bloodwork is defined because no systemic toxicity target is known — but that reflects absence of study, not proven safety. No dosing protocol is provided.

Caveats

Read this as a cautionary pipeline entry, not a validated nootropic. (1) The strongest data are preclinical (in vitro and rodent); the one large, adequately powered human efficacy trial (PSP) was flatly negative on both co-primary endpoints. (2) The MCI and schizophrenia 'positives' were small, exploratory, mixed (schizophrenia failed its primary MCCB endpoint and only hit a secondary functional measure; the MCI trial has no publicly posted results), and never confirmed in a pivotal trial. (3) The 'works in women / 3R-tau' storyline comes largely from post-hoc analyses and from one research group (Gozes/Tel Aviv, the discoverers, who have an intellectual stake) — treat as hypothesis, not established fact, until independently replicated. (4) There is NO evidence base for use in the fitness/PED/longevity setting, no established dose for healthy people, and no long-term human safety data beyond ~1 year in disease populations. (5) 'Neuroprotective peptide' marketing outruns the human data. This monograph is mechanism education and harm-reduction only — not a protocol and not medical advice.
