Bimagrumab

A muscle-sparing "recomp" antibody: blocks the activin type II receptor to build muscle while stripping fat — impressive body-composition data, but still investigational and not approved by any regulator.

Strong human data 13 citations
Class
Fully human anti-activin type II receptor (ActRIIA/ActRIIB) IgG1 monoclonal antibody (myostatin/activin pathway blocker)
Primary target
Activin type II receptors ActRIIA and ActRIIB on the muscle (and adipocyte) cell surface
Human status
Investigational / pipeline — NOT approved by FDA, EMA or any regulator for any indication; not legally marketed. Extensively studied in humans, though: originally developed by Novartis as BYM338 across sporadic inclusion body myositis (sIBM), sarcopenia, hip-fracture recovery, COPD/cancer cachexia and mechanical-ventilation muscle loss. Novartis's pivotal Phase 2b/3 sIBM program (RESILIENT, NCT01925209, n=251, completed) failed its functional primary endpoint (6-minute walk distance), and the sarcopenia confirmatory RCT (InvestiGAIT, NCT02333331, PMID 33074327) showed no functional benefit, so muscle-disease development was largely abandoned. The asset was later acquired by Versanis Bio and then Eli Lilly, who repositioned it for obesity/recomposition. The completed Phase 2 BELIEVE trial (bimagrumab ± semaglutide, n=507, NCT05616013) and further Phase 2 studies with tirzepatide define its current status: NCT06643728 (Lilly, active, not recruiting) and an academic MGH combination study (NCT05933499, recruiting). A completed Phase 1 PK study explored co-administration/co-formulation with tirzepatide (NCT06890611). A planned Lilly Phase 2b in obesity + type 2 diabetes (NCT06901349) was WITHDRAWN with zero enrollment. As of mid-2026 it remains a Phase 2 obesity candidate — promising body-composition data, no Phase 3 obesity read-out, no approval. Administered as an intravenous infusion (historically every 4 weeks in muscle trials; every ~12 weeks in the obesity trials).

At a glance

  • Increases lean/skeletal muscle mass (thigh muscle volume up within ~2 weeks; lean mass rises ~2–7%)
  • Reduces total fat mass and preferentially visceral (belly) fat — up to ~20% fat-mass loss over 48 weeks as monotherapy in type 2 diabetes/obesity
  • Drives body 'recomposition' — muscle up and fat down simultaneously, sometimes with little net change in scale weight when used alone
  • Improves insulin sensitivity and lowers HbA1c in people with insulin resistance / type 2 diabetes
  • When combined with semaglutide, produces the largest weight loss of any BELIEVE arm; bimagrumab alone increases lean mass and the combination preserves it
  • Does NOT reliably improve muscle strength, gait speed or physical function despite the mass gains
  • Common tolerability signals: muscle spasms/cramps, diarrhea, and acne

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps3 human 4 established 1 rodent / preclinical

  1. Antibody occupies ActRII, blocking every ligand at once Established

    Bimagrumab binds the extracellular part of both ActRIIA and ActRIIB. Because myostatin, activin A and GDF-11 all funnel through these same two receptors, one antibody shuts off the whole family of 'muscle brakes' — this is broader than blocking myostatin alone. Shown directly in human myoblasts and mice. Lach-Trifilieff 2014, Mol Cell Biol, PMID 24298022

  2. Downstream Smad2/3 signaling is switched off Established

    Normally myostatin/activin binding drives phosphorylation of the Smad2/3 proteins, which act as a catabolic (muscle-shrinking) signal inside the cell. With the receptor blocked, Smad2/3 phosphorylation drops — demonstrated in vitro and in mouse muscle. Lach-Trifilieff 2014, PMID 24298022 (in vitro human myoblasts + mouse); review Lisco 2026, PMID 41954678

  3. The muscle-growth program is released Evidence-rodent

    With the Smad2/3 brake off, protein synthesis rises and myosin heavy chain is spared from degradation. In cells and mice this drives strong fibre hypertrophy and protects muscle from wasting (e.g. glucocorticoid-induced atrophy). The specific downstream disinhibition of Akt/mTOR is the proposed mechanism but is not fully pinned down. Lach-Trifilieff 2014, PMID 24298022 (mouse hypertrophy beyond myostatin-null); review PMID 41954678

  4. In humans, lean/thigh-muscle mass measurably increases Established

    A single IV dose raises thigh muscle volume within ~2 weeks and lean body mass by roughly 2–7%, sustained with repeat dosing — shown consistently across healthy older adults, sarcopenia and obesity/diabetes trials. Rooks 2017 sarcopenia PoC, PMID 28653345; Rooks 2020 PK/PD, PMID 33264516; meta-analysis Kanbay 2024, PMID 39251484 (fat-free mass +1.9 kg)

  5. Fat mass — especially visceral fat — falls at the same time Evidence-human

    Unusually for an anabolic, blocking ActRII also drives large fat loss. In type-2-diabetes/obesity, total fat mass dropped ~20% (about 7.5 kg) over 48 weeks while lean mass rose ~3.6%. The receptor is also present on fat cells, and part of the effect is HYPOTHESIZED to be a direct action on adipose tissue plus a higher metabolic rate rather than only 'more muscle burning more calories' — but that specific mechanism is not established in humans. Heymsfield 2021 JAMA Netw Open, PMID 33439265 (NCT03005288); Garito 2018, PMID 28643356

  6. Insulin sensitivity and HbA1c improve Evidence-human

    Alongside the body-composition shift, insulin sensitivity improved ~20–40% and HbA1c fell ~0.2–0.76% — consistent with losing visceral/ectopic fat and gaining insulin-responsive muscle. Garito 2018, PMID 28643356; Heymsfield 2021, PMID 33439265

  7. Added to a GLP-1 drug, it redirects weight loss toward fat and protects muscle Evidence-human

    The headline 'recomp' idea: GLP-1 drugs like semaglutide cause big weight loss but a substantial share can be lean mass. In the Phase 2 BELIEVE trial, bimagrumab + semaglutide produced the largest weight loss of any arm (−17.8 kg vs −3.3 kg placebo at week 48), and bimagrumab alone increased lean mass while combination preserved it. Note: the published trial reports body weight and safety as primary/secondary endpoints; the detailed fat-vs-lean breakdown is a described feature of the ActRII mechanism. Heymsfield 2026 BELIEVE, Nat Med, PMID 41772149 (NCT05616013); context review Stefanakis 2024, PMID 39481534

  8. But muscle mass ≠ muscle strength/function Established

    A recurring caveat: extra muscle mass has NOT reliably translated into stronger or faster patients. In sarcopenia and inclusion-body-myositis trials, mass went up but strength, gait speed and walk distance often did not improve versus placebo. This is the single biggest question mark over the drug. Rooks 2020 JAMA Netw Open sarcopenia RCT, PMID 33074327 (no functional benefit); Sivakumar 2020 sIBM, PMID 32690797 ('no evidence of clinical improvement'); meta-analysis PMID 39251484

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Fully human IgG1 monoclonal antibody that binds with high affinity to the extracellular domain of activin type II receptors ActRIIA and ActRIIB, competitively preventing binding of the natural ligands myostatin (GDF-8), activin A, GDF-11 and related TGF-β superfamily members. Because it blocks the shared receptor rather than a single ligand, its anabolic effect exceeds that of myostatin inhibition alone (demonstrated by greater hypertrophy than a myostatin-specific inhibitor and additional hypertrophy even in myostatin-null mice).

Signalling cascade. Receptor blockade leads to loss of ligand-driven ActRII activation, reduced Smad2/3 phosphorylation (the catabolic arm), and — by proposed disinhibition of Akt/mTOR anabolic signaling — increased myofibrillar protein synthesis, enhanced myoblast differentiation, and protection of myosin heavy chain from degradation, producing muscle fibre hypertrophy and resistance to atrophy. In parallel, blockade of ActRII signaling in adipose tissue is HYPOTHESIZED to contribute (possibly with a rise in resting metabolic rate) to fat-mass loss and improved insulin sensitivity, though the direct-adipocyte/metabolic-rate mechanism is not established in humans.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Intravenous infusion. Muscle-disease trials used fixed doses (e.g. up to 700 mg) or weight-based dosing every 4 weeks; the obesity trials (Heymsfield/BELIEVE) used 10 or 30 mg/kg every 12 weeks. A Lilly Phase 1 study explored co-administration/co-formulation with tirzepatide (NCT06890611, completed).
Absorption / bioavailability
As an IV biologic, 100% systemically available; no oral form. Large monoclonal antibody (~150 kDa) confined largely to plasma/extracellular fluid.
Half-life
Exhibits target-mediated drug disposition (TMDD): clearance is faster at low concentrations when receptor is available and slows once the receptor is saturated, giving non-linear, dose-dependent kinetics. Effects on muscle/fat are sustained for weeks-to-months after a single dose, consistent with durable receptor engagement.
Metabolism
Catabolized like other IgG antibodies via proteolytic/reticuloendothelial degradation into peptides and amino acids; not metabolized by CYP enzymes, so classic small-molecule drug–drug interactions are not expected.
Excretion
Not renally or hepatically excreted as intact drug; eliminated by catabolism to amino acids that are recycled.
Notes
The long duration of pharmacodynamic effect (thigh muscle volume still elevated many weeks after one infusion) is why obesity dosing could be spaced to every 12 weeks. Target-mediated disposition was documented in the healthy-older/obese PK study.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Muscle-mass and lean-mass gains appear rapidly and near-maximally even at single/low exposures (e.g. thigh muscle volume rose ~5–6% at both 3 and 30 mg/kg by week 4), consistent with high receptor occupancy — i.e. the anabolic effect plateaus. Higher doses mainly extend the DURATION of effect rather than the peak magnitude. In the sarcopenia confirmatory RCT (InvestiGAIT, 700 mg monthly) mass increased but function did not. In obesity/diabetes (NCT03005288) 10 mg/kg every 4 weeks produced ~20% fat-mass loss; in BELIEVE the 30 mg/kg arm and the 30 mg/kg + semaglutide 2.4 mg combination gave the largest effects (LS-mean weight change −17.8 kg for combination vs −3.3 kg placebo at week 48). NO dosing protocol is provided here — this describes the shape of the published dose–response only.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Myostatin-specific inhibitors (trevogrumab, apitegromab)
    same: All target the myostatin/activin–ActRII axis to protect or build muscle, and all are being paired with incretin (GLP-1/GIP) drugs to improve the quality of weight loss.
    different: Bimagrumab blocks the RECEPTOR, so it neutralizes myostatin AND activin A AND GDF-11 together — broader, larger anabolic effect than blocking myostatin alone, and it also drives substantial fat loss. Ligand-specific antibodies (e.g. apitegromab, which in the EMBRAZE trial preserved ~1.9 kg of lean mass during tirzepatide weight loss, PMID 42260100) mainly aim to PRESERVE muscle during GLP-1 weight loss and generally don't produce bimagrumab's large independent fat loss.
  • vs ActRIIB ligand traps (e.g. ACE-031/ramatercept)
    same: Both raise muscle mass by removing ActRII ligand signaling; both show mass gains that don't cleanly translate to function.
    different: Ligand traps are decoy soluble receptors that mop up ligands in circulation; bimagrumab sits on the receptor itself. ACE-031 development was limited by off-target vascular/bleeding signals; bimagrumab's main issues are muscle spasms/acne and lack of functional benefit.
  • vs GLP-1 / GIP incretin drugs (semaglutide, tirzepatide) alone
    same: Both reduce fat mass and improve glycemia; both are being co-developed for metabolic disease.
    different: Incretins cause large total weight loss driven by appetite suppression but a meaningful fraction of the loss can be lean mass; bimagrumab does the opposite — modest scale-weight change alone but muscle UP and visceral fat DOWN. That's why the strategy is to COMBINE them, not choose one.
  • vs Anabolic-androgenic steroids / SARMs
    same: Both can increase lean mass; both raise the same 'mass without proven strength/health benefit' and safety-surveillance concerns.
    different: Bimagrumab is a non-hormonal antibody that works by disinhibiting a growth pathway, not by activating androgen receptors — so it lacks the androgenic hormonal profile (though acne is reported). It is an IV biologic given in trials, not an illicit oral/injectable PED, and it uniquely drives large fat loss.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Discovered and developed by Novartis as BYM338, with the mechanism established in 2014 (Lach-Trifilieff, PMID 24298022). Novartis pursued muscle-wasting indications, including sporadic inclusion body myositis (sIBM); however, the pivotal Phase 2b/3 sIBM program (RESILIENT, NCT01925209, n=251, completed 2016) failed to improve its functional primary endpoint (6-minute walk distance), and the confirmatory sarcopenia RCT (InvestiGAIT, PMID 33074327) likewise showed no functional benefit despite clear mass gains — leading Novartis to shelve the muscle-disease program. (Bimagrumab is widely reported to have held FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation for sIBM, though that designation is not verifiable from the databases used here.) The metabolic 'recomp' signal (fat loss + insulin-sensitivity gains, PMID 28643356, 33439265) redirected interest to obesity. Rights moved to Versanis Bio, which ran the BELIEVE trial (bimagrumab ± semaglutide, NCT05616013); Eli Lilly acquired Versanis in 2023, renaming the asset LY3985863 and advancing Phase 2 combinations with its own tirzepatide (NCT06643728 active/not recruiting; a completed Phase 1 co-formulation study NCT06890611; and an academic MGH study NCT05933499 recruiting). A planned Lilly Phase 2b in obesity + type 2 diabetes (NCT06901349) was WITHDRAWN with zero enrollment. As of mid-2026: no marketing approval anywhere; a Phase 2 obesity asset with strong body-composition data and an unresolved function question.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Does the muscle mass gain EVER translate into clinically meaningful strength, function, mobility or hard outcomes (falls, disability)? A decade of trials says mass yes, function no.
  • How much of the fat loss is a direct adipose/metabolic-rate effect versus secondary to more muscle — and does it persist long term? The direct-adipocyte mechanism remains a hypothesis.
  • Phase 3 obesity efficacy and safety, and whether the recomp advantage over GLP-1 alone holds at scale and over years.
  • Long-term safety of chronic ActRII blockade: pancreatic (lipase), bone, reproductive/FSH axis, and cardiovascular effects beyond 1–2 years.
  • Durability after stopping — how fast do muscle gains and fat loss reverse once infusions end?
  • Who benefits most (older/sarcopenic-obese, those with large GLP-1-related lean loss) and whether strength training is needed to convert mass into function.
  • Optimal partner and regimen (semaglutide vs tirzepatide; co-formulation), and whether IV dosing is a practical barrier vs subcutaneous competitors.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is monitoring guidance for the mechanism/harm-reduction context ONLY — not a protocol and not medical advice; bimagrumab should only be used within a supervised clinical trial. Things clinicians track in trials and that matter for this pathway: (1) Muscle symptoms — cramps/spasms are the hallmark AE; watch severity. (2) Body composition, not just scale weight — DXA/imaging for fat vs lean mass is the meaningful readout, since weight alone under-reads a 'recomp' drug. (3) FUNCTION, not just mass — grip strength, gait speed, walk distance: mass gains that don't improve function are the drug's known weakness. (4) Pancreatic enzymes (amylase/lipase) and symptoms of pancreatitis. (5) Glycemia (HbA1c, glucose) — expect improvement, but monitor especially if combined with GLP-1 agents. (6) GI tolerability, hydration and nutrition (protein intake) — important because combining with GLP-1 drugs compounds nausea/appetite loss. (7) Skin (acne) and, given pathway biology, awareness of possible reproductive-hormone (FSH) effects. (8) Infusion reactions, as with any IV monoclonal antibody. Anyone encountering non-trial 'bimagrumab' from grey-market sources should treat it as unverified and unsafe — there is no legitimate consumer supply.

Caveats

Read the hype carefully. (1) MASS IS NOT STRENGTH: the most consistent negative finding across a decade of human data is that bimagrumab reliably grows muscle mass and shrinks fat but has repeatedly FAILED to improve strength, gait speed, walk distance or physical function versus placebo (sarcopenia RCT PMID 33074327; sIBM PMID 32690797; meta-analysis PMID 39251484). Extra 'lean mass' on a DXA scan is not the same as being stronger or more functional. (2) NO APPROVAL, NO PHASE 3 OBESITY DATA: every obesity result is Phase 2. Long-term safety, durability, and whether the recomp benefit survives a Phase 3 trial are unknown; note that a planned Lilly Phase 2b in diabetes (NCT06901349) was withdrawn. (3) IV ONLY and antibody-based: not an oral or injectable-pen 'stack' item; it is an infusion with target-mediated, non-linear pharmacokinetics. (4) NOT a consumer/PED product: there is no legitimate supply; anything sold as 'bimagrumab' outside a trial is unverified and unsafe. (5) OFF-TARGET HORMONE/ORGAN SIGNALS: ActRII blockade is not muscle-specific — the pathway touches the pituitary/reproductive (FSH) axis and pancreas (asymptomatic enzyme/lipase elevations reported in trials), so effects beyond muscle and fat deserve monitoring; the FSH/reproductive concern is a mechanistic/rodent inference, not a demonstrated human effect of bimagrumab. This entry is mechanism education and harm-reduction only — it is NOT medical advice and contains no dosing protocol.
