MOTS-c

A tiny 16-amino-acid peptide your own mitochondria make under stress and exercise — it acts like a built-in "metabolic stress signal" that flips on the cell's energy-sensing master switch (AMPK) and can even march into the nucleus to turn on antioxidant defense genes.

Limited human data 17 citations
Class
Mitochondrial-derived peptide (MDP); endogenous peptide hormone / signaling microprotein
Primary target
No single classical receptor
Human status
Endogenous in humans: MOTS-c circulates in human plasma, rises with exercise, declines with age, and tracks with metabolic state (e.g., PCOS cohort PMID 31066084; exercise/aging reviews PMID 36670507, 32910336). Human genetics support its functional relevance: the natural K14Q MOTS-c variant (common in some East Asian populations), which binds/activates CK2 poorly, associates with higher type-2-diabetes and sarcopenia risk in men in an activity-dependent way (PMID 39559755). HOWEVER — the headline metabolic benefits (insulin sensitization, anti-obesity, exercise-mimetic, improved mitochondrial function) come almost entirely from mouse and cell studies; the mechanism (folate→AICAR→AMPK, CK2 binding, NRF2/ARE) was worked out in rodents/cells (PMID 25738459, 29983246, 39559755, 41520850). There are essentially NO published randomized controlled trials of administering synthetic MOTS-c to humans for performance, fat loss, or longevity, and one 2026 human exercise study found skeletal muscle is likely NOT the source of exercise-induced circulating MOTS-c (PMID 41520850), complicating the simple 'muscle exerkine' story. So for an audience: the biology is real and the mouse data are striking, but injecting MOTS-c in people is unproven and off-label/research-only.

At a glance

  • Activates AMPK (the cellular energy/low-fuel master switch) — established mechanism, mostly rodent
  • Increases skeletal-muscle glucose uptake and improves insulin sensitivity (rodent; human data indirect)
  • Prevented diet-induced obesity and age/diet-induced insulin resistance in mice (rodent)
  • Shifts metabolism toward fat-burning (beta-oxidation), reducing fat accumulation and fatty liver (rodent)
  • Triggers nuclear antioxidant (NRF2/ARE) gene program under stress, lowering oxidative damage (rodent/cell)
  • Improves intrinsic muscle mitochondrial efficiency via PGC-1alpha/AMPK (rodent)
  • Behaves as an exercise-induced/responsive signal in humans; circulating levels fall with age (human, observational)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It's a peptide encoded INSIDE your mitochondria Established

    MOTS-c is a 16-amino-acid peptide read out of a short hidden gene tucked inside the mitochondrial 12S rRNA region — so the cell's 'power plants' literally write their own hormone, rather than the nucleus making it. Its level in blood is highest with exercise/stress and declines with age. PMID 25738459 (Lee 2015, Cell Metab); PMID 36233287 (review)

  2. It jams the folate cycle, so AICAR piles up and switches on AMPK Evidence-rodent

    Inside the cell MOTS-c blocks the folate one-carbon cycle and the purine-synthesis branch hanging off it. That causes the metabolite AICAR to accumulate, and AICAR is a natural activator of AMPK — the cell's 'low-fuel' master switch that tells the body to burn fuel and take up glucose. This is the core energy-metabolism mechanism. PMID 25738459 (Lee 2015, Cell Metab)

  3. In muscle it also grabs and turns on the kinase CK2 directly Evidence-rodent

    A 2024 study showed MOTS-c physically binds the enzyme CK2 and activates it in muscle (while suppressing it in fat) — and blocking CK2 cancels MOTS-c's benefits. This is its most concrete 'direct target,' and it's tissue-specific. PMID 39559755 (Kumagai 2024, iScience)

  4. Under metabolic stress it travels to the nucleus and turns on antioxidant genes Evidence-rodent

    When glucose is low or the cell is stressed, MOTS-c moves out of the mitochondria into the nucleus, where (in an AMPK-dependent way) it teams up with the stress transcription factor NRF2 to switch on genes carrying antioxidant response elements (ARE) — a 'retrograde' signal from mitochondria back to the genome. PMID 29983246 (Kim 2018, Cell Metab)

  5. The net result: more glucose uptake and better insulin sensitivity Evidence-rodent

    By activating AMPK (and CK2) in skeletal muscle — its main target organ — MOTS-c increases glucose uptake and improves insulin sensitivity, and in mice it prevented age-related and high-fat-diet-induced insulin resistance and obesity. Metabolomics shows it shifts fat-handling pathways toward fat-burning. PMID 25738459 (Lee 2015); PMID 31293078 (Kim 2019, metabolomics)

  6. It tunes up the mitochondria themselves and lowers oxidative stress Evidence-rodent

    Beyond signaling, MOTS-c improves the intrinsic bioenergetic efficiency of muscle mitochondria and lowers reactive-oxygen-species (ROS) damage, working through PGC-1α and AMPK — so the power plants run cleaner and more efficiently, not just bigger. PMID 41520850 (Gudiksen 2026, Free Radic Biol Med)

  7. In humans it behaves like an exercise-responsive signal — but the data are thin Evidence-human

    In people, blood MOTS-c rises with exercise and tracks with metabolic state (e.g., regulated by lipids/insulin in PCOS cohorts), and a natural MOTS-c variant (K14Q) that binds CK2 poorly is linked to higher type-2-diabetes/sarcopenia risk in men. But there are essentially NO controlled trials of injecting MOTS-c into healthy humans for performance or fat loss — the therapeutic claims rest on mouse data. PMID 39559755 (K14Q variant); PMID 31066084 (PCOS human cohort); PMID 41520850 (human exercise arm)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. MOTS-c is a 16-amino-acid mitochondrial-derived peptide (MDP), sequence MRWQEMGYIFYPRKLR, encoded by a short open reading frame within the mitochondrial 12S rRNA gene (Established) [PMID 25738459]. It has NO single classical cell-surface receptor; it acts as an intracellular/intracrine and circulating 'mitokine' with multiple intracellular protein partners rather than a defined Ki/EC50 against a GPCR. (1) DIRECT TARGET — Casein kinase 2 (CK2): MOTS-c directly binds and ALLOSTERICALLY ACTIVATES CK2 in cell-free systems, and CK2 is required for its muscle effects (anti-atrophy, glucose uptake); notably the effect is tissue-specific — it activates CK2 in muscle but suppresses it in fat by differentially modifying CK2-interacting proteins (Evidence-rodent / in vitro) [PMID 39559755]. No published equilibrium Kd is available — affinity is qualitative. (2) METABOLIC-ENZYME TARGET — the folate one-carbon cycle: MOTS-c inhibits the folate cycle and its tethered de novo purine biosynthesis, causing accumulation of the AMPK-activating metabolite AICAR (ZMP) — i.e. it acts UPSTREAM of AMPK as a metabolite-level agonist rather than binding AMPK directly (Established) [PMID 25738459, 36677050]. (3) NUCLEAR/TRANSCRIPTIONAL — under stress, MOTS-c is carried into the nucleus and associates with stress-responsive transcription factors (e.g. NRF2) and binds promoters bearing antioxidant response elements (ARE), e.g. HMOX1 and NQO1, shown by ChIP-seq (Evidence-rodent) [PMID 40403491, 31131297]. Net pharmacological character: a positive modulator/activator of the AMPK energy-sensing axis and an inducer of NRF2/ARE antioxidant transcription; not an antagonist. Selectivity is poorly defined; specificity arises from tissue context (muscle vs fat CK2) rather than receptor subtype selectivity.

Signalling cascade. Step 1 (metabolic trigger): Exogenous or stress-induced MOTS-c partitions to the cytosol and inhibits the methionine–folate one-carbon cycle; because de novo purine synthesis is physically tethered to this cycle, the intermediate AICAR/ZMP accumulates [PMID 25738459]. Step 2 (energy sensor): AICAR mimics AMP and allosterically activates AMPK (the 'Folate–AICAR–AMPK' axis), tipping the AMP:ATP-sensing kinase toward the energy-deficit 'catabolic' state [PMID 25738459, 36670507, 36677050]. Step 3 (acute metabolic output): Active AMPK drives GLUT4-dependent glucose uptake and fatty-acid oxidation in skeletal muscle (its 'primary target organ'), improving insulin sensitivity independent of insulin in part (Evidence-rodent + human ex vivo) [PMID 25738459, 36677050]. In parallel, MOTS-c binding/activating CK2 in muscle contributes to enhanced glucose uptake and resistance to atrophy [PMID 39559755]. Step 4 (mitochondrial bioenergetics): MOTS-c improves intrinsic mitochondrial bioenergetic efficiency and lowers ROS emission in a PGC-1α/AMPK-DEPENDENT manner — changing quality/efficiency of OXPHOS rather than mitochondrial mass; it can also acutely suppress mitochondrial respiration (Evidence-rodent) [PMID 41520850, 36490309]. Step 5 (retrograde mitonuclear signalling under stress): Metabolic stress (e.g. glucose restriction, oxidative stress, exercise) prevents the normal cytosolic sequestration of MOTS-c and triggers translocation to the nucleus. Mechanistically (in endothelium), ROS activate CK2A which phosphorylates the cytoskeletal motor MYH9 at Ser1943; phospho-MYH9 plus γ-actin forms the transport complex that ferries MOTS-c into the nucleus [PMID 40403491]. Step 6 (transcriptional adaptation): Nuclear MOTS-c co-operates with NRF2 and binds ARE-containing promoters (HMOX1, NQO1, and a broad antioxidant/proteostasis program), driving an adaptive cytoprotective gene-expression response — the basis of its 'mitonuclear communication' role [PMID 40403491, 31131297, 33473109]. Net result: an exercise-/AMPK-mimetic state coupling acute energy-sensing (AMPK/GLUT4) to longer-term antioxidant transcriptional adaptation (NRF2/ARE).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Endogenously an intracellular/secreted mitokine measurable as a circulating hormone in plasma. There is NO human pharmacokinetic study of administered MOTS-c; all exogenous data are from rodents given intraperitoneal (i.p.) or intravenous injection of synthetic peptide. (No oral or transdermal human PK exists; the peptide-marketplace 'subcutaneous' route is unstudied in published trials.) (Evidence-rodent for dosed PK; human only for endogenous levels) [PMID 25738459, 33473109].
Absorption / bioavailability
As a 16-aa unmodified peptide it would have negligible oral bioavailability (gut proteolysis) — no published oral-bioavailability figure exists. Rodent efficacy studies use parenteral injection [PMID 25738459, 33473109]. Endogenously it is detectable in plasma by ELISA across humans; circulating concentrations are in the ng/mL range and DECLINE with age and with sedentary/insulin-resistant states (Evidence-human) [PMID 36233287, 31066084].
Half-life
No rigorously published plasma half-life for human or exogenously dosed MOTS-c. As a small unstructured peptide it is expected to be short-lived (minutes) and to require repeated or intermittent dosing — consistent with the Reynolds aging study using INTERMITTENT (3x/week) i.p. dosing rather than continuous exposure (Speculative for exact t1/2; Evidence-rodent for intermittent-dosing necessity) [PMID 33473109].
Metabolism
Presumed cleared by ubiquitous plasma and tissue peptidases/proteases (no compound-specific metabolizing enzyme identified). Functionally, intracellular 'inactivation' under nutrient-replete conditions involves sequestration in the cytoplasm (preventing nuclear action) rather than chemical metabolism; stress relieves this sequestration (Evidence-rodent) [PMID 31131297, 40403491]. (Established peptide biology; compound-specific data: none.)
Excretion
Not characterized for MOTS-c specifically. Small peptides and their fragment amino acids are expected to undergo renal filtration/tubular catabolism and reuse of constituent amino acids; no excretion-balance study exists (Speculative).
Notes
No ester/prodrug forms are described in the literature — the marketed material is the plain 16-aa peptide. BBB penetration is not established. Because endogenous secretion is exercise- and stress-INDUCED and falls with age, MOTS-c is described as an 'exercise-mimetic'/mitokine; SGLT2 inhibitors and intralipid raise endogenous levels in humans, while insulin acutely suppresses the lipid-induced rise [PMID 39182552, 31066084, 36490309]. The central PK gap: essentially everything about administered-MOTS-c disposition in humans is unknown.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human dose-response for ADMINISTERED MOTS-c is UNKNOWN — there are no published human trials of exogenous MOTS-c, no NOAEL, no dose-finding, and no efficacy-by-dose data. Human evidence is limited to ENDOGENOUS levels: exercise acutely raises skeletal-muscle and circulating MOTS-c [PMID 33473109]; an intralipid infusion raised plasma MOTS-c to ~232% of baseline in healthy controls and ~349% in PCOS women, with insulin blunting that rise [PMID 31066084]; SGLT2 inhibitors raise serum MOTS-c in diabetic heart-failure patients [PMID 39182552]. RODENT dose-response (the basis for any magnitude claims): in the original Cell Metab work, i.p. MOTS-c (reported around 0.5 mg/kg/day in mice) prevented high-fat-diet-induced obesity and both age- and diet-induced insulin resistance, improving glucose tolerance [PMID 25738459]. In the aging study, intermittent i.p. MOTS-c (≈15 mg/kg, 3x/week) increased running capacity and physical performance in young, middle-aged and old mice, and improved healthspan even when started in late life (~23.5 months) [PMID 33473109]. Mitochondrial-efficiency and anti-atrophy effects in muscle are seen at injected doses in transgenic mice and are abolished when PGC-1α/AMPK or CK2 signalling is blocked, establishing the pathway-dependence of the dose effect [PMID 41520850, 39559755]. Translating rodent mg/kg to humans is not validated; effect magnitudes in people at any given dose are genuinely unknown. (Established that effects are dose- and pathway-dependent in rodents; Evidence-human only for endogenous modulation.)

How it compares

  • vs Humanin (the other principal mitochondrial-derived peptide)
    same: Both are MDPs encoded by short ORFs in the mitochondrial 16S/12S rRNA region, both are cytoprotective 'mitokines' that circulate, decline with age, and are studied for metabolic/aging benefit [PMID 26289118, 39182552].
    different: Humanin acts largely as a secreted, RECEPTOR-mediated cytoprotective/anti-apoptotic peptide (e.g. via formyl-peptide-receptor-like and gp130 receptor complexes) and is anti-apoptotic; MOTS-c is primarily an INTRACELLULAR AMPK/CK2 metabolic regulator that translocates to the NUCLEUS to drive ARE/antioxidant transcription. In the SGLT2i cohort, MOTS-c rose but humanin did NOT, showing distinct regulation [PMID 39182552, 31131297].
  • vs Metformin (AMPK-activating biguanide)
    same: Both ultimately converge on the AICAR/AMPK energy-sensing axis, improve insulin sensitivity and glucose utilization, and suppress mitochondrial respiration — MOTS-c is explicitly described as having MoA 'largely overlapping' metformin [PMID 36490309, 25738459].
    different: Metformin is an orally bioavailable small molecule (acts partly via complex I inhibition and the lysosomal AMP-sensing pathway) with decades of human RCT/safety data; MOTS-c is an injectable peptide with NO human efficacy/safety trials. Metformin raises circulating MOTS-c, suggesting MOTS-c may be partly downstream of metformin's effect rather than a substitute [PMID 36490309].
  • vs AICAR (direct AMPK activator / 'exercise mimetic')
    same: Both raise AMPK signalling and are framed as exercise-mimetics improving endurance/metabolic phenotype; AICAR/ZMP is literally the metabolite MOTS-c causes to accumulate [PMID 25738459, 33473109].
    different: AICAR is the small-molecule nucleotide AMP-mimetic that activates AMPK directly; MOTS-c works UPSTREAM (by blocking the folate cycle to raise endogenous AICAR) and additionally has AMPK-independent actions (direct CK2 binding, NRF2/ARE nuclear transcription) that AICAR lacks [PMID 39559755, 40403491]. AICAR is also a known sports anti-doping target.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Insulin compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-rodent
    MOTS-c increases skeletal-muscle glucose disposal INSULIN-INDEPENDENTLY (via AMPK and CK2 driving GLUT4-mediated uptake), while exogenous insulin drives GLUT4 uptake through the insulin-receptor (INSR) pathway. Because the two act through separate upstream routes onto the same downstream GLUT4 glucose-clearance machinery, their glucose-lowering effects can add together, compounding hypoglycemia risk. (The MOTS-c contribution is demonstrated in rodents/cells; its magnitude in dosed humans is unproven.) PMID 25738459 (Lee 2015 - AMPK/GLUT4 glucose uptake); PMID 39559755 (Kumagai 2024 - CK2-dependent muscle glucose uptake); mots-c.json side_effect_mechanisms (hypoglycemia stacking on insulin) Watch: Blood glucose and hypoglycemia symptoms (sweating, tremor, palpitations, confusion), especially around injection timing; note that insulin's neuroglycopenic symptoms can precede adrenergic warning signs.
  • Semaglutide compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-rodent
    Both lower glucose but by different routes: semaglutide via glucose-dependent insulin secretion, glucagon suppression, delayed gastric emptying and weight loss; MOTS-c via AMPK/CK2-driven insulin-independent muscle glucose uptake. GLP-1 agonists alone carry low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk, but layering MOTS-c's additional insulin-independent glucose disposal on top could compound glucose lowering, especially with reduced food intake from GLP-1-induced appetite suppression. (MOTS-c's human glucose-lowering contribution is unproven, so this is a mechanistic-plausibility concern.) PMID 29431519 (semaglutide - glucose-dependent insulin release, low hypoglycemia risk alone); PMID 25738459 (MOTS-c AMPK/GLUT4 glucose uptake, rodent) Watch: Blood glucose and hypoglycemia symptoms, particularly during GLP-1-driven appetite/intake reduction; watch for additive glucose lowering against reduced caloric intake.
  • Tirzepatide compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-rodent
    Tirzepatide (GIP + GLP-1 co-agonist) produces large improvements in glycemia and insulin sensitivity plus marked appetite/intake reduction; MOTS-c adds insulin-independent AMPK/CK2-mediated muscle glucose uptake. The two lower glucose through complementary routes, so glucose-lowering (and hypoglycemia risk against a backdrop of reduced caloric intake from the incretin) could add. As with semaglutide, MOTS-c's human glucose-lowering contribution is unproven, so this is a mechanistic-plausibility concern. PMID 36750526 (tirzepatide SURPASS - glycemic control/insulin sensitivity); PMID 25738459 (MOTS-c glucose uptake, rodent) Watch: Blood glucose and hypoglycemia awareness, especially when intake is suppressed by tirzepatide; additive glucose lowering is the concern.
  • Cardarine potentiates ↑ Evidence-rodent
    Cardarine's endurance/fat-oxidation effect is not purely PPARδ — the landmark mouse work showed PPARδ agonism works hand-in-hand with AMPK, the cellular energy sensor, to switch muscle toward oxidative fibers (Cardarine alone in sedentary mice did NOT confer the full endurance benefit; training/AMPK input was required). MOTS-c is a mitochondrial-derived peptide that acts primarily through the folate-AICAR-AMPK pathway to improve metabolic/insulin sensitivity and fat oxidation. Adding an AMPK activator therefore feeds the very upstream node Cardarine's full effect depends on, so the two can reinforce the same FAO/oxidative-fiber transcriptional program (AMPK→PGC-1α→PPARδ axis). Cardarine–AMPK co-action for oxidative fibers/endurance (Cardarine alone insufficient without AMPK/training): Narkar 2008 PMID 18674809 (monograph mechanism_steps 4, evidence_base). MOTS-c folate-AICAR-AMPK metabolic mechanism: MOTS-c monograph MoA (PMID 35299233; 36670507). Watch: Fasting glucose and insulin (both push toward fat use and can lower glucose), lipid panel; watch for exertional hypoglycemia symptoms during fasted/low-carb training since both bias fuel away from glucose.
  • CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin opposes Contested
    Same GH-axis opposition as the MK-677 edge, but as a class inference: CJC-1295 (GHRH-R) plus ipamorelin (GHS-R1a) raise GH and IGF-1, and elevated GH characteristically induces peripheral insulin resistance and can raise fasting glucose (a human-documented GH class effect). That GH-driven insulin resistance would counteract MOTS-c's AMPK-mediated improvement in insulin sensitivity/muscle glucose uptake. Glucose effects have NOT been quantified for this specific peptide stack, so the opposition is extrapolated from GH physiology, not measured for CJC-1295/ipamorelin. PMID 11914108 (GH induces insulin resistance/raises glucose in healthy people - class mechanism); PMID 18981485 (GH-secretagogue-induced insulin resistance/glucose rise); PMID 25738459 (MOTS-c insulin sensitization, rodent) Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c for a GH-driven upward drift that would oppose any MOTS-c insulin-sensitizing effect; note morning glucose creep.
  • MK-677 opposes Evidence-human
    Antagonistic on the insulin-sensitivity axis. MOTS-c improves insulin sensitivity and glucose uptake (rodent-grounded), whereas MK-677 (via sustained GH/IGF-1 elevation) is documented in humans to RAISE fasting glucose and REDUCE insulin sensitivity. The GH-driven insulin resistance from MK-677 works against MOTS-c's core intended metabolic benefit, so the two pull glucose control in opposite directions. PMID 18981485 (Nass 2008, Ann Intern Med - MK-677 2-yr trial, raised glucose/reduced insulin sensitivity); PMID 8954023 (Chapman 1996 - MK-677 GH/IGF-1 axis); PMID 25738459 (MOTS-c insulin sensitization, rodent) Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c (watch for the GH-induced rise from MK-677 blunting any MOTS-c insulin-sensitizing effect); HOMA-IR if tracking insulin sensitivity.
  • 5-Amino-1MQ shares target Speculative
    Both push on the same cellular energy-sensing network from different entry points, but they do NOT share a single molecular target. 5-Amino-1MQ inhibits NNMT, sparing nicotinamide and raising intracellular NAD+ and SAM; higher NAD+ feeds NAD+-dependent SIRT1 and downstream PGC-1alpha/mitochondrial and fatty-acid-oxidation programs. MOTS-c acts through the folate-AICAR-AMPK axis (and CK2 in muscle) to raise metabolic flexibility and mitochondrial efficiency. AMPK and the NAD+/SIRT1/PGC-1alpha axis are mutually reinforcing nodes of one energy-stress network (AMPK can raise NAD+, and NAD+/SIRT1 feeds back on PGC-1alpha-driven mitochondrial biogenesis), so the two compounds converge on overlapping fat-oxidation and insulin-sensitivity machinery. This is convergent pathway stacking, not receptor competition, so effects on energy expenditure and insulin sensitivity are plausibly additive rather than antagonistic. MoA-gen MOTS-c (PMID 25738459 folate-AICAR-AMPK; PMID 36670507 review); 5-Amino-1MQ monograph (PMID 24717514 NAD+/SIRT1/PGC-1alpha axis; PMID 29155147). Convergence on the shared energy-sensing network inferred; no co-administration study exists. Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c (both nudge insulin sensitivity, so watch for lower-than-expected glucose), plus general markers of metabolic rate/body composition; there is no validated human protocol for either compound, so treat any observed change as an unstudied signal.
  • GHK-Cu shares target Speculative
    MOTS-c activates AMPK (and directly binds CK2) and engages the Nrf2/ARE antioxidant program; GHK-Cu (in rodent models) binds and activates SIRT1, raising Nrf2-driven antioxidant enzymes and PGC-1alpha for mitochondrial biogenesis. AMPK (MOTS-c) and the SIRT1/PGC-1alpha node (GHK-Cu) are the interconnected arms of one integrated AMPK/SIRT1/PGC-1alpha/Nrf2 energy-and-antioxidant program, and both feed the shared Nrf2/ARE output - a genuine shared downstream pathway. Certainty is limited because GHK-Cu's systemic SIRT1/PGC-1alpha effect is rodent-only and its systemic human PK is unstudied. PMID 36905132 (GHK-Cu binds/activates SIRT1 -> FoxO3a/Nrf2/PGC-1alpha in rodent muscle); PMID 41268687 (AMPK/SIRT1/PGC-1alpha as one integrated hub); PMID 27517151 (GHK-Cu antioxidant/SOD/NF-kB) Watch: No specific interaction lab is validated; both are systemic gray-market peptides, so general harm-reduction applies (injection-site signs, and GHK-Cu copper caution - serum copper/ceruloplasmin - with injectable use). No established additive toxicity.
  • Metformin redundant Evidence-human
    Both converge on the same energy-sensing node: MOTS-c disrupts the folate/de-novo-purine (AICAR/ZMP) cycle so the AICAR intermediate builds up and tips on AMPK, and metformin also activates AMPK (via a rising AMP/ATP ratio from complex-I inhibition, LKB1-dependent). Their mechanisms of action are described in the literature as 'largely overlapping' (insulin sensitization, enhanced glucose utilization, suppression of mitochondrial respiration, folate-AICAR-AMPK convergence). Because they both funnel into the AMPK/glucose-disposal axis, running them together is largely redundant on that axis while still able to additively lower glucose. (Note: whether metformin actually RAISES circulating MOTS-c is unresolved - the one clinical paper concluded the metformin-MOTS-c relationship needs further study and that muscle production may limit metformin's effect on circulating levels.) PMID 36490309 (Cuyas 2022, Aging - MoA overlap; metformin-MOTS-c regulatory relationship unresolved); PMID 25738459 (Lee 2015, Cell Metab - folate-AICAR-AMPK) Watch: Fasting glucose, HbA1c and hypoglycemia-awareness (shakiness, sweating, confusion), since the glucose-lowering effect can stack; a metabolic panel to gauge whether MOTS-c adds anything beyond metformin's AMPK activation.
  • SLU-PP-332 redundant Evidence-rodent
    Both are exercise-mimetics whose benefits funnel through the PGC-1alpha/oxidative-metabolism axis. MOTS-c acts via AMPK -> PGC-1alpha to raise fatty-acid oxidation, mitochondrial efficiency and endurance; SLU-PP-332 (ERRalpha/beta/gamma agonist) increases oxidative muscle fibers, fatty-acid oxidation and energy expenditure, and the ERR receptors are the canonical transcriptional partners of PGC-1alpha. Stacking them is largely redundant on the same oxidative-metabolism transcriptional program. SLU-PP-332 ERRalpha/beta/gamma - oxidative fibers, fatty-acid oxidation, energy expenditure (slu-pp-332.json); mots-c.json (AMPK/PGC-1alpha oxidative program, PMID 41520850) Watch: Given overlapping oxidative/energy-expenditure drive, watch resting heart rate and exercise tolerance; SLU-PP-332 has a rodent cardiac signal at high exposure (separate concern). Effect overlap means limited independent benefit.

History & legal status

"Discovered and named in 2015 by Changhan Lee, Pinchas Cohen and colleagues at USC, reported in Cell Metabolism as the second mitochondrial-derived peptide after humanin [PMID 25738459]. The same year, Fuku et al. proposed that the MOTS-c-region m.1382A>C polymorphism — specific to Northeast Asian/Japanese populations — might contribute to Japanese exceptional longevity, framing MOTS-c as a putative 'longevity peptide' [PMID 26289118]. The mitonuclear/nuclear-translocation concept was developed 2018-2019 [PMID 31131297], the pivotal aging/exercise-capacity work published 2021 [PMID 33473109], and the detailed nuclear-transport (MYH9/CK2) and direct CK2-target mechanisms in 2024-2025 [PMID 40403491, 39559755]. REGULATORY STATUS: MOTS-c is NOT an approved drug anywhere — there is no FDA, EMA or other regulatory approval, and (as of this writing) no completed published human therapeutic trial; it remains an investigational/preclinical molecule. It is not a dietary-supplement ingredient. It is sold by 'research chemical'/peptide vendors as research-use-only material, which is unregulated for human use and of uncertain purity. Because it is a performance- and metabolism-modifying peptide, use in sport would fall under anti-doping scrutiny (peptide hormones / metabolic modulators categories), though MOTS-c is not individually named the way AICAR is. Legal status varies by country but it is generally NOT a licensed medicine and its sale 'for human consumption' is typically not permitted."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Human pharmacokinetics of administered MOTS-c are entirely unknown — no published plasma half-life, bioavailability (especially for any subcutaneous route used informally), metabolism or excretion data exist; rodent intermittent dosing only implies a short-lived peptide.
  • No human efficacy or dose-finding trial has been completed — every magnitude/benefit claim (insulin sensitivity, endurance, healthspan) rests on rodent or in-vitro data, and one human study found 8 weeks of training did NOT change basal circulating levels, complicating the exercise-mimetic narrative [PMID 31066084].
  • Whether a defined cell-surface receptor exists is unresolved — actions are attributed to intracellular CK2 binding, the folate/AMPK axis, and nuclear ARE transcription, but how an injected peptide reliably enters target cells (uptake mechanism, transporter) is not established [PMID 39559755, 40403491].
  • Tissue-divergent CK2 modulation (activation in muscle, suppression in fat) raises unquantified off-target risk; the systemic consequences of chronically perturbing this pleiotropic kinase in humans are unknown [PMID 39559755].
  • Long-term safety, including any oncologic implication of chronically activating NRF2/ARE antioxidant programs and CK2 (pathways co-opted by some tumors), has never been studied for MOTS-c [PMID 40403491].
  • The causal weight of endogenous MOTS-c in human aging/longevity is unproven — the m.1382A>C longevity association is correlational and population-specific, not a demonstrated mechanism [PMID 26289118].

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (NOT a protocol, and not an endorsement of use — no human dosing is established). Because the core pharmacology is AMPK/glucose-lowering and antioxidant-transcriptional: (1) Glucose status — fasting glucose, HbA1c, and symptomatic hypoglycemia awareness, especially for anyone also using insulin, sulfonylureas, SGLT2 inhibitors or metformin, since effects may stack [PMID 25738459, 36490309]. (2) Metabolic panel — lipid profile and insulin/HOMA-IR if tracking the intended metabolic effect. (3) General safety labs for any injected research peptide — CBC, renal and liver function as baseline/periodic checks, and vigilance for injection-site reactions, signs of infection or allergic/immunogenic response (a non-pharmaceutical-grade peptide carries impurity/endotoxin risk). (4) Exercise tolerance / unusual fatigue — given acute mitochondrial-respiration-suppressing effects, note any drop in performance or unexplained fatigue. (5) Source verification — independent purity/identity testing, since unregulated 'research-grade' material is the only form available and content cannot be assumed. Any concerning symptom (hypoglycemia, palpitations, persistent fatigue, injection-site infection) warrants stopping and seeking medical evaluation. There is no validated MOTS-c blood level to target."

Caveats

Direction/magnitude honesty: (1) AMPK activation is the established core, but the exact upstream trigger is debated — the folate→AICAR→AMPK route (PMID 25738459) and direct CK2 binding (PMID 39559755) are both proposed and may be tissue-specific (CK2 activated in muscle, suppressed in fat). (2) Almost all efficacy data are rodent/cell ('Evidence-rodent'); do not present mouse magnitudes as human effects. (3) No RCTs in humans for the marketed uses; circulating MOTS-c associations are observational. (4) Some MDP literature flags context-dependent or even pro-senescence effects (PMID 30058454), so 'pure anti-aging' framing is oversimplified/Contested. (5) A 2026 human study questions whether exercising muscle is even the source of blood MOTS-c (PMID 41520850). (6) Research-peptide/grey-market sources are unregulated; purity and dosing in DIY use are unvalidated.
