Arimistane

The most popular over-the-counter "estrogen blocker" — a real irreversible aromatase inhibitor sold as a supplement, which the FDA says is not a lawful dietary ingredient.

Human / approved 11 citations
Class
Steroidal, mechanism-based (irreversible/"suicide") aromatase inhibitor of the exemestane / 6-OXO / ATD family; sold OTC as a supplement or research chemical, not an approved drug
Primary target
Aromatase (CYP19A1)
Human status
Sold and consumed by humans widely as an OTC "estrogen blocker" — but it is an unapproved drug, not an approved medicine and not (per FDA) a lawful dietary ingredient. There are essentially NO published human pharmacokinetic or clinical-safety trials of arimistane (androsta-3,5-diene-7,17-dione) itself; the peer-reviewed literature on this exact molecule is limited to forensic identification of it as a "designer steroid" in supplements (PMID 30892803) and anti-doping detection work. Everything about its human effects is inferred from its structural cousins in the same suicide-inhibitor class — 6-OXO (androst-4-ene-3,6,17-trione), which has a small human supplement trial (PMID 17949492), ATD (PMID 19089863), and the licensed drug exemestane (PMID 12455303). Treat human efficacy/safety claims for arimistane specifically as unverified by direct study.

At a glance

  • Lowers estradiol and estrone by irreversibly inactivating aromatase (class effect for licensed steroidal AIs; not quantified for arimistane in any human trial)
  • In men, tends to raise LH/FSH and therefore testosterone and DHT — the intended anti-estrogen / test-boost effect
  • Marketed to blunt gynecomastia, water retention and other estrogenic effects on/after anabolic-steroid cycles
  • Per FDA, not a lawful dietary ingredient — an unapproved, unlicensed aromatase inhibitor sold as a supplement
  • Banned by WADA as an aromatase inhibitor; produces detectable, partly androgenic metabolites -> failed drug tests
  • Class risk if estrogen driven too low: reduced HDL cholesterol, bone-density loss, joint/mood/libido complaints

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It is a real designer aromatase-inhibitor steroid, not a vitamin Established

    Arimistane is the steroid androsta-3,5-diene-7,17-dione (C19H24O2, MW 284; protonated mass 285.18). FDA Forensic Chemistry Center chemists isolated and structurally confirmed it (GC-FT-IR-MS, LC-HRAM-MS, NMR) as an undeclared 'designer steroid' in performance supplements. So the starting point is that this is a synthetic steroid molecule marketed as a supplement, not a food-derived nutrient. PMID 30892803

  2. Its whole chemical family are suicide inhibitors of aromatase Established

    Arimistane belongs to the same steroidal-AI family as exemestane (Aromasin), 6-OXO (androst-4-ene-3,6,17-trione) and ATD (androsta-1,4,6-triene-3,17-dione). These are 'mechanism-based' or 'suicide' inhibitors: the aromatase enzyme mistakes them for its natural substrate and starts processing them. PMID 12455303

  3. The enzyme turns the drug into a reactive trap and destroys itself Evidence-in vitro

    During catalysis, aromatase converts this class of steroids into a reactive intermediate that forms a covalent bond inside the enzyme's active site. For the close analog series (19-substituted androst-4-ene-3,6,17-triones, the 6-OXO scaffold), inactivation is time-dependent, needs NADPH (i.e. needs the enzyme to be catalytically working), and is blocked by the natural substrate androstenedione — the fingerprint of a true suicide substrate. Arimistane is presumed to act the same way by structural analogy (this exact experiment has NOT been published for arimistane itself, and the precise chemical structure of the covalent adduct has not been defined for it). PMID 8430444

  4. Irreversible means the block outlasts the drug in the blood Established

    Because the bond is covalent, the enzyme molecule is permanently dead — the body must synthesize new aromatase protein to recover. So the estrogen-lowering effect persists after the drug has cleared, unlike reversible AIs (anastrozole, letrozole) that simply occupy the site and wash out. This is the defining pharmacodynamic property of the exemestane class. PMID 12455303

  5. Less aromatase means less estradiol and less estrone Established

    Aromatase (CYP19A1) is the only enzyme that makes estrogens from androgens. Inhibiting it lowers circulating estradiol (E2) and estrone (E1). This is the intended effect and is well established for the licensed drug class; for arimistane specifically the estrogen-lowering is inferred from class behavior and user reports, not from a published human trial — and note that the closest OTC proxy, 6-OXO, did not itself lower estrogen in its one trial (it raised estrone), so extrapolation to arimistane is uncertain. PMID 12455303

  6. Lower estrogen releases the brake on testosterone (in men) Evidence-human

    In men, estradiol provides negative feedback to the hypothalamus/pituitary; lowering E2 disinhibits LH and FSH, which drives the testes to make more testosterone. In the closest human evidence — the 6-OXO supplement trial (same class) — free testosterone rose ~84-90%, DHT rose ~192-265%, and the free-testosterone/estrogen ratio rose, though aromatase was only partially blocked and estrone actually increased. This feedback pathway is the mechanism users are chasing. PMID 17949492

  7. It leaves a metabolic and doping-test trail Evidence-human

    These steroidal AIs are not inert: they are metabolized to hydroxylated and reduced steroids, some androgenic. The related ATD is excreted largely unchanged and as reduced/androgenic metabolites (including boldenone-type compounds), and 6-OXO yields 6-hydroxy metabolites. That is why WADA prohibits this whole class as aromatase inhibitors and why 'estrogen-blocker' supplements cause failed drug tests. Arimistane's own metabolism is characterized mainly for anti-doping detection, not for clinical safety. PMID 19089863

  8. The catch: driving estrogen too low has its own harms Evidence-human

    Estrogen in men is not just a 'female hormone' — it maintains bone and HDL cholesterol. Selectively removing estradiol in men lowers HDL2 cholesterol, and aromatase inhibition in older men reduced spine bone mineral density over a year. So the same mechanism that raises testosterone can, if overdone, harm bone and lipids. This is a class effect, not measured for arimistane specifically. PMID 8157711

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Acts at the catalytic active site of aromatase (CYP19A1) as a steroidal pseudo-substrate. Like the whole exemestane/6-OXO/ATD family, it is a mechanism-based ('suicide') inhibitor: the enzyme's own catalytic cycle converts the steroid into a reactive electrophilic intermediate that forms a covalent bond in the active site, permanently inactivating that enzyme molecule (irreversible, NADPH-dependent, substrate-protectable in the characterized 6-OXO/androst-4-ene-3,6,17-trione series). No published Ki/kinact value exists for arimistane itself; the mechanistic constants come from the 6-OXO series (19-substituted androst-4-ene-3,6,17-trione analogs, kinact on the order of 0.06-0.25 min-1).

Signalling cascade. Aromatase inactivation -> fall in estradiol (E2) and estrone (E1) -> in men, reduced estrogenic negative feedback -> increased LH/FSH -> increased testicular testosterone and DHT, raising the testosterone:estrogen ratio. Caveat: this estrogen-lowering cascade is anchored on the licensed drug class; the closest OTC human trial (6-OXO) raised testosterone/DHT but did not itself lower estrogen (estrone rose), so the estrogen-lowering step for arimistane specifically is inferred, not demonstrated.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsules/tablets sold as supplement); no licensed formulation.
Absorption / bioavailability
Not characterized in any published human study for arimistane. Oral steroidal AIs of this class are absorbed but subject to extensive first-pass metabolism; exemestane, the licensed analog, has low oral bioavailability that is increased by food.
Half-life
Not established for arimistane. Pharmacodynamically, the estrogen-lowering effect outlasts plasma drug levels because inhibition is covalent/irreversible — recovery requires new aromatase synthesis.
Metabolism
Extensive hepatic steroid metabolism (reduction of ring double bonds, reduction/hydroxylation at C3/C17/D-ring), by analogy to ATD and 6-OXO. Some metabolites are androgenic (the ATD pathway yields boldenone-type products). No arimistane-specific human metabolic map is published beyond anti-doping detection context.
Excretion
Renal, largely as conjugated (sulfate/glucuronide) steroid metabolites, by class analogy (ATD detectable in urine up to ~3 days in equine work; human 6-OXO metabolites excreted as conjugates).
Notes
The single most important PK caveat: essentially all of the above is inferred from related molecules (6-OXO PMID 16213800, ATD PMID 19089863 and PMID 26031748, exemestane PMID 12455303), not measured for arimistane.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

No validated human dose-response curve exists for arimistane. The closest signal is the 6-OXO trial: 300 mg and 600 mg/day for 8 weeks both raised free testosterone (~84-90%) and DHT (~192-265%) and the T:E ratio, yet did NOT fully inhibit aromatase and did NOT change body composition — with only modest separation between the two doses (PMID 17949492). Because these are irreversible inhibitors, effect accumulates with repeated dosing and persists after stopping, so 'more is better' reasoning is dangerous: it risks driving estradiol below the healthy range, at which point bone (PMID 19820017) and HDL (PMID 8157711) suffer. NO dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs Exemestane (Aromasin)
    same: Both are steroidal, irreversible/suicide aromatase inactivators with durable estrogen suppression.
    different: Exemestane is an FDA-approved drug with defined dose, PK, and safety data in trials; arimistane is an unapproved OTC 'designer' analog with no clinical dataset and unregulated product quality.
  • vs 6-OXO (androst-4-ene-3,6,17-trione)
    same: Closely related OTC steroidal suicide AI; the best human proxy for arimistane's likely effects.
    different: 6-OXO has an actual (small) human trial (PMID 17949492); arimistane does not. They are different molecules, so potency and metabolite mix are not interchangeable.
  • vs ATD (androsta-1,4,6-triene-3,17-dione)
    same: Same OTC irreversible-AI niche; both metabolized to androgenic products and WADA-banned.
    different: ATD's human/equine metabolism is characterized (PMID 19089863, PMID 26031748); arimistane's is mostly anti-doping detection work.
  • vs Anastrozole / letrozole (non-steroidal AIs)
    same: All lower estradiol and can raise T in men.
    different: Anastrozole/letrozole are reversible, competitive, prescription drugs with dose titration and trial data (PMID 19820017, PMID 23949882); arimistane is irreversible and unregulated, so its estrogen suppression is harder to titrate and reverse.

History &amp; legal status

Arimistane (androsta-3,5-diene-7,17-dione) emerged in the late-2000s/2010s wave of OTC 'estrogen blockers' that followed 6-OXO and ATD-containing products (e.g. Novedex Xtreme). It became one of the most popular supplement AIs, marketed for 'on-cycle' estrogen control and post-cycle therapy. Regulatory position: the FDA does not consider it a lawful dietary ingredient — it is an unapproved aromatase-inhibitor drug, and FDA's own Forensic Chemistry Center has documented it as an undeclared 'designer steroid' in supplements (PMID 30892803). It is prohibited in sport by WADA as an aromatase inhibitor, in the same class action that covers 6-OXO and ATD (PMID 19089863). Bottom line for this monograph's focus: it should be understood as an unapproved AI masquerading as a supplement, not a benign 'natural test booster.'

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Arimistane's own human pharmacokinetics (bioavailability, half-life, Cmax) — never published.
  • Its actual potency (Ki/kinact) against human aromatase versus exemestane/6-OXO — inferred, not measured.
  • How much and how fast it lowers estradiol in humans, and at what intake — no human hormone trial on the molecule itself (and its closest OTC proxy, 6-OXO, did not itself lower estrogen).
  • Its full human metabolite profile and whether metabolites are meaningfully androgenic/anabolic.
  • Long-term safety: bone, lipid, cardiovascular, mood and fertility effects with repeated real-world use.
  • Product reliability: real-world dose accuracy, purity, and co-formulants across brands.
  • Rebound/withdrawal behavior after stopping an irreversible inhibitor.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction framing, not a recommendation to use, and no dosing is given. If someone has used or is using an OTC AI like arimistane, the physiologic domains worth objective monitoring (with a clinician) are: (1) Estradiol (sensitive/LC-MS assay in men) and testosterone/LH/FSH — to detect over-suppression of estrogen, the central hazard; symptoms of crashed estrogen include low libido, joint aches, low mood, and fatigue. (2) Lipid panel, especially HDL — AI-driven low estrogen lowers HDL in men (PMID 8157711). (3) Bone health over longer exposure — chronic low estrogen reduces BMD (PMID 19820017); a clinician may consider DXA if use is prolonged. (4) Liver panel if products are of unknown purity/adulteration (PMID 30892803). (5) For athletes: recognize this WILL cause anti-doping violations and yields detectable androgenic metabolites (PMID 19089863). The safest 'monitoring' action is medical review and discontinuation rather than dose adjustment, given the absence of any validated dosing or safety data for the molecule.

Caveats

Read this as mechanism education, NOT medical advice and NOT an endorsement of use. Three honesty flags dominate this entry. (1) EVIDENCE GAP: there is no published human trial, no PK study, and no clinical safety dataset for arimistane (androsta-3,5-diene-7,17-dione) itself — its human profile is entirely extrapolated from 6-OXO, ATD, and exemestane, which are related but not identical molecules, so potency, half-life, and metabolite mix could differ. (2) LEGAL/QUALITY: FDA treats it as not a lawful dietary ingredient — it is an unapproved aromatase-inhibitor drug sold outside the medicine system, so products are unregulated, frequently mislabeled or adulterated, and dose/purity are unknown; supplement analyses repeatedly find hidden or misdeclared steroid ingredients (PMID 30892803, PMID 38691359). (3) HYPE vs DATA: even the best-studied cousin, 6-OXO, did NOT fully block aromatase and did NOT change body composition in 8 weeks despite raising testosterone (PMID 17949492) — so the 'massive test boost / body recomposition' marketing outruns the data. Estrogen is protective in men (bone, HDL, mood, libido, joints); over-suppression is a real harm (PMID 8157711, PMID 19820017). Anyone drug-tested should assume it will trigger an anti-doping violation and produce androgenic metabolites (PMID 19089863). No dosing protocol is provided here by design.
