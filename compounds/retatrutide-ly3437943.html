Retatrutide

One molecule that pulls three metabolic levers at once: it tells your body to eat less AND burn more — the first "triple agonist" to take fat-burning seriously.

Limited human data 15 citations
Class
Single-peptide triple incretin receptor agonist (GLP-1R / GIPR / GCGR agonist); once-weekly injectable
Primary target
Three G-protein-coupled receptors simultaneously: the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R), the GIP receptor (GIPR), and — the key differentiator — the glucagon receptor (GCGR)
Human status
SUBSTANTIAL human data, but not yet approved (investigational as of 2026). Human evidence: Phase 1 single-dose, Phase 1b multiple-ascending-dose in T2D (Urva 2022, Lancet, PMID 36354040), Phase 2 obesity (Jastreboff 2023, NEJM, PMID 37366315), Phase 2 T2D (Rosenstock 2023, Lancet, PMID 37385280), Phase 2a MASLD (Sanyal 2024, Nat Med, PMID 38858523), and Phase 3 now reporting (e.g. TRANSCEND-T2D-1, Bajaj 2026, Lancet, PMID 42250575; TRIUMPH program, PMID 41090431). Human data CONFIRM the magnitude of weight loss, glycemic improvement, and liver-fat reduction, and confirm once-weekly dosing (half-life ~6 days). What remains MORE rodent-grounded is the precise molecular accounting of the glucagon arm — i.e. the demonstration that GCGR-mediated energy expenditure ADDS to appetite-driven intake reduction comes most cleanly from the obese-mouse studies in Coskun 2022 (PMID 35985340); human energy-expenditure/body-composition data are emerging (e.g. body-composition substudy, PMID 40609566) but the mechanistic split is inferred. Long-term cardiovascular outcomes and safety are still being established in Phase 3.

At a glance

  • Weight loss: up to ~24% of body weight at 48 weeks in adults with obesity without diabetes (Phase 2, dose-dependent, no plateau yet at trial end) — PMID 37366315
  • Glycemic control: HbA1c reduced up to ~2.2% and fasting glucose substantially lowered in type 2 diabetes — PMID 37385280, PMID 36354040
  • Liver: marked reduction/clearance of liver fat in MASLD/MASH (a direct readout of the glucagon-driven hepatic fat-burning arm) — PMID 38858523
  • Increased resting energy expenditure (calories burned) — the GCGR contribution; demonstrated mechanistically in obese mice, inferred in humans — PMID 35985340
  • Glucose-dependent insulin secretion (low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk) plus appetite suppression and delayed gastric emptying — PMID 39114288
  • Main side effects are GI (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea), dose-related and managed by slow titration; mild heart-rate increase observed — PMID 37366315, PMID 36354040

Mechanism, step by step

  1. One peptide, three receptors Established

    Retatrutide is a single engineered peptide (based on the GIP backbone) that simultaneously binds and switches ON three gut/pancreatic hormone receptors at once — GLP-1R, GIPR, and the glucagon receptor (GCGR). That third one, glucagon, is what makes it different from Ozempic (GLP-1 only) and Mounjaro (GLP-1 + GIP). PMID 35985340 (Coskun 2022, Cell Metab); PMID 37385280 (Rosenstock 2023, Lancet)

  2. GLP-1 arm: insulin up, glucagon down, appetite down Established

    Activating GLP-1R makes the pancreas release insulin only when blood sugar is high (glucose-dependent, so low hypoglycemia risk), tamps down the sugar-raising hormone glucagon from alpha-cells when sugar is high, and acts on the brain's appetite centers to make you feel full. This is the same proven lever as semaglutide. PMID 39114288 (Frontiers Endocrinol review); PMID 35985340

  3. GIP arm: more insulin help and better tolerance Evidence-human (GIPR insulinotropic role established; its central/glucose-handling magnitude is contested)

    Activating GIPR adds a second push on glucose-dependent insulin release and appears to improve how well the body handles the drug and reduces nausea, while also acting in the brain to curb intake. Retatrutide actually has its strongest activity at this receptor. PMID 36509857 (Nat Rev Endocrinol); PMID 35985340

  4. Glucagon arm: the fat-burning accelerator Evidence-rodent

    Here's the twist — glucagon is normally a sugar-RAISING hormone, but turning on the glucagon receptor in a controlled way revs up the liver to burn fat and increases whole-body energy expenditure (calories burned at rest). The other two arms keep blood sugar in check so the glucagon doesn't backfire. PMID 35985340 (energy-expenditure mechanism shown in obese mice); PMID 40364529 (MASLD mechanism review)

  5. Two forces stack: eat less + burn more Evidence-rodent

    The net effect is additive. GLP-1R and GIPR drive DOWN calorie intake (appetite, fullness, slower stomach emptying); GCGR drives UP calorie burn. In obese mice, the weight loss from the appetite arms was augmented by the glucagon-driven boost in energy expenditure — that combination is the whole design rationale. PMID 35985340 (Coskun 2022 — explicit additive mechanism in mice)

  6. In humans: large, dose-dependent weight loss Evidence-human

    In a Phase 2 obesity trial, people without diabetes lost up to ~24% of body weight at 48 weeks on the high dose — larger than any single- or dual-agonist seen to date — confirming the triple-lever idea translates from mice to people. It also lowered HbA1c up to ~2.2% in type 2 diabetes and cleared liver fat in MASLD. PMID 37366315 (Jastreboff 2023, NEJM, Phase 2 obesity); PMID 37385280 (Rosenstock 2023, Lancet, Phase 2 T2D); PMID 38858523 (Sanyal 2024, Nat Med, MASLD)

  7. Slow clearance enables once-weekly dosing Established

    The peptide is engineered with a fatty-acid tail that lets it ride on blood albumin, giving a half-life of about 6 days — so a single weekly injection keeps all three receptors gently engaged. Doses are titrated up slowly to manage nausea. PMID 36354040 (Urva 2022, Lancet, Phase 1b PK)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Retatrutide is a single 39-amino-acid synthetic peptide (a GIP-analog backbone engineered to gain GLP-1R and GCGR activity) carrying a C20 fatty-diacid (acyl) chain via a linker on a lysine, which drives reversible albumin binding for half-life extension. It is a balanced/full AGONIST at all three class-B GPCRs but NOT equally: in vitro it shows roughly balanced glucagon-receptor (GCGR) and GLP-1R activity with GREATER relative GIPR activity (Coskun 2022 discovery paper, PMID 35985340 — 'balanced GCGR and GLP-1R activity but more GIPR activity'). Note: because it is a peptide biologic, it is characterized by functional potency (cAMP EC50, typically sub-nanomolar to low-nanomolar at each receptor) rather than the small-molecule Ki language; exact published EC50 values per receptor are in the Coskun 2022 source. Certainty: Established that it is a balanced tri-agonist with GIPR-skewed potency; the precise numeric EC50 at each receptor should be quoted directly from PMID 35985340. It has no meaningful antagonist activity. Confirmed functionally active at all three human receptors in native human tissue: in isolated human atrial muscle, retatrutide (10-100 nM) raised contractile force, and that effect was blocked individually by a GLP-1R antagonist, a GIPR antagonist, AND a GCGR antagonist — direct evidence each receptor arm is engaged (Neumann 2025, PMID 40613938, Evidence-human ex vivo).

Signalling cascade. All three targets (GLP-1R, GIPR, GCGR) are Gs-coupled class-B GPCRs. Receptor binding activates adenylyl cyclase -> rises in intracellular cAMP -> PKA activation (confirmed mechanistically in human atrium where the cAMP/PKA-dependent inotropy was potentiated by the PDE3 inhibitor cilostamide and depended on sarcoplasmic-reticulum ryanodine-receptor Ca2+ release; PMID 40613938). Tissue-specific outputs: (1) GLP-1R on pancreatic beta-cells -> glucose-DEPENDENT insulin secretion (low hypo risk); on alpha-cells suppresses glucagon; on hypothalamic/hindbrain (area postrema, NTS) neurons -> satiety and slowed gastric emptying. (2) GIPR -> augments glucose-dependent insulin secretion and modulates adipose/CNS energy handling; the GIPR arm is thought to improve nausea tolerability and add to weight effect. (3) GCGR on hepatocytes and other tissues -> increased energy expenditure / thermogenesis and hepatic lipid oxidation (the 'burn more' and liver-fat-clearing arm). The net cascade = central appetite suppression + delayed gastric emptying (eat less) layered on top of glucagon-driven increased energy expenditure and hepatic fat oxidation (burn more), which together overcome the metabolic-adaptation plateau seen with single-incretin drugs. Certainty: Established for the receptor coupling and the three-arm physiology; the human-tissue cAMP/PKA confirmation is Evidence-human ex vivo.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Once-weekly subcutaneous injection (abdomen/thigh/upper arm); clear solution, no oral or transdermal form. It is a peptide, so it must be injected — it is destroyed by gut proteases if swallowed.
Absorption / bioavailability
Subcutaneous depot absorption; PK is dose-proportional across the studied range (Evidence-human, PMID 36354040). Like other acylated peptides, the fatty-diacid tail binds circulating albumin, slowing absorption and clearance enough to support weekly dosing. Exact absolute bioavailability % is not separately published in the human reports reviewed (key unknown).
Half-life
Approximately 6 days in people with type 2 diabetes (Evidence-human, phase 1b, PMID 36354040). This long half-life is engineered by the C20 fatty-diacid/albumin-binding modification, not an intrinsic peptide property. A single dose produced measurable weight loss persisting to ~day 43 (PMID 35985340), consistent with the ~6-day half-life and slow washout.
Metabolism
As an acylated peptide it is expected to be cleared by general proteolytic catabolism (peptidases/proteases) into amino acids and small peptide fragments rather than by cytochrome-P450 enzymes — meaning classic CYP-mediated drug-drug interactions are not anticipated (Evidence-human for the drug class; specific retatrutide ADME-enzyme mapping is not fully published — labeled as inference from peptide-drug class, Established for class).
Excretion
Catabolic/proteolytic breakdown with the resulting amino acids/fragments handled by normal endogenous pathways; no intact-drug renal or biliary elimination is the expected route for this peptide class. Dedicated human renal/hepatic-impairment PK studies specific to retatrutide were not located in this run (key unknown).
Notes
Not a prodrug and not an ester in the classic small-molecule sense — the 'ester/acyl' feature is a fatty-diacid lipidation that mediates reversible albumin binding for half-life extension, the same design principle used in semaglutide and tirzepatide. As a large lipidated peptide it does NOT freely cross the blood-brain barrier; CNS appetite effects are thought to act largely via circumventricular organs (area postrema/NTS) and vagal afferents where the BBB is permeable, rather than broad CNS penetration. Dosing is escalated stepwise (e.g., low 2 mg start) specifically to mitigate GI side effects during titration (PMID 37366315).

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Human dose-response IS well characterized for efficacy across 0.5-12 mg weekly, and is steeply dose-dependent. OBESITY (phase 2, NEJM, n=338, 48 wk; PMID 37366315): mean weight change at 24 wk -7.2% (1 mg), -12.9% (4 mg pooled), -17.3% (8 mg pooled), -17.5% (12 mg) vs -1.6% placebo; at 48 wk -8.7%, -17.1%, -22.8%, and -24.2% (12 mg) vs -2.1% placebo. At 48 wk on 12 mg, 100% lost >=5%, 93% lost >=10%, 83% lost >=15%. TYPE 2 DIABETES (phase 2, Lancet, n=281, 36 wk; PMID 37385280): HbA1c fell up to ~-2.0% and weight up to -16.94% at 12 mg; notably a flatter/partly inverted dose-response for glycemia (lower doses already strongly lower glucose) vs a steep weight dose-response. PHASE 3 monotherapy T2D (TRANSCEND-T2D-1, n=537, 40 wk; PMID 42250575): HbA1c -1.69%/-1.86%/-1.94% and weight -11.5%/-13.9%/-15.3% at 4/9/12 mg vs placebo -2.6% weight. LIVER FAT (phase 2a MASLD, n=98; PMID 38858523): liver-fat reduction -42.9% (1 mg) up to -82.4% (12 mg); 86% reached normal liver fat at 12 mg. ANIMAL: in diet-induced obese mice, weight loss combined GCGR-driven energy-expenditure increase with GIPR/GLP-1R-driven calorie-intake reduction (PMID 35985340). What is UNKNOWN: the long-term (multi-year) dose-response plateau, the optimal maintenance dose, and dose-response for hard cardiovascular/renal outcomes — phase 3 TRIUMPH/TRANSCEND outcome data are still maturing.

How it compares

  • vs Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) — dual GIP/GLP-1 agonist
    same: Acylated once-weekly injectable peptide using albumin-binding fatty-acid modification; shares GLP-1R and GIPR agonism, GI side-effect profile, and glucose-dependent insulin mechanism
    different: Retatrutide ADDS a third arm — glucagon-receptor (GCGR) agonism — adding energy-expenditure/thermogenesis and potent liver-fat clearance on top of appetite suppression, producing larger weight loss in phase 2 (~24% at 48 wk vs tirzepatide ~21-22%); tirzepatide is FDA-approved while retatrutide is still investigational
  • vs Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) — single GLP-1R agonist
    same: Once-weekly acylated peptide, albumin-binding half-life extension, glucose-dependent insulin secretion, satiety/slowed gastric emptying, GI AEs
    different: Semaglutide hits only GLP-1R; retatrutide's added GIPR and GCGR arms give substantially greater weight loss and direct hepatic/energy-expenditure effects, but also a distinct dose-dependent heart-rate and glucagon-driven hepatic-glucose dynamic
  • vs Survodutide / mazdutide / pemvidutide — GLP-1R/GCGR DUAL agonists
    same: Both engage the glucagon arm for energy expenditure and liver-fat reduction; weekly peptides aimed at obesity/MASH
    different: Retatrutide is a TRIPLE agonist that retains GIPR activity (skewed toward GIPR potency), which the GLP-1/GCGR duals lack; the GIPR arm is thought to add weight efficacy and improve nausea tolerability
  • vs Native glucagon / GLP-1 / GIP hormones
    same: Acts through the same three endogenous class-B Gs-coupled receptors and the same cAMP/PKA second-messenger cascade
    different: Retatrutide is a single non-endogenous engineered peptide combining all three activities with a ~6-day half-life, versus the native hormones' minutes-long half-lives and single-receptor selectivity

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Amycretin compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Retatrutide is a GLP-1R+GIPR+GCGR triple agonist and amycretin is a GLP-1R+amylin agonist, so they share the GLP-1R arm (redundant/additive appetite suppression and GI effects). Both also raise resting heart rate via the incretin class effect (retatrutide's HR rise is human-observed; amycretin's own magnitude is not separately quantified), so the tachycardia and GI-driven dehydration risk compound. amycretin.json (GLP-1R agonist; class heart-rate rise, magnitude unquantified); retatrutide.json (GLP-1R agonist; mild heart-rate increase); PMID 41582189 (GLP-1 RA heart-rate rise) Watch: Watch for additive resting heart-rate rise/palpitations, severe combined GI symptoms and dehydration, and excessive/too-fast weight loss; monitor heart rate, blood pressure and hydration.
  • Cagrilintide compounds risk ⚠ Speculative
    Retatrutide (incretin/glucagon agonist) and cagrilintide (amylin/CTR agonist) both slow gastric emptying and stimulate the area postrema, so nausea, vomiting and GI dysmotility would stack. Retatrutide's glucagon arm can additionally raise heart rate, an effect not offset by amylin. This is a mechanistic inference; the stack has not been studied clinically. monograph retatrutide-ly3437943.json (GCGR arm, HR rise); PMID 41324524 (incretin GI effects); cagrilintide.json side_effect_mechanisms Watch: Watch GI tolerability and dehydration (renal function with vomiting). Monitor resting heart rate and report palpitations/syncope. Report severe persistent abdominal pain (pancreatitis class concern).
  • Ephedrine compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Incretin agonists produce a resting heart-rate increase, and retatrutide showed a dose-dependent HR rise in its phase 2 trial (a class effect across the GLP-1/GIP/GCGR agonists), plus GCGR-driven positive chronotropy/inotropy in human atrial tissue; its glucagon-receptor arm additionally raises resting energy expenditure. Ephedrine independently drives tachycardia (beta-1) and thermogenesis. Co-use adds a second chronotropic stimulus on top of the incretin's baseline HR bump, so resting heart rate and any tachyarrhythmia tendency compound; the additive energy-expenditure/thermogenic load is a secondary consideration. PMID 37366315; PMID 36354040 Watch: Resting heart rate and rhythm (this is the shared, additive readout), blood pressure; palpitations or a sustained resting tachycardia warrant reassessment and ECG.

History &amp; legal status

"Originated at Eli Lilly as LY3437943 (INN 'retatrutide'); first-in-class unimolecular triple GIP/GLP-1/glucagon receptor agonist. Discovery-to-proof-of-concept published 2022 (Coskun, Cell Metab; PMID 35985340), phase 1b in 2022 (Urva, Lancet; PMID 36354040), landmark phase 2 obesity trial in 2023 (Jastreboff, NEJM; PMID 37366315) and phase 2 T2D (Rosenstock, Lancet; PMID 37385280). As of mid-2026 it is INVESTIGATIONAL and NOT approved by the FDA, EMA, or other major regulators for any indication. The pivotal phase 3 program includes the TRIUMPH basket trials (TRIUMPH-1 to -4, >5800 participants, covering obesity, obstructive sleep apnea, knee osteoarthritis, and a CVD population; PMID 41090431) and the TRANSCEND T2D program — TRANSCEND-T2D-1 (phase 3 monotherapy) reported positive results in 2026 (PMID 42250575). Because it is unapproved, retatrutide marketed/sold as a 'research chemical' or via compounding/gray-market peptide vendors is unregulated, not quality-controlled, and outside any approved supply chain — a major harm-reduction concern. Legal status varies by jurisdiction but it is not a licensed medicine anywhere as of this writing."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Long-term (multi-year) safety and durability of weight loss, and whether weight regains rapidly on discontinuation as seen with other incretin drugs — phase 3 extension data are still maturing.
  • Cardiovascular outcome data: heart rate rises dose-dependently and the glucagon arm directly stimulates human atrial contractility (PMID 40613938) — whether this translates to net cardiovascular benefit or harm (arrhythmia risk) over years is unresolved; dedicated CVOT data are pending.
  • Whether the glucagon (GCGR) arm causes clinically meaningful long-term effects on hepatic glucose output, blood pressure, or cardiac remodeling at scale, beyond the favorable short-term liver-fat and glycemic results.
  • Retatrutide-specific ADME detail: absolute bioavailability, exact metabolizing pathways, and PK in renal/hepatic impairment were not established in the human reports reviewed — largely inferred from the acylated-peptide class.
  • Precise published per-receptor potency values (cAMP EC50 at GLP-1R vs GIPR vs GCGR) and the degree of signaling bias — qualitatively 'GIPR-skewed balanced agonist,' but the exact numbers need to be read directly from the discovery paper, not assumed.
  • Real-world safety of unregulated gray-market/compounded retatrutide (dose accuracy, purity, contaminants) — entirely uncharacterized and distinct from the trial product.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a how-to-dose guide): (1) Heart rate and blood pressure — dose-dependent HR increase is expected and peaked ~24 wk in trials; persistent tachycardia, palpitations, or new arrhythmia symptoms warrant medical review given the direct cardiac-stimulant glucagon arm. (2) Glycemia — fasting glucose/HbA1c; hypoglycemia risk is low alone but rises if combined with insulin or sulfonylureas. (3) GI tolerance — track nausea/vomiting/diarrhea/constipation, hydration, and signs of dehydration; severe persistent vomiting raises concern for pancreatitis or volume depletion. (4) Pancreatitis red flags — severe persistent abdominal pain radiating to the back (seek care). (5) Gallbladder — right-upper-quadrant pain, especially with rapid large weight loss. (6) Body composition — monitor for lean/muscle loss (grip strength, performance); adequate protein and resistance training are protective. (7) Liver and lipids — LFTs and lipid panel (liver fat improves but baseline/trend matters). (8) Renal function and hydration during heavy GI losses. (9) Injection-site reactions/hypersensitivity. (10) Because non-prescription/gray-market product purity is unverifiable, that itself is the single biggest uncontrolled risk. None of this constitutes dosing advice — only what to watch and when to seek a clinician."

Caveats

(1) Not FDA-approved — investigational; do not present as an available prescription. (2) The headline "burns more calories" fat-loss mechanism (GCGR → energy expenditure) is most rigorously shown in MICE; in humans it is strongly inferred from liver-fat clearance and weight loss but the clean mechanistic decomposition is rodent-grounded — label honestly as Evidence-rodent for the mechanism, Evidence-human for the outcomes. (3) Receptor potency wording matters: in vitro it is BALANCED at GCGR and GLP-1R with MORE GIPR activity (Coskun 2022) — do NOT say it is "strongest at glucagon." (4) Glucagon is normally a sugar-RAISING hormone; the design works only because the GLP-1/GIP arms keep glucose in check — get this direction right or the explanation is misleading. (5) The ~24% figure is Phase 2, 48 weeks, high dose, in people without diabetes — not a universal number; weight loss was smaller in T2D and at lower doses. (6) GI side effects and a small heart-rate rise are real; titration is required. (7) No long-term (multi-year) human safety/CV-outcome data yet. (8) The illicit "research-chemical / gray-market retatrutide" sold to fitness consumers is unregulated, not dose-verified, and outside any of the cited trial conditions — none of the efficacy/safety data transfer to that use.
