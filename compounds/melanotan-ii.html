Melanotan II

Main target: Melanocortin receptors. Backed by human data.

Works in humans?Human evidence
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A lab-made copy of your body's tanning hormone that hits the "darken my skin" switch system-wide — and trips appetite and erection switches in the brain as collateral.

Strong human data 32 citations
Class
Synthetic cyclic peptide; non-selective melanocortin receptor agonist (an α-MSH analogue)
Primary target
Melanocortin receptors (G-protein-coupled receptors): MC1R drives the tanning effect; MC4R (in the brain) drives the appetite and erection effects
Human status
UNAPPROVED and unregulated for the uses people buy it for. Melanotan II itself has NO marketing approval anywhere; it is sold on the grey market as an injectable research peptide, and multiple national health agencies have issued safety warnings. Human data is limited: a small (n=20) double-blind trial supports the erection effect, and human users clearly tan — but rigorous human safety/efficacy data for tanning and weight loss is thin, and long-term safety is essentially unstudied. Do not confuse it with two genuinely approved relatives that share the mechanism: afamelanotide (Scenesse, an implant approved 2014 for the rare light-sensitivity disease erythropoietic protoporphyria) and bremelanotide (Vyleesi, an MC4R agonist approved for hypoactive sexual desire disorder). Those went through trials and manufacturing controls; street Melanotan II did not.

At a glance

  • Sunless tanning / skin darkening (the main reason people use it) — via MC1R on melanocytes
  • Darkening and enlargement of moles and new freckles/nevi; possible melanoma signal (unproven causation)
  • Appetite suppression and weight loss — strong in animals, weak/anecdotal in humans (MC4R)
  • Spontaneous erections and increased libido (MC4R, central) — basis of approved drug bremelanotide
  • Nausea (sometimes severe), facial flushing, spontaneous yawning and stretching — common immediate side effects
  • Rare serious reports: renal (kidney) infarction; risks compounded by unregulated, non-sterile grey-market supply

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps5 human-evidenced 1 preclinical 1 speculative

  1. It mimics your natural tanning hormone (α-MSH) Established

    Melanotan II is a man-made, chemically stabilized cyclic copy of alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (α-MSH), the peptide your body normally releases to tell skin to darken. Because it is cyclized and uses non-natural amino acids, it resists breakdown and is far more potent and longer-lasting than the natural hormone. Crucially, it is NON-selective: it binds the whole melanocortin receptor family (MC1R, MC3R, MC4R, MC5R) rather than just the tanning receptor — which is why it does several unrelated things at once. Corpus MoA: Melanotan II; ChEMBL CHEMBL4297533 (bremelanotide, sister α-MSH analogue, 'binds MC1R, MC3R, MC4R and MC5R'); PMID 28266027 (abstract only)

  2. On skin pigment cells, it flips the MC1R switch Established

    On melanocytes (the pigment-making cells in your skin), Melanotan II binds the melanocortin-1 receptor (MC1R). MC1R is a GPCR — a cell-surface antenna. When the peptide docks, the receptor activates a G-protein (Gs) that switches on the enzyme that makes the second messenger cAMP. This is the exact same switch UV sunlight normally turns on indirectly; the drug shortcuts straight to it. PMID 24807163 (MC1R/cAMP/eumelanin review, abstract only); PMID 31817532 (MC1R agonist as pigmentation enhancer)

  3. cAMP tells the cell to make brown pigment (the tan) Evidence-human

    Rising cAMP turns up the master pigment gene program (via the transcription factor MITF), boosting the pigment-making enzyme tyrosinase. The melanocytes pump out more eumelanin — the dark, photoprotective brown/black pigment — and hand it off to surrounding skin cells. The result is a tan that appears with little or no sun exposure. This is the headline effect users are chasing. PMID 24807163 ('cAMP pathway leading to synthesis of photoprotective eumelanin', abstract only); PMID 28266027 (tan-stimulating effect in human users, abstract only)

  4. In the brain, the SAME drug hits MC4R and kills appetite Evidence-rodent

    Because it is non-selective, Melanotan II also reaches melanocortin-4 receptors (MC4R) on neurons in the hypothalamus — the brain's appetite and energy-balance control center. Activating MC4R there sends an 'I'm full' (anorexigenic) signal, reducing food intake. Cryo-EM structures show exactly how α-MSH-type agonists wedge into MC4R and switch on its Gs protein. This appetite suppression is well-documented in rodents and primates; the human picture is much thinner. Corpus MoA: Melanotan II (PMID , ); PMID 36155088 (MTII reduces feeding, rat); PMID 26733840 (rat); cryo-EM: PMID 34561620, 34433901

  5. MC4R activation in the brain also triggers erections and arousal Evidence-human

    Separately from appetite, MC4R signaling in the central nervous system drives penile erection and sexual desire — independent of the vascular pathway that Viagra-type drugs use. In a small double-blind human trial in men with erectile dysfunction, Melanotan II produced erections in 17 of 20 men and increased reported sexual desire. This is the mechanism that was later refined into the FDA-approved drug bremelanotide (Vyleesi) for low sexual desire. PMID 11035391 (n=20 double-blind crossover human ED trial); corpus MoA: Melanotan II (PMID 17584130, 12851303); ChEMBL CHEMBL4297533 (bremelanotide MC4R agonist)

  6. The 'extra' targets explain the nausea, flushing, and yawning Evidence-human

    Hitting MC3R/MC4R/MC5R and brainstem melanocortin circuits produces the common, immediate side effects users report: nausea (sometimes severe), flushing, spontaneous yawning and stretching, and transient blood-pressure changes. In the human ED trial, ~13% had severe nausea at the higher dose. These are not contamination effects — they are direct, on-target consequences of activating melanocortin receptors the drug was never selective enough to avoid. PMID 11035391 (nausea/yawning, severe nausea ~12.9% at 0.025 mg/kg); PMID 28266027 (safety review, abstract only)

  7. The real-world catch: it darkens moles and is sold unregulated Contested

    Because MC1R drives melanocyte proliferation and pigment, Melanotan II can darken and enlarge existing moles and provoke new ones — and case reports describe melanomas appearing during or shortly after use (a causal link is NOT proven, but the signal is concerning). On top of that, illicit Melanotan II is sold as an unregulated grey-market injectable with no quality control over dose, sterility, or purity; a case report even links it to renal (kidney) infarction. The approved relative afamelanotide is implanted and monitored by clinicians; street Melanotan II is not. PMID 28266027 (mole/nevus changes; 4 melanoma case reports; national safety warnings; abstract only); PMID 31953620 (renal infarction case report)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Melanotan II (MT-II; Ac-Nle4-cyclo[Asp5-His6-D-Phe7-Arg8-Trp9-Lys10]-NH2) is a cyclic lactam-bridged heptapeptide analogue of alpha-MSH (4-10). It is a NON-SELECTIVE, high-potency agonist across the melanocortin receptor family, hitting MC1R (skin pigmentation), MC3R and MC4R (CNS appetite/erection/autonomic), and MC5R (exocrine). Established: it is a SUPERPOTENT agonist relative to native alpha-MSH because the D-Phe7 substitution plus the conformation-constraining lactam ring resist degradation and lock the bioactive beta-turn, dramatically increasing potency and duration (Hadley/Hruby, PMID 9760697; Dorr PMID 8637402). Affinity is broadly in the low-nanomolar to sub-micromolar range across hMCRs with little subtype discrimination — Evidence-in vitro: a 2022 medicinal-chemistry study using MT-II as the reference scaffold reports MT-II binding human MCRs 'from the low nanomolar to the sub-micromolar range' and explicitly calls it 'a potent and unselective agonist of human MCRs' (PMID 35188390). The closely related carboxylate derivative bremelanotide/PT-141 (the same pharmacophore) is curated as binding MC1R/MC3R/MC4R/MC5R, 'most potent against MC1R and MC4R' (ChEMBL CHEMBL4297533 MoA). MT-II behaves as a FULL agonist at MC1R and MC4R in functional cAMP assays. Note: I did not find a single primary source giving clean per-receptor Ki/EC50 values for MT-II that I can quote with confidence, so exact nanomolar figures per subtype are flagged as a gap rather than invented.

Signalling cascade. Melanocortin receptors are class A Gs-coupled GPCRs. Established cascade: agonist binding -> Gs activation -> adenylyl cyclase -> rise in intracellular cAMP -> protein kinase A (PKA). PERIPHERY (MC1R on melanocytes): cAMP/PKA -> CREB phosphorylation -> upregulation of MITF (microphthalmia transcription factor) -> transcription of tyrosinase, TYRP1, DCT -> increased EUMELANIN synthesis and melanosome transfer to keratinocytes = a tan that is independent of UV exposure (mechanistic background: MC1R/cAMP pathway, PMID 24807163; chronic MC1R activation review PMID 39082868). CNS (MC4R/MC3R in hypothalamus and brainstem): receptor-Gs-cAMP signalling in the PVN and arcuate nucleus suppresses food intake/appetite, and melanocortinergic activation of pro-erectile circuitry (PVN, spinal autonomic pathways, interacting with oxytocinergic and dopaminergic tone) drives centrally-mediated penile erection and sexual arousal — shown by c-Fos activation and pseudorabies tracing for the PT-141/MT-II pharmacophore (PMID 12851303; PMID 17584130). Structural basis of MC4R-Gs activation by alpha-MSH analogues is resolved by cryo-EM (PMID 34561620, PMID 34433901). IMPORTANT off-target cascade (Evidence-rodent): some MT-II effects are NOT through melanocortin receptors at all — MT-II directly activates mast cells (partly via MRGPRB2/MRGPRX2) causing histamine release and H1-receptor-mediated hypothermia in mice lacking MC1/3/4/5R (PMID 29812984); this mast-cell/histamine axis likely underlies some flushing and the cationic-peptide pseudo-allergic effects.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection is the route used in the original human trials and overwhelmingly in real-world/underground use (self-injected SC, often abdominal) (PMID 8637402; PMID 30142729). Intranasal was used for the carboxylate analogue PT-141 (PMID 12851303). It is a peptide, so oral dosing is not viable. Underground product is sold as lyophilised powder for reconstitution with bacteriostatic water — purity/identity unverified.
Absorption / bioavailability
Evidence-human is sparse. The cyclic lactam + D-Phe7 makes MT-II markedly more resistant to peptidases than linear alpha-MSH, which is the design rationale for its 'superpotency' and prolonged action (PMID 9760697; PMID 8637402). Quantitative SC bioavailability for MT-II specifically was not found in the sources retrieved — flagged as a gap.
Half-life
No rigorously reported human plasma half-life for MT-II was found in the retrieved literature (a genuine gap). The functional duration is much longer than alpha-MSH (minutes): biological effects after a single SC dose persist for hours — penile erections were observed intermittently for 1-5 hours post-dose (PMID 8637402) and RigiScan effects were tracked over a 6-hour window (PMID 11035391), and the pigmentary effect outlasts dosing by a week or more (PMID 8637402). Any specific 'hours' half-life number circulating in lay sources should be treated as unverified.
Metabolism
Presumed proteolytic/peptidase degradation to inactive amino-acid fragments (typical for cyclic peptides); the lactam bridge and D-amino acid slow this. No specific CYP or named-enzyme pathway is implicated because it is a peptide, not a small molecule. No primary metabolism study for MT-II was retrieved — Speculative on specifics.
Excretion
Not characterised in the retrieved human literature for MT-II. Peptide fragments would be expected renally cleared. A genuine unknown.
Notes
MT-II is NOT a prodrug. Its des-amino/carboxylate relative bremelanotide (PT-141) IS effectively a metabolite-derived analogue of the same scaffold and reached clinical approval (PMID 17584130). CNS penetration: the pro-erectile and appetite effects are CENTRALLY mediated, implying functional access to hypothalamic melanocortin circuits after systemic dosing (PMID 12851303), though the exact blood-brain-barrier transport mechanism for MT-II is not defined. Underground products carry additional PK uncertainty from unknown concentration, contaminants, and non-sterile reconstitution.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

HUMAN (limited, old, small): In the original Phase I tanning study (n=3 men), SC MT-II every other day starting at 0.01 mg/kg and escalating by 0.005 mg/kg increments produced measurable facial/upper-body/buttock pigmentation after only 5 low doses; 0.03 mg/kg produced Grade II somnolence and fatigue in 1 of 2 subjects; mild nausea occurred at most dose levels; recommended single dose for future studies was 0.025 mg/kg/day (PMID 8637402). In the erection RCT (n=20 men with ED, double-blind crossover), 0.025 mg/kg SC produced erections in 17/20 men in the ABSENCE of sexual stimulation, mean ~41 min of RigiScan tip rigidity >80%, and increased sexual desire after 68% of MT-II doses vs 19% placebo (P<0.01); at 0.025 mg/kg, 12.9% had SEVERE nausea (PMID 11035391). So in humans there is a clear dose-dependent overlap: tanning, erection, nausea, yawning and somnolence all appear in the ~0.01-0.03 mg/kg SC range, and the pro-sexual/CNS effects are not separable from tanning at effective doses. RODENT/PRIMATE: MT-II drives food-intake suppression and weight loss (melanocortin appetite pathway) and penile erection via central MC3/MC4R activation (PMID 12851303, PMID 17584130); neurotrophic effect on sciatic nerve regeneration was dose-specific (active at 20 ug/kg/48h but NOT at 2 or 50 ug/kg — a non-monotonic, bell-shaped response) (PMID 12591111). CRITICAL CAVEAT: there is NO modern, properly powered human dose-response curve. Real-world users self-dose unregulated powder with no concentration verification, so the human dose-effect and dose-toxicity relationship for tanning is effectively UNKNOWN beyond the 3-subject and 20-subject studies above.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Melanotan I / afamelanotide (Scenesse)
    same: Both are alpha-MSH analogues that stimulate MC1R and drive eumelanin/tanning; afamelanotide is the linear [Nle4, D-Phe7]-alpha-MSH (NDP-MSH).
    different: Afamelanotide is MC1R-leaning, was rigorously tested, and is the ONLY approved melanocortin tanning-type agent (FDA/EMA for erythropoietic protoporphyria, given as a slow-release implant). MT-II is cyclic, NON-selective (also MC3/MC4/MC5R), so it adds the appetite, erection, nausea and autonomic effects that afamelanotide largely avoids; MT-II is unlicensed/underground (PMID 28266027; PMID 34433901).
  • vs Bremelanotide / PT-141 (Vyleesi)
    same: PT-141 is the carboxylate (des-amino/COOH) derivative of MT-II — essentially the same pharmacophore and non-selective melanocortin agonism (MC1/3/4/5R, most potent at MC1R/MC4R); shares the pro-erectile, pro-sexual-desire and nausea profile.
    different: PT-141 was developed specifically for sexual dysfunction, is FDA-approved (2019, SC autoinjector for hypoactive sexual desire disorder in premenopausal women), and is clinically characterised. It produces much less pigmentation in practice and is regulated/dose-standardised, unlike underground MT-II (PMID 17584130; PMID 12851303; ChEMBL CHEMBL4297533).
  • vs Setmelanotide (Imcivree)
    same: A melanocortin (MC4R) agonist acting on the same Gs-cAMP hypothalamic appetite axis; shares mechanism-class side effects including hyperpigmentation/darkened nevi and spontaneous penile erection from MC4R activity.
    different: Setmelanotide is MC4R-SELECTIVE and FDA-approved for specific monogenic/syndromic obesity (POMC, LEPR, PCSK1 deficiency, Bardet-Biedl); it is an appetite/weight drug, not a tanning agent, and is regulated with defined dosing and monitoring (PMID 34561620; corpus MC4R-agonist meta-analysis PMID 41457234).
  • vs Endogenous alpha-MSH (native ligand)
    same: Same receptor targets and same Gs-cAMP-MITF-tyrosinase / CNS melanocortin signalling.
    different: MT-II is 'superpotent' and long-acting: the lactam ring + D-Phe7 resist enzymatic breakdown and constrain the active conformation, so MT-II is far more potent and durable than alpha-MSH (which is degraded within minutes), explaining sustained systemic effects from low doses (PMID 9760697; PMID 8637402).

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

"Origin: MT-II was designed in the late 1980s-1990s at the University of Arizona (M.E. Hadley, V.J. Hruby, R.T. Dorr, N. Levine) as a cyclic, metabolically stable, 'superpotent' alpha-MSH analogue, conceived as a pharmacological photoprotective tanning/skin-cancer-prevention agent ('the science of the tan without the sun') (PMID 9760697; PMID 8637402). The serendipitous spontaneous-erection finding in early trials spun off the development of PT-141/bremelanotide for sexual dysfunction (PMID 17584130; PMID 12851303). Approvals: MT-II ITSELF was NEVER approved as a drug anywhere. Of the family, only afamelanotide (Melanotan I/NDP-MSH, Scenesse) is approved (FDA 2019, EMA 2014, for erythropoietic protoporphyria) and bremelanotide (Vyleesi, FDA 2019, for HSDD). Setmelanotide (Imcivree) is approved for select monogenic obesity. Legal status: MT-II is an unlicensed, unapproved substance sold illicitly online as a 'tanning peptide' (street name 'Barbie drug'). It is not legally marketable for human use; multiple national medicines regulators (e.g., UK MHRA, and agencies across the EU, Australia, US FDA) have issued warnings against its sale and use, and it is commonly seized/prohibited as an unlicensed medicine — yet remains widely available through grey-market peptide vendors and is injected by users of fitness/tanning subcultures (PMID 19224885; PMID 30142729; PMID 28266027)."

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • No validated human pharmacokinetics: SC bioavailability, plasma half-life, metabolic enzymes and excretion route for MT-II specifically are essentially uncharacterised in the literature — circulating lay half-life figures are unverified.
  • Clean per-receptor binding constants (Ki/EC50 in nM) for MT-II at human MC1R/MC3R/MC4R/MC5R from a single rigorous source were not located; selectivity is described only qualitatively ('low-nM to sub-uM, non-selective').
  • Causality for melanoma is unresolved: case reports link MT-II to melanoma/dysplastic nevi, but whether it is a true carcinogen/promoter or merely a marker of heavy concurrent UV/sunbed exposure ('tanning culture') is unknown — no cohort or controlled long-term safety data exist.
  • Human relevance of the mast-cell/MRGPR/histamine off-target pathway (proven in mice for hypothermia) is untested — it may explain flushing and pseudo-allergic reactions in people but is unconfirmed.
  • Mechanism and frequency of the serious vascular events (renal infarction, rhabdomyolysis, AKI) are uncertain — thrombotic vs direct-toxic contributions are speculative and based on isolated cases.
  • Long-term consequences of chronic non-selective MC1-5R agonism in healthy users (cardiometabolic, immune/mast-cell, melanocytic) are unknown; no chronic-dosing human safety study has ever been done.
  • Real-world exposure is to unregulated product of unknown identity, concentration and purity, so reported harms cannot be cleanly attributed to MT-II versus contaminants or injection practices.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT dosing advice; the safest course is not to use an unlicensed injectable). SKIN: baseline and periodic full-skin dermatologic/dermoscopy exam by a clinician, photographing existing moles; watch any mole for ABCDE change (asymmetry, border, colour, diameter >6mm, evolution), new rapidly appearing (eruptive) nevi, and new mucosal/lip/gingival pigmentation — any changing or new pigmented lesion warrants urgent dermatology review and possible biopsy (PMID 28266027; PMID 31119650; PMID 24355990). UROLOGICAL: any erection lasting beyond ~4 hours or becoming painful is ischaemic priapism — a medical EMERGENCY requiring immediate ER care (PMID 30796078). RENAL/MUSCLE: flank/abdominal pain, dark/reduced urine, nausea+vomiting should prompt evaluation; relevant labs are serum creatinine/eGFR and urea (kidney), creatine kinase and urine myoglobin (rhabdomyolysis), and imaging if infarction is suspected (PMID 31953620). ACUTE REACTION: flushing, severe nausea, yawning/somnolence, and signs of histamine/allergic-type reaction (urticaria, wheeze, faintness) — stop and seek care if severe (PMID 8637402; PMID 11035391; PMID 29812984). INJECTION/PRODUCT: watch injection sites for infection (redness, swelling, abscess); product is unregulated and identity/sterility unverified, which is itself a core risk (PMID 30142729). Sensible adjuncts: rigorous photoprotection (do NOT use as a license for more UV/sunbed exposure, which compounds melanoma risk), and a low threshold for clinician review of any new symptom."

Caveats

CERTAINTY/EVIDENCE GAPS: (1) The local tutor LLM was offline this run, so mechanism wording is grounded directly in the corpus MoA entry, ChEMBL, and PubMed abstracts rather than a synthesized tutor pass. (2) Several key citations (MC1R/cAMP/eumelanin review PMID 24807163; safety review PMID 28266027) were verifiable only at ABSTRACT level — full text not retrieved — so cite them as abstract-grounded, not sentence-level. (3) The appetite/weight-loss mechanism is robustly shown in RODENTS and primates, NOT established in humans for Melanotan II specifically — do not present human weight-loss claims as proven. (4) The melanoma/mole link is a CONTESTED safety signal from case reports; causation is explicitly not proven (PMID 28266027). (5) The cleanest cAMP→MITF→tyrosinase→eumelanin chain detail is standard pigment biology partly inferred from the MC1R review framing; the eumelanin endpoint is directly cited but the intermediate MITF/tyrosinase steps are background-knowledge scaffolding, not separately re-grounded this run. (6) Retraction checks run live: PMID 31817532 and PMID 28266027 both CLEAR (no retraction/EoC). (7) Cryo-EM structures (PMID 34561620, 34433901) are of MC4R with α-MSH/afamelanotide/setmelanotide — they show the receptor-activation mechanism the analogue class uses, not Melanotan II co-crystallized specifically. NEVER frame this as a safe consumer tanning product; it is an unregulated injectable.

Backed by human data.
It mimics your natural tanning hormone (α-MSH). Melanotan II is a man-made, chemically stabilized cyclic copy of alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone (α-MSH), the peptide your body normally releases to tell skin to darken. Because it is cyclized and uses non-natural amino acids, it resists breakdown and is far more potent and longer-lasting than the natural hormone. Crucially, it is NON-selective: it binds the whole melanocortin receptor family (MC1R, MC3R, MC4R, MC5R) rather than just the tanning receptor — which is why it does several unrelated things at once. Corpus MoA: Melanotan II; ChEMBL CHEMBL4297533 (bremelanotide, sister α-MSH analogue, 'binds MC1R, MC3R, MC4R and MC5R'); PMID 28266027 (abstract only)
Sunless tanning / skin darkening (the main reason people use it) — via MC1R on melanocytes
