Library / Anabolic-androgenic steroids

DHB

A non-aromatizing, DHT-like androgen that binds the receptor directly and, molecule-for-molecule, matched testosterone propionate's anabolic/androgenic punch in rats — but the human safety file is essentially empty, and its oral 17-alpha-methylated cousin (M1T) is a documented liver toxin.

Limited human data 15 citations
Class
Synthetic anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS)
Primary target
The androgen receptor (AR), a ligand-activated nuclear transcription factor
Human status
CRITICAL GAP: There are essentially NO controlled human pharmacology, efficacy, or safety studies of injectable DHB/1-testosterone. The mechanistic evidence base is rodent (Hershberger castrated-rat assay) and in vitro (yeast AR transactivation) — Friedel 2006 (PMID 16621347) and Parr 2011 (PMID 21990312). 1-Testosterone is a banned doping agent and former "prohormone" supplement, not a licensed drug, so dosing, kinetics, and long-term outcomes in humans are unknown. Human-relevant harm data come ONLY by class extrapolation from AAS reviews and case reports (liver, lipids, cardiovascular, HPTA suppression), plus the specific finding that the oral 17-alpha-methylated version M1T caused strong liver effects in animals and the M1T-as-supplement era produced real liver-injury cases. Bodybuilding-forum claims (e.g. "DHB builds dry lean mass with no estrogen, painful injections") are anecdote, NOT evidence, and must not be presented as human data.

At a glance

  • Potent direct AR agonism without needing 5-alpha-reductase — in rats an equimolar dose of 1-testosterone matched testosterone propionate at stimulating prostate, seminal vesicle, and levator ani (muscle) growth (Friedel 2006, PMID 16621347; rodent/in vitro)
  • Non-aromatizing: the 5-alpha-reduced A-ring cannot be converted to estrogen by aromatase, so estrogenic effects (gynecomastia, estrogen-driven water retention) are not expected mechanistically — but this is inferred from structure/metabolism, not measured human endocrine data
  • Androgenic effects are NOT spared: it strongly grew prostate and seminal vesicles in rats — it is a typical androgen, NOT a tissue-selective 'SARM-like' agent (Friedel 2006; Parr 2011 explicitly found no selective AR modulator characteristics for M1T)
  • Liver burden: even non-methylated 1-testosterone significantly increased liver weight in rats (Friedel 2006); the oral 17-alpha-methylated form M1T showed strong hepatic activity, much greater after oral dosing (Parr 2011) — consistent with the 17-alpha-alkyl hepatotoxicity class effect
  • Expected class harms by extrapolation: HPTA suppression of natural testosterone, adverse lipid shifts (HDL suppression / cardiovascular risk), and 17-alpha-alkyl cholestatic liver injury for the oral form (Petrovic 2022, PMID 36051334; Nieschlag 2015, PMID 26373946; Santos 2014, PMID 24832723; Abeles 2020, PMID 33214235)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds the androgen receptor directly — no 5-alpha-reductase needed Evidence-in vitro

    Testosterone gets amplified in some tissues by the enzyme 5-alpha-reductase, which converts it to the stronger DHT. DHB skips that step: it is already 5-alpha-reduced, so the molecule out of the vial is the active key. In a yeast androgen-receptor assay, 1-testosterone bound the AR with high selectivity and strongly switched on AR-controlled genes. Friedel 2006, Toxicol Lett, PMID 16621347 (yeast AR transactivation; abstract-grounded, full text not retrieved)

  2. The activated receptor goes to the nucleus and turns on muscle-building genes (the genomic mechanism) Established

    Once DHB occupies the AR, the receptor changes shape, moves into the cell nucleus, and clamps onto DNA at 'androgen-response elements,' acting as a transcription factor that ramps up production of muscle proteins. This receptor-to-nucleus-to-gene route is the shared core mechanism of all anabolic steroids. It is firmly established for the AR in general; for DHB specifically it is demonstrated through AR-dependent reporter-gene activation, not human muscle biopsies. Friedel 2006, PMID 16621347 (AR-dependent transactivation, in vitro); class mechanism per AAS reviews Nieschlag 2015, PMID 26373946

  3. Anabolic AND androgenic in living tissue — roughly as strong as testosterone, not tissue-selective Evidence-rodent

    In castrated rats (the standard Hershberger test), an equimolar dose of 1-testosterone grew the levator ani muscle (anabolic readout) about as much as testosterone propionate — but it equally grew the prostate and seminal vesicles (androgenic readouts). So it is a powerful, run-of-the-mill androgen, not a 'muscle-only' agent. The closely related M1T was explicitly shown to have no selective AR-modulator (SARM-like) behavior. Friedel 2006, PMID 16621347; Parr 2011, Endocrinology, PMID 21990312

  4. Cannot be aromatized to estrogen Evidence-rodent

    Aromatase, the enzyme that converts testosterone into estradiol, needs a particular A-ring shape (the 4-ene/3-keto setup) to work. DHB's A-ring is 5-alpha-reduced with a 1-ene double bond, which aromatase cannot act on — the same reason DHT does not aromatize. Mechanistically this means no estrogen conversion, so estrogen-driven gynecomastia and water retention are not expected. Note: this is a structure- and metabolism-based inference (the parent compound and its metabolites have been characterized), not a measured human estradiol study. Friedel 2006, PMID 16621347 (acts 'even without being metabolised'); 1-testosterone metabolism characterized in Kwok 2013, Drug Test Anal, PMID 22715048

  5. The liver gets hit — and the ORAL 17-alpha-methyl version is the dangerous one Evidence-rodent

    Two layers here. First, even plain injectable 1-testosterone significantly increased liver weight in rats — a signal it stresses the liver. Second, to survive being swallowed, oral versions are 17-alpha-methylated (that is what 'methyl-1-testosterone / M1T' is). That same 17-alpha-alkyl group that lets it survive first-pass metabolism is exactly what makes oral steroids hepatotoxic: M1T showed strong liver activity, far greater after oral dosing. This is the well-known 17-alpha-alkylated cholestatic liver-injury class effect. Friedel 2006, PMID 16621347 (liver weight up); Parr 2011, PMID 21990312 (M1T strong hepatic activity, oral>>sc); class hepatotoxicity Petrovic 2022, PMID 36051334; Abeles 2020, PMID 33214235

  6. Expected downstream hormone and cardiometabolic effects (class extrapolation) Evidence-human

    Like any strong androgen, DHB will signal the brain that there's plenty of androgen around, shutting down the body's own testosterone production (HPTA suppression). And like the AAS class broadly, it is expected to crush HDL ('good') cholesterol and worsen the cardiovascular risk profile. These specific outcomes have not been measured for DHB in humans — they are inferred from how the steroid class behaves. Class effects: Nieschlag 2015, PMID 26373946 (non-reproductive organ harms); Santos 2014, PMID 24832723 (cardiovascular/lipid). NOT measured for DHB specifically.

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. DHB = 1-testosterone = 17β-hydroxy-5α-androst-1-en-3-one. Structurally it is 5α-dihydroboldenone: boldenone (Δ1-testosterone) with the 4,5 double bond reduced to a 5α-saturated A-ring carrying a Δ1 (1,2) double bond. TARGET: the nuclear androgen receptor (AR, NR3C4), acting as a full AGONIST. SELECTIVITY: Friedel et al. (PMID 16621347) showed 1-testosterone 'binds highly selective to the androgen receptor' and is a strong AR transactivator in a yeast AR reporter assay — it is a conventional AR ligand, NOT a SARM (the methylated analog M1T was explicitly shown to have 'no selective AR modulator characteristics', PMID 21990312). [Certainty: Established that AR is the target and DHB is a high-affinity agonist; Evidence-in vitro for the transactivation potency.] NUMBERS — important honesty note: no peer-reviewed Ki/Kd or relative-binding-affinity (RBA) value for DHB itself was located in this search. The widely repeated 'anabolic rating ~200, androgenic ~100' figures trace to bioassay potency claims (e.g. PMID 22715048 cites an 'anabolic potency of 200 vs 26 for testosterone'), NOT to a measured receptor Kd. Like its parent class, DHB is expected to bind AR with high (low-nanomolar) affinity comparable to DHT/testosterone, but a compound-specific affinity constant for DHB is a genuine data gap. As a 5α-reduced steroid with a Δ1 bond, it is NON-AROMATIZABLE (cannot be converted to estradiol by CYP19) and, being already 5α-configured, is not a substrate for further 5α-reductase reduction.

Signalling cascade. Classic genomic AR pathway. (1) Lipophilic DHB diffuses across the plasma membrane and binds cytosolic AR, displacing chaperone heat-shock proteins (HSP90/HSP70/HSP56). (2) Ligand binding drives a conformational change in the ligand-binding domain (helix-12 repositioning) enabling the AR N-terminal/C-terminal (N/C) interaction characteristic of high-affinity agonists (the assay framework in PMID 10077001 shows high-affinity agonists like DHT/testosterone induce this N/C interaction >40-fold). (3) Receptor homodimerization, nuclear translocation, and binding of the DNA-binding domain to androgen-response elements (AREs) in target-gene promoters. (4) Recruitment of coactivators (SRC/p160 family, CBP/p300) and the basal transcription machinery → transcription of anabolic genes. In skeletal muscle this upregulates IGF-1 and AR itself and drives myonuclear accretion and net protein synthesis (Parr 2011 PMID 21990312 documented dose-dependent modulation of IGF-I and AR mRNA in rat gastrocnemius by the DHB analog M1T). Because DHB is non-aromatizing, it contributes no estrogen-receptor signaling — biological effects are AR-mediated only, which is why estrogenic side effects (gynecomastia, estrogen-driven water retention) are largely absent. Rapid non-genomic (membrane/kinase) androgen signaling may also occur as a class effect but is not specifically characterized for DHB. [Certainty: Established for the canonical AR genomic cascade; Evidence-rodent for the specific IGF-1/AR transcriptional readouts via the M1T analog.]

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Almost exclusively non-medical/illicit. Injectable as an esterified oil (most commonly the cypionate ester — FDA forensically confirmed '17β-hydroxy 5α-androst-1-en-3-one cypionate' in an injectable bodybuilding product, PMID 34791803; enanthate and propionate also marketed). Also taken orally either as the 17α-methylated analog methyl-1-testosterone/M1T (orally bioavailable, PMID 21990312) or as oral 'prohormone' precursors (e.g. 1-androsterone/1-DHEA, 1-androstenediol) that convert to 1-testosterone in vivo.
Absorption / bioavailability
Non-methylated DHB (the base/ester) has poor oral bioavailability — like testosterone, the unmodified 17β-OH steroid is largely cleared by first-pass hepatic metabolism, hence the use of injectable esters or oral 17α-alkylated/prohormone work-arounds. The 17α-methyl group on M1T blocks 17β-OH oxidation and confers oral activity (PMID 21990312: M1T active orally, though the parent DHB given orally in the Hershberger assay was comparatively weak by that route). The oral prohormone 1-androsterone is absorbed and converted to 1-testosterone systemically in humans (PMID 21310167). [Certainty: Evidence-human for prohormone conversion; Established by analogy for first-pass limitation of 17β-OH steroids.]
Half-life
No human pharmacokinetic half-life for DHB or its esters was found in this search — a genuine data gap. Apparent duration is governed by the ESTER (depot release): short propionate vs longer enanthate/cypionate, by analogy to other testosterone esters, not by measured DHB data. For free/unmetabolized parent in horse urine after oral dosing, the parent drug was detectable ~30 h (PMID 22715048) — an excretion-detection window, not a plasma half-life. [Certainty: human half-life unknown; the ester-controlled-release principle is Established for AAS esters generally.]
Metabolism
Hepatic. Phase I: reduction of the 3-keto group and Δ1 bond and epimerization yield a family of diol/dione metabolites. In horses (in vitro hepatic microsomes + in vivo), Kwok 2013 (PMID 22715048) identified parent + numerous metabolites including 5α-androst-1-ene-3α,17β-diol, 5α-androstane-3β,17β-diol, epiandrosterone, 5α-androst-1-ene-3,17-dione and several 17-epimers. In humans the corresponding 1-ene diols are the urinary signature (PMID 21310167). Enzymes are the standard hepatic steroid-handling set: 3α/3β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenases (AKR1C family), 17β-HSDs, and CYP-mediated hydroxylations; Phase II glucuronidation/sulfation (UGT/SULT) precedes excretion. The 17α-methyl analog M1T additionally yields 17-epimeric and 18-nor rearrangement metabolites and is more hepatically active (PMID 21990312). [Certainty: Evidence-human/Evidence-animal for the metabolite identities; specific enzyme isoforms are inferred from class biochemistry.]
Excretion
Renal — as glucuronide/sulfate conjugates of the reduced diol metabolites, the basis of anti-doping detection. Parent and metabolites detectable in urine (human and equine). [Certainty: Established for the excretion route.]
Notes
Key PK identity points: (1) NON-AROMATIZABLE — the 5α/Δ1 A-ring cannot be aromatized, so no estradiol is produced and no estrogenic PK pathway exists. (2) Already 5α-reduced — not a 5α-reductase substrate, so unlike testosterone its androgenic action is NOT amplified or modulated by finasteride/dutasteride. (3) The injectable base is essentially a prodrug strategy: the ester is cleaved by plasma/tissue esterases to liberate free 1-testosterone. (4) Oral exposure routes (M1T or 1-DHEA prohormone) shift toxicity toward the liver. No CNS/BBB-specific PK data located.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human dose-response is essentially UNCHARACTERIZED in the peer-reviewed literature — there are no controlled human dosing trials of DHB. What exists: (1) RODENT Hershberger bioassay (castrated rat) — Friedel 2006 (PMID 16621347) showed that an EQUIMOLAR dose of 1-testosterone produced the SAME growth of prostate, seminal vesicles and the (anabolic-marker) levator ani muscle as the reference androgen testosterone propionate, i.e. roughly testosterone-equipotent on a molar basis by injection, while ALSO significantly increasing liver weight (which TP did not). (2) The frequently cited 'anabolic 200 / androgenic 100' relative-potency numbers (e.g. the 'anabolic potency 200 vs 26 for testosterone' figure quoted in PMID 22715048) derive from older myotrophic-androgenic bioassay ratios, not from modern dose-titration or human data, and should be treated as indicative folklore-grade ratios rather than validated human potency. (3) For the oral methyl analog M1T, Parr 2011 (PMID 21990312) found dose-dependent prostate and levator-ani stimulation after subcutaneous dosing in rats, with a dose-dependent hepatic transcriptional signature (tyrosine aminotransferase induction) that was much stronger orally — i.e. as dose/oral exposure rises, hepatic effect rises disproportionately. BOTTOM LINE for the audience: real human dose-effect magnitudes (mg → kg lean mass, mg → ALT/AST, mg → blood-pressure change) are simply not established in controlled studies; all human dosing knowledge is anecdotal. [Certainty: Evidence-rodent for equipotency and dose-dependence; human dose-response = unknown.]

How it compares

  • vs Boldenone (Equipoise, Δ1-testosterone)
    same: DHB is literally the 5α-reduced ('dihydro') form of boldenone — same Δ1 A-ring modification, both AR agonists, both used as long injectable esters.
    different: Boldenone retains the 4,5 double bond and therefore AROMATIZES (to a small extent → estrogenic potential, water retention, possible gyno) and is a 5α-reductase substrate; DHB is 5α-saturated so it does NOT aromatize and is not 5α-reduced. DHB is markedly more potent/androgenic per mg and gives drier, harder gains but a worse lipid hit and (anecdotally) much more injection pain; boldenone is milder and longer-acting.
  • vs Methyl-1-testosterone (M1T)
    same: M1T is the 17α-methylated, orally active version of the SAME 1-testosterone skeleton; both are potent non-aromatizing AR agonists, not SARMs (PMID 21990312).
    different: The 17α-methyl group makes M1T orally bioavailable but substantially more HEPATOTOXIC (strong hepatic TAT induction, oral liver toxicity, PMID 21990312); DHB base is typically injected to avoid first-pass and is comparatively less hepatically aggressive than the methylated oral form (though DHB still raised liver weight, PMID 16621347).
  • vs 5α-dihydrotestosterone (DHT)
    same: Both are non-aromatizing, 5α-reduced 'pure androgens' acting only through AR with no estrogenic activity; both produce dry, androgenic effects and strong HPG suppression.
    different: DHT is rapidly inactivated to weak 3α-diol metabolites and is a poor muscle-builder systemically; DHB's Δ1 bond resists that inactivation, so DHB retains potent ANABOLIC (muscle) activity at the levator ani while DHT does not — DHB behaves like a 'metabolically stable, muscle-active DHT.'
  • vs Testosterone (propionate, the bioassay reference)
    same: By injection, an equimolar dose of DHB matched testosterone propionate for androgenic (prostate/seminal vesicle) and anabolic (levator ani) stimulation in castrated rats (PMID 16621347) — i.e. broadly testosterone-grade potency at the receptor level.
    different: Testosterone aromatizes to estradiol (HDL-protective, water retention, gyno risk) and is 5α-reduced to DHT; DHB does neither. DHB therefore gives no estrogenic benefits/side effects, a harsher lipid/cardiovascular profile, and — unlike testosterone — raised liver weight in the rat assay (a hepatic liability testosterone lacked).

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Anadrol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Two hazards stack. (1) Erythrocytosis: oxymetholone is one of the most potent hematocrit-raising androgens (its FDA anemia use), boosting EPO and suppressing hepcidin, and DHB independently drives the same axis, so combined red-cell mass rises faster than either alone. (2) Hepatic burden: Anadrol is 17-alpha-alkylated and overtly hepatotoxic (cholestasis, enzyme rise, peliosis, tumors), and DHB's ORAL form (M1T) is also 17-alpha-methylated with strong hepatic activity; injectable non-methylated DHB raised liver WEIGHT in rats (an organ-mass signal, not proven cholestatic injury). If DHB is taken as oral M1T alongside Anadrol, the two 17aa orals compound cholestatic liver-injury risk (impaired BSEP/ABCB11 bile transport); injectable DHB carries mainly the hematocrit half of this interaction. Erythropoiesis MoA card + Anadrol monograph (oxymetholone EPO up/hepcidin down); 17aa hepatotoxicity PMID 27372877, PMID 29521706; DHB/M1T hepatic activity Friedel 2006 (PMID 16621347), Parr 2011 (PMID 21990312) Watch: Hematocrit/hemoglobin; ALT/AST/GGT/ALP and bilirubin; jaundice, dark urine, right-upper-quadrant pain. Injectable DHB carries mainly the hematocrit risk; the additive hepatotoxicity applies specifically to the oral M1T form.
  • Boldenone undecylenate compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Both are androgen-receptor agonists that drive erythropoiesis (raise EPO, act on EPOR, suppress hepcidin, raise erythroferrone), so red-cell mass and hematocrit climb from two directions at once. This pathway does NOT require aromatization: aromatase-deficient men still show androgen-driven erythrocytosis (PMID 19159688), so DHB's non-aromatizing nature does not spare it. Boldenone is a classically strong hematocrit-raiser, and DHB is its 5-alpha-reduced sibling (dihydroboldenone) sharing the same delta-1 skeleton, so the two additively push polycythemia. Elevated hematocrit raises blood viscosity and clot/stroke/MI risk. Erythropoiesis & iron MoA card + PMID 24158761 (T raises EPO, lowers hepcidin); PMID 19159688 (erythropoiesis independent of aromatization); DHB = 5-alpha-boldenone per monograph comparison (Friedel 2006, PMID 16621347) Watch: Serial hematocrit/hemoglobin (and ferritin/EPO trend); watch for headaches, hypertension, ruddy complexion, or visual disturbance signaling hyperviscosity.
  • RAD-140 compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both act through the androgen receptor and both suppress HDL cholesterol and the HPTA. RAD-140, as a SARM, raises hepatic lipase and suppresses HDL-C (PMID 35822201) - the same hepatic-lipase route by which non-aromatizing androgens like DHB depress HDL; combined, HDL falls further. Both also drive androgen-mediated negative feedback on GnRH/LH/FSH, so co-use deepens HPTA shutdown and slows recovery. Neither aromatizes, so there is no estrogenic buffer for lipids or libido. SARM hepatic-lipase/HDL suppression PMID 35822201; RAD-140 AR agonism + HPTA suppression (RAD-140 monograph); DHB HDL/HPTA class effect (DHB monograph side_effect_mechanisms) Watch: Fasting lipids (HDL-C/ApoB); LH, FSH, total testosterone and hypogonadal symptoms (libido, mood, testicular atrophy) to gauge combined suppression.
  • Stanozolol compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both are non-aromatizing androgens that suppress HDL cholesterol. Androgen-receptor activation in the liver upregulates hepatic lipase, which lowers HDL-C - a mechanism directly demonstrated for a SARM (hepatic triglyceride lipase rose, HDL-C fell, PMID 35822201) and shared by non-aromatizing AAS - and because neither compound aromatizes, there is no offsetting estrogenic HDL support, so the lipid hit is more severe than with aromatizable androgens. Stacking two such compounds drives HDL down and non-HDL/ApoB up additively. Stanozolol adds its own 17aa hepatotoxicity on top. SARM/hepatic-lipase HDL-suppression PMID 35822201; DHB non-aromatizing worse-lipid mechanism (DHB monograph side_effect_mechanisms); Stanozolol hepatotoxicity PMID 37948000 Watch: Fasting lipid panel (HDL-C, LDL-C, ApoB / non-HDL-C); blood pressure. If DHB is run as oral M1T, also track liver enzymes given the shared 17aa hepatic burden.
  • Human chorionic gonadotropin counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    DHB is a potent androgen that triggers hypothalamic-pituitary negative feedback, shutting down LH/FSH and therefore endogenous and intratesticular testosterone, causing testicular atrophy; and because DHB cannot aromatize, it also removes the estradiol that endogenous testosterone would have supplied. hCG mimics LH at the testicular LHCGR to directly stimulate intratesticular testosterone (and, via aromatization of that testosterone, some estradiol), partially counteracting DHB's suppression of testicular function and the estrogen deficit inherent to a non-aromatizing compound. This mitigates DHB's testicular/HPTA effect; it does not restart the shut-down axis (LH/FSH stay suppressed by androgen feedback). hCG monograph (LHCGR agonist, raises intratesticular T and, via aromatization, E2); DHB HPTA suppression (DHB monograph side_effect_mechanisms HPG entry, PMID 16621347) Watch: Testicular size/consistency, LH/FSH (will stay suppressed by androgen feedback), total and free testosterone, and estradiol; watch for over-stimulation and rising estradiol once intratesticular testosterone is restored.
  • Anastrozole opposes Established
    Anastrozole blocks aromatase (CYP19A1) to lower estradiol. DHB provides no aromatase substrate (it cannot be converted to estrogen), so there is no DHB-derived estrogen for the AI to remove. Running an AI 'to be safe' on a non-aromatizing DHB regimen therefore has nothing to act on except whatever residual estrogen comes from endogenous or co-administered aromatizable androgen - and it can crash that estradiol below the healthy range. Because estrogen has a U-shaped risk curve, over-suppression causes joint pain, low libido, poor mood, worse lipids and bone loss; 'more AI = safer' is mechanistically wrong here. Anastrozole monograph (U-shaped E2 role; bone/joint risk of low E2); DHB non-aromatizable Friedel 2006 (PMID 16621347); AIMSS/low-E2 arthralgia PMID 41506128 Watch: Estradiol (sensitive assay) before and during any AI use; symptoms of crashed estrogen (aching joints, low libido, low mood). On DHB alone an AI is generally unnecessary because there is no substrate to inhibit.
  • Testosterone interacts Evidence-human
    DHB cannot aromatize, so a DHB-only regimen produces essentially no estradiol; because estrogen supports male libido, mood, bone, joint comfort and a favorable lipid contribution, a non-aromatizing androgen alone risks symptomatic low-estrogen. An aromatizable base such as testosterone converts to estradiol via CYP19A1 and supplies the estrogen DHB lacks - the classic rationale for running a non-aromatizing 'dry' compound over a testosterone base. The flip side is a compounded hematocrit rise, since both raise red-cell mass. Testosterone monograph (E2 roles: bone, libido, lipids); DHB non-aromatizing / expected-low E2 (DHB monograph, Friedel 2006 PMID 16621347) Watch: Estradiol (sensitive assay) plus low-estrogen symptoms (joint pain, low libido, flat mood); and hematocrit, since adding testosterone to DHB compounds erythrocytosis rather than relieving it.
  • Finasteride redundant Established
    Finasteride works by blocking 5-alpha-reductase (SRD5A2), the enzyme that converts testosterone to the stronger DHT, to reduce DHT-driven scalp and prostate effects of 5AR-substrate androgens. DHB is already 5-alpha-reduced (it is dihydroboldenone) and is NOT a 5-alpha-reductase substrate, so finasteride has no enzyme step to block in DHB's pathway. Adding finasteride is therefore a pointless/redundant intervention here - it does NOT blunt DHB's androgenic hair-loss, acne, or prostate effects, a common but mechanistically mistaken expectation. (The same limitation applies to dutasteride.) Finasteride monograph (SRD5A2 inhibitor, T to DHT); DHB is 5-alpha-reduced / not a 5AR substrate (DHB monograph pharmacokinetics notes + scalp side_effect_mechanisms; Friedel 2006 PMID 16621347) Watch: Do not rely on finasteride/dutasteride to protect the scalp or prostate while on DHB; track hairline, acne, and prostate/urinary symptoms directly, since 5AR inhibitors will not modify DHB's action.

History & legal status

"Origin: 1-Testosterone was first synthesized/characterized in the early 1960s era of androgen pharmacology (the 1-ene-5α-androstane series). It was NEVER developed or approved as a human or veterinary therapeutic — there is no marketing authorization anywhere. It rose to prominence in the early 2000s US 'prohormone' supplement boom, sold directly (1-testosterone) and as oral precursors (1-androstene-3β-ol-17-one / '1-androsterone' / 1-DHEA, 1-androstenediol) that convert to DHB in the body (human conversion documented in PMID 21310167). Regulatory status: In the United States the Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2004 (effective Jan 2005) added 1-testosterone and many prohormones to Schedule III controlled substances; the Designer Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2014 further closed loopholes and explicitly captured analogs/prohormones — so 1-testosterone and its named precursors are Schedule III (illegal without prescription, and there is no legitimate prescription product). The World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) prohibits 1-testosterone and its metabolites at all times (class S1 anabolic agents), and it is a controlled doping agent in horse racing (PMID 22715048). It remains widely sold on the gray/black market as injectable esters — FDA forensic chemists confirmed a DHB cypionate injectable in a bodybuilding product as recently as 2022 (PMID 34791803). In the EU/UK it is likewise a controlled anabolic substance with no approved product. [Certainty: Established for US Schedule III status, WADA prohibition, and absence of any approval; specific dates from the named statutes.]"

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No compound-specific human AR binding affinity (Ki/Kd or RBA) has been published for DHB — the cited 'anabolic 200 / androgenic 100' ratings come from old bioassays, not a measured receptor constant. What is DHB's actual AR affinity relative to DHT/testosterone?
  • No human pharmacokinetic data exist: plasma half-life of free DHB, ester-specific release kinetics (propionate vs enanthate vs cypionate), bioavailability, and volume of distribution are all unmeasured in humans.
  • No controlled human dose-response for ANY outcome (lean mass, strength, ALT/AST, lipids, blood pressure) — all human dosing knowledge is anecdotal. How much DHB produces how much effect, and where the hepatic/cardiovascular thresholds lie, is unknown.
  • The widely reported severe post-injection pain ('PIP') has no peer-reviewed mechanistic study — is it crystallization, vehicle, ester, or an inflammatory response, and how does it differ by ester/concentration?
  • DHB-specific cardiovascular and hepatic toxicity in humans is unquantified; current claims extrapolate from the AAS class and from rat liver-weight/TAT signals. What is the real magnitude and reversibility of DHB's lipid and cardiac effects in users?
  • Long-term and prostate-cancer-promotion risk specific to DHB is unstudied — its potent rodent prostate stimulation (PMID 16621347) raises a theoretical concern that has no human data behind it.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT dosing advice). LIVER: baseline and periodic ALT, AST, GGT, ALP and bilirubin — watch for cholestatic patterns (raised ALP/bilirubin, jaundice, dark urine, pruritus, right-upper-quadrant pain), which carry higher risk on oral/methylated (M1T) or prohormone routes; note that GGT may stay normal in AAS cholestasis, so don't rely on it alone, and CK can elevate from training and confound transaminases (consider CK to interpret high AST/ALT). LIPIDS/CARDIOVASCULAR: fasting lipid panel (expect HDL-C to fall and LDL-C/ApoB to rise, often sharply because DHB is non-aromatizing); home and clinic blood-pressure monitoring; consider hematocrit/hemoglobin (androgen-driven erythrocytosis raises clot risk); be alert to chest pain, exertional breathlessness, palpitations or reduced exercise tolerance suggesting cardiac strain. ENDOCRINE: total testosterone, LH and FSH (expect suppression), plus signs of hypogonadism (low libido, mood change, testicular atrophy) and fertility concerns; estradiol is expected to be LOW (no aromatization) so estrogenic symptoms are not the watch-point here. RENAL/GENERAL: creatinine/eGFR and overall wellbeing. ANDROGENIC: track acne, scalp hair loss (note 5α-reductase inhibitors will NOT help, as DHB is not a 5α-reductase substrate), and — critically for women — any virilization (voice deepening, clitoromegaly, hirsutism), which can be irreversible and warrants immediate cessation and medical review. Any jaundice, severe abdominal pain, syncope, or cardiac symptoms = stop and seek urgent medical care. [Framing per AAS-class evidence: PMID 36051334, PMID 27372877, PMID 23750872, PMID 35460300; DHB-specific hepatic signal PMID 16621347/21990312.]"

Caveats

HARM-REDUCTION + ACCURACY NOTES: (1) No dosing is provided and none should be inferred — there are no human dosing data for DHB at all. (2) The headline 'as strong as testosterone' and 'doesn't aromatize' claims rest on RODENT (Hershberger) and IN VITRO (yeast AR) data plus structural inference — they have NOT been confirmed in humans; do not upgrade them to human fact. (3) 'Non-aromatizing' does NOT mean 'side-effect-free': it is a full androgen (prostate/androgenic effects intact), it raised liver weight in rats, and as an AAS it is expected to suppress natural testosterone and harm lipids. (4) Distinguish the two forms clearly: injectable DHB is non-methylated; the oral M1T is 17-alpha-methylated and carries the documented hepatotoxic 17-alpha-alkyl risk — the M1T 'prohormone' supplement era is the source of real-world liver-injury concern. (5) Bodybuilding-forum lore (painful injections, 'dry' gains, lean mass with no bloat) is anecdote, not evidence, and is excluded from the mechanism claims above. (6) Mechanistic citations Friedel 2006 (PMID 16621347) and Parr 2011 (PMID 21990312) were checked live and are NOT retracted; both are abstract-grounded (full text not retrieved), so treat effect sizes cautiously.
