The most powerful anabolic hormone you can buy — and the only one on the shelf that can kill you before you finish the workout.

Limited human dataFDA-approved 12 citations
Class
Peptide hormone — anabolic glucose-disposal agent (injectable; rapid/short/intermediate/long-acting formulations and analogs)
Primary target
Insulin receptor (INSR), a receptor tyrosine kinase — drives GLUT4 translocation and the Akt/mTOR nutrient-uptake and anti-catabolic program
Human status
Established, indispensable prescription medicine for type 1 and advanced type 2 diabetes (discovered 1921–22, one of the oldest biologic drugs). It is NOT approved, studied, or safe as a physique/performance drug. In that context it is used off-label/illicitly and is uniquely dangerous: unlike steroids or GH, a single miscalculation can cause fatal hypoglycemia within an hour. Human evidence for its metabolic and anti-catabolic actions is extensive; evidence that it adds muscle in already well-fed, insulin-sensitive healthy lifters is weak-to-absent, while the acute lethality risk is very real and documented in case reports of bodybuilders found comatose (PMID 30527564, PMID 9728265).

At a glance

  • Drives glucose out of the blood and into muscle and fat by moving GLUT4 transporters to the cell surface — the effect that both lowers blood sugar and, in excess, causes lethal hypoglycemia
  • Powerfully anti-catabolic: in humans its dominant protein effect is suppressing muscle protein breakdown rather than directly building new muscle (PMID 10212837)
  • Suppresses fat breakdown (lipolysis) and shuts off the liver's glucose output — pushing the body into a fat-storing, nutrient-hoarding state
  • Activates the Akt/mTOR growth pathway, the same nutrient-sensing hub that resistance training and amino acids feed into
  • Acute overdose risk: neuroglycopenia (brain starved of glucose) begins around 72 mg/dL — BEFORE the shakiness/sweating warning symptoms, which appear only near 49 mg/dL (PMID 3403713). This inverted warning order is why insulin can drop someone into a coma with little warning
  • The GH-stack counterpart: growth hormone raises blood sugar and causes insulin resistance in healthy people (PMID 11914108), which is the pharmacological reason the two are so often stacked — insulin is the drug the library keeps implying but never names

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps4 human / established 3 rodent / preclinical 1 contested / speculative

  1. Insulin binds its receptor and switches on a tyrosine kinase Established

    Injected insulin reaches the bloodstream and binds the insulin receptor (INSR) on muscle, fat, and liver cells. The receptor is a receptor tyrosine kinase: binding makes its two halves clamp together and phosphorylate (tag) each other, switching the receptor 'on'. Analog insulins are engineered to change how fast this happens by altering how the molecule clumps (hexamers) at the injection site — the basis of 'rapid' vs 'long' acting. PMID 30639280

  2. The receptor recruits IRS proteins and fires the PI3K/Akt pathway Evidence-rodent

    The activated receptor tags docking proteins called IRS-1/IRS-2, which recruit the enzyme PI3K. PI3K makes a membrane lipid (PIP3) that pulls in and activates Akt (also called PKB). Akt is the master switch for insulin's metabolic actions. The broad shape of this cascade is textbook, but much of the step-by-step molecular detail cited here is worked out in muscle-cell and rodent models, not directly in humans. PMID 33062046

  3. Akt triggers GLUT4 transporters to surface, pulling glucose into the cell Evidence-rodent

    Akt phosphorylates a brake protein (AS160/TBC1D4). Releasing that brake lets vesicles carrying GLUT4 glucose transporters fuse with the cell surface. Glucose then floods from blood into muscle and fat. The end result — insulin lowering blood glucose — is established in humans, but the AS160/GLUT4 vesicle mechanics themselves are mapped in cell and rodent muscle models. PMID 23274898

  4. Akt/mTOR switches the cell into 'grow and store' mode Evidence-rodent

    Akt also activates mTORC1, the nutrient-sensing hub that ramps up protein-synthesis machinery — the same pathway resistance training and dietary amino acids push on. This is the molecular reason insulin is described as 'anabolic'. Importantly, in humans insulin needs amino acids present to translate this into net muscle protein; insulin alone mostly primes the machinery. The mTOR-priming mechanics are largely rodent/cell-derived; the amino-acid-dependence is the human-anchored part. PMID 11790952

  5. In humans, the dominant muscle effect is BLOCKING breakdown, not adding new protein Evidence-human

    This is the honest, human-specific caveat. Controlled human infusion studies show that at physiological doses insulin's main protein effect is anti-catabolic — it suppresses muscle protein degradation — whereas it stimulates new synthesis much less than IGF-1 does. So insulin is better understood as a powerful 'muscle-sparing' hormone than a 'muscle-building' one in healthy, fed people. PMID 10212837

  6. Insulin shuts down fat burning and liver glucose release Established

    In fat tissue insulin suppresses lipolysis (fat breakdown) and in the liver it shuts off glucose output (gluconeogenesis/glycogenolysis). The combined effect is a whole-body switch toward storing nutrients — glucose, fat, and amino acids all get locked into tissues. This is why insulin misuse is associated with fat gain, and why the liver can't rescue a crashing blood sugar fast enough once too much insulin is on board. PMID 3403713

  7. Overdose path: the brain runs out of fuel BEFORE you feel the warning signs Evidence-human

    The brain has almost no fuel reserve and depends minute-to-minute on blood glucose. Human clamp studies show cognitive impairment (neuroglycopenia) begins around 72 mg/dL, while the classic adrenaline warning symptoms — sweating, shaking, palpitations — only kick in near 49 mg/dL. So thinking and judgment fail first; the person may become confused, then unconscious, before ever recognizing the danger. This inverted order is the core reason exogenous insulin is lethal in a way steroids and GH are not. PMID 3403713

  8. Some analogs also poke the IGF-1 receptor (a theoretical growth/cancer flag) Contested

    Because insulin and IGF-1 receptors are close cousins, certain long-acting analogs (notably glargine) bind the IGF-1 receptor more strongly than native insulin and are more mitogenic (growth-promoting) on cancer cells in the dish. Whether this translates to real-world cancer risk in humans is unresolved and contested — retrospective studies conflict and the one prospective trial found no increase. PMID 29609864

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Insulin binds the extracellular alpha-subunits of the insulin receptor (INSR), a preformed disulfide-linked (alpha2-beta2) receptor tyrosine kinase. Binding induces a conformational change that activates the intracellular beta-subunit kinase domains, causing trans-autophosphorylation on tyrosine residues. INSR exists as A and B isoforms and can form hybrid receptors with the IGF-1 receptor, which is the structural basis for cross-talk with growth signaling. Native human insulin is a 51-amino-acid, two-chain (A and B) peptide that self-associates into zinc-coordinated hexamers; analogs are point-mutated to speed up or slow down dissociation into absorbable monomers.

Signalling cascade. Activated INSR phosphorylates IRS-1/IRS-2 docking proteins -> PI3K generates PIP3 -> PDK1 and mTORC2 activate Akt/PKB. Akt then branches: (a) phosphorylates AS160/TBC1D4 to permit GLUT4 vesicle translocation (glucose uptake); (b) activates mTORC1 (protein synthesis machinery) and inhibits GSK3 (glycogen synthesis); (c) phosphorylates/excludes FOXO transcription factors, suppressing the muscle atrophy program and gluconeogenic genes. A separate Ras-MAPK arm mediates the more mitogenic/growth actions and is where IGF-1R cross-reactivity matters. Note: this branch-by-branch map is chiefly established in cell and rodent models; in humans the confirmed readouts are the net effects (glucose uptake, glycogen storage, anti-catabolism).

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection is standard; intravenous only in medically supervised settings. Oral is not viable (peptide is digested). Absorption from SC depot is the rate-limiting step and varies with formulation, injection site, blood flow, and dose.
Absorption / bioavailability
Governed by how fast the injected hexamers dissociate into monomers. Rapid-acting analogs (lispro/aspart/glulisine) are engineered to stay monomeric and absorb in ~10-20 min; regular human insulin is slower; long-acting analogs (glargine/detemir/degludec) are designed to precipitate or bind albumin for slow, flat release over ~24 h. This variability is itself a hazard: the same 'unit' behaves very differently across products.
Half-life
Circulating (IV) half-life of the insulin molecule is only ~4-6 minutes, but the clinically relevant 'duration of action' is set by the SC absorption depot, ranging from ~3-5 h (rapid) to >24-42 h (degludec).
Metabolism
Cleared largely by receptor-mediated uptake and degradation in the liver (roughly half of endogenous insulin is extracted on first pass) and in the kidney via insulin-degrading enzyme (IDE). Renal impairment prolongs action and raises hypoglycemia risk.
Excretion
Degraded rather than excreted intact; metabolites cleared renally. No meaningful unchanged renal excretion.
Notes
Because the depot, not the molecule, sets duration, a long-acting product that has already been injected cannot be 'turned off' — this is central to why insulin overdose can require many hours of continuous glucose support to survive.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

No dosing is provided here, by design. The essential pharmacodynamic fact for harm reduction is that the dose-response for glucose lowering is steep and open-ended: unlike a receptor that saturates at a safe plateau, more insulin keeps pushing blood glucose lower until the brain fails. There is no built-in ceiling that protects against overdose, no antidote that instantly reverses an already-injected long-acting depot, and inter-individual and day-to-day variability (carbohydrate intake, prior exercise, alcohol, injection site, renal function) is large. Effect is also potentiated by anything that empties glycogen or blocks counterregulation — fasting, endurance exercise, and alcohol all deepen and prolong hypoglycemia. The combination of a steep curve, long uncontrollable depots, and a warning system that fails late (see side effects) is what makes exogenous insulin acutely lethal in non-medical use.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Anabolic-androgenic steroids (e.g., testosterone)
    same: Both are used illicitly to gain muscle and both work partly through nutrient-partitioning/anti-catabolic effects.
    different: Steroids act via a nuclear hormone receptor over days-weeks and rarely cause acute death; insulin acts within minutes on blood glucose and can cause fatal hypoglycemia the same hour. Insulin's acute lethality is categorically higher and faster.
  • vs Growth hormone (GH)
    same: Both are peptide hormones used in physique stacks and both feed anabolic/nutrient pathways.
    different: GH RAISES blood sugar and induces insulin resistance in healthy subjects (PMID 11914108); insulin LOWERS it. This is the pharmacological reason the two are stacked — and why the library keeps invoking 'GH-induced insulin resistance' but needs insulin profiled as the counterpart. GH's harms are chronic (IGF-1-driven growth, acromegaly-type changes); insulin's headline harm is acute and immediate.
  • vs IGF-1 (its receptor cousin)
    same: Closely related receptors and overlapping PI3K/Akt signaling; both promote growth.
    different: IGF-1 predominantly stimulates new protein SYNTHESIS; insulin predominantly SUPPRESSES protein breakdown at physiologic doses (PMID 10212837). IGF-1 is more mitogenic; insulin is more metabolic/glucose-lowering.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Amycretin adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Amycretin's GLP-1R arm lowers glucose (glucose-dependent, so low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk alone) and its amylin/calcitonin arm suppresses prandial glucagon, which can blunt counter-regulation. Added to exogenous insulin, which lowers glucose independent of glucose level, the glucose-lowering effects stack and hypoglycemia risk rises. Amycretin also slows gastric emptying, delaying meal-carbohydrate absorption so insulin action can mismatch food timing, further raising hypo risk. amycretin.json (glucose-dependent insulin, glucagon suppression, slowed emptying; hypoglycemia risk rises with insulin); insulin-exogenous.json (glucose-independent glucose lowering; hypoglycemia) Watch: Watch for hypoglycemia symptoms (shakiness, sweating, confusion); monitor blood glucose closely, especially around meals given delayed gastric emptying. Harm-reduction only.
  • Anadrol adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    Anadrol improved insulin sensitivity in hemodialysis patients (HOMA-IR fell alongside lean-mass gain), i.e. it tends to LOWER the glucose-lowering work needed from a given insulin amount. Exogenous insulin already drives glucose down and carries acute hypoglycemia risk. Because the androgen's metabolic effect pushes glucose in the SAME downward direction, the two effects ADD - a compounding hypoglycemia hazard where a fixed insulin amount can drop glucose lower than expected. Anadrol monograph key_effects + evidence (HOMA-IR fell / improved insulin sensitivity in dialysis RCT, PMID 19356374); insulin monograph key_effects (glucose disposal via GLUT4 -> acute hypoglycemia risk) Watch: Blood glucose (fasting and symptomatic checks) for hypoglycemia - sweating, tremor, confusion. HbA1c/HOMA-IR to track shifting insulin sensitivity.
  • Cagrilintide adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    Amylin is physiologically co-secreted with insulin and complements it: cagrilintide slows gastric emptying, blunts post-meal glucagon, and reduces food intake, lowering post-prandial glucose. On a fixed exogenous-insulin regimen, this reduced glucose load plus reduced/variable nutrient delivery from delayed emptying compounds hypoglycemia risk and makes meal-time insulin matching harder (a well-documented hazard for amylin analogues plus insulin — pramlintide, the approved first-gen amylin analogue, carries a boxed hypoglycemia warning when combined with insulin). cagrilintide.json (blunts post-meal glucagon, slows emptying; monitoring section); pramlintide class boxed warning (amylin analogue + insulin hypoglycemia) Watch: Monitor blood glucose closely for hypoglycemia (highest risk with insulin or sulfonylureas), especially around meals given variable gastric emptying. Watch for symptomatic lows as weight loss improves insulin sensitivity.
  • Clenbuterol adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Both drive potassium out of the blood into cells, so their hypokalemia is additive. Clenbuterol does this via beta-2/cAMP/PKA activation of the Na/K-ATPase; insulin does it by directly stimulating the same Na/K-ATPase and cellular glucose/K+ uptake (both are, in fact, used clinically to treat hyperkalemia). Layering insulin's intracellular potassium shift on top of clenbuterol's can produce deeper hypokalemia, which itself worsens arrhythmia risk from clenbuterol's adrenergic cardiac stimulation. There is also an opposing glucose axis worth noting (acute clenbuterol raises glucose via glycogenolysis/gluconeogenesis while insulin lowers it), making blood-glucose swings less predictable when both are present. clenbuterol.json (beta-2/cAMP Na/K-ATPase -> hypokalemia; acute hyperglycemia, PMID 31887249); insulin monograph (Na/K-ATPase K+ shift, glucose lowering, GLUT4) Watch: Serum potassium is the key shared watchpoint (additive hypokalemia); also blood glucose (opposing effects make it swing), and heart rate/ECG given hypokalemia plus adrenergic drive raises arrhythmia risk. Hypoglycemia warning signs from insulin can be blunted/confused by clenbuterol's tremor and palpitations.
  • DNP adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    DNP creates a state of high glucose demand and impaired oxidative ATP production: with oxidative phosphorylation uncoupled, cells fall back on glycolysis and burn through glucose, which is why glucose supplementation is a rational supportive measure in DNP poisoning (DNP monograph pharmacology cascade + Lindeman 2026). Insulin independently drives glucose out of the blood into cells via GLUT4 and carries an open-ended, ceiling-less hypoglycaemia risk. Layering insulin's glucose-lowering on top of DNP's hypermetabolic glucose consumption can produce abrupt, severe hypoglycaemia - and the danger is amplified because hypoglycaemic sweating/tremor/tachycardia/confusion are nearly identical to early DNP toxicity, so a crashing blood sugar can be misread as (or hidden by) DNP toxicity, and vice versa. Both agents also drive potassium into cells (insulin via Na+/K+-ATPase), a shared additional electrolyte effect early on. DNP monograph (glucose supplementation as supportive care; glycolytic shift; hypermetabolic glucose demand) + Lindeman 2026 PMID 41487961; Insulin monograph (GLUT4, steep open-ended hypoglycaemia, late-arriving warning symptoms; PMID 3403713) Watch: Blood glucose (frequent objective checks - insulin's warning symptoms lag brain impairment, so 'feeling fine' is unreliable), and awareness that sweating, tremor, palpitations and confusion overlap between a hypo and early DNP toxicity. Any of these plus feeling hot is an emergency; carbohydrate/glucose support is part of DNP supportive care. Serum potassium.
  • Dutasteride adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    Dutasteride (dual blockade, not finasteride) reduces whole-body insulin sensitivity and blunts insulin's suppression of non-esterified fatty acids in controlled human clamp studies — i.e. it makes peripheral tissues less responsive to insulin. In someone using exogenous insulin, this added insulin resistance works against insulin's glucose-lowering action, making glucose control more erratic and shifting the effective insulin response. The interaction is metabolic (peripheral insulin signaling), not hormonal-androgenic. PMID 24823464; PMID 26574953 Watch: Blood glucose patterns and HbA1c; watch for a reduced glucose-lowering response; liver fat/enzymes given dutasteride's hepatic-lipid signal.
  • MOTS-c adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-rodent
    MOTS-c increases skeletal-muscle glucose disposal INSULIN-INDEPENDENTLY (via AMPK and CK2 driving GLUT4-mediated uptake), while exogenous insulin drives GLUT4 uptake through the insulin-receptor (INSR) pathway. Because the two act through separate upstream routes onto the same downstream GLUT4 glucose-clearance machinery, their glucose-lowering effects can add together, compounding hypoglycemia risk. (The MOTS-c contribution is demonstrated in rodents/cells; its magnitude in dosed humans is unproven.) PMID 25738459 (Lee 2015 - AMPK/GLUT4 glucose uptake); PMID 39559755 (Kumagai 2024 - CK2-dependent muscle glucose uptake); mots-c.json side_effect_mechanisms (hypoglycemia stacking on insulin) Watch: Blood glucose and hypoglycemia symptoms (sweating, tremor, palpitations, confusion), especially around injection timing; note that insulin's neuroglycopenic symptoms can precede adrenergic warning signs.
  • Metformin adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Both lower blood glucose but by different routes: injected insulin forces glucose into muscle/fat via GLUT4 and shuts off hepatic glucose output, while metformin suppresses hepatic gluconeogenesis and raises insulin sensitivity. Metformin's insulin-sensitizing action makes a given amount of injected insulin act more strongly, and the two glucose-lowering effects stack. Metformin alone almost never causes hypoglycemia (it does not drive insulin secretion), but layered onto exogenous insulin it lowers the amount of insulin needed and widens the window for a dangerous hypoglycemic crash. Insulin's hypoglycemia is uniquely hazardous because neuroglycopenia (cognitive impairment) sets in around 72 mg/dL, ahead of the adrenergic warning symptoms (tremor, sweating) that appear near 49 mg/dL, so a person cannot reliably 'feel' the low coming. metformin.json (glucose-lowering without stimulating insulin secretion; low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk, risk rises when combined with insulin/secretagogues); insulin-exogenous.json PMID 3403713, PMID 10212837 (neuroglycopenia ~72 mg/dL precedes adrenergic warning ~49 mg/dL) Watch: Watch for hypoglycemia signs (tremor, sweating, confusion) but note these warning symptoms may be blunted or arrive late relative to brain impairment; objective capillary/continuous glucose especially around exercise, fasting, alcohol, and any change; keep fast-acting carbohydrate on hand. No dosing guidance.
  • Semaglutide adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    Semaglutide (GLP-1 receptor agonist) lowers glucose via glucose-dependent insulin release, glucagon suppression, and delayed gastric emptying, with intrinsically low hypoglycemia risk in monotherapy because the insulin-secreting effect only fires when glucose is elevated. Added to exogenous insulin, glucose-lowering is additive and that glucose-dependence safeguard is bypassed by the fixed injected insulin dose; delayed gastric emptying and appetite suppression also cut incoming carbohydrate, removing the buffer against an insulin-driven low. semaglutide monograph key_effects / side_effect_mechanisms (glucose-dependent insulin secretion, low hypo risk alone, risk rises with insulin; delayed gastric emptying) Watch: Blood glucose (fingerstick/CGM); additive lowering plus reduced food intake/slowed emptying can produce or deepen lows. Keep fast carbohydrate ready and account for delayed absorption of oral rescue carbs from slowed gastric emptying.
  • Tirzepatide adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    Tirzepatide (GIP/GLP-1 co-agonist) lowers glucose via glucose-dependent insulin secretion, glucagon suppression, delayed gastric emptying and reduced appetite. On its own hypoglycemia risk is low because its insulinotropic effect is glucose-dependent — but that safety brake does NOT apply to injected insulin. Combined, glucose-lowering is additive, and the delayed gastric emptying and reduced carbohydrate intake remove the incoming glucose that would otherwise buffer an insulin-driven low, so hypoglycemia risk rises (clinical guidance calls for insulin dose reduction when the two are combined). tirzepatide monograph key_effects / side_effect_mechanisms (glucose-dependent insulinotropy, low standalone hypo risk, risk rises with exogenous insulin; delayed gastric emptying) Watch: Blood glucose (fingerstick/CGM); expect additive lowering, and account for delayed/erratic gastric emptying and reduced food intake, which make oral carbohydrate rescue slower to absorb. Nausea and appetite loss cutting intake are a setup toward a low.
  • GHRP-2 counteracts ↓ Established (GH-induced insulin resistance in humans; insulin hypoglycemia lethality documented)
    Opposing actions on the glucose axis. GHRP-2 raises the body's own GH, and GH is a counter-regulatory hormone that induces insulin resistance and raises blood glucose (documented for GH in healthy humans). Insulin does the opposite - it drives glucose into cells and lowers blood glucose. So on the glucose axis insulin directly counteracts GHRP-2's GH-driven hyperglycemia. The danger is that this is not a clean cancel-out: layering a hypoglycemia agent onto a hyperglycemia-inducing peptide widens the glucose swing, and insulin is the one agent that can cause fatal hypoglycemia within an hour, with warning symptoms appearing only AFTER cognition (neuroglycopenia) already begins. GHRP-2 monograph side_effect_mechanisms (worsened insulin sensitivity/hyperglycemia, PMID 28400207); Insulin monograph key_effects (GH-induced insulin resistance PMID 11914108; inverted hypoglycemia warning order PMID 3403713) Watch: Frequent blood glucose (both the GH-driven rise and the insulin-driven crash); HbA1c over time. Harm-reduction note: insulin's acute hypoglycemia risk is life-threatening and its warning signs are delayed/inverted - this is a monitoring red flag, not a stacking endorsement.
  • AOD-9604 works against Established
    Direct antagonism at the adipocyte lipolysis switch. AOD-9604 activates hormone-sensitive lipase to break down stored triglyceride. Insulin is the master ANTI-lipolytic hormone: it suppresses HSL and promotes lipogenesis and fat storage — the opposite of AOD's intracellular action. So exogenous insulin blunts the exact enzymatic step AOD-9604 turns on. The monograph highlights that AOD was engineered to avoid GH's insulin-antagonism, so it does not itself perturb insulin signalling — but exogenous insulin still opposes its lipolytic effect and carries its own acute hypoglycemia risk. AOD-9604 monograph (HSL activation / lipolysis; no insulin resistance unlike GH); insulin monograph (INSR → GLUT4/storage, anti-lipolytic, hypoglycemia) Watch: Blood glucose (exogenous insulin's acute hypoglycemia risk is the dominant hazard); recognise the two agents pull adipose metabolism in opposite directions (storage vs release).
  • CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin works against Evidence-human
    Growth hormone is a direct counter-regulatory ('diabetogenic') hormone: the sustained GH/IGF-1 rise this stack produces drives lipolysis, hepatic gluconeogenesis and post-receptor insulin resistance in muscle and liver, pushing glucose UP. Exogenous insulin pushes glucose DOWN. The two forces oppose each other at the level of glucose homeostasis, so GH secretagogue exposure raises the insulin requirement while making blood-sugar control less predictable, and any over-correction of insulin risks hypoglycemia. This is a GH-class effect, not specific to the peptides. PMID 16352683 (CJC-1295-DAC raises GH/IGF-1, monograph side_effect 'Insulin resistance'); GH-class insulin resistance is well established in humans Watch: Fasting glucose, fasting insulin and HbA1c; watch for rising glucose or worsening insulin resistance with sustained (DAC) exposure, and for signs of hypoglycemia if insulin is present (shakiness, sweating, confusion).
  • Cardarine works against Speculative
    These pull muscle fuel handling in opposite directions. Insulin's core action is to drive glucose INTO muscle/fat via GLUT4 translocation and to suppress lipolysis (a fat-storing, nutrient-hoarding state). Cardarine's PPARδ program does the reverse at the fuel-selection level — it reprograms muscle to prefer fatty acids over glucose (glucose-sparing). The net metabolic picture is unpredictable: Cardarine's glucose-sparing fuel switch could blunt the substrate side of insulin's effect, while insulin still forces glucose uptake — a theoretical mismatch layered on top of insulin's already narrow margin for acute hypoglycemia. No data exist on this specific pairing. Cardarine glucose→fat fuel switch / glucose-sparing: monograph mechanism_steps 2 (Dressel 2003 PMID 14525954; Risérus 2008 PMID 18024853). Insulin GLUT4 glucose disposal, lipolysis suppression, and acute hypoglycemia risk: insulin-exogenous monograph. Watch: Frequent blood-glucose monitoring for hypo- AND hyper-glycemia; be alert to hypoglycemia symptoms (sweating, tremor, confusion) — noting neuroglycopenia can begin before the classic warning symptoms. This is a high-risk pairing on glycemic grounds regardless of the fuel-switch logic.
  • GHRP-6 works against Established
    GH released by GHRP-6 is a counter-regulatory, anti-insulin hormone: sustained GH/IGF-1 elevation reduces peripheral insulin sensitivity and raises glucose, directly working against exogenous insulin's glucose-lowering action. The GHS-R1a is also annotated for negative regulation of insulin secretion. The result is a moving insulin-sensitivity target — glucose control becomes harder to predict when GH stimulation and injected insulin are combined. Monograph side-effect table (GH counter-regulatory/anti-insulin; GHSR GO annotation negative regulation of insulin secretion, UniProt Q92847; PMID 9893708) Watch: Fasting glucose, HbA1c, and glucose-response patterns; watch for both rising glucose (GH-driven resistance) and, conversely, hypoglycemia when insulin is present — meaning tighter glucose surveillance is warranted.
  • Hexarelin works against Evidence-human
    Hexarelin pulses growth hormone via GHS-R1a, and GH is a counter-regulatory, anti-insulin hormone: it lowers peripheral insulin sensitivity and can raise fasting glucose. That directly opposes insulin's glucose-lowering action, so the same insulin effect is blunted while background glucose drifts up. This is the recognized metabolic pitfall of the GH-secretagogue class, not unique to hexarelin. Hexarelin monograph side_effect_mechanisms (GH-driven insulin resistance, class-level, PMID 28400207); corpus on GH counter-regulation of insulin Watch: Fasting glucose, HbA1c and fasting insulin; watch for creeping hyperglycemia. Because GH raises glucose while insulin lowers it, the net picture is unpredictable and episodes of both high and low glucose warrant physician review.
  • MK-677 works against Evidence-human
    MK-677 is an oral ghrelin-receptor (GHSR-1a) agonist that sustainedly raises GH and IGF-1. As an on-target consequence of chronic GH elevation it raises fasting glucose and reduces insulin sensitivity (in a 12-month human RCT fasting glucose rose ~5 mg/dL and insulin sensitivity fell). This opposes insulin's glucose-lowering on the shared glucose-disposal axis: the GH-driven resistance blunts insulin's effect and biases glucose upward, so exogenous insulin's net action becomes a moving target against MK-677's chronic hyperglycemic drift. MK-677 monograph side_effect_mechanisms / key_effects (human RCT: raised fasting glucose, reduced insulin sensitivity); insulin monograph key_effects PMID 11914108 Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c (expect fasting glucose to drift up on MK-677); an oral glucose tolerance test if available. Track glucose objectively and treat confusion/drowsiness as a possible insulin low regardless, since warning symptoms lag brain impairment.
  • T3 works against Evidence-human
    T3 raises basal metabolic rate and increases hepatic glucose handling (gluconeogenesis/glycogenolysis via TRβ) and gut glucose absorption, tending to raise blood glucose and worsen glucose tolerance — directly opposing insulin's suppression of hepatic glucose output and its glucose-lowering. The two act antagonistically on the glucose axis, making control erratic: T3's counter-regulatory push can partially mask, then unpredictably unmask, an insulin effect. T3 monograph key_effects / mechanism_steps (metabolic rate, hepatic glucose handling via TRβ); insulin monograph mechanism_step 6 PMID 3403713 (insulin suppresses hepatic glucose output) Watch: Blood glucose (fingerstick/CGM) — expect T3 to push glucose up and destabilize control; do not assume a 'normal' glucose means no active insulin. Note T3's cardiac and (stacked with clenbuterol) hypokalemia context overlaps the same monitoring.
  • Trenbolone works against Contested
    Anabolic-androgenic steroids can impair glucose tolerance and induce insulin resistance at supraphysiologic doses; a case report describes new-onset diabetes (glucose ~1166 mg/dL) in a young bodybuilder using trenbolone plus GH plus testosterone. AAS-driven insulin resistance opposes insulin's glucose-lowering, shifting the effective insulin requirement upward, while insulin's own steep, unforgiving dose-response and lethal-low risk remain — chronic hyperglycemic drift set against acute crash risk. PMID 21558143 (case report: trenbolone + GH + testosterone new-onset diabetes; AAS decrease glucose tolerance / increase insulin resistance — confounded, single case); insulin monograph dose_response Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c for insulin-resistance drift; treat confusion/drowsiness as a possible insulin low regardless of glucose 'feel'. Use objective glucose monitoring, since AAS-induced resistance changes how much insulin effect is present at a given glucose.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Insulin was isolated by Banting, Best, Macleod, and Collip in 1921-22 in Toronto — one of the first life-saving biologic drugs; the 1923 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine went to Banting and Macleod. It transformed type 1 diabetes from a fatal disease into a manageable one. Human insulin (recombinant) replaced animal-sourced insulin in the 1980s, and engineered analogs (lispro 1996 onward; glargine 2000; degludec ~2015) followed. It is a globally approved prescription medicine and appears on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines. It is NOT approved for athletic/physique use; the World Anti-Doping Agency prohibits insulin in sport (peptide-hormone class) except for medically documented diabetes, though current anti-doping tests cannot reliably detect insulin abuse (PMID 38536564). Its misuse as an 'anabolic' entered bodybuilding culture in the 1990s, coinciding with the case reports cited here.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Whether exogenous insulin adds net muscle in healthy, well-fed, insulin-sensitive lifters (versus merely being anti-catabolic in deficit/catabolic states) — controlled human hypertrophy data in this population are essentially absent.
  • Whether analog-specific IGF-1R mitogenicity (glargine) translates into real human cancer risk — retrospective studies conflict and the single prospective trial was null.
  • The true dose-toxicity relationship in non-diabetic, glycogen-loaded athletes, since it varies enormously with food, exercise, alcohol, and renal function and has never been (and ethically cannot be) formally studied.
  • How chronic supraphysiologic insulin exposure interacts with concurrent GH/steroid use on long-term cardiometabolic and cancer risk.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Framed strictly as harm reduction, with NO dosing implied. The dominant monitorable danger is hypoglycemia: because brain impairment (~72 mg/dL) precedes the sweating/shaking warning signs (~49 mg/dL), a person cannot rely on 'feeling' a low — objective blood-glucose measurement (fingerstick or continuous glucose monitor) is the only reliable signal, and confusion or drowsiness should be treated as an emergency, not waited out. Fast-acting oral carbohydrate must be immediately at hand; a glucagon rescue kit and a companion who knows to call emergency services and NOT let the person 'sleep it off' are essential, since long-acting depots can re-crash glucose for many hours after the first rescue. Serum potassium is a second monitorable (insulin drives hypokalemia and arrhythmia). Alcohol, fasting, and endurance exercise all deepen and prolong lows and should be understood as multipliers of risk. Anyone found drowsy/confused/unconscious with possible insulin exposure needs hospital-level continuous glucose support — the single most important message is that this is a medical emergency and the danger long outlasts the initial injection. This library gives no doses because with insulin the dosing information itself is the hazard.

Caveats

Human evidence is lopsided. The end effects — glucose disposal, blood-sugar lowering, hepatic and lipolytic suppression, and the hypoglycemia physiology — are established in humans and rock-solid. But the detailed molecular signaling chain (INSR->IRS->PI3K->Akt->AS160->GLUT4 and Akt->mTOR priming) is largely worked out in cell and rodent muscle models and extrapolated to humans, so those mechanism steps are honestly rodent/cell-tier, not human-proven. The claim that insulin adds muscle in healthy, well-fed, insulin-sensitive lifters is NOT well supported — controlled human data point to an anti-catabolic (breakdown-blocking) effect that requires amino acids to matter, not a standalone muscle-building effect (PMID 10212837). The lethality, by contrast, is not theoretical: bodybuilders have been found comatose from self-injected insulin (PMID 30527564, PMID 9728265). This monograph deliberately gives NO doses, timing, or protocols — with insulin, dosing information is the dangerous part. The IGF-1-receptor/cancer signal is contested and should not be stated as established fact.
