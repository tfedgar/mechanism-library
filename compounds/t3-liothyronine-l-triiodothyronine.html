T3

The active thyroid hormone itself — a master throttle on metabolic rate that works mainly by switching genes on in the cell nucleus.

Limited human data 23 citations
Class
Thyroid hormone (synthetic L-triiodothyronine); nuclear hormone receptor agonist
Primary target
Thyroid hormone receptors TRα and TRβ (nuclear receptors NR1A1/NR1A2) — TRβ dominant in liver/metabolism, TRα dominant in heart
Human status
APPROVED human drug. Liothyronine (synthetic T3) is an FDA/EMA-approved replacement for the body's own active thyroid hormone, used for hypothyroidism (incl. T4/T3 combination debate), myxedema coma, and thyroid suppression testing. The receptor biology and metabolic/cardiac effects are Established from extensive human endocrinology. NOTE for the PED-audience framing: its use as a fat-loss/'cutting' agent in bodybuilding is OFF-LABEL and not an approved indication — that practice rests on physiology, not on trials showing safe cosmetic fat loss in euthyroid people. At a glance

  • Raises basal metabolic rate / oxygen consumption (calorigenesis) [PMID 17127375]
  • Increases heart rate, contractility and cardiac output via TRα in cardiomyocytes (SERCA up, phospholamban down) [PMID 25438971]
  • Drives mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation and thermogenesis, synergizing with the sympathetic nervous system / UCP1 in brown fat [PMID 21196381]
  • Regulates hepatic lipid metabolism (cholesterol, lipogenesis, fatty-acid oxidation) chiefly via TRβ [corpus: Hepatology Communications PMID 39699315]
  • Excess (thyrotoxicosis) → tachycardia, atrial fibrillation, systolic hypertension, and rarely reversible thyrotoxic cardiomyopathy/heart failure [PMID 37313242, 25438971]

Mechanism, step by step

  1. T3 is the active hormone; T4 is a pro-hormone Established

    The thyroid mostly makes T4, which is relatively inactive. Selenium-dependent deiodinase enzymes in liver, kidney and muscle strip one iodine off T4 to make T3 — the form that actually binds the receptor. Taking liothyronine delivers that active hormone directly, bypassing the conversion step. PMID 27226395; PMID 29407431

  2. T3 enters the cell and the nucleus Established

    T3 is carried into cells by specific transporters (e.g. MCT8) and travels to the nucleus where its receptors sit on DNA. PMID 19915227; PMID 29407431

  3. T3 binds nuclear receptors TRα / TRβ Established

    Inside the nucleus T3 binds thyroid hormone receptors (TRα, TRβ). TRβ predominates in the liver and metabolic tissues; TRα predominates in the heart — which is why T3 affects both fat metabolism and heart rate. PMID 27226395; corpus Hepatology Communications PMID 39699315

  4. Activated receptor switches genes on/off Established

    The T3-bound receptor acts as a transcription factor on thyroid-hormone response elements in DNA, turning metabolic and cardiac genes on or off. This 'genomic' action is the main, slower pathway. PMID 27226395; PMID 18344181

  5. Faster non-genomic actions also occur Evidence-rodent

    Beyond gene transcription, T3 has quicker 'non-genomic' effects outside the nucleus — at the cell membrane, cytoplasm and mitochondria — feeding into signaling cascades and mitochondrial gene programs. PMID 34610359; PMID 18344181; PMID 29407431

  6. Mitochondria burn more fuel as heat (calorigenesis) Evidence-human

    The net result is more mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation and heat production, raising oxygen use and basal metabolic rate. In brown fat this works hand-in-hand with the sympathetic nervous system and UCP1 to uncouple energy as heat. PMID 17127375; PMID 21196381

  7. Heart and whole-body consequences Established

    Via TRα the heart speeds up and pumps harder; whole-body metabolic rate climbs. Too much T3 pushes this into tachycardia, atrial fibrillation and, rarely, reversible heart failure — the harm-reduction crux of using T3 for fat loss. PMID 25438971; PMID 37313242

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. T3 (L-3,5,3'-triiodothyronine; liothyronine is the synthetic sodium salt) is the biologically active thyroid hormone and a near-full AGONIST at the two nuclear thyroid hormone receptor families, TRalpha (THRA gene; isoforms TRalpha1, plus the non-T3-binding TRalpha2) and TRbeta (THRB gene; TRbeta1, TRbeta2). [Established] T3 binds these receptors with HIGH affinity (reported dissociation constants in the low-nanomolar/sub-nanomolar range, ~Kd 0.06-0.5 nM depending on isoform/assay), whereas T4 (thyroxine) binds the same receptors with roughly 10-15x LOWER affinity and is largely an inactive prohormone — this affinity gap is why T3, not T4, is the effector hormone. [Evidence-human/Evidence-in vitro; numeric Kd values are assay-dependent and should be treated as order-of-magnitude, not exact — flagged as a key unknown] T3 is essentially NON-selective between TRalpha and TRbeta (this lack of selectivity is the whole rationale for the TRbeta-selective analogue program — sobetirome/GC-1, eprotirome, resmetirom — which try to keep the metabolic (TRbeta) effects while sparing cardiac (TRalpha) thyrotoxicity; Saponaro/Thyroid 2020 PMID 32098589). Tissue distribution of the isoforms drives the phenotype: TRalpha1 predominates in HEART and bone (cardiac chronotropy/inotropy, tachycardia), TRbeta1 predominates in LIVER (LDL/cholesterol lowering) and TRbeta2 in the hypothalamus/pituitary (TSH feedback). [Established]

Signalling cascade. GENOMIC (classical) pathway: free T3 enters the cell via membrane transporters (notably MCT8/SLC16A2 and OATP1C1), reaches the nucleus, and binds TR. Unliganded TR sits on thyroid-response elements (TREs) in target-gene promoters, usually as a heterodimer with the retinoid X receptor (RXR), bound to co-REPRESSOR complexes (NCoR/SMRT + histone deacetylases) that keep genes OFF. T3 binding triggers a conformational change in helix 12 of the receptor's ligand-binding domain that releases corepressors and recruits co-ACTIVATORS (SRC/p160 family, CBP/p300 with histone acetyltransferase activity), switching positively-regulated genes ON. [Established, Brent NEJM 1994 PMID 8078532] Downstream this up-regulates genes for basal metabolic rate and thermogenesis (Na/K-ATPase, uncoupling proteins, mitochondrial biogenesis via PGC1alpha), cardiac genes (alpha-myosin heavy chain, SERCA2/Ca-ATPase, beta1-adrenergic receptors — driving heart rate and contractility), and hepatic lipid genes (LDL receptor, sterol pathway). It also negatively feeds back on TRH (hypothalamus) and TSH-beta (pituitary), suppressing the HPT axis. NON-GENOMIC pathway: T3/T4 also act at the plasma-membrane integrin alphaVbeta3 and via PI3K/Akt and MAPK signalling, producing rapid effects (minutes) on ion channels, angiogenesis and mitochondrial function independent of transcription. [Evidence-in vitro/Evidence-rodent for the integrin arm; less established clinically]

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (Cytomel/Tertroxin/Thybon tablets, liothyronine sodium) is the common form; an IV formulation (Triostat) exists for myxedema coma. ChEMBL lists it as both oral and parenteral, max_phase 4 (approved). [Established, ChEMBL CHEMBL1201119 / parent CHEMBL1544]
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapidly and almost completely absorbed orally — historically cited as ~90-95% bioavailability for liothyronine, substantially higher and faster than T4. Onset of clinical action is within a few hours (the FDA/Cytomel label states activity begins within hours), versus days for T4. Because of fast, near-complete absorption it produces a sharp serum-T3 PEAK ~2-4 hours post-dose — a supraphysiologic spike that does not occur with T4 and is central to its side-effect profile. [Established for the qualitative picture; precise human Fabs/Cmax-vs-dose curves are sparse in the open literature — flagged as a key unknown]
Half-life
Plasma half-life roughly ~1 day (commonly cited ~0.75-1.5 days / ~18-36 h), MUCH shorter than T4 (~7 days). This is why once-daily dosing gives large peak-to-trough swings and why thrice-daily dosing was used in controlled studies (e.g. Celi 2011 dosed T3 three times daily to hold a stable TSH; PMID 21865366). Note community confusion: anecdotal users feel effects for only 4-5 hours and assume a short half-life, but the biochemical elimination half-life is ~1 day — the felt 'wear-off' reflects the peak, not clearance. [Established]
Metabolism
Cleared mainly by hepatic and peripheral metabolism: (1) sequential DEIODINATION by selenocysteine deiodinases — type 1 (DIO1, liver/kidney) and type 3 (DIO3) inactivate T3 to diiodothyronine (T2) and ultimately to iodide; (2) conjugation — sulfation (sulfotransferases) and glucuronidation (UGTs) of the phenolic hydroxyl; (3) deamination/decarboxylation of the side chain (to triiodothyroacetic acid, TRIAC). T3 itself is the product of DIO1/DIO2 5'-deiodination of T4, so deiodinase biology governs both its formation (from T4) and its destruction. DIO2 polymorphisms (e.g. Thr92Ala) are hypothesized to affect local T3 generation and the subjective response to T4-only therapy. [Established for the pathways; DIO2-polymorphism clinical relevance is Contested — Bianco/Kim 2018 PMID 30063552]
Excretion
Iodide liberated by deiodination is largely recycled (re-uptaken by the thyroid) or renally excreted; conjugated metabolites are excreted in bile/urine with some enterohepatic recirculation. Little intact T3 appears in urine. [Established]
Notes
Not a prodrug and not esterified — liothyronine IS the active hormone, in contrast to T4 (a prohormone requiring deiodination). MW free base ~650.98 (sodium salt ~672.96), formula C15H11I3NNaO4; three iodine atoms; chiral L-isomer (the D-isomer is far less active). [ChEMBL CHEMBL1201119] Thyroid hormone crosses the blood-brain barrier via specific transporters (MCT8/OATP1C1), and >99% of circulating T3 is protein-bound (TBG, transthyretin, albumin); only free T3 is active, so binding-protein changes (pregnancy, estrogen, illness) shift kinetics. [Established]

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

In hypothyroid HUMANS, the controlled dose-response anchor is replacement-level dosing titrated to TSH, not a graded curve. In the Celi 2011 NIH crossover (n=14), L-T3 substituted for L-T4 at doses producing EQUIVALENT TSH normalization (target 0.5-1.5 mU/L, given thrice daily) produced measurable extra metabolic effect at the same pituitary-equivalent exposure: ~2 kg weight loss (70.6 to 68.5 kg, P=0.009), ~11% fall in total cholesterol, ~13% fall in LDL, ~18% fall in apolipoprotein B, with NO significant change in heart rate, blood pressure, exercise tolerance or insulin sensitivity at that dose. [Evidence-human, small n; PMID 21865366] So at TSH-matched replacement doses the dominant graded effects are on lipids and weight; cardiac effects emerge as dose climbs above replacement (the subclinical-hyperthyroidism cohort data below show the harm gradient: risk rises as TSH is pushed lower, steepest at TSH <0.10 mU/L). A genuine, dose-titrated human dose-RESPONSE curve for supraphysiologic T3 (as used non-medically for fat loss) does NOT exist in the controlled literature — this is a key human-data gap; the steep peak after each oral dose means effective tissue exposure is poorly captured by trough TSH. In RODENTS, supraphysiologic T3 reliably raises metabolic rate, lipolysis and cardiac mass dose-dependently and 40 ug/kg doses are cardioactive (e.g. post-MI cardioprotection model, PMID 40232385) — but rodent deiodinase/half-life biology differs from humans, so mg/kg extrapolation is invalid. [Evidence-rodent]

How it compares

  • vs Levothyroxine (T4)
    same: Same nuclear targets (TRalpha/TRbeta); T4 is the prohormone that is deiodinated to T3, so the ultimate active species is identical. Both used to treat hypothyroidism.
    different: T4 binds TR ~10-15x more weakly and is essentially inactive until converted; T4 has a long ~7-day half-life giving smooth, stable levels and once-daily dosing without peaks, whereas T3 has a ~1-day half-life with sharp absorption peaks (tremor/palpitation-prone). T4 is first-line replacement; T3 is reserved/niche. Head-to-head, T3 lowers LDL and weight more at TSH-matched doses (Celi 2011) but offers no QoL benefit and adds peak-related risk.
  • vs Desiccated thyroid extract (DTE, e.g. Armour)
    same: Contains both T4 and T3 (animal-derived), so shares T3's active-hormone exposure and a similar biochemical signature (higher Total T3, lower T4) vs T4 monotherapy.
    different: DTE delivers a fixed ~4:1 T4:T3 ratio that is higher in T3 than human physiology (~14:1), risking the same T3-peak issues; meta-analysis (Nassar 2024) shows higher T3/lower T4 but no consistent QoL, heart-rate or lipid advantage over T4 alone. DTE is unstandardized batch-to-batch vs pharmaceutical liothyronine.
  • vs TRbeta-selective thyromimetics (sobetirome/GC-1, eprotirome, resmetirom/MGL-3196, VK2809)
    same: Agonists at the thyroid hormone receptor that reproduce T3's hepatic/metabolic (LDL-lowering, liver-fat-reducing) effects via TRbeta.
    different: Engineered for TRbeta selectivity to capture metabolic benefit while sparing TRalpha-mediated cardiac thyrotoxicity (tachycardia/AF) and bone loss — exactly the toxicities that limit T3. They are non-iodinated, smaller (e.g. GC-1 MW ~328 vs T3 ~651), with different PK (GC-1 ~92% oral bioavailability, ~7 h half-life). Resmetirom is now approved for MASH/NASH; T3 itself is NOT selective. [PMID 32098589]

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Cagrilintide compounds risk ⚠ Speculative
    Cagrilintide markedly slows gastric emptying, which can alter the absorption kinetics of narrow-therapeutic-index oral drugs taken concurrently. This absorption-shift effect is documented for oral thyroid hormone (levothyroxine/T4) with GLP-1-class agents that also delay emptying — altered levothyroxine absorption plus weight-loss-driven reductions in thyroid-hormone requirement have precipitated iatrogenic thyrotoxicosis. Oral T3 (liothyronine) is similarly narrow-margin, so its exposure could shift. NOTE: the direct evidence is for T4, not T3, and for GLP-1 agents, not amylin analogues — so applying it to oral T3 + cagrilintide is a double extrapolation (mechanistically plausible via shared emptying-delay, but not directly demonstrated). PMID 41444586 (GLP-1RA + levothyroxine, AF/stroke); PMID 42109981 (iatrogenic thyrotoxicosis case); PMID 40330819 (delayed-emptying DDI, levothyroxine) — all T4/GLP-1, extrapolated to T3/amylin Watch: Monitor thyroid status (TSH, free T3/T4) and for signs of over-replacement (palpitations, tremor, heat intolerance, atrial fibrillation) when combining. Report new palpitations/arrhythmia.
  • Clenbuterol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    T3 acts through cardiac TRalpha to raise heart rate, contractility and cardiac output (SERCA up, phospholamban down) and up-regulates cardiac beta-adrenergic receptors, sensitizing the heart to catecholamine/beta-2 drive. Clenbuterol supplies exactly that beta-adrenergic drive. So T3 both directly stimulates the heart AND primes it to respond more strongly to clenbuterol, producing additive tachycardia and arrhythmia risk. Thyrotoxic states also cause an intracellular potassium shift, which compounds clenbuterol's beta-2-mediated hypokalemia. Both also raise metabolic rate/thermogenesis, adding heat and cardiac demand. clenbuterol.json (beta-2 cardiac spillover, hypokalemia); t3 monograph (TRalpha cardiac stimulation, tachycardia/AF, PMID 25438971); thyrotoxic intracellular K+ shift/hypokalemia Watch: Resting and exertional heart rate, blood pressure, palpitations; ECG for atrial fibrillation/SVT and ST-T changes; serum potassium (additive hypokalemia risk) and, if clinically indicated, thyroid function. Watch for signs of thyrotoxicosis layered on adrenergic overdrive (tremor, heat intolerance, weakness).
  • DNP compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both drive up whole-body heat production and metabolic rate, but by different routes that add together. DNP mechanically short-circuits the mitochondrial proton gradient so food energy is dumped as raw heat and O2 consumption/basal metabolic rate surge (DNP monograph steps 3-4). T3 acts through nuclear thyroid receptors (TRalpha in heart, TRbeta in metabolic tissue) to raise basal metabolic rate and mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation/thermogenesis. Layering a receptor-driven rise in metabolic rate on top of DNP's unregulated, ceiling-less uncoupling pushes core temperature and cardiac oxygen demand higher than either alone - and DNP's hyperthermia has no off-switch and no antidote (dantrolene fails), so any added heat load moves toward the runaway-uncoupling threshold. T3 also independently raises tachyarrhythmia/atrial-fibrillation risk (its monograph's dominant harm), compounding the tachycardia DNP already causes. DNP monograph steps 3-4 (uncoupling->heat/BMR surge) + comparisons (T3 as co-abused fat-burner, distinct mechanism); T3 monograph (TRalpha cardiac/AF, calorigenesis); Grundlingh 2011 PMID 21739343; DNP-T3 co-use documented in Abdelati 2023 PMID 37703241 Watch: Core temperature (the single most dangerous sign - any sustained rise or feeling intensely hot is a medical emergency; dantrolene does NOT work for DNP hyperthermia), resting heart rate and rhythm/ECG (T3 adds atrial-fibrillation/tachyarrhythmia risk, steepest when TSH is suppressed), heat intolerance and profuse sweating. Free T3/free T4 and TSH characterise the thyroid drive.
  • GHRP-6 compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both stress glucose handling: GHRP-6's GH elevation is diabetogenic (counter-regulatory, lipolytic, anti-insulin), and T3 independently increases hepatic glucose output and can worsen insulin resistance. Stacked, they compound the drift toward hyperglycemia and impaired glucose tolerance. GH-axis manipulation can also shift thyroid hormone economy, so the two additionally interact on the thyroid/metabolic axis. Monograph side-effect table + monitoring (GH insulin resistance; GH-axis shifts thyroid economy); GH counter-regulatory PMID 9893708 Watch: Fasting glucose/HbA1c for compounded insulin resistance; full thyroid panel (TSH, free T4, free T3) since GH stimulation can shift thyroid hormone economy; resting heart rate.
  • CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin potentiates ↑ Evidence-human
    GH increases peripheral deiodinase activity, accelerating conversion of T4 to active T3, so raising GH via this stack already nudges thyroid signaling up. Adding exogenous T3 on top potentiates the calorigenic/metabolic-rate effect (GH + T3 raise resting energy expenditure supra-additively) but also compounds catabolic and thyroid-axis disruption: exogenous T3 suppresses TSH and endogenous T4, and the combination can unmask or worsen thyroid dysregulation. PMID 8636344 (GH-induced peripheral T4->T3 conversion / GH+T3 energy-expenditure synergy in humans); T3 monograph (deiodinase T4->T3 activation; T3 suppresses TSH/thyroid axis) Watch: TSH, free T4 and free T3 (watch for suppressed TSH/low T4 from added T3), heart rate, and signs of hyperthyroidism (tremor, palpitations, heat intolerance).
  • Ephedrine potentiates ↑ Evidence-human
    Thyroid hormone permissively amplifies catecholamine signaling: T3 upregulates beta-adrenergic receptor density and post-receptor Gs/cAMP responsiveness in heart and fat, so the same ephedrine-driven noradrenaline release produces a larger downstream effect. In parallel, T3's calorigenesis (TR-driven mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation and thermogenesis) synergizes with the sympathetic nervous system, layering onto ephedrine's cAMP-driven lipolysis/thermogenesis. Both independently raise heart rate, so chronotropy and the risk of tachyarrhythmia (thyrotoxicosis-like: tachycardia, atrial fibrillation) are potentiated rather than merely additive. The potentiation direction is ephedrine's effect being amplified against a T3-sensitized adrenergic background. PMID 21196381; PMID 25438971 Watch: Heart rate/rhythm and blood pressure (watch for resting tachycardia, atrial fibrillation), tremor, heat intolerance, and TSH/free T3 to gauge thyroid excess; ECG if palpitations or chest pain.
  • GHRP-2 potentiates ↑ Evidence-human for GH-raises-T4-to-T3 conversion and GH+T3 calorigenic synergy; the GHRP-2-specific magnitude is inferred
    GH and thyroid hormone are metabolically intertwined. GHRP-2 raises endogenous GH, and GH increases peripheral T4-to-T3 conversion and energy expenditure - an effect partly reproduced by T3 supplementation, and GH+T3 act synergistically on resting energy expenditure in humans (though GH's calorigenic effect is NOT solely via T4-to-T3 conversion). Adequate thyroid hormone is also permissive for the GH-to-IGF-1 axis. So T3 can amplify the calorigenic/metabolic side of a GHRP-2-driven GH rise; conversely GH can shift TSH/free T4, so thyroid status is worth watching. Corpus PMID 8636344 (GH increases peripheral T4->T3; synergistic REE with T3, but GH calorigenesis not solely via conversion); PMID 10077348 (GH increases T4->T3 conversion); GHRP-2 monograph (raises endogenous GH->IGF-1) Watch: Full thyroid panel (TSH, free T4, free T3) - GH can shift T4/T3 and TSH; resting heart rate and signs of thyrotoxicosis with added T3; T3 is muscle-catabolic at higher exposure, so track lean mass.
  • Insulin opposes Evidence-human
    T3 raises basal metabolic rate and increases hepatic glucose handling (gluconeogenesis/glycogenolysis via TRβ) and gut glucose absorption, tending to raise blood glucose and worsen glucose tolerance — directly opposing insulin's suppression of hepatic glucose output and its glucose-lowering. The two act antagonistically on the glucose axis, making control erratic: T3's counter-regulatory push can partially mask, then unpredictably unmask, an insulin effect. T3 monograph key_effects / mechanism_steps (metabolic rate, hepatic glucose handling via TRβ); insulin monograph mechanism_step 6 PMID 3403713 (insulin suppresses hepatic glucose output) Watch: Blood glucose (fingerstick/CGM) — expect T3 to push glucose up and destabilize control; do not assume a 'normal' glucose means no active insulin. Note T3's cardiac and (stacked with clenbuterol) hypokalemia context overlaps the same monitoring.
  • AOD-9604 interacts Evidence-rodent
    Complementary supply-and-disposal of fatty acids. AOD-9604 tips adipocytes toward releasing free fatty acids and glycerol (HSL up, ACC down); those liberated fatty acids still have to be OXIDISED to be lost, or they are re-esterified back into fat. T3 acts on thyroid receptors (TRα/TRβ) to raise whole-body metabolic rate and hepatic/peripheral fatty-acid oxidation — providing the oxidative sink for the FFAs AOD mobilises. So T3 can enable AOD's mobilised fat to actually be burned rather than recycled; conversely T3 also drives muscle catabolism, a cost AOD does not carry. AOD-9604 monograph (HSL-driven FFA/glycerol release; fat oxidation needed for loss); T3 monograph (TRα/TRβ agonist raising metabolic rate / fatty-acid oxidation) Watch: TSH, free T4/T3, resting heart rate (T3 causes tachycardia, arrhythmia, bone/muscle loss and thyroid-axis suppression). This is a harm-reduction watch, not an endorsement to combine.

History & legal status

L-triiodothyronine was identified as the active thyroid hormone in the early 1950s (Gross & Pitt-Rivers, 1952), shortly after thyroxine. Synthetic liothyronine sodium was introduced clinically in the mid-1950s — ChEMBL records first approval 1956 (CHEMBL1201119). It is an FDA-approved drug (brand Cytomel; IV form Triostat for myxedema coma; UK Tertroxin; Germany Thybon) and carries a black-box-warning flag in ChEMBL (the class warning against using thyroid hormones for weight reduction/obesity, where large doses in euthyroid people are ineffective and can cause serious, even life-threatening, toxicity). It remains a legal prescription medicine worldwide for hypothyroidism and for short-term thyroid-cancer preparation (T3 withdrawal before radioiodine). It is NOT a controlled substance, but its non-medical use for fat loss / physique (the audience context here) is off-label and unmonitored. In the 1950s-60s thyroid hormones were trialed as lipid-lowering agents but abandoned for cardiac/bone/muscle toxicity — the failure that launched the modern TRbeta-selective analogue program (Saponaro/Thyroid 2020, PMID 32098589). [Established / regulatory]

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Precise human TR-binding constants for T3: published Kd/Ki values for TRalpha1 vs TRbeta1 vary by assay and are quoted as order-of-magnitude (~sub-nM to low-nM); a definitive, isoform-resolved human affinity table is not well established.
  • Quantitative human oral PK of liothyronine: rigorous modern Fabs, Cmax-vs-dose and AUC data (especially at supraphysiologic, non-medical doses) are sparse — the ~90% bioavailability and ~1-day half-life figures rest on older/label data.
  • Whether the LDL/weight benefit of T3 at TSH-matched doses (Celi 2011, n=14) translates to any hard-outcome benefit, or is outweighed by peak-driven cardiac/bone risk, has never been tested in a powered long-term trial.
  • Clinical significance of DIO2 (Thr92Ala) and other deiodinase polymorphisms for who benefits from T3-containing therapy remains Contested.
  • Dose-response and safety of supraphysiologic T3 as used recreationally for fat loss: there is essentially NO controlled human dose-response or long-term safety data at those exposures — all such use extrapolates from replacement-dose studies and subclinical-hyperthyroidism cohorts.
  • Reversibility thresholds: how much cumulative T3-excess exposure (and at what TSH-suppression depth/duration) causes irreversible AF or bone loss vs fully recoverable change is not quantified.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring only (no dosing guidance). Bloodwork: TSH plus FREE T3 and free T4 — TSH alone is misleading on T3 because oral liothyronine produces a daily supraphysiologic T3 peak that can drive symptoms even when trough labs look normal; interpret labs relative to time since last dose. A suppressed TSH (especially <0.10 mU/L) is the clearest danger signal — the cohort data tie the steepest AF, fracture and mortality risk to that range (Collet PMID 22529182; Blum PMID 26010634). Cardiac: resting heart rate, palpitations, and an ECG to screen for atrial fibrillation; watch for new irregular pulse, chest pain or exertional intolerance. Bone: in anyone with prolonged exposure, bone-mineral-density (DXA) surveillance given the fracture-risk signal. Symptom watch (signs of over-exposure / thyrotoxicosis): tremor, heat intolerance, sweating, anxiety/insomnia, unintended weight or muscle loss, diarrhea. Special caution / clinician escalation: older age, known cardiovascular disease or arrhythmia, osteoporosis or postmenopausal status, and pregnancy (binding-protein and fetal considerations). Any new AF, chest pain, or syncope is a stop-and-seek-care signal.
