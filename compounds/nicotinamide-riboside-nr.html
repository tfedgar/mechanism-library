Nicotinamide Riboside

A vitamin-B3 NAD+ precursor that reliably raises blood NAD+ in humans — but the human payoff in performance, metabolism, and aging is mostly still missing.

Limited human data 24 citations
Class
NAD+ precursor / pyridine nucleoside vitamin (form of vitamin B3); dietary supplement, not an AAS or a drug
Primary target
Nicotinamide riboside kinases (NRK1/NRK2) — the enzymes that phosphorylate NR to NMN, feeding the NAD+ salvage pool
Human status
Evidence-human for the upstream step only: multiple double-blind RCTs show oral NR is safe/well-tolerated and reliably raises blood NAD+ (e.g., Conze 2019 PMID 31278280; Martens 2018 PMID 29599478; Trammell 2016 PMID 27721479). Downstream functional benefits are largely NOT demonstrated in humans: muscle/metabolic and disease trials are mostly null or mixed (skeletal muscle SR/meta-analysis Prokopidis 2025 PMID 40275690 found no muscle mass/function benefit; Parkinson's NADPARK PMID 35235774; PAD NICE trial PMID 38871717; obese men PMID 29992272; muscle mitochondria unchanged PMID 31710095). Treat "raises NAD+" and "improves health/performance" as two separate claims — only the first is established in humans.

At a glance

  • Established (human): raises circulating/whole-blood NAD+ and NAD+ metabolome dose-dependently
  • Established (human): safe and well-tolerated at studied doses in short-to-medium trials
  • Contested/Evidence-human (mostly null): no consistent benefit on skeletal muscle mass, strength, or mitochondrial function
  • Contested: insulin sensitivity / metabolic endpoints in obese men not improved in RCT
  • Evidence-rodent / in vitro: NAD+ rise proposed to boost sirtuin and mitochondrial activity, reduce inflammation — not yet proven to drive clinical outcomes in humans
  • Caveat: much circulating NR is degraded to nicotinamide before reaching tissues; how much oral NR actually loads target-tissue NAD+ in humans is uncertain

Mechanism, step by step

  1. NR enters cells and is phosphorylated by NRK1/NRK2 Established

    Unlike niacin or nicotinamide, NR is a ready-made nicotinamide-plus-sugar building block. Inside the cell, nicotinamide riboside kinases (NRK1, and NRK2 in muscle) tack a phosphate onto it, turning NR into NMN. This is a Preiss-Handler-independent shortcut into the NAD+ pathway discovered in 2004. PMID 15137942 (Bieganowski & Brenner, Cell 2004); PMID 17914902 (NRK structures, PLoS Biol 2007); PMID 28752046 (NRK1/2 redundancy in muscle, Mol Metab 2017)

  2. NMN is converted to NAD+ Established

    NMN is adenylylated by NMNAT enzymes to make NAD+ itself. So the chain is NR to NMN to NAD+ — NR is two enzymatic steps from the finished coenzyme, which is why it loads the NAD+ pool efficiently in cells that express NRK. PMID 15137942 (Bieganowski & Brenner, Cell 2004); PMID 19355893 (NAD(P) biosynthesis enzymes, Curr Med Chem 2009)

  3. Much oral NR is first broken down to nicotinamide (the bioavailability catch) Evidence-human

    Tracer studies show a large fraction of swallowed NR is cleaved (by enzymes like purine nucleoside phosphorylase) to nicotinamide before or as it reaches tissues, then re-enters NAD+ via the standard salvage route. So 'NR raised my NAD+' does not prove the intact NR molecule reached a given tissue — the rise can come via the common nicotinamide pool. PMID 27721479 (Trammell, Nat Commun 2016, double-isotope tracer in mice+humans); PMID 19001417 (Urh1/PNP salvage, J Biol Chem 2009)

  4. Oral NR reliably raises blood NAD+ in people Established

    This is the best-supported human claim. Across placebo-controlled trials, daily NR raises whole-blood/PBMC NAD+ by roughly a quarter to twofold within days to weeks, dose-dependently, and is well tolerated. This is the upstream pharmacodynamic effect. PMID 31278280 (Conze 2019 RCT, NIAGEN); PMID 29599478 (Martens 2018, Nat Commun); PMID 31412242 (Elhassan 2019, aged muscle NAD+ metabolome)

  5. Raised NAD+ is meant to fuel sirtuins, PARPs and CD38 — and NAD+ falls with age Evidence-rodent

    NAD+ is both a redox cofactor and a substrate consumed by sirtuins (which need NAD+ to deacetylate proteins), PARPs (DNA repair), and CD38. NAD+ levels decline with age partly because consumers like CD38 rise. The theory is that topping up NAD+ re-energizes these pathways (mitochondrial function, DNA repair, anti-inflammatory signaling). PMID 34509469 (Strømland 2021, NAD+ biosynthesis vs consumption in ageing); PMID 31412242 (anti-inflammatory transcriptomic signature in human muscle)

  6. The downstream functional payoff is mostly unproven in humans Contested

    Here is the honest gap: even when NR raises NAD+, randomized human trials have largely failed to show the hoped-for benefits. A 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis found no effect on muscle mass or function; trials in obese men (insulin sensitivity), muscle mitochondria, Parkinson's, and peripheral artery disease were null or mixed. NAD+ going up is not the same as you getting better. PMID 40275690 (Prokopidis 2025 SR/meta-analysis, muscle); PMID 29992272 (Dollerup 2018, obese men); PMID 31710095 (no change in muscle mitochondria); PMID 35235774 (NADPARK, Parkinson's); PMID 38871717 (NICE, peripheral artery disease)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. NR is NOT a classical receptor ligand — it has no agonist/antagonist affinity at any GPCR or nuclear receptor. It is a SUBSTRATE for the salvage enzyme nicotinamide riboside kinase 1/2 (NRK1/NMRK1, NRK2/NMRK2). NRK1 is the rate-limiting, non-redundant gatekeeper for converting exogenous NR (and, via prior extracellular dephosphorylation, NMN) into NMN, then NAD+ (Ratajczak 2016, PMID 27725675 — Evidence-rodent/in vitro: NRK1-knockout cells cannot use NR or NMN, but use of nicotinamide/nicotinic acid is unaffected). The yeast/human NRKs are highly specific for phosphorylating NR and the cancer drug tiazofurin (Bieganowski & Brenner 2004, PMID 15137942; crystal structures Tempel 2007, PMID 17914902). NR also feeds NAD+ via a kinase-independent route: purine nucleoside phosphorylase (PNP) and/or other phosphorylases can cleave NR to nicotinamide (NAM), which then enters the canonical NAMPT salvage pathway. Published enzyme-kinetic Km/kcat for human NRK1 toward NR exist in the structural literature but no Ki/EC50 is meaningful because NR is a metabolic substrate, not an inhibitor. Crucially, NR raises NAAD (nicotinic acid adenine dinucleotide), a deamidated-pathway metabolite once thought off-route — its rise is the most sensitive biomarker of effective NAD+ repletion (Trammell 2016, PMID 27721479).

Signalling cascade. NR (oral) → taken up by cells (and erythrocytes act as a circulating reservoir) → phosphorylated by NRK1/2 to NMN → adenylylated by NMNAT1/2/3 to NAD+. Elevated NAD+ raises the NAD+/NADH ratio and supplies substrate for three families of NAD+-consuming enzymes: (1) SIRTUINS (SIRT1-7) — NAD+-dependent deacylases that deacetylate PGC-1α, FOXO, p53, NF-κB, histones; SIRT1/SIRT3 activation drives mitochondrial biogenesis and oxidative metabolism (Cantó 2012, PMID 22682224, Evidence-rodent). (2) PARPs (PARP1/2) — DNA-damage repair via poly-ADP-ribosylation. (3) CD38/CD157 and SARM1 — NAD+ glycohydrolases (CD38 is the dominant NAD+-consumer that rises with age; SARM1 drives axon degeneration). The headline downstream signature in humans: increased blood/tissue NAD+ metabolome, transcriptional upregulation of mitochondrial/lysosomal/proteasomal genes, and lowered inflammatory cytokines (NADPARK PD trial, PMID 35235774, Evidence-human). Whether modest NAD+ rises actually drive sirtuin flux in vivo in healthy humans remains Contested — most boosting studies show the metabolite rise but inconsistent functional downstream effects.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsule/tablet), typically as nicotinamide riboside chloride (NIAGEN). Most marketed forms are the chloride salt; a triacetylated prodrug-style ester (NRTA) and NR-malate exist but most human data are on NR-Cl.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally bioavailable in mice and humans — the defining feature versus NAD+ itself, which is not absorbed intact (Trammell 2016, PMID 27721479, Evidence-human). Single oral doses of 100, 300 and 1000 mg produce DOSE-DEPENDENT rises in the blood NAD+ metabolome; a single dose raised whole-blood NAD+ up to 2.7-fold in a pilot individual. Much oral NR is cleaved presystemically (gut/liver) to nicotinamide before reaching tissues — i.e. a large fraction reaches the NAD+ pool indirectly as NAM rather than as intact NR. Intact NR appears only transiently in plasma.
Half-life
Intact NR in plasma is short-lived (rises and clears within hours of a dose). The NAD+ metabolome response is far more durable — whole-blood NAD+ elevation builds over ~1-2 weeks of daily dosing and is sustained for the duration of chronic dosing (8 weeks in Conze 2019, PMID 31278280, Evidence-human). No single clean terminal t1/2 is established for the functional NAD+ effect because it reflects a metabolic pool, not the parent molecule.
Metabolism
NR → (NRK1/2) → NMN → (NMNAT1/2/3) → NAD+. Parallel catabolic route: NR → NAM (via phosphorylases) → NAMPT salvage → NAD+. NAD+ is consumed (by sirtuins/PARPs/CD38) back to NAM, which is then methylated by nicotinamide N-methyltransferase (NNMT) to 1-methylnicotinamide (MeNAM/me-2PY/me-4PY) for excretion.
Excretion
Renal — terminal methylated/oxidized metabolites N1-methylnicotinamide, N1-methyl-2-pyridone-5-carboxamide (2-PY) and 4-PY appear in urine. These rise dose-dependently and serve as exposure markers.
Notes
Brain penetration: oral NR (1000 mg/d) significantly but VARIABLY raised cerebral NAD measured by 31P-MRS in Parkinson's patients (NADPARK, PMID 35235774) — so CNS delivery occurs but is inconsistent across individuals. NR did NOT cause niacin-type flushing and did not elevate LDL or disrupt one-carbon/methylation metabolism at up to 1000 mg/d over 8 weeks (Conze 2019, PMID 31278280). NIAGEN (NR-Cl) holds GRAS status.

How it compares

  • vs Nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN)
    same: Both are 'amidated' NAD+ precursors that feed the same NRK1-gated salvage route and raise the NAD+ metabolome; overlapping metabolic effects.
    different: NMN must be DEPHOSPHORYLATED extracellularly to NR for cellular uptake, then re-phosphorylated by NRK1 — i.e. NMN largely acts THROUGH NR (Ratajczak 2016, PMID 27725675). NR has the deeper human PK/safety dataset and clearer oral bioavailability; NMN's putative direct transporter (Slc12a8) is rodent gut data and contested in humans.
  • vs Nicotinic acid (niacin, NA)
    same: NAD+ precursor; raises NAD+ via the Preiss-Handler (deamidated) pathway; cheap, long history.
    different: NA is a GPR109A agonist causing PROSTAGLANDIN-mediated FLUSHING and has lipid-modifying (LDL-lowering, HDL-raising) effects — NR does neither (no flush, no LDL change; Conze 2019, PMID 31278280). NR is NRK1-dependent; NA is not.
  • vs Nicotinamide (NAM, niacinamide)
    same: Both ultimately feed NAD+ via the NAMPT salvage pathway; NR is partly catabolized to NAM en route; no flushing for either.
    different: NAM uses NAMPT (the canonical rate-limiting salvage enzyme) and bypasses NRK1; at high doses NAM can INHIBIT sirtuins/PARPs (product inhibition) and burden methylation, whereas NR is claimed to raise NAD+ without that sirtuin-inhibition liability. NAM is far cheaper and the established vitamin form.
  • vs NRPT (NR + pterostilbene, e.g. Basis)
    same: NR is the NAD+-raising component.
    different: Adds pterostilbene, a putative sirtuin-activating polyphenol; in an AKI safety study NRPT raised NAD+ (PMID 32791973) but a separate combination raised a signal of concern in one ALS trial context — the pterostilbene component changes the risk/benefit beyond NR alone.

History & legal status

NR was discovered as a vitamin precursor and the NRK pathway defined by Bieganowski & Brenner (Dartmouth) in 2004 (PMID 15137942), identifying NR as a natural nutrient in milk that reaches NAD+ via a Preiss-Handler-independent route. Crystal structures of human/yeast NRK followed in 2007 (PMID 17914902). The compound was commercialized as nicotinamide riboside chloride under the brand NIAGEN by ChromaDex (much of the foundational human work was ChromaDex-affiliated — a commercial-interest flag for evidence weighting). Regulatory status: NIAGEN NR-Cl received US FDA GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) notifications and is the subject of New Dietary Ingredient notifications; it is sold as a DIETARY SUPPLEMENT in the US, not an approved drug for any indication. It is NOT a controlled substance and is not banned by WADA. It is legally marketed in many jurisdictions as a supplement; the EU has treated novel NAD-precursor forms under Novel Food rules. No NR product is approved to treat, cure or prevent any disease.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does the reliable rise in blood/tissue NAD+ actually translate into meaningful sirtuin/PARP flux and clinical benefit in humans? Most human trials show the biomarker move but null or marginal functional outcomes (insulin sensitivity, muscle, mitochondria).
  • Tissue-specific and brain delivery: cerebral NAD rise is real but highly VARIABLE between individuals (NADPARK) — what determines responders vs non-responders?
  • Optimal dose for any functional endpoint is unknown; the NAD+ biomarker is dose-responsive but no clinical endpoint has an established dose-response, and higher doses (2000 mg/d) did not outperform.
  • Long-term safety beyond ~1 year and the theoretical cancer/proliferation concern from chronically elevated NAD+ are unresolved in humans.
  • How much benefit (if any) requires a pre-existing NAD+ DEFICIT (aging, disease) versus a healthy baseline — i.e. is NR only useful in the NAD+-depleted?
  • Real-world product variability and degradation: NR-Cl can degrade to nicotinamide on the shelf, so label dose may not equal delivered NR — quantified poorly in independent testing.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a protocol): NR is among the better-tolerated supplements, so monitoring is mostly to confirm absence of harm and detect idiosyncratic responses. Reasonable baseline-and-periodic bloodwork: comprehensive metabolic panel (liver enzymes AST/ALT, renal function/eGFR), fasting glucose/HbA1c and a lipid panel (NR did not raise LDL in trials, but confirm), and CBC. Because NAD+ catabolism consumes methyl groups, homocysteine can be checked if very high doses are used over long periods. Watch for and self-report: GI symptoms (nausea, bloating, diarrhea), unusual fatigue or sleep change, and — importantly — note that NR should NOT cause niacin flushing, so flushing would be unexpected and worth flagging. Anyone with a history of cancer should discuss the theoretical NAD+/proliferation concern with a clinician before chronic use. Track subjective and objective endpoints honestly against the trial reality that biomarker (NAD+) rises are reliable but clinical benefits in healthy people are largely unproven. None of this constitutes dosing guidance — dose selection and any medical decision belong with a qualified clinician.

KEY ACCURACY FLAGS for the writer: (1) Separate 'raises NAD+' (Established, human) from 'improves health/performance' (Contested/unproven, human) — forum hype conflates them. (2) NR is NOT a 17-alpha-alkylated oral AAS; the hepatotoxicity/lipid/AR-genomic framing in the seed prompt does not apply to NR — do not import it. NR's safety profile is benign in trials; no AR mechanism. (3) Several 'NR' trials actually test combination products (NRPT with pterostilbene PMID 32791973; combined metabolic activators PMID 36703196/34180141; NR+CoQ10 PMID 37159264) — do not attribute their results to NR alone. (4) Compound studies (mitochondrial respiration unchanged PMID 31710095) are the honest counterweight to mechanistic enthusiasm. Never write dosing.
