Semaglutide

A lab-made copy of your gut's own "I'm full" hormone, redesigned to last a week instead of minutes, that turns down hunger mainly by talking to your brain.

Human / approvedFDA-approved 36 citations
Class
Long-acting GLP-1 receptor agonist (a GLP-1 mimetic / incretin mimetic)
Primary target
The GLP-1 receptor (GLP1R) — a class B G-protein-coupled receptor
Human status
Semaglutide is FDA/EMA-approved and very heavily studied in humans. KNOWN IN HUMANS: the ~15% weight loss (STEP program), the direct measurements of reduced appetite/energy intake/slowed gastric emptying (Friedrichsen 2021, Blundell 2017), the glucose-lowering with low hypoglycaemia, the ~20% cardiovascular event reduction (SELECT, n>17,000), the lean-mass loss, and the plateau. KNOWN MAINLY IN ANIMALS (extrapolated to humans): the fine-grained neuronal wiring — i.e. that the area postrema is THE primary brain entry point, the specific Gs+Gq cAMP signalling, and the POMC-up/AgRP-down circuit logic — comes largely from mouse studies (e.g. Nature Metabolism 2025). We can see the appetite effect in people but cannot directly map which neurons fire in a living human. CONTESTED: how much of the cardiovascular benefit is independent of weight loss versus a direct vascular/anti-inflammatory effect.

At a glance

  • ~15% average body-weight loss at 68 weeks at the 2.4 mg dose (Evidence-human)
  • Reduced hunger, fewer food cravings, ~35% lower ad-libitum energy intake (Evidence-human)
  • Improved glycaemic control via glucose-dependent insulin up / glucagon down, with low hypoglycaemia risk (Established)
  • Slowed gastric emptying that partially wanes over time; drives most GI side effects (nausea, fullness) (Established)
  • ~20% reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events in obesity without diabetes (SELECT) (Evidence-human)
  • A substantial fraction of weight lost is lean/muscle mass, not only fat — relevant for a physique-focused audience (Evidence-human)
  • Weight loss plateaus rather than continuing indefinitely, due to metabolic counter-regulation (Evidence-human)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It impersonates a natural gut hormone Established

    Your gut normally releases a hormone called GLP-1 after you eat, which signals fullness and tells the pancreas to release insulin. Semaglutide is a re-engineered copy of that hormone built to resist the enzyme (DPP-4) that destroys natural GLP-1 in about 1-2 minutes, and to stick to blood albumin so it survives for roughly a week instead. That long half-life is the whole point of the redesign. PMID 40281304 (Drucker, Nat Rev Drug Discov 2025); PMID 38952487 (Yang, pharmacokinetics systematic review 2024)

  2. It switches on the GLP-1 receptor Established (Gs/cAMP); Evidence-rodent (additional Gq pathway in brain neurons)

    Semaglutide binds and activates the GLP-1 receptor, a class B GPCR. Classically this is described as a Gs-coupled receptor that raises the second messenger cAMP inside the cell, but recent work shows semaglutide also engages Gq signalling in the key brain neurons — so it's not purely one pathway. Think of it as pressing a cell's 'fullness/insulin' button and holding it down. PMID 42174184 (Nature Metabolism 2025, mouse area postrema); PMID 40281304 (Drucker 2025)

  3. Its main weight-loss action is in the brain, not the stomach Evidence-human (appetite/intake reduction) + Evidence-rodent (specific neuron circuitry)

    The receptors that drive weight loss are mostly in the brain — the area postrema/NTS in the brainstem (a region without a tight blood-brain barrier, so the big drug molecule can reach it) and appetite circuits in the hypothalamus. Activating them ramps up 'stop eating' POMC neurons and quiets 'keep eating' AgRP neurons, so you feel full sooner and think about food less. This central appetite effect, not blocking calorie absorption, is the primary driver. PMID 39892489 (central & peripheral pathways review 2025); PMID 39813121 (Jones & Brierley, neurobiology of eating control 2025); PMID 42174184 (Nature Metabolism 2025)

  4. It makes you eat less and prefer less fatty/dense food Evidence-human (randomised mechanistic trials)

    Measured directly in humans: semaglutide 2.4 mg cut energy intake at an all-you-can-eat meal by about 35% versus placebo, lowered hunger and cravings, improved control of eating, and shifted food preference away from high-fat foods. This is the behavioural readout of the brain effect in step 3 — the reduced calorie intake is what produces the weight loss. PMID 33269530 (Friedrichsen, sema 2.4 mg, DOM 2021); PMID 28266779 (Blundell, DOM 2017)

  5. It slows stomach emptying — a real but secondary contributor Established (slowing occurs); Evidence-human (partial tolerance over time)

    Semaglutide slows how fast the stomach empties, so food stays in your gut longer and you feel full earlier in a meal. This adds to the fullness effect and is the main source of the nausea/early-satiety side effects. Important nuance: with long-term dosing the slowing partly fades (tachyphylaxis), so it's a contributor rather than the main long-run mechanism — and it does not block calorie absorption. PMID 39096914 (Lancet Gastroenterol Hepatol 2024, GI effects/mechanisms); PMID 33269530 (Friedrichsen 2021)

  6. On blood sugar it works glucose-dependently (low hypo risk) Established

    In the pancreas it boosts insulin release and suppresses glucagon (the sugar-raising hormone) — but only when blood glucose is high. Because the effect switches off as glucose normalises, semaglutide alone rarely causes dangerous low blood sugar, unlike insulin or sulfonylureas. This is the original diabetes mechanism; for a lean person using it purely for fat loss, the appetite pathway matters far more than this one. PMID 40281304 (Drucker 2025); PMID 33068776 (Nauck, Mol Metab 2021)

  7. Bigger picture: weight comes off, then plateaus — and there's a heart benefit beyond weight Evidence-human (magnitude, plateau, CV benefit); Contested (how much CV benefit is independent of weight loss)

    Net result in trials: about 15% body-weight loss at ~68 weeks (the largest of any single anti-obesity drug to date), but weight plateaus as the body's energy-balance feedback fights back — it does not keep falling forever. Separately, in people with obesity and heart disease but no diabetes, semaglutide cut major cardiovascular events by ~20% (SELECT trial), an effect only partly explained by weight loss, pointing to additional direct anti-inflammatory/vascular actions. A real-world caveat: a meaningful share of the weight lost is lean mass, not just fat. PMID 34942372 (sema for obesity review, ~15% figure); PMID 37952131 (SELECT, NEJM 2023); PMID 38644683 (Hall, weight-loss plateau physiology 2024); PMID 38629387 (lean mass systematic review 2024)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. PRIMARY TARGET: GLP-1 receptor (GLP1R), a class B1 (secretin-family) G-protein-coupled receptor. Semaglutide is a full AGONIST, not antagonist [Established; ChEMBL CHEMBL2108724 -> target CHEMBL1784, action_type AGONIST]. AFFINITY (numbers): GLP-1R receptor-binding affinity = 0.38 +/- 0.06 nM, which is ~3-fold WEAKER than its predecessor liraglutide; the trade-off was deliberate -- the molecule was engineered for much higher serum-ALBUMIN affinity (~5.6x liraglutide) to extend half-life, accepting slightly lower receptor potency [Evidence-human/biochem; Lau et al. J Med Chem 2015, PMID 26308095]. SELECTIVITY: highly selective for GLP1R over the related glucagon receptor and GIP receptor (unlike the dual GIP/GLP-1 agonist tirzepatide, which deliberately hits both). STRUCTURE behind the binding: semaglutide is a 31-amino-acid peptide based on native human GLP-1(7-37) with three modifications -- (1) Aib (alpha-aminoisobutyric acid) substituted at position 8 to block degradation by the enzyme DPP-4; (2) Arg34 substitution; (3) a C18 fatty DI-ACID chain attached via a gamma-Glu/short-PEG (ADO) spacer to Lys26. That fatty-acid 'anchor' is what grabs albumin [Established; Lau 2015 PMID 26308095]. The peptide engages GLP1R by a 'two-domain' mechanism typical of class B GPCRs: the C-terminal half docks into the receptor's large extracellular domain (ECD), then the N-terminal half inserts into the 7-transmembrane bundle to trigger activation -- recent cryo-EM work maps these conformational changes in detail [Evidence-in vitro/structural; Trends Pharmacol Sci 2025, PMID 40221226; JACS 2024 'G-protein-first' activation, PMID 39266057].

Signalling cascade. Once the N-terminus inserts into the transmembrane core, GLP1R changes shape and couples to intracellular G-proteins. CLASSICAL pathway: Gs -> activates adenylyl cyclase -> raises the second messenger cAMP -> activates PKA and the cAMP-sensor Epac2 -> in pancreatic beta-cells this amplifies GLUCOSE-DEPENDENT insulin secretion (it only works when blood glucose is already elevated, which is why GLP-1 agonists rarely cause hypoglycaemia on their own) and suppresses glucagon. NEWLY DEFINED brain pathway: in GLP1R-expressing neurons of the area postrema (the brainstem 'vomiting/satiety' hub and the primary site of semaglutide's weight action), semaglutide engages BOTH Gs AND Gq signalling. The Gs arm drives graded cAMP increases; blocking the cAMP-degrading enzyme PDE4 amplifies and prolongs the response, while disrupting Gs or cAMP in these neurons ABOLISHES semaglutide-induced weight loss -- direct causal evidence that cAMP in hindbrain neurons is the weight-loss engine [Evidence-rodent; Gao et al. Nat Metab 2026, PMID 42174184]. Downstream, this hindbrain activation projects brain-wide to reduce energy intake and shift food preference away from energy-dense food; slowed gastric emptying is a SECONDARY contributor, not the main driver. The receptor also recruits beta-arrestin (relevant to receptor desensitisation and to 'biased agonism' research aimed at separating efficacy from nausea) [Evidence-in vitro; J Endocrinol 2024, PMID 38451873].

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Two formulations: (1) subcutaneous injection ONCE WEEKLY (Ozempic for type 2 diabetes; Wegovy for weight management, up to 2.4 mg/wk) and (2) an ORAL once-daily tablet (Rybelsus) co-formulated with the absorption enhancer SNAC (salcaprozate sodium / sodium N-[8-(2-hydroxybenzoyl)amino]caprylate) [Established; ChEMBL: oral=true, parenteral=true].
Absorption / bioavailability
Subcutaneous bioavailability is high (~89%). Oral bioavailability is very LOW and variable, only ~0.4-1%, because it is a peptide that the gut normally destroys; SNAC works by transiently neutralising stomach acid and fluidising the gastric epithelial membrane to let a small fraction of intact semaglutide cross. Oral absorption is highly sensitive to dosing conditions (must be taken fasting, with little water) [Evidence-human; Pharmaceutics 2025, PMID 42076118; J Control Release 2026, PMID 41672308]. Tmax after s.c. dosing ~24-36 h [textbook PK review].
Half-life
~165-184 hours (~7 days) in humans -- this ~1-week half-life is what enables once-weekly injection [Evidence-human; PMC PK reviews; SUSTAIN-6 describes 'extended half-life of approximately 1 week', PMID 27633186]. In the mini-pig discovery model the i.v. half-life was 46.1 h with a mean residence time of 63.6 h after s.c. dosing [Evidence-animal; Lau 2015, PMID 26308095]. Steady state is reached in ~4-5 weeks, which is why dose-escalation is gradual.
Metabolism
NOT confined to one organ and NOT dependent on cytochrome-P450 (CYP) enzymes. Semaglutide is metabolised throughout the body by proteolytic cleavage of the peptide backbone plus sequential beta-oxidation of the C18 fatty di-acid side chain. The Aib8 substitution specifically protects it from DPP-4. Because it bypasses CYPs, classic CYP-mediated drug-drug interactions are minimal [Established; PMC systematic PK review].
Excretion
Degradation metabolites are eliminated via both urine and faeces; the parent peptide is extensively metabolised before excretion. >99% is bound to plasma albumin in circulation, which both prolongs half-life and limits renal filtration of intact drug [Established; PMC oral-semaglutide PK analysis].
Notes
Not a prodrug in the classic sense (the active molecule is the administered peptide), though the fatty-di-acid 'depot via albumin' behaves like a slow-release reservoir. CNS access: semaglutide is a large, albumin-bound peptide and does NOT freely cross the blood-brain barrier; it is thought to act on circumventricular organs such as the AREA POSTREMA and arcuate nucleus that sit outside the tight BBB, plus vagal afferents -- this is why the brainstem is the key site [Evidence-rodent; Gao 2026, PMID 42174184]. No meaningful CYP interactions; oral form has documented food/water-timing effects on exposure.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

HUMAN dose-response is well characterised for the approved doses. WEIGHT (non-diabetic obesity, STEP 1): once-weekly s.c. 2.4 mg for 68 weeks gave mean -14.9% body weight vs -2.4% placebo (treatment difference -12.4 percentage points; -15.3 kg vs -2.6 kg); 86.4% lost >=5%, 69.1% lost >=10%, 50.5% lost >=15% [Evidence-human; Wilding NEJM 2021, PMID 33567185]. GLYCAEMIA/CV (type 2 diabetes, SUSTAIN-6): lower doses of 0.5 mg and 1.0 mg/wk reduced HbA1c and MACE [Evidence-human; Marso NEJM 2016, PMID 27633186]. There is a clear DOSE-RESPONSE: weight loss is greater at 2.4 mg (Wegovy) than at the 0.5-1.0 mg diabetes doses, but so are GI side effects -- hence mandatory slow up-titration (start ~0.25 mg/wk). The relationship plateaus: weight loss continues over ~65 weeks then stabilises and is sustained up to 4 years (SELECT, mean -10.2% at 208 weeks) [Evidence-human; Nat Med 2024, PMID 38740993]. SPECIES NOTE: the original dose-finding and half-life work was done in mini-pigs and rodents [Lau 2015, PMID 26308095], and the causal weight-loss circuit work is rodent [PMID 42174184]; the precise human dose at which the central hindbrain cAMP circuit saturates is NOT directly measurable in people. (This entry deliberately gives NO individualised dosing protocol -- numbers are population trial results.)

How it compares

  • vs Liraglutide (Victoza/Saxenda) -- the direct predecessor
    same: Both acylated (fatty-acid) GLP-1 analogues, both albumin-binding, both selective GLP1R full agonists with the same core mechanism (glucose-dependent insulin, appetite suppression, slowed gastric emptying); both made by Novo Nordisk
    different: Semaglutide has a C18 di-acid (vs liraglutide's C16) and an Aib8 substitution giving ~7-day half-life and ONCE-WEEKLY injection, vs liraglutide's ~13 h half-life and DAILY dosing. Semaglutide's receptor affinity is ~3x weaker (0.38 nM) but albumin binding is ~5.6x higher. Head-to-head, semaglutide produces substantially greater weight loss [PMID 26308095; PMID 41820778 network meta-analysis]
  • vs Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound)
    same: Once-weekly injectable incretin peptide that also activates GLP1R, suppresses appetite, slows gastric emptying, lowers glucose and weight
    different: Tirzepatide is a DUAL agonist -- it activates BOTH the GIP receptor and GLP1R (semaglutide is GLP1R-only). In head-to-head meta-analyses tirzepatide produces GREATER weight loss than semaglutide (e.g. higher odds of >=10% loss) [PMID 41723034; PMID 40666599; PMID 41820778]. Different signalling 'bias' profile at GLP1R [PMID 38451873]
  • vs Exenatide (Byetta/Bydureon)
    same: GLP-1 receptor agonist for type 2 diabetes; same incretin mechanism
    different: Exenatide is exendin-4-based (derived from Gila monster venom peptide), NOT a human-GLP-1 analogue, and is non-acylated/not albumin-bound (extended-release version uses microspheres). It produces less weight loss than semaglutide; GI side-effect profiles differ (some FAERS data suggest exenatide has fewer of certain GI events except gastroparesis) [PMID 39385875; PMID 38399414]
  • vs Oral semaglutide (Rybelsus) -- same molecule, different delivery
    same: Identical active peptide and identical GLP1R mechanism
    different: Oral form is co-formulated with the absorption enhancer SNAC, taken DAILY, with only ~0.4-1% bioavailability and strict fasting/water requirements; far more exposure variability than the s.c. form [PMID 42076118; PMID 41672308]

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Cagrilintide compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Both delay gastric emptying and act on brainstem emetic/satiety circuits: cagrilintide via amylin/CTR receptors in the area postrema (the chemoreceptor trigger zone) and NTS, semaglutide via GLP-1 receptors on the same NTS/AP hub. Combining them makes the emptying delay additive and stacks nausea, vomiting, constipation and diarrhea (GI adverse events ~80% in REDEFINE 1 vs ~40% placebo; nausea rate rose with the combination, RR ~1.64 vs semaglutide alone). PMID 40544433 (Garvey, NEJM 2025); PMID 41834765 (Ahmed 2026 meta-analysis); PMID 39096914 (Lancet Gastro Hepatol 2024) Watch: Watch GI tolerability (persistent/severe nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation) and signs of dehydration; with prolonged vomiting check renal function (creatinine/eGFR). The markedly delayed emptying is an aspiration-risk consideration to flag before anesthesia/endoscopy.
  • Insulin compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Semaglutide (GLP-1 receptor agonist) lowers glucose via glucose-dependent insulin release, glucagon suppression, and delayed gastric emptying, with intrinsically low hypoglycemia risk in monotherapy because the insulin-secreting effect only fires when glucose is elevated. Added to exogenous insulin, glucose-lowering is additive and that glucose-dependence safeguard is bypassed by the fixed injected insulin dose; delayed gastric emptying and appetite suppression also cut incoming carbohydrate, removing the buffer against an insulin-driven low. semaglutide monograph key_effects / side_effect_mechanisms (glucose-dependent insulin secretion, low hypo risk alone, risk rises with insulin; delayed gastric emptying) Watch: Blood glucose (fingerstick/CGM); additive lowering plus reduced food intake/slowed emptying can produce or deepen lows. Keep fast carbohydrate ready and account for delayed absorption of oral rescue carbs from slowed gastric emptying.
  • MOTS-c compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-rodent
    Both lower glucose but by different routes: semaglutide via glucose-dependent insulin secretion, glucagon suppression, delayed gastric emptying and weight loss; MOTS-c via AMPK/CK2-driven insulin-independent muscle glucose uptake. GLP-1 agonists alone carry low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk, but layering MOTS-c's additional insulin-independent glucose disposal on top could compound glucose lowering, especially with reduced food intake from GLP-1-induced appetite suppression. (MOTS-c's human glucose-lowering contribution is unproven, so this is a mechanistic-plausibility concern.) PMID 29431519 (semaglutide - glucose-dependent insulin release, low hypoglycemia risk alone); PMID 25738459 (MOTS-c AMPK/GLUT4 glucose uptake, rodent) Watch: Blood glucose and hypoglycemia symptoms, particularly during GLP-1-driven appetite/intake reduction; watch for additive glucose lowering against reduced caloric intake.
  • Metformin compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Two overlapping harms. (1) Shared GI toxicity: metformin already causes nausea/diarrhea/abdominal pain (20-30% of an oral dose sits unabsorbed in the gut lumen), and semaglutide adds its own nausea/vomiting via delayed gastric emptying and central appetite signalling, so the GI effects compound. (2) A rarer but serious route to metformin-associated lactic acidosis (MALA): GLP-1-driven vomiting and reduced intake cause dehydration and acute kidney injury, and because metformin is cleared unchanged by the kidney, impaired renal elimination lets it accumulate, worsening lactate build-up from Complex I inhibition. Metformin's own monograph flags that GLP-1 RA co-use can additively raise GI side effects and, in renal impairment, MALA risk. metformin.json PMID 42164410 (metformin + GLP-1 RA co-use -> additive GI + MALA risk in renal impairment); metformin.json (renal-only clearance; GI intolerance; MALA mechanism from Complex I inhibition + impaired lactate clearance) Watch: Hydration status and renal function (eGFR/creatinine), especially during vomiting or poor fluid intake; symptoms of lactic acidosis (deep/rapid breathing, severe nausea, muscle pain, malaise, drowsiness) warrant urgent care; GI tolerance. No dosing guidance.
  • AOD-9604 potentiates ↑ Evidence-rodent
    Additive fat loss through independent, non-overlapping mechanisms. Semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist that reduces body weight mainly via appetite suppression, delayed gastric emptying and reduced energy intake (a central/gut mechanism). AOD-9604 acts peripherally on the adipocyte (HSL up, ACC down, fat oxidation). Because the two hit different nodes — intake reduction vs adipose fat mobilisation — their fat-loss effects can stack without sharing a receptor or enzyme. A relevant caveat: GLP-1 weight loss includes substantial lean-mass loss, which AOD does not counteract (it is non-anabolic, no IGF-1). AOD-9604 monograph (peripheral adipocyte mechanism, non-anabolic, no IGF-1); semaglutide monograph (GLP-1R agonist; appetite/intake reduction; substantial lean-mass loss) Watch: Body composition / lean mass (GLP-1 agents strip lean tissue — resistance training and protein intake matter); GI tolerance and, if relevant, glucose. AOD-9604 itself has no proven human fat-loss benefit to offset any added risk.
  • CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    GLP-1 receptor agonism lowers blood glucose (glucose-dependent insulin secretion, glucagon suppression, slowed gastric emptying) and improves insulin sensitivity largely via weight loss. This opposes the GH-induced insulin resistance and glucose rise from the secretagogue stack, so a GLP-1 agonist can blunt the diabetogenic side effect. The counteraction is on glucose homeostasis, not on the GH/IGF-1 axis itself, so GH's other effects are unchanged. Semaglutide monograph (GLP-1R agonist lowers glucose, low hypoglycemia risk, weight loss); CJC-1295+Ipa monograph side_effect 'Insulin resistance' Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c (to see the GH glucose effect being offset); because both alter appetite/GI, watch overall nutrition and for GI intolerance.
  • GHRP-6 opposes Evidence-rodent
    GHRP-6's signature effect is strong hunger, driven by GHS-R1a agonism on hypothalamic arcuate NPY/AgRP (orexigenic) neurons — the same circuit ghrelin uses. GLP-1 receptor agonists such as semaglutide act on the opposite side of that circuit: they quiet AgRP neurons and activate POMC neurons to suppress food intake, and slow gastric emptying. So GHRP-6 pushes appetite up while semaglutide pulls it down at the same arcuate node — directly opposing effects on food intake. GHRP-6 appetite via arcuate NPY/AgRP (monograph step 5, side-effect table, PMID 39813130); GLP-1 AgRP-down/POMC-up appetite suppression is rodent-mapped, human net effect observed (semaglutide monograph) Watch: Appetite/food-intake and weight trend as the behavioral readout of the opposing signals; glucose/HbA1c (GHRP-6 worsens, GLP-1 improves insulin sensitivity — net effect is unpredictable and worth tracking).
  • Amycretin shares target Established
    Amycretin is a full GLP-1 receptor agonist and semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist, so combining them means two drugs occupying the identical GLP-1R (hindbrain satiety, slowed gastric emptying, glucose-dependent insulin release). This is redundant on the GLP-1 arm and additively compounds the shared on-target GI effects (nausea/vomiting) and gastric-emptying delay without engaging a new pathway. Both oral forms also share the same SNAC (salcaprozate) absorption technology. amycretin.json (GLP-1R full agonist; shared SNAC oral tech vs semaglutide); semaglutide.json (GLP-1R agonist; SNAC oral formulation) Watch: Watch for stacked GI adverse events and dehydration; monitor fasting glucose/HbA1c and resting heart rate (small class-typical rise). No added benefit expected from doubling the same receptor arm.
  • 5-Amino-1MQ redundant Evidence-rodent
    Both reduce fat mass but by mechanistically DISTINCT routes, which is exactly why the 5-Amino-1MQ monograph flags it as 'mechanistically different from GLP-1 drugs.' Semaglutide drives weight loss centrally via GLP-1R agonism on hindbrain/area-postrema and hypothalamic neurons, suppressing appetite and food intake (Established-human). 5-Amino-1MQ reduces fat in rodents via increased adipocyte energy expenditure with NO change in food intake. Because the two lower fat through non-overlapping pathways (appetite vs. cellular energy burn), their fat-loss effects are additive/redundant toward the same endpoint rather than competing. The caution is that stacking two independent fat-loss drivers deepens the total caloric deficit. Semaglutide central appetite mechanism (STEP program, human); 5-Amino-1MQ fat loss without appetite change (PMID 29155147, rodent); distinctness stated in monograph. Additivity toward the shared endpoint inferred. Watch: Body weight and body composition for excessive or too-rapid loss (especially in already-lean users), plus lipid panel; watch nutritional adequacy given semaglutide-reduced intake layered on an energy-expenditure agent.

History & legal status

Developed by Novo Nordisk (research codes NN9535 / NNC-0113-0217; USAN assigned 2010) as the once-weekly successor to liraglutide; the design was published by Lau et al. in 2015 (J Med Chem, PMID 26308095). FIRST APPROVAL 2017 for type 2 diabetes as subcutaneous Ozempic (FDA label 209637; EMA EPAR 'Ozempic'). Oral semaglutide (Rybelsus, with SNAC) was approved in 2019/2020 -- the first oral GLP-1 agonist (EMA EPAR 'Rybelsus'). The higher-dose 2.4 mg/week weight-management formulation (Wegovy) was approved in 2021 on the strength of the STEP program. In 2024 the U.S. FDA expanded the Wegovy label to include cardiovascular risk reduction based on SELECT. Carries an FDA BOXED WARNING for rodent thyroid C-cell tumours and is contraindicated in personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2 (ChEMBL black_box_warning=1). It is a legitimately prescribed, non-controlled medicine (not a scheduled/banned substance), but a large gray-market exists in compounded and research-chemical 'semaglutide' sold outside the regulated supply chain -- those products are not quality-assured. Sport context: as a peptide hormone-pathway drug it is not a classic anabolic, but athletes should verify status under current anti-doping rules and product purity. Current status (2026): widely approved globally for T2D, weight management, and CV risk reduction; multiple combination products in development (e.g. CagriSema with the amylin analogue cagrilintide).

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does the rodent thyroid C-cell tumour signal translate to humans at all? Human C-cells express far fewer GLP1Rs, and no causal human medullary-thyroid-cancer link has been shown -- the boxed warning rests on rodent data of uncertain human relevance [PMID 20203154].
  • Is the NAION (sudden vision loss) association causal, and if so by what mechanism? The signal comes from observational cohorts with wide confidence intervals and conflicting follow-up studies; the biological mechanism is entirely unestablished [PMID 38958939; PMID 40146102].
  • What happens to weight, muscle mass and cardiometabolic benefit after STOPPING? Weight regain is common, but the long-term trajectory, optimal exit strategy, and how much lean-mass loss is permanent are not well defined.
  • How much of the long-term benefit is weight loss versus weight-independent effects? SELECT showed CV benefit that may partly precede major weight change, suggesting direct vascular/anti-inflammatory actions that are not yet mechanistically pinned down.
  • Can the nausea/appetite circuits be separated? Because satiety and nausea share the area postrema and overlapping Gs/Gq-cAMP signalling, whether 'biased agonists' or PDE4-targeted designs can keep efficacy while cutting GI side effects is an open, actively researched question [PMID 42174184; PMID 38451873].
  • Long-term safety of chronic use in non-diabetic, younger, and adolescent populations (decades of exposure), and the true incidence of rare events (pancreatitis, bowel obstruction/ileus from profound gut slowing) remain incompletely characterised.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT dosing guidance). GI tolerability: watch for persistent vomiting, inability to keep fluids down, or signs of dehydration -- and severe, persistent abdominal pain radiating to the back (possible pancreatitis) warrants urgent evaluation; new RUQ pain, fever, or jaundice may signal gallbladder disease. Because gastric emptying is markedly slowed, retained stomach contents are a real anaesthesia/aspiration consideration before any procedure or surgery -- flag GLP-1 use to any anaesthetist. Glycaemia: in people also on insulin or sulfonylureas, monitor blood glucose for hypoglycaemia (low risk on semaglutide alone). In type 2 diabetes with existing eye disease, baseline and follow-up retinal exams are prudent given the SUSTAIN-6 retinopathy signal; report any sudden vision change (NAION). Renal/hydration: severe vomiting/diarrhoea can cause volume depletion and acute kidney injury -- watch hydration. Thyroid: report any neck lump, hoarseness, or dysphagia (boxed-warning precaution); routine calcitonin screening is generally not recommended but family history of medullary thyroid cancer/MEN2 is a contraindication. Body composition: with rapid weight loss, track lean-mass preservation (adequate protein + resistance training) rather than scale weight alone. Mental health: monitor mood. Pregnancy: stop well before a planned pregnancy (long half-life). Source quality: gray-market/compounded 'semaglutide' is not quality-assured -- purity and dose cannot be assumed.

Caveats

Direction/magnitude to keep straight: it is an AGONIST (switches the receptor ON, not off); ~15% weight loss is the average at high dose, not a guarantee, and it PLATEAUS. The brain is the primary weight-loss site; slowed gastric emptying is a real but secondary and partly self-limiting contributor, and it does NOT block calorie/fat absorption (that's orlistat, a different drug). Glucose effects are glucose-DEPENDENT, hence low hypo risk. The detailed neuron-circuit story is rodent-derived; do not present mouse area-postrema wiring as if directly observed in humans. A large share of lost weight can be muscle — important to flag for a fitness audience. Not covered here and worth a separate note for scripts: thyroid C-cell tumour signal in rodents (boxed warning), pancreatitis/gallbladder risk, contraindication in pregnancy, and that compounded/grey-market 'semaglutide' peptides are not the verified clinical product. This package is mechanistic education, not medical or dosing advice.
