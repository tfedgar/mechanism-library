Library / Anabolic-androgenic steroids

Superdrol

Main target: The androgen receptor. Backed by human data.

EvidenceHuman evidence
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A 17-alpha-methylated oral "designer" DHT-derivative that builds muscle by hammering the androgen receptor while its survive-first-pass chemistry makes it notoriously hard on the liver and brutal on cholesterol.

Strong human data 28 citations
Class
Oral 17α-alkylated (specifically 17α-methylated) anabolic-androgenic steroid; a dihydrotestosterone (DHT / 5α-androstane) derivative
Primary target
The androgen receptor (AR, gene NR3C4) — a ligand-activated nuclear/steroid receptor
Human status
Never an approved human therapeutic. Sketched in the 1950s, it re-emerged ~2005 as the over-the-counter 'prohormone' supplement Superdrol; after a wave of liver-injury case reports and FDA warnings, the DEA classified methasterone (and prostanozol) as Schedule III anabolic steroids in 2012. Human evidence is limited to case reports and drug-induced-liver-injury (DILI) cohort/observational data plus class-wide AAS reviews — there are no controlled efficacy trials in humans. It still circulates in grey-market 'designer steroid' supplements, sometimes unlabeled.

At a glance

  • Marked increase in muscle protein synthesis and lean mass / strength via potent androgen-receptor activation (anabolic effect)
  • Non-aromatizing 'dry, hard' physique — no estrogenic water retention or gynecomastia from the compound itself
  • Cholestatic liver injury: jaundice, severe itching (pruritus), high bilirubin, often with only modestly elevated ALT/AST (a classic Superdrol presentation)
  • Severe dyslipidemia: sharp drop in HDL, rise in LDL — cardiovascular risk
  • Strong suppression of the body's own testosterone (HPG-axis shutdown)
  • Reported in case literature alongside acute kidney injury / renal involvement at the severe end
  • Class-level rare structural liver pathology with heavy/prolonged use: peliosis hepatis, hepatic adenoma/carcinoma

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps6 human / established 1 rodent / preclinical

  1. It's built to survive the first pass — the 17α-methyl group Established

    Swallowed steroids normally get gutted by the liver before reaching the bloodstream. Adding a methyl group at the 17-alpha position blocks the liver's main off-switch (17β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase oxidising the 17β-OH), so the molecule survives first-pass metabolism and is orally active. The 2α-methyl group further boosts anabolic potency and resists 3α-reduction. This same metabolic stubbornness — forcing the liver to process a drug it can't easily clear — is the root of the hepatotoxicity, not a side issue. Bond, Llewellyn, Van Mol 2016, Med Hypotheses, PMID 27372877; Bond, Smit, de Ronde 2022, Front Endocrinol, PMID 36644692; structural review Wiacek & Zubrzycki 2026, Int J Mol Sci, PMID 41898445

  2. Binds and activates the androgen receptor (the genomic core) Established

    Methasterone is a DHT-type androgen, so it diffuses into cells and binds the androgen receptor (AR) sitting in the cytoplasm. Ligand binding kicks off the classic genomic cascade: chaperone heat-shock proteins (HSP90/70) drop off, the receptor changes shape and dimerises, translocates into the nucleus, and docks onto androgen-response elements (AREs) in DNA. There it recruits co-activators and switches on transcription of genes that increase muscle protein synthesis and satellite-cell activity — the actual muscle-building signal. C17 substitution is a key determinant of AR affinity and tissue selectivity. Bond, Smit, de Ronde 2022, Front Endocrinol, PMID 36644692; Wiacek & Zubrzycki 2026, Int J Mol Sci, PMID 41898445

  3. No aromatization, strong androgenic backbone — why it feels 'dry' and harsh Established

    As a 5α-reduced (DHT-derived) steroid it cannot be converted to estrogen by aromatase, so it produces no estrogenic water retention or gynecomastia from itself — users report a 'dry, hard' look. The flip side: with no estrogen buffer and a potent androgen suppressing the body's own hormone axis, it strongly shuts down natural testosterone and worsens the cardiovascular/lipid picture rather than softening it. Bond, Smit, de Ronde 2022, Front Endocrinol, PMID 36644692; Wiacek & Zubrzycki 2026, Int J Mol Sci, PMID 41898445

  4. Cholestatic liver injury: it jams bile flow Evidence-human

    The signature Superdrol injury is cholestasis — bile stops flowing, so bilirubin backs up (deep jaundice, intense itching) often with only mildly raised liver enzymes. Mechanistically, 17α-alkylated AAS appear to interfere with the canalicular bile-transport machinery (the bile-salt export pump BSEP/ABCB11 and related transporters); in susceptible people underlying transporter gene variants (e.g., ABCB11) may be unmasked. Biopsies typically show 'bland' cholestasis with little structural duct damage, and recovery is slow (weeks to months). El Sherrif et al. 2013, Liver Int, PMID 23750872; Stolz et al./DILIN 2019, Aliment Pharmacol Ther, PMID 30934130; Nasr & Ahmad 2009, Dig Dis Sci, PMID 18720005

  5. Oxidative stress is the leading mechanistic hypothesis for the cell damage Evidence-rodent

    Beyond blocking bile, the leading explanation for why 17α-alkylated steroids damage hepatocytes is oxidative stress — the drug's slow, forced metabolism generates reactive oxygen species and depletes the cell's antioxidant glutathione (GSH). In rat hepatocyte cultures, 17α-alkylated steroids (methyltestosterone, oxymetholone, stanozolol) were directly toxic and depleted GSH while non-alkylated steroids like testosterone were not — a clean structure-toxicity link. The exact human mechanism is still not fully pinned down. Bond, Llewellyn, Van Mol 2016, Med Hypotheses, PMID 27372877 (hypothesis); Welder et al. 1995, J Pharmacol Toxicol Methods, PMID 8527826 (in vitro rat hepatocytes)

  6. Wrecks the cholesterol profile (HDL crash, LDL spike) Evidence-human

    Oral 17α-alkylated AAS are especially aggressive on blood lipids: they sharply lower protective HDL ('good' cholesterol) and raise LDL ('bad' cholesterol), partly via hepatic enzyme effects (e.g., upregulating hepatic lipase). Because methasterone doesn't aromatize, there's no estrogenic counterbalance, so the unfavorable lipid shift can be severe even on short cycles — a real cardiovascular concern, not just a number on a panel. Bond, Smit, de Ronde 2022, Front Endocrinol, PMID 36644692 (dyslipidemia in AAS users); class effect referenced in Bond et al. 2016, PMID 27372877

  7. At higher/longer exposure: rarer structural liver damage Evidence-human

    With heavier or prolonged use the class has been linked to more serious structural liver problems — peliosis hepatis (blood-filled cavities), and hepatocellular adenoma/carcinoma — and Superdrol case reports include severe cholestasis accompanied by acute kidney injury. These are less common than the reversible cholestasis but are the worst-case end of the spectrum. Bond, Llewellyn, Van Mol 2016, Med Hypotheses, PMID 27372877; Nasr & Ahmad 2009, Dig Dis Sci, PMID 18720005 (cholestasis + renal failure); Jasiurkowski et al. 2006, Am J Gastroenterol, PMID 16952289 (cholestatic jaundice + IgA nephropathy)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Methasterone (17β-hydroxy-2α,17α-dimethyl-5α-androstan-3-one) is a synthetic 5α-reduced (DHT-derived) full agonist of the nuclear androgen receptor (AR / NR3C4). Established: it is structurally dihydrotestosterone bearing two methyl additions (C2α and C17α). CRITICAL DATA GAP — no experimentally measured AR Ki/EC50/Kd for methasterone itself exists in ChEMBL (compound_search returned 0 records) or in the indexed primary literature; the only published direct binding study on methasterone is a bovine-serum-albumin (carrier-protein, NOT AR) spectroscopy/docking paper (PMID 39983858, Evidence-in vitro). Anabolic:androgenic 'ratios' cited commercially (~400:20, i.e. very high anabolic, low androgenic) trace to in-house/unpublished bioassay claims, NOT peer-reviewed AR affinity numbers — treat as Speculative. The closest peer-reviewed receptor-pharmacology anchor for a near-identical designer 17α-methyl steroid is methyl-1-testosterone, characterised in a yeast AR-transactivation assay as a 'potent androgen' and in the rat Hershberger assay as potently anabolic/androgenic with strong hepatic activity (PMID 21990312, Evidence-rodent + in vitro). Mechanistically methasterone behaves as an AR agonist; the C2α/C17α methylations and absent C4–C5 double bond block aromatization (no estrogen conversion) and impair 3α/5α/17β reductive metabolism (Established by DEA structure-activity review and metabolism studies, PMID 22844688 / corpus DEA NPRM).

Signalling cascade. Established AR cascade: lipophilic methasterone diffuses across the plasma membrane and binds the ligand-binding domain of cytoplasmic AR, displacing HSP90/HSP70 chaperones; AR then dimerizes, undergoes nuclear translocation, and binds androgen-response elements (AREs) in promoter/enhancer regions, recruiting coactivators (e.g. SRC/p160 family, CBP/p300) to drive transcription of anabolic genes — increasing muscle protein synthesis and IGF-1/AR expression in skeletal muscle (Evidence-rodent for the M1T analog: oral dosing modulated IGF-I and AR expression in gastrocnemius and stimulated levator ani muscle, PMID 21990312). Because it is already 5α-reduced, methasterone is not a substrate for further 5α-reduction and acts directly at AR in tissues including liver, where AR activation drives a hepatic transcriptional program. The hepatic arm of the cascade is the best-characterised analog-level mechanism: in human hepatocytes the close analog epistane activated AR and, via AR, upregulated the bile-acid-synthesis gene CYP8B1 (and CYP7A1), upregulated transporters NTCP/OSTα/BSEP, downregulated FGF19, and trans-activated nuclear receptors LXR and PXR — producing bile-acid accumulation (PMID 31894354, Evidence-human in vitro). Methasterone-specific cascade data beyond AR agonism are largely extrapolated from these analogs.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral. Marketed historically as tablets/capsules (e.g. the product 'Superdrol' and later grey-market clones) and as a raw powder; no injectable/ester pharmaceutical form. Established.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally bioavailable specifically because of the 17α-methyl group, which sterically hinders hepatic 17β-hydroxysteroid oxidation and thereby allows the molecule to survive first-pass metabolism — the defining property of all 17α-alkylated AAS (Established; PMID 27372877, PMID 29521706). No quantitative human %F (absolute bioavailability) value has been published — Evidence gap. Methasterone binds serum albumin via hydrogen-bonding/van der Waals forces (spontaneous ground-state complex), which is consistent with plasma protein binding affecting free-fraction distribution (Evidence-in vitro, PMID 39983858).
Half-life
No directly measured human plasma elimination half-life has been published (Evidence gap). Commercial/community estimates of roughly 6–8 hours are Speculative (not from a pharmacokinetic study). Urinary parent and metabolites are detectable for an extended window in anti-doping work — long-term GC/LC-HRMS metabolite tracking has been developed precisely because detection persists (PMID 39883123, PMID 27669235, Evidence-human/analytical).
Metabolism
Hepatic. Phase I: human hepatocyte and liver-S9/microsome studies on methasterone and the closely related 17-methyldrostanolone identify oxidative and reductive metabolites (3-keto reduction, hydroxylation, A-ring reduction products), with the 17α-methyl group retained (PMID 19056412 drostanolone/17-methyldrostanolone human hepatocytes; PMID 40866528 and PMID 39883123 human liver-S9 / in vivo GC-Orbitrap; PMID 21762781 chimeric-mouse model). Phase II: sulfation (and glucuronidation) of the 17β-OH and reduced metabolites (PMID 28635171 sulfation with liver S9). Compound-level enzyme target: methasterone is a confirmed in vitro inhibitor of human AKR1D1 (Δ4-3-oxosteroid 5β-reductase), an enzyme central to bile-acid synthesis (PMID 37451653, Evidence-in vitro) — a metabolism/toxicity crossover point. Specific CYP isoform assignments for methasterone clearance are not firmly established (gap).
Excretion
Renal/urinary as sulfate and glucuronide conjugates of parent and reduced/hydroxylated metabolites; this is the basis of all the anti-doping detection assays (PMID 27669235, PMID 39883123, PMID 40866528). Quantitative fractional excretion / biliary-vs-renal split not published.
Notes
Not a prodrug and not an ester — already the active 5α-reduced AR agonist. Non-aromatizable (C2α-methyl + 5α-reduced A-ring block aromatase), so it produces no estrogenic metabolites by aromatization. CNS/BBB penetration is expected on lipophilicity grounds (high logP, albumin-bound) but no quantitated BBB data exist. Frequently sold mislabeled or as an undeclared adulterant in 'dietary supplements,' so real-world exposure dose is often unknown (PMID 36796473, PMID 18563865, PMID 30934130).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

HUMAN DOSE-RESPONSE IS ESSENTIALLY UNCHARACTERISED — there is NO controlled human pharmacology trial of methasterone establishing dose-vs-effect for muscle gain or for toxicity. This must be stated plainly. What exists: (1) Historical human use of the parent concept '2,17-dimethyltestosterone' in metastatic breast cancer (1963, Talley et al., PMID 13984784) documents biological/clinical activity in humans but reports oncology tumor-response endpoints, not anabolic dose-response, and uses the dimethyl-testosterone (not the 5α-reduced methasterone marketed later as Superdrol). (2) The toxicity 'dose-response' that IS documented in humans is the hepatotoxic threshold: case reports and the DILIN cohort show severe cholestatic injury after only weeks of OTC bodybuilding-supplement use — median latency ~56–73 days from start, with profound jaundice (peak bilirubin up to ~705 µmol/L) (PMID 33214235 n=52 pooled; PMID 30934130 n=44; PMID 18720005; PMID 16952289) — i.e. clinically meaningful hepatotoxicity occurs at commonly-used supplement doses, but exact ingested mg is frequently unknown because products were mislabeled/adulterated. RODENT analog data (methyl-1-testosterone, PMID 21990312) show DOSE-DEPENDENT stimulation of prostate and levator ani muscle weight after subcutaneous dosing and dose-dependent hepatic marker (tyrosine aminotransferase) induction, especially orally — establishing a dose-dependent anabolic-AND-hepatic signal for this structural class, but for an analog, not methasterone itself. Bottom line: anabolic potency claims are extrapolated/Speculative; the only reproducible human dose-response signal is toxicity at real-world supplement exposures.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Drostanolone (Masteron / 2α-methyl-DHT)
    same: Both are 5α-reduced (DHT-derived), 2α-methylated, non-aromatizable AR agonists; methasterone is essentially the 17α-methyl (orally active) version of drostanolone — its metabolites overlap with 17-methyldrostanolone.
    different: Drostanolone is the injectable 17β-ester form (propionate/enanthate) and is NOT 17α-alkylated, so it is far less hepatotoxic; methasterone's added 17α-methyl makes it oral but markedly hepatotoxic/cholestatic. Both inhibit AKR1D1 in vitro (PMID 37451653).
  • vs Methyl-1-testosterone (M1T)
    same: Both are designer oral 17α-methylated AAS sold as 'supplements,' both potent AR agonists with strong hepatic activity and HPG suppression risk.
    different: M1T retains a Δ1 double bond (1-ene) and is testosterone- rather than DHT-skeleton-derived; M1T has actual peer-reviewed receptor/Hershberger characterization (PMID 21990312) whereas methasterone's AR affinity is essentially unpublished. M1T is the best-studied structural neighbor used to infer methasterone pharmacology.
  • vs Epistane (methepitiostane / 2a,3a-epithio-17α-methyl)
    same: Both are oral 17α-alkylated designer steroids that cause severe, protracted cholestatic DILI via hepatic AR activation; share the AR→bile-acid-synthesis cascade.
    different: Epistane carries a 2,3-epithio ring instead of methasterone's 2α-methyl/3-keto; epistane has the most detailed human-hepatocyte mechanistic study (PMID 31894354), which is the principal source for the AR→CYP8B1 cholestasis mechanism extrapolated to methasterone.
  • vs Stanozolol (Winstrol)
    same: Both are oral 17α-alkylated, 5α-reduced, non-aromatizable AAS with hepatotoxic and HDL-suppressing profiles; both implicated in cholestatic DILI.
    different: Stanozolol has a fused pyrazole A-ring (no 3-keto), tends toward a somewhat different (sometimes more hepatocellular/hypersensitivity) injury pattern, and has long pharmaceutical/clinical history; methasterone never had legitimate marketing approval and reaches users mainly via grey-market supplements.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Anadrol adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Both are oral 17-alpha-alkylated AAS. The 17aa 'armor' that makes them orally active is exactly what makes them hepatotoxic - they resist first-pass clearance, linger in hepatocytes, impair canalicular bile transporters (BSEP/ABCB11, MRP2) and drive oxidative stress. Taking two 17aa orals together stacks first-pass hepatic load, and reviews note that using multiple orals simultaneously increases the frequency and severity of liver injury (raised ALT/AST/GGT, cholestasis, and at the severe end peliosis/tumors). Both also crash HDL, so atherogenic lipid toxicity compounds too. Anadrol monograph mechanism_step 6 + side_effect_mechanisms (17aa -> BSEP/MRP2 cholestasis, oxidative stress; HDL crash via hepatic lipase); superdrol monograph key_effects (cholestatic injury, severe HDL drop); MoA hepatotoxicity_17aa_orals; PMID 29521706 (multiple orals simultaneously increase hepatotoxicity) Watch: ALT, AST, GGT, bilirubin and ALP; watch for jaundice, dark urine, right-upper-quadrant pain (cholestasis/peliosis signals). Full lipid panel for the additive HDL crash.
  • DNP adds to a side effect ⚠ Evidence-human
    Superdrol is a 17-alpha-methylated oral steroid whose survive-first-pass chemistry makes it markedly hepatotoxic - classically cholestatic (jaundice, high bilirubin, pruritus) with hepatocellular injury and, at the severe end, structural liver damage. DNP delivers high first-pass hepatic exposure and energy-starves hepatocytes (impaired ATP supply), and human DNP poisoning includes transaminase elevation and hepatic injury. Combining a directly hepatotoxic 17aa oral with an uncoupler that ATP-depletes the same organ compounds drug-induced liver injury via two independent hepatotoxic mechanisms (steroid cholestasis/oxidative stress + uncoupler ATP depletion) hitting the liver simultaneously. Both are also independently linked to renal involvement/AKI at the severe end. Superdrol monograph (17aa cholestatic/hepatocellular DILI; PMID 23750872, 30934130, 18720005) + DNP monograph side_effect_mechanisms (hepatic ATP depletion, transaminase elevation, liver as high-exposure organ; PMID 39659715) Watch: Liver enzymes/transaminases, bilirubin (direct/total), and signs of cholestasis (jaundice, dark urine, itching, pale stools); renal function. Any rise on a 17aa oral plus DNP warrants stopping and clinical review.
  • Dianabol adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Superdrol is another oral 17-alpha-methylated AAS and among the most hepatotoxic and lipid-hostile of the class. Paired with Dianabol, the two share the identical 17aa injury pathway - cholestasis via impaired canalicular bile transport (BSEP/ABCB11) plus a hepatic-lipid shift that destroys HDL and raises LDL - so cholestatic hepatotoxicity and atherogenic dyslipidemia are additively amplified. Because Superdrol is a non-aromatizing DHT-derivative while Dbol aromatizes weakly, they diverge on estrogen but converge hard on the liver and lipids; Superdrol's lack of any estrogenic buffer makes its lipid hit especially harsh. Dbol monograph mechanism_steps 4-5; superdrol-methasterone-2-17-dimethyl-5-a.json (oral 17a-methylated non-aromatizing DHT derivative, cholestatic DILI, severe HDL/LDL shift); corpus MoA cards 'Hepatotoxicity (17aa orals)' and 'Hepatic lipase & lipids' Watch: ALT/AST/GGT and bilirubin, with jaundice/dark-urine/RUQ-pain and severe pruritus as cholestasis signals; full lipid panel (expect a severe HDL crash from two orals); blood pressure.
  • Halotestin adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Two oral 17-alpha-methylated, non-aromatizing 'dry' AAS taken together compound both hepatotoxicity (additive cholestatic/hepatocellular injury from the shared 17a-alkyl group) and atherogenic dyslipidemia. Both induce hepatic lipase and crash HDL / raise LDL with no offsetting estrogenic HDL support because neither aromatizes, so the HDL fall is deeper than with an aromatizing injectable. Superdrol characteristically causes cholestatic jaundice with high bilirubin and severe HDL suppression, matching fluoxymesterone's profile. Halotestin monograph steps 6-7 & side_effect_mechanisms (hepatic-lipase HDL crash, 17aa hepatotoxicity; PMID 2915439, 36051334); superdrol monograph key_effects (cholestatic injury; sharp HDL drop; non-aromatizing) Watch: Full lipid panel (expect sharp HDL-C drop, higher LDL/ApoB) plus the full liver panel (ALT/AST/GGT/bilirubin); watch for jaundice and pruritus. Statin/ezetimibe blunt LDL but do not restore HDL; cessation is what recovers it.
  • Methyltestosterone adds to a side effect ⚠ Established
    Both are 17-alpha-alkylated oral AAS. The 17aa group that lets each survive first-pass metabolism also forces slow, abnormal hepatic processing that impairs canalicular bile transport (BSEP/ABCB11, MRP2) and drives oxidative stress, producing cholestasis, peliosis, and adenoma risk. Running two 17aa orals together stacks the same hepatotoxic hit on one organ, and Superdrol is among the most hepatotoxic of the class (bland canalicular cholestasis with high bilirubin), so the additive cholestatic/hepatocellular burden is greater than either alone. History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

"Origin: The 2,17-dimethyl-testosterone scaffold dates to late-1950s/early-1960s pharmaceutical chemistry; '2,17-dimethyltestosterone' was tested clinically in metastatic breast cancer in 1963 (Talley et al., PMID 13984784), and methasterone (methyldrostanolone) was described by Syntex-era chemists (Zaffaroni 1960, cited in the DEA structure-activity review). It was NEVER approved or marketed as a prescription drug. It resurfaced ~2005 as the OTC 'prohormone/dietary supplement' product 'Superdrol' (marketed by Anabolic Xtreme), explicitly (and falsely) sold as non-hormonal. The US FDA issued a Warning Letter on Superdrol in March 2006 after adverse-event reports, and a broader 2009 FDA warning on steroid-containing bodybuilding products (corpus DEA NPRM; PMID 18563865). The US DEA classified methasterone (and prostanozol) as Schedule III anabolic steroids under the Controlled Substances Act via Final Rule effective 2012 (Fed Regist, PMID 22844688); the Designer Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2014 further tightened US control of such designer steroids. It is a WADA-prohibited substance (anabolic agent) at all times in sport, with dedicated anti-doping detection methods (PMID 27669235, PMID 39883123). Current status: controlled/illicit for human performance use in the US, UK and most jurisdictions; still encountered as an undeclared adulterant in 'supplements' and on the grey market (PMID 36796473, PMID 35867601)."

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • No experimentally measured human androgen-receptor affinity (Ki/Kd/EC50) or validated anabolic:androgenic ratio for methasterone exists — the widely-quoted potency numbers are unsubstantiated; the actual AR binding affinity and tissue selectivity remain genuinely unknown.
  • Human pharmacokinetics are uncharacterised: no published absolute oral bioavailability, plasma half-life, volume of distribution, or definitive CYP-isoform clearance pathway for methasterone.
  • Why methasterone-induced cholestasis is idiosyncratic — only a subset of users develop severe injury. The roles of ABCB11/BSEP, ATP8B1, and other transporter polymorphisms versus dose/duration are not resolved (DILIN found ABCB11 variants but no overall genetic enrichment).
  • Relative contribution of the three candidate mechanisms (AR-driven bile-acid synthesis upregulation, AKR1D1 inhibition, and oxidative stress/GSH depletion) to methasterone-specific hepatotoxicity has not been dissected — most mechanistic data come from analogs (epistane, M1T).
  • Long-term/irreversible risks (peliosis hepatis, hepatic adenoma/HCC, persistent HPG suppression, cardiovascular outcomes) are essentially undocumented for methasterone specifically, owing to absent prospective follow-up.
  • Dose-response for both efficacy (muscle/strength gain) and toxicity in humans is unknown — no controlled human trial exists, and real-world exposure is confounded by product mislabeling, adulteration, and polypharmacy (stacking).

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a protocol, and not an endorsement of use). LIVER is the priority given the cholestatic DILI signal: baseline and periodic liver panel — ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, and especially total/direct bilirubin (the hallmark is striking hyperbilirubinemia with disproportionately modest transaminases). Watch for clinical red flags that warrant immediate cessation and medical review: jaundice (yellow eyes/skin), dark urine, pale stools, and pruritus (itching) — pruritus and jaundice are the most common presenting signs and can appear or worsen even AFTER stopping (bilirubin often peaks ~4 weeks post-cessation). Because cholestasis can drive secondary kidney injury, monitor renal function (creatinine, eGFR, urinalysis for proteinuria/hematuria). Given strong HDL suppression by oral 17α-alkylated AAS, monitor a fasting lipid panel (HDL, LDL, total cholesterol) and blood pressure for cardiovascular risk. Endocrine: monitor total/free testosterone, LH and FSH for HPG-axis suppression, and watch for testicular atrophy or reduced libido; women should watch for virilization (voice deepening, hirsutism, clitoromegaly), some of which is irreversible. Also monitor hematocrit/hemoglobin (polycythemia risk with androgens). Any RUCAM-style assessment of suspected injury should account for undeclared/adulterated supplement content — product identity is often unreliable. Emphasis: signs of liver injury (jaundice, itching, dark urine) are the single most important thing to act on, because the documented human harm from this compound is overwhelmingly hepatic. Sources: PMID 33214235, PMID 30934130, PMID 18720005, PMID 16952289, PMID 29521706, PMID 26373946."

Caveats

Harm-reduction framing only — this is mechanism education, not endorsement, and no dosing is provided. Important accuracy notes: (1) The genomic AR pathway and the 17α-methyl 'oral survivability' chemistry are Established; the specific molecular cause of hepatotoxicity is NOT settled — oxidative-stress/GSH depletion is the leading hypothesis (strong in rodent/in-vitro, e.g. Welder 1995; framed as hypothesis by Bond 2016) and the bile-transporter (BSEP/ABCB11) story comes from human case series and the DILIN cohort, where no consistent genetic enrichment was found (Stolz/DILIN 2019). (2) Much methasterone-specific human data is case reports (Nasr 2009; Jasiurkowski 2006); broader claims lean on AAS-class reviews. (3) A major real-world confound: grey-market 'Superdrol'-labeled products are frequently mislabeled or contain undisclosed/other anabolic steroids (DILIN product analysis), so individual case outcomes can't always be cleanly attributed to methasterone alone. (4) Lipid (HDL/LDL) and HPG-suppression effects are class-level oral-17α-AAS findings applied to methasterone, consistent with its non-aromatizing DHT chemistry. It's built to survive the first pass — the 17α-methyl group. Swallowed steroids normally get gutted by the liver before reaching the bloodstream. Adding a methyl group at the 17-alpha position blocks the liver's main off-switch (17β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase oxidising the 17β-OH), so the molecule survives first-pass metabolism and is orally active. The 2α-methyl group further boosts anabolic potency and resists 3α-reduction. This same metabolic stubbornness — forcing the liver to process a drug it can't easily clear — is the root of the hepatotoxicity, not a side issue.
