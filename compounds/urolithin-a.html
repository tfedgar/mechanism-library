Urolithin A

A gut-bug-made molecule that helps your cells take out the trash — recycling worn-out mitochondria so muscles run cleaner.

Human / approved 21 citations
Class
Gut-microbiome metabolite of ellagitannins (postbiotic); mitophagy inducer / mitochondrial-health nutraceutical
Primary target
Mitochondrial quality-control machinery — the PINK1/Parkin and TFEB mitophagy pathways
Human status
REAL human RCTs exist — this is NOT a pure rodent-hype compound. Placebo-controlled trials in middle-aged and older adults (Singh 2022 Cell Rep Med; Liu 2022 JAMA Netw Open) show UroA raises muscle mitochondrial-health biomarkers and produces modest gains in muscle endurance/strength; a 2024 athlete RCT (Zhao) and 2025 runner RCT (Whitfield) show smaller/mixed performance effects, and a 2025 Nat Aging RCT (Denk) tested immune aging. A 2024 systematic review (Kuerec, Ageing Res Rev) and a 2025 human SR conclude effects on mitochondrial biomarkers are fairly consistent but functional/performance benefits are modest and inconsistent. The LONGEVITY/lifespan-extension story is rodent- and C. elegans-only (Ryu 2016 Nat Med) — no human lifespan data. So: ergogenic/mitochondrial-biomarker effects = Evidence-human (modest); anti-aging/lifespan = Evidence-rodent / Speculative in humans.

At a glance

  • Raises markers of mitochondrial health and turnover in human muscle — Evidence-human
  • Modest improvement in muscle endurance/strength in middle-aged and older adults; smaller/mixed in athletes — Evidence-human (modest)
  • Induces mitophagy then mitochondrial biogenesis — Established mechanism, biomarker-supported in humans
  • Lowers ROS/NLRP3 inflammatory signaling — Evidence-rodent
  • Extends lifespan and boosts muscle in worms and rodents — Evidence-rodent (no human lifespan data)
  • Effect depends on supplementing UroA directly because only some gut microbiomes make it — Established

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It comes from your gut, not the plant Established

    Foods like pomegranate, walnuts and berries contain ellagitannins. You don't absorb those directly — your gut bacteria break them down into urolithin A. Only some people have the right microbes to make meaningful amounts, so supplements deliver UroA directly to bypass that lottery. PMID 27400265 (Ryu 2016, Nat Med); PMID 41866331 (review, J Food Sci 2025)

  2. It flags worn-out mitochondria for recycling Established (cell/animal); mechanism

    Mitochondria are the cell's power plants and they wear out. UroA switches on 'mitophagy' — the cell's system for tagging damaged mitochondria for disposal — chiefly through the PINK1/Parkin pathway, the same quality-control sensors the cell normally uses. PMID 27400265 (Ryu 2016); MoA-gen Urolithin A (PMID 37925671)

  3. A second 'clean-up' switch: TFEB Evidence-rodent

    UroA also activates TFEB, a master regulator that ramps up the lysosomes — the cell's recycling centers — so the tagged, broken mitochondria actually get digested and their parts reused. PMID 41172819 (TFEB-mediated mitophagy, rat/Schwann cell 2025)

  4. Clearing the junk makes room for new power plants Evidence-human (modest/mixed; primary endpoints often missed)

    Once damaged mitochondria are removed, the cell rebuilds a fresher mitochondrial pool (mitochondrial biogenesis). The net result is a healthier, better-functioning set of power plants rather than a pile-up of broken ones. PMID 35584623 (Singh 2022, Cell Rep Med); PMID 39649792 (muscle cells 2024)

  5. Less inflammation and oxidative stress Evidence-rodent

    Damaged mitochondria leak reactive oxygen species and trigger the NLRP3 inflammasome, a key inflammation alarm. By clearing those mitochondria, UroA dials down this ROS/NLRP3 inflammatory signaling. MoA-gen Urolithin A (PMID 37925671); PMID 41172819

  6. In muscle: better mitochondrial fitness, modestly better function Evidence-human (modest/mixed; primary endpoints often missed)

    In aging muscle this translates to improved mitochondrial-health markers and small gains in endurance and strength in human trials — a real but modest effect, strongest in middle-aged/older adults and weaker in already-fit athletes. PMID 35050355 (Liu 2022, JAMA Netw Open); PMID 39487653 (Zhao 2024); PMID 40839339 (Whitfield 2025)

  7. The longevity leap is rodent-only Evidence-rodent / Speculative (human longevity)

    In worms and mice, UroA's mitophagy boost extended lifespan and improved muscle. In humans we only have shorter-term biomarker and function data — no evidence it extends human lifespan. Treat the anti-aging headline as promising biology, not a proven human outcome. PMID 27400265 (Ryu 2016); PMID 39002645 (Kuerec 2024, Ageing Res Rev systematic review)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Urolithin A (UA; 3,8-dihydroxy-6H-dibenzo[b,d]pyran-6-one) is NOT a classic high-affinity receptor ligand with a single well-defined Ki — it acts largely as an upstream inducer of mitochondrial quality-control pathways rather than by docking a named orthosteric pocket. No entry exists in ChEMBL for UA (compound_search returned 0 hits as of this run), and no published Kd/Ki for a primary mitophagy receptor target — flag this as a genuine data gap. The best-characterized DIRECT molecular interaction is at the estrogen receptors: UA behaves as a mixed/selective estrogen-receptor modulator (SERM), binding both ERalpha and ERbeta and producing context-dependent agonist or antagonist transcriptional output (Evidence-in vitro/Evidence-rodent; PMID 37345359, 27342949). It also acts on aryl-hydrocarbon receptor signaling and inhibits several CYP and phase-II enzymes in vitro. Its mitophagy effect is best described as 'target-pathway' rather than 'receptor-binding': UA raises the PINK1/Parkin and TFEB axes without a proven single receptor. Certainty: the SERM binding is Evidence-in vitro; the absence of a defined mitophagy receptor is Established (it is a pathway inducer).

Signalling cascade. Core mechanism (Established that UA induces mitophagy; specific upstream trigger Contested): UA promotes selective autophagic clearance of damaged mitochondria. (1) PINK1/Parkin arm — on depolarized mitochondria PINK1 (PTEN-induced kinase 1) accumulates on the outer membrane, recruits and phosphorylates the E3 ubiquitin ligase Parkin (PRKN), ubiquitinates outer-membrane proteins, and tags the organelle for autophagosomal engulfment; UA increases flux through this arm (Evidence-rodent/in vitro; PMID 27400265, 37925671). (2) TFEB arm — UA promotes nuclear translocation of transcription factor EB (TFEB), the master regulator of the autophagy-lysosome (CLEAR) gene network, increasing lysosomal biogenesis and degradative capacity (Evidence-rodent/in vitro; corpus MoA, PMID 37925671). (3) Downstream consequences — clearing dysfunctional mitochondria lowers mitochondrial ROS, which suppresses NLRP3 inflammasome activation and lowers IL-1beta/IL-6/CRP (Evidence-human for CRP drop: PMID 35584623, 35050355). UA secondarily raises mitochondrial biogenesis markers (PGC-1alpha-associated gene expression) and improves fatty-acid oxidation, reflected in lower plasma acylcarnitines in humans (Evidence-human; PMID 32694802, 35050355). In immune cells UA augmented CD8+ T-cell mitochondrial biogenesis and fatty-acid-oxidation capacity (Evidence-human; PMID 41174221). Net effect: improved mitochondrial respiratory capacity and reduced inflammaging — but the precise proximal molecular sensor UA engages to trigger PINK1 stabilization is not established.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral. Marketed as 'Mitopure' (Amazentis/Timeline) — synthetic UA delivered as a powder/softgel; doses studied 250-1000 mg/day. Direct UA supplementation bypasses the need for gut-microbiome conversion of dietary ellagitannins.
Absorption / bioavailability
Endogenous route: dietary ellagitannins/ellagic acid (pomegranate, walnuts, berries) are converted by specific gut bacteria (e.g., Gordonibacter, Ellagibacter, some Streptococcus) to UA in the colon. Critically, only a minority of people are efficient producers — in a 100-person study only 12% had detectable plasma UA at baseline and only ~40% converted pomegranate-juice precursors into meaningful UA, distinguished by higher gut-microbiome diversity and Firmicutes:Bacteroides ratio (Evidence-human; PMID 34117375). Direct UA supplementation gives a >6-fold higher plasma exposure than pomegranate juice and works regardless of microbiome (PMID 34117375). Absolute oral bioavailability of free UA is LOW — UA is poorly water-soluble and is extensively first-pass conjugated, so most systemic UA circulates as conjugates, not free aglycone (Evidence-human + modeling; PMID 32694802, 37195009).
Half-life
Plasma conjugates persist long after dosing (UA-glucuronide detectable for many hours, supporting once-daily dosing), but a single clean terminal t1/2 for free UA is not firmly established in the human literature reviewed here — treat any specific number as uncertain. Free aglycone is cleared rapidly via conjugation; conjugate exposure dominates the AUC.
Metabolism
Extensive phase-II conjugation: glucuronidation (UGT enzymes; human S9-fraction glucuronidation rates were measured to build the PBPK model) and sulfation, producing UA-glucuronide and UA-sulfate as the main circulating species; further microbial/host conversion to urolithin B and methylated metabolites occurs (Evidence-human/in vitro; PMID 37195009, 32694802).
Excretion
Conjugated urolithins are eliminated in urine and bile/feces; urinary urolithin excretion is the standard biomarker of producer status and dietary exposure (Evidence-human; e.g., PMID 22631214, 24532260).
Notes
Free, unconjugated UA in plasma/tissue is the bioactive species for mitophagy, but PBPK modeling predicts that achievable free-UA tissue concentrations after oral supplementation are ORDERS OF MAGNITUDE below the in vitro concentrations that produce either toxicity (genotoxic/estrogenic) OR some of the beneficial in vitro effects — a key translational caveat: the in vivo benefit may be driven by lower-concentration/chronic mechanisms or by conjugates acting as a reservoir, not by the high concentrations used in cell models (Evidence-modeling; PMID 37195009). Formulation work (liposomal, PEGylated, milk-exosome carriers) aims to raise bioavailability (PMID 37865359, 33404864, 40351018). BBB penetration: UA is reported to reach the CNS in rodent neuro models and PBPK predicts some neuro exposure, but human CNS data are absent (Evidence-rodent/modeling; PMID 37195009, 40722629).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human doses studied: 250, 500, and 1000 mg/day orally. (1) Bioavailability/biomarker dose-response: 500 mg and 1000 mg over 4 weeks both modulated plasma acylcarnitines and skeletal-muscle mitochondrial gene expression in sedentary elderly; UA was bioavailable at all doses (Evidence-human; PMID 32694802). (2) Muscle strength/performance: in middle-aged adults, 4 months at 500 mg or 1000 mg/day significantly improved muscle strength (~12% increase in leg/hand strength) and showed clinically meaningful gains in 6-min walk and peak VO2, though the PRIMARY endpoint (peak power output) was NOT significantly improved (Evidence-human; PMID 35584623). (3) Older adults (65-90 y), 1000 mg/day x 4 months: improved muscle endurance (more contractions to fatigue in tibialis anterior and first dorsal interosseus at 2 months) and lowered plasma acylcarnitines/ceramides/CRP, but did NOT significantly beat placebo on the co-primary 6-min walk distance or maximal ATP production (Evidence-human; PMID 35050355). (4) Immune aging: 1000 mg/day x 4 weeks expanded naive-like CD8+ T cells and raised CD8+ fatty-acid-oxidation capacity (Evidence-human; PMID 41174221). (5) Athletes: results MIXED — an 8-week RCT in resistance-trained men reported endurance/strength and anti-inflammatory benefit (PMID 39487653), whereas a trial in highly trained distance runners found limited ergogenic effect on running performance (PMID 40839339). A formal dose-RESPONSE curve in humans (clear monotonic mg-to-effect relationship) has NOT been established — most trials test only one or two doses, and the optimal dose is genuinely unknown. Rodent: UA improved exercise capacity and muscle function across mouse aging models and in young rats at oral doses in the tens of mg/kg range (Evidence-rodent; PMID 27400265); 50 mg/kg/day was neuroprotective in ALS-model mice (Evidence-rodent; PMID 39292338).

How it compares

  • vs Spermidine
    same: Both are dietary/microbiome-linked 'longevity' compounds that enhance autophagic recycling and are marketed for cellular-aging/muscle health.
    different: Spermidine is a polyamine that induces general (macro)AUTOPHAGY primarily via AMPK/SIRT1 and inhibition of acetyltransferase EP300; UA is comparatively SELECTIVE for MITOPHAGY via PINK1/Parkin + TFEB. Different molecular triggers and different breadth (whole-cell autophagy vs mitochondria-specific clearance). (Evidence-rodent/in vitro; PMID 41404767)
  • vs Ellagic acid / ellagitannins (the dietary precursors)
    same: UA is the gut-bacterial METABOLITE of these — same dietary source (pomegranate, walnuts, berries); ellagic acid itself has antioxidant activity.
    different: Ellagic acid is poorly converted and only ~12-40% of people produce meaningful UA from it; direct UA supplementation gives >6-fold higher, microbiome-independent exposure. Eating pomegranate is NOT pharmacologically equivalent to taking UA. (Evidence-human; PMID 34117375)
  • vs Urolithin B (sister metabolite)
    same: Both are gut-derived urolithins from the same ellagitannin pathway; structurally related dibenzo-pyranones.
    different: Urolithin A (3,8-diOH) is the lead mitophagy inducer with the clinical data; urolithin B (8-OH, monohydroxy) is generally less potent at mitophagy and has been studied more for muscle anabolism via other routes. UA is the form with human RCT evidence. (Evidence-in vitro)
  • vs NAD+ precursors (NMN / NR)
    same: Both are 'mitochondrial health / healthy-aging' supplements targeting muscle and metabolic function with human trials but modest/mixed clinical endpoints.
    different: NMN/NR raise NAD+ to fuel sirtuins and mitochondrial BIOGENESIS/energetics; UA works upstream on mitochondrial QUALITY CONTROL (removing damaged mitochondria via mitophagy). Complementary mechanisms rather than the same one. (Established mechanistic distinction)

History & legal status

"Urolithin A was identified as a gut-microbial ellagitannin metabolite decades ago as a urinary biomarker of pomegranate/nut/berry consumption, but its bioactivity was 'poorly characterized' until the landmark 2016 Ryu et al. Nature Medicine paper (PMID 27400265) from the Auwerx/EPFL group and spin-out company Amazentis identified it as a first-in-class oral mitophagy inducer. Amazentis developed a synthetic, food-grade UA branded 'Mitopure' (consumer brand 'Timeline'). REGULATORY STATUS: UA/Mitopure received US FDA GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) status (No Objection letters, GRAS notices around 2018) for use in foods/beverages at defined levels, and is marketed as a dietary supplement / food ingredient — NOT an approved DRUG for any disease. It is sold legally over the counter in the US and many markets as a supplement; it is not a controlled substance and is not banned (no WADA prohibition as a mitophagy nutraceutical). It carries no approved therapeutic indication; all disease claims remain investigational. (Regulatory specifics here are from the supplement/GRAS framing in the cited human-trial and review literature — verify exact GRAS notice numbers separately as they were not retrieved by database tools this run.)"

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does UA produce clinically meaningful functional benefit (not just biomarker shifts) on HARD endpoints? Two pivotal RCTs (PMID 35584623, 35050355) MISSED their primary endpoints (peak power output; 6-min walk / ATP production) while hitting secondary ones — the real-world functional payoff is unresolved.
  • The translational paradox: PBPK modeling (PMID 37195009) predicts achievable free-UA tissue levels are orders of magnitude below the concentrations that drive effects in cell models — so HOW UA produces its in vivo effects (conjugate reservoir? chronic low-dose signaling? a more sensitive in vivo target?) is genuinely unknown.
  • No defined molecular RECEPTOR / no Kd: the proximal sensor UA engages to trigger PINK1 stabilization is uncharacterized (no ChEMBL entry, no binding constant). Is mitophagy induction direct or downstream of a metabolic/ROS shift?
  • Optimal dose and a true human dose-RESPONSE curve are unestablished — trials cluster at 500-1000 mg with no clear monotonic relationship and no head-to-head dose optimization.
  • Long-term safety beyond ~4 months, and the real-world significance of in vitro estrogenic/SERM and genotoxic signals (PMID 37345359, 34884865) in estrogen-sensitive populations, are unknown.
  • Responder heterogeneity: does baseline microbiome producer status, age, training status, or sex modify response? Athlete trials already diverge (PMID 39487653 positive vs 40839339 limited).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a dosing protocol). UA is well tolerated in trials, but a person using it can reasonably watch: (1) GI tolerance — loose stool / mild GI upset is the most common real-world complaint; persistent symptoms warrant stopping. (2) Inflammatory markers — hs-CRP is the biomarker most consistently moved by UA in trials (it fell); tracking CRP gives an objective read on the anti-inflammatory signal (PMID 35584623, 35050355). (3) Metabolic/mitochondrial biomarkers used in studies — plasma acylcarnitines and ceramides fell with UA (research-grade panels, not routine clinic tests). (4) Liver function (LFTs/AST/ALT) and basic metabolic panel as general supplement-safety monitoring, since UA is hepatically conjugated and no long-term human safety data exist. (5) Estrogen-sensitive caution flag — given in vitro SERM/ER activity, individuals with hormone-sensitive conditions (breast/endometrial history) should monitor with and consult their physician rather than self-experiment, even though in vivo exposure is predicted to be below estrogenic thresholds (PMID 37345359, 37195009). (6) Drug interactions — anyone on medications cleared by glucuronidation/sulfation should be alert for altered drug effect (theoretical, from in vitro enzyme inhibition; PMID 37195009). None of these substitute for clinician oversight; UA is a supplement, not an approved therapy."

Caveats

Be precise with this YouTuber: (1) UroA has genuine human RCTs — unusual for a longevity supplement — but the human wins are MODEST mitochondrial-biomarker and endurance effects, not dramatic strength or lifespan gains; trials in trained athletes show weak/mixed results (PMID 39487653, 40839339). (2) The lifespan-extension and broad 'anti-aging' claims come from worms and mice (PMID 27400265) — there is NO human longevity data. (3) Most mechanistic detail (TFEB, NLRP3, organelle crosstalk) is from cells and rodents. (4) Much of the human trial work was sponsored by Amazentis/Timeline, the company selling Mitopure — note the commercial interest. (5) Natural dietary intake is unreliable because UroA production depends on individual gut microbiome composition. (6) Doses in trials were ~500–1000 mg/day of direct UroA; you cannot reliably reach these from food. Bottom line: solid mitochondrial-biology story, real but modest human ergogenic data, and speculative human longevity claims. NOTE: the two main human RCTs were small and did not hit their primary muscle-strength endpoints — benefits are modest/mixed, not established.
