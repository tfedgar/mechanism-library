Yohimbine

Main target: Alpha-2 adrenergic receptors. Backed by human data.

Works in humans?Human evidence
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A stimulant alkaloid that flips the "brake" off fat cells and floods the body with adrenaline's cousin (noradrenaline) — best known as a fasted-cardio fat-loss aid for stubborn lower-body fat, but with mixed human results and a real cardiovascular kick.

Strong human data 26 citations
Class
Indole alkaloid; alpha-2 adrenergic receptor antagonist (sympathomimetic stimulant)
Primary target
Alpha-2 adrenergic receptors (ADRA2A, ADRA2B, ADRA2C) — competitive antagonist
Human status
Used in humans for decades. Yohimbine (as the prescription form yohimbine hydrochloride) was historically used to treat erectile dysfunction; its efficacy there has been reviewed in randomized trials. For FAT LOSS / body composition it is NOT an approved drug — it is sold as an over-the-counter dietary supplement, with no standardized dosing and high product-to-product and person-to-person variability. The fat-mobilizing mechanism is well documented in humans, but controlled human trials on actual fat/weight loss are small, old, and conflicting. Regulatory status varies by country (restricted or banned as an unapproved drug in several jurisdictions). Harm-reduction note: it is deceptively potent, dose-sensitive, and carries genuine cardiovascular/CNS risk — especially when stacked with other stimulants or used by people with hypertension, anxiety disorders, or on clonidine/MAOIs. This package explains mechanism only and is not dosing or medical advice.

At a glance

  • Promotes lipolysis (release of stored fat as free fatty acids), strongest in the fasted state and amplified by exercise (human evidence)
  • Raises circulating noradrenaline by ~40-100% via presynaptic alpha-2 autoreceptor blockade — the dominant in-body driver of its metabolic effects (human evidence)
  • May preferentially affect 'stubborn' alpha-2-receptor-rich fat depots (lower body / lower abdomen) — mechanistically plausible, not proven for spot reduction
  • Effect is abolished by a meal (insulin) and blunted by beta-blockers — confirming it acts through the beta-adrenergic pathway (human evidence)
  • Acute stimulant/ergogenic effects: increased alertness, sympathetic 'fight-or-flight' activation, possible performance benefits in some exercise modes (Evidence-human, mixed)
  • Inconsistent real-world fat-loss/weight-loss outcomes across controlled human trials (Contested)
  • Cardiovascular and CNS side effects: raised heart rate and blood pressure, anxiety/panic, tremor; serious harm (hypertensive crisis, intracranial hemorrhage) reported at high doses or with interactions
  • NOT hepatotoxic in the 17-alpha-alkylated-steroid sense and does not act on androgen receptors — different drug class entirely

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps6 human-evidenced 1 speculative

  1. Two adrenergic 'switches' sit on every fat cell Established

    Fat cells carry two opposing adrenaline-sensing switches. Beta-adrenergic receptors are the 'release fat' accelerator; alpha-2 receptors are the 'hold fat' brake. In some body regions — classically lower-body and lower-abdominal subcutaneous fat — alpha-2 brakes outnumber beta accelerators, which is a leading explanation for why that fat is metabolically 'stubborn' and comes off last. Yohimbine's whole premise is to release the alpha-2 brake. PMID 6105967 (Lafontan & Berlan, Eur J Pharmacol 1980); PMID 1345885 (Lafontan et al., Am J Clin Nutr 1992, review) ; α2-adrenoceptor regional fat distribution: Galitzky/Lafontan

  2. Normally, alpha-2 activation tells the fat cell to keep fat locked in Established

    The alpha-2 receptor is coupled to an inhibitory Gi protein. When adrenaline/noradrenaline hits it, Gi shuts down adenylyl cyclase, which lowers cAMP inside the cell. Low cAMP means protein kinase A (PKA) stays quiet and hormone-sensitive lipase — the enzyme that chops stored triglyceride into free fatty acids — stays switched off. Net result: fat stays in the cell (this is the 'antilipolytic' effect). PMID 6105967 (Lafontan & Berlan 1980); PMID 1345885 (Lafontan et al. 1992 review)

  3. Yohimbine blocks that brake, so the accelerator wins Established

    Yohimbine sits on the alpha-2 receptor and blocks it (it's an antagonist, not an activator). With the Gi brake released, adenylyl cyclase is free to run, cAMP rises, PKA activates hormone-sensitive lipase, and stored fat gets broken down into free fatty acids that exit the cell — lipolysis. In the test tube, yohimbine reliably amplifies adrenaline's fat-releasing effect on human fat cells, and it's the most potent of the classic alpha-2 blockers tested. PMID 2906290 (Galitzky et al., Eur J Clin Invest 1988); PMID 1345885 (Lafontan et al. 1992 review)

  4. The bigger in-body effect: it cranks up your own noradrenaline Evidence-human

    Here is the key nuance most people miss. Yohimbine also blocks alpha-2 autoreceptors on nerve endings. Those autoreceptors are a feedback 'enough already' signal that normally tells nerves to stop dumping noradrenaline. Block them and noradrenaline release climbs (~40-100% rise in plasma noradrenaline in human studies). That extra noradrenaline then hits beta-receptors on fat cells to drive lipolysis. In live humans under fasting conditions, this nerve-driven noradrenaline surge is the MAIN engine of yohimbine's fat-mobilizing effect — direct fat-cell alpha-2 blockade is the minor partner. PMID 2906290 (Galitzky et al. 1988, healthy men); PMID 1885256 (Berlan et al., Int J Obes 1991, women)

  5. This is why fasted, caffeine/beta-pathway-intact, insulin-low conditions matter Evidence-human

    Because yohimbine works THROUGH the beta-adrenergic pathway, anything that blocks beta-receptors (e.g. propranolol) blunts or abolishes its fat-releasing effect — proven directly in humans. And eating a meal completely shut off the effect: insulin is a powerful antilipolytic signal that re-applies the brake yohimbine just released. This is the mechanistic basis for the 'take it fasted, before cardio' practice — exercise adds its own sympathetic drive on top. PMID 2906290 (Galitzky et al. 1988 — propranolol blocked it, food suppressed it, exercise reinforced it)

  6. Mobilizing fat is not the same as losing fat — human results are genuinely mixed Contested

    Releasing fatty acids into the blood only translates to fat loss if they get burned rather than re-stored. Controlled human trials disagree: some show added weight loss on a diet (e.g. ~3.6 kg vs ~2.2 kg on placebo over 3 weeks) and reduced fat mass in athletes, while others found no effect on body weight or fat distribution. So the mechanism is solid but the real-world fat-loss payoff is inconsistent and probably small. Treat 'spot reduction of stubborn fat' as plausible-but-unproven, not established. Positive: PMID 1955308 (Kucio 1991 RCT), PMID 17214405 (Ostojic 2006, soccer players). Null: PMID 1960007 (Sax 1991), PMID 2595649 (Berlin 1989)

  7. Same sympathetic surge that burns fat is what spikes heart rate and anxiety Evidence-human

    The noradrenaline flood doesn't only hit fat cells — it raises sympathetic tone body-wide. That drives the well-documented side effects: elevated heart rate and blood pressure, anxiety/panic, tremor, sweating, nausea. Yohimbine is deceptively potent with high person-to-person variability in absorption, and overdoses or stacking with stimulants (or in people on blood-pressure meds like clonidine) have caused serious events including hypertensive crisis and intracranial hemorrhage. It is directly cardiotoxic to human heart cells at high concentrations in vitro. PMID 20442348 (Kearney et al., poison-control case series); PMID 39728757 (Porrill et al. 2024 review); PMID 34817810 (Gong et al. 2022, human iPSC-cardiomyocytes, in vitro)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Yohimbine is a competitive antagonist (no intrinsic agonist activity) at α2-adrenergic receptors. Verified ChEMBL binding constants (Ki): human α2A ~0.42 nM (pKi ~9.4), human α2B ~2.0 nM (pKi ~8.7); pooled human α2 values cluster 0.4-4 nM; rat α2 0.6-12.6 nM (Established, in vitro radioligand binding). Crucially it is NOT cleanly α2-selective: human α1 subtype affinities are also low-nanomolar (α1D ~1.6 nM, α1B 1.1-7.2 nM, bovine α1A ~3.6 nM), so on human cloned receptors α2-over-α1 selectivity is roughly only ~3-10x, far less than the textbook caricature of a 'pure α2 blocker' (Evidence-in vitro). In rat tissue the α2/α1 separation looks larger (α2 ~5-12 nM vs α1 ~398 nM), a species-dependent discrepancy. It also weakly inhibits/binds human CYP2D6 (Ki ~140-520 nM) and at higher exposures has serotonergic (5-HT1A) and weak dopaminergic actions reported in older literature (Contested). The closely related diastereomer rauwolscine (alpha-yohimbine) and corynanthine are also present in many 'yohimbe bark' products and differ in subtype bias.

Signalling cascade. Mechanism is disinhibition of noradrenergic tone. Presynaptic α2A autoreceptors on noradrenergic neurons are Gi/Go-coupled; their normal job is negative feedback: when synaptic noradrenaline (NA) is high, α2 activation inhibits adenylyl cyclase (lowering cAMP), opens GIRK K+ channels and closes N/P-type Ca2+ channels, shutting down further NA release. By BLOCKING these autoreceptors, yohimbine removes the brake, so NA release from sympathetic terminals and from brainstem locus coeruleus neurons surges. In humans an IV dose triples circulating NA within 15 min and raises the NA metabolite MHPG (Evidence-human, PMID 1493849, PMID 6865647). The downstream consequences are whatever NA does at the now-unopposed postsynaptic receptors: β1 cardiac stimulation (rate/contractility), α1 vasoconstriction, central α1/β arousal (anxiety, tremor, sweating), and in fat tissue removal of the α2 anti-lipolytic brake on adipocytes so that β-adrenergic, Gs/cAMP/PKA/hormone-sensitive-lipase lipolysis runs unopposed (Evidence-human/rodent). A separate, receptor-independent layer exists at high concentrations: direct block of cardiac voltage-gated Na+ and Ca2+ channels (Evidence-in vitro, PMID 34817810).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (hydrochloride salt, e.g. historic Yocon/Yohimex tablets; also widespread in unregulated 'yohimbe bark extract' supplements with highly variable/mislabelled content). IV used in research and veterinary reversal of xylazine sedation.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally absorbed but bioavailability is low and erratic (often cited ~7-87% across individuals; large interindividual variability), driven by extensive first-pass and high systemic extraction. Supplement label content is frequently inaccurate, so real absorbed dose is poorly defined (Evidence-human / Contested).
Half-life
Short and variable. In healthy men given IV, the distribution half-life was 0.4-18 min and the main elimination half-life ranged 0.25-2.5 h, with a slower terminal phase of ~13 h suggested (Evidence-human, PMID 1493849). Commonly summarized as a ~0.5-2 h plasma half-life — clinically short, hence the rapid on/off and tendency to repeat-dose.
Metabolism
Predominantly HEPATIC and extrahepatic oxidative metabolism; total plasma clearance (~117 L/h) EXCEEDS hepatic plasma flow, indicating a high-extraction drug with substantial extrahepatic metabolism (PMID 1493849). CYP2D6 is a major metabolizing enzyme: a human drug-drug-interaction study showed the CYP2D6 inhibitor clomipramine raised yohimbine AUC to ~137% of control, consistent with CYP2D6-mediated clearance (Evidence-human, PMID 39428980). Implication: CYP2D6 poor metabolizers and people on CYP2D6 inhibitors (e.g. SSRIs like fluoxetine/paroxetine, bupropion) can have markedly higher, longer exposure. The principal metabolite is 11-hydroxy-yohimbine.
Excretion
Renal excretion of UNCHANGED yohimbine is trivial — only ~0.5-1% appears in urine (PMID 1493849); elimination is essentially by metabolism.
Notes
Lipophilic, low molecular weight (354 Da, PSA 65.6 A2, 0 Lipinski violations per ChEMBL) — readily crosses the blood-brain barrier, which is why CNS effects (anxiety, arousal, the classic 11C-yohimbine PET noradrenergic tracer use) dominate. Not a prodrug and not esterified. Practical PK problem for users: short half-life + erratic absorption + CYP2D6 pharmacogenetic variability + mislabelled supplement content together make dose-effect highly unpredictable person-to-person.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Human dose-response is only loosely characterized and confounded by erratic bioavailability and inaccurate supplement labeling. Erectile dysfunction trials typically used ~15-30 mg/day (often 5.4 mg three times daily); meta-analyses pooling these found a modest benefit over placebo (Evidence-human, PMID 9649257 OR 3.85; PMID 35118966). Body-composition/fat-loss signals appear around ~20 mg/day: a soccer-player RCT used 20 mg/day for 21 days and saw body fat fall 9.3%->7.1% with no change in weight, muscle or performance (Evidence-human, PMID 17214405, n=20); an obesity RCT using 20 mg/day (5 mg x4) plus a 1000 kcal diet produced ~1.3 kg greater weight loss vs placebo over 3 weeks (Evidence-human, PMID 1955308, n=20). Anxiogenic and pressor effects scale with dose and noradrenergic surge: a single 30 mg dose reliably raised subjective anxiety, blood pressure and plasma MHPG in healthy adults (Evidence-human, PMID 6865647). Per the clinical review, doses in the therapeutic ED range are relatively well tolerated, while clearly higher doses bring blood-pressure elevation, anxiogenesis and urinary frequency (Evidence-human, PMID 11744068). Body-weight (mg/kg) dosing makes lean/low-body-fat users hit sympathetic side effects at supplement 'serving sizes' that others tolerate. Direct cardiac ion-channel toxicity is a HIGH-concentration phenomenon in vitro (Na+ IC50 ~14 uM, Ca2+ IC50 ~140 uM, PMID 34817810); these micromolar levels are far above typical therapeutic plasma concentrations but become relevant in overdose. NO well-powered human dose-ranging study defines a clean efficacy-vs-toxicity dose window — this is a genuine gap (Established gap).

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Rauwolscine (alpha-yohimbine) / corynanthine
    same: Diastereomers/isomers co-occurring in yohimbe bark; all are α-adrenoceptor antagonists with the same indole-alkaloid scaffold and overlapping sympathomimetic/'fat-loss' marketing
    different: Differ in receptor subtype bias — rauwolscine is often cited as more α2-selective/potent at α2, while corynanthine leans α1-antagonist; consumer 'yohimbine' products are uncontrolled mixtures, so the real pharmacology a user gets is product-dependent, not a single clean molecule
  • vs Clonidine / dexmedetomidine / guanfacine (α2 AGONISTS)
    same: Act on the SAME α2 receptors and same noradrenergic feedback circuit
    different: Opposite direction — agonists ENGAGE the autoreceptor brake (lower NA, sedation, lower BP/HR, anxiolysis), and clonidine directly reverses yohimbine-induced anxiety and pressor effects (PMID 6865647). Guanfacine is a partial α2A agonist; head-to-head in obese rats it was a more effective and safer anorectic than yohimbine (PMID 26506439)
  • vs PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil/tadalafil) for ED
    same: Both used for erectile dysfunction
    different: PDE5 inhibitors act peripherally on the NO/cGMP pathway in the corpus cavernosum with large, robust RCT effect sizes and clear regulatory approval; yohimbine works centrally/peripherally via α2 blockade with only MODEST efficacy and a worse cardiovascular/anxiety side-effect profile. The two are mechanistically complementary, and yohimbine's clinical interest is mainly as adjunct/combination, not monotherapy (PMID 11744068)
  • vs Mirtazapine / mianserin (α2-antagonist antidepressants)
    same: Also block α2 (auto- and hetero-) receptors, increasing noradrenergic (and serotonergic) transmission
    different: These are CNS-acting antidepressants whose α2 blockade is paired with strong antihistamine/5-HT2 activity giving sedation and weight GAIN — opposite peripheral clinical profile to yohimbine's stimulant, appetite-suppressing, pressor effects, illustrating that α2 antagonism per se does not equal 'fat burner'

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

"Yohimbine is an indole alkaloid isolated from the bark of the West/Central African tree Pausinystalia johimbe (yohimbe), with a long traditional use as an aphrodisiac. As yohimbine hydrochloride it was a prescription drug in the United States (e.g. Yocon, Yohimex, Aphrodyne) historically indicated for erectile dysfunction/as a sympatholytic, and it remains a veterinary reversal agent for alpha-2 sedatives (xylazine). Its prescription role faded after PDE5 inhibitors arrived (sildenafil 1998). It is NOT FDA-approved as a dietary supplement ingredient; the FDA has never affirmed it as a lawful supplement, yet crude 'yohimbe bark extract' is sold widely in fat-burner/pre-workout and 'male enhancement' products — frequently mislabelled, with actual yohimbine content varying from a fraction to many multiples of the stated dose. Regulators differ: it is banned or restricted as an unapproved/prescription substance in several countries (e.g. restricted in the UK, Australia, Canada and parts of the EU), while remaining loosely available in the US supplement market. In sport, yohimbine is NOT currently on the WADA Prohibited List (a point fitness communities exploit), though many products containing it are contaminated with banned stimulants. Per ChEMBL it sits at clinical 'max phase 3', reflecting its drug—but-not-fully-approved status."

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • No adequately powered human dose-ranging study defines a clean efficacy-vs-toxicity window — the gap between an effective fat-loss/ED dose and the dose that triggers dangerous blood-pressure/anxiety/arrhythmia responses is undefined, especially in lean, low-body-fat users dosing by mg/kg.
  • True oral bioavailability and steady-state exposure in real-world supplement users is unknown because product content is erratic and mislabelled; published PK comes mostly from IV studies and tightly controlled tablets.
  • How much of the cardiovascular risk is NA-mediated (β1/α1) versus the α2-INDEPENDENT direct Na+/Ca2+ channel block (PMID 34817810) at human-achievable concentrations — the in-vitro toxic IC50s are micromolar, but the overdose threshold in vivo is not established.
  • Clinical impact of CYP2D6 pharmacogenetics: poor metabolizers and patients on CYP2D6-inhibiting SSRIs may get much higher exposure, but no outcome study quantifies the added adverse-event risk.
  • Whether yohimbine produces meaningful net fat LOSS in already-lean, exercising humans (vs merely acutely mobilizing fatty acids that get re-esterified) — the controlled human fat-loss evidence is small (n~20) and the one trial measuring glycerol found no lipolysis signal (PMID 1955308).
  • Long-term safety of chronic use is essentially unstudied; almost all human data are single-dose or weeks-long small trials, with chronic real-world safety inferred only from adverse-event surveillance of contaminated supplements.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (not a protocol). Cardiovascular: blood pressure and heart rate are the priority — yohimbine reliably raises both via noradrenergic surge, and pre-existing hypertension, arrhythmia or structural heart disease is a clear red flag; palpitations, chest pain, or an irregular pulse warrant stopping and medical review (mechanistic basis: NA/β1 drive plus direct Na+/Ca2+ channel effects, PMID 6865647, PMID 34817810). Neuropsychiatric: watch for anxiety, panic attacks, tremor, insomnia, agitation — yohimbine is a known panicogen and is especially risky in anyone with panic disorder, generalized anxiety or PTSD (PMID 6865647). Drug-interaction screen: flag CYP2D6 inhibitors and serotonergic/noradrenergic drugs (SSRIs/SNRIs, bupropion, MAOIs, stimulants, other sympathomimetics) which can raise exposure or stack adrenergic load (PMID 39428980); MAOI co-use is particularly dangerous. Because clearance is CYP2D6-dependent, individuals respond very differently to the same dose. Practical signs to watch: rising resting BP/HR, sweating, nausea, urinary frequency, anxiety. Bloodwork is not specifically diagnostic, but in anyone using it chronically a sensible baseline includes blood pressure logs and, where cardiovascular symptoms arise, an ECG; product testing/third-party certification matters because mislabelled content is a primary driver of accidental overdose. None of this constitutes dosing guidance — the safest monitoring conclusion for at-risk individuals (cardiac, hypertensive, anxiety-prone, pregnant, on interacting meds) is avoidance."

Caveats

CRITICAL DISTINCTION: 'mobilizing' fat (what the mechanism studies measure — glycerol/free-fatty-acid release) is NOT the same as 'losing' fat (net body-fat reduction). Yohimbine's lipolytic mechanism is well-established (Established), but whether that reliably produces meaningful fat loss in real people is Contested — human RCTs split between modest benefit and no effect. The dominant in-vivo mechanism is indirect (boosting your own noradrenaline via nerve-ending autoreceptor blockade → beta-receptor activation), NOT primarily direct fat-cell alpha-2 blockade — a point often oversimplified in fitness content. The 'spot reduction of stubborn lower-body fat' idea rests on real regional differences in alpha-2 vs beta receptor density, but direct human proof that yohimbine selectively strips those depots is lacking — label it plausible, not proven. The whole effect is switched off by food (insulin) and blunted by beta-blockers. Cardiotoxicity data (PMID 34817810) is in-vitro (human heart cells in a dish) at high concentrations — informative for the upper-dose risk picture, not a claim about normal supplement doses. Several human studies cited are decades old and small. Two adrenergic 'switches' sit on every fat cell. Fat cells carry two opposing adrenaline-sensing switches. Beta-adrenergic receptors are the 'release fat' accelerator; alpha-2 receptors are the 'hold fat' brake. In some body regions — classically lower-body and lower-abdominal subcutaneous fat — alpha-2 brakes outnumber beta accelerators, which is a leading explanation for why that fat is metabolically 'stubborn' and comes off last. Yohimbine's whole premise is to release the alpha-2 brake.
