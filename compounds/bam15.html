BAM15

Main target: Inner mitochondrial membrane. Evidence is animal- and lab-only — no human trials yet.

EvidenceRodent / preclinical
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

The "designer" mitochondrial uncoupler pitched as a safer DNP: it makes cells burn fuel less efficiently by leaking protons across the inner mitochondrial membrane, with an apparently wider safety margin in rodents — but zero human data.

Rodent / preclinical 14 citations
Class
Small-molecule mitochondrial protonophore uncoupler (mild/self-limiting); furazano[3,4-b]pyrazine (1,2,5-oxadiazolo[3,4-e]pyrazine) diarylamine
Primary target
Inner mitochondrial membrane — acts as a lipophilic weak-acid protonophore that shuttles H+ into the matrix independent of ATP synthase, dissipating the proton-motive force (Δp)
Human status
No human data. BAM15 has never been tested in a registered clinical trial: a ClinicalTrials.gov intervention search for 'BAM15' returns only one unrelated study (NCT04634409, Eli Lilly's BLAZE-4 bamlanivimab/monoclonal-antibody COVID-19 trial), and the compound itself has no NCT record. It is not approved by any regulator, has no INN, and returns no record in ChEMBL (confirmed: ChEMBL v34 compound search for BAM15 = 0 hits), i.e. it is not catalogued as a clinical-phase drug. All efficacy and safety evidence is preclinical: cell culture, isolated mitochondria/bilayers, C. elegans, and rodents (mice, rats). It was discovered in 2013-2014 (Kenwood et al., PMID 24634817; University of Virginia / Virginia Tech) and the metabolic proof-of-concept papers appeared in 2020 (Alexopoulos, Nat Commun; Axelrod, EMBO Mol Med). It circulates in the biohacker/PED/longevity community as a grey-market research chemical positioned as a 'next-generation, safer DNP'; that safety framing rests entirely on rodent comparisons and has NOT been demonstrated in humans, where pharmacokinetics, the effective-vs-toxic window, drug interactions, and chronic-exposure effects are all unknown. (For context, a different, engineered liver-targeted uncoupler, TLC-6740/OrsoBio, HAS entered human trials — NCT05822544, Phase 1/1b in healthy subjects and obesity, including a tirzepatide combination arm — but that is a separate molecule, not BAM15.)

At a glance

  • Mild, mitochondria-selective uncoupling: raises respiration and heat production while lowering ATP-coupling efficiency (Established, cell/isolated-mito data)
  • Usually lowers mitochondrial ROS/oxidative stress in mild metabolic exposure — but this reverses with cell type and higher dose (Evidence-rodent / Contested overall)
  • Potent AMPK activation → increased glucose uptake and fat oxidation (Evidence-rodent in vivo, plus in vitro potency)
  • Reduces fat mass, hepatic steatosis and reverses insulin resistance in obese/diabetic mice without cutting food intake or lean mass (Evidence-rodent)
  • Did NOT raise core body temperature at effective doses in the diet-induced-obesity mouse study — the key claimed advantage over DNP (Evidence-rodent)
  • Tissue protection in acute-injury models: renal ischemia-reperfusion, sepsis/AKI (Evidence-rodent / Evidence-in vitro)
  • Anti-tumor / immune-modulating signals in breast-cancer models, especially at low dose (Evidence-rodent / Evidence-in vitro — early, not a validated indication)
  • Neuroprotection and modest MEAN-lifespan extension in C. elegans; muscle-quality preservation via PINK1 mitophagy in aged mice (Evidence-invertebrate / Evidence-rodent — the basis of longevity hype)
  • NO human efficacy or safety data of any kind — no BAM15 clinical trials, not approved anywhere (Established fact about the evidence gap)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps3 human / established 5 rodent / preclinical

  1. Weak-acid protonophore inserts into the inner mitochondrial membrane Established

    BAM15 is a lipophilic weak acid. In its protonated form it dissolves into the lipid bilayer of the inner mitochondrial membrane, then releases its proton on the matrix side and shuttles back as the anion — cycling protons across the membrane. Direct planar-bilayer and liposome electrophysiology confirmed it behaves as a classical anionic protonophore, with proton-carrying potency close to the reference uncoupler CCCP, and its activity drops when membrane dipole potential is lowered (the hallmark of a true protonophore). Firsov 2021 Bioelectrochemistry PMID 32971482; Kenwood 2014 Mol Metab PMID 24634817

  2. Proton leak dissipates the proton-motive force and depolarizes mitochondria Established

    Normally the electron transport chain pumps protons out of the matrix, building an electrochemical gradient (the proton-motive force, part voltage/membrane potential and part pH). BAM15 gives protons a back-door route in, collapsing that gradient. In isolated rat-liver mitochondria BAM15 collapsed the membrane potential, increased respiration and triggered calcium efflux — confirming genuine depolarization (at concentrations slightly higher than the equipotent CCCP). Firsov 2021 PMID 32971482; Kenwood 2014 PMID 24634817

  3. Uncoupling: fuel oxidation is disconnected from ATP synthesis, so respiration speeds up and energy is lost as heat Established

    Because protons leak back in without passing through ATP synthase, the chain runs faster to try to rebuild the gradient, but less of that effort makes ATP — the extra energy is dissipated as heat. Measured as a higher oxygen consumption rate (respiration) with lower coupling efficiency. In cultured cells BAM15 raised maximal respiration even more than the equally potent FCCP while being less cytotoxic. Kenwood 2014 PMID 24634817; Axelrod 2020 EMBO Mol Med PMID 32519812

  4. Mitochondrial (not plasma-membrane) selectivity — the feature that distinguishes it from FCCP/CCCP Evidence-in vitro

    The reason BAM15 was singled out in the original screen (Kenwood 2014): unlike FCCP and 2,4-DNP, it does NOT depolarize the plasma membrane at uncoupling concentrations, so it avoids a class of off-target effects (ion dysregulation, cytotoxicity) that plague older uncouplers. Separately, its uncoupling appears partly self-limiting: in isolated rat-liver mitochondria the effect was partially reversed by carboxyatractyloside (an ANT inhibitor) — a 'surprising' finding reported by Firsov 2021 (not in the original Kenwood paper) implying the adenine nucleotide translocase contributes to proton entry, a plausible molecular basis for 'mild', ceiling-limited uncoupling rather than the runaway depolarization seen with DNP. Note: in molluscan neurons and bacteria at higher concentrations BAM15 CAN depolarize plasma membranes (more slowly than CCCP), so 'selectivity' is relative and concentration-dependent, not absolute. Kenwood 2014 PMID 24634817; Firsov 2021 PMID 32971482

  5. Mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (ROS): usually lowered by mild uncoupling, but context/concentration-dependent Evidence-rodent (ROS-lowering); Contested/context-dependent overall

    A partly-collapsed gradient lets electrons flow through the chain more freely and spend less time in states that leak electrons onto oxygen to form superoxide, so mild uncoupling typically LOWERS mitochondrial ROS. In diet-induced-obese mice BAM15 had 'strong antioxidant effects', and in a sepsis/kidney-injury model it dissipated the gradient WITHOUT generating mtROS and reduced downstream oxidative damage and mtDNA release. IMPORTANT nuance: this is not uniform — in A10 vascular smooth-muscle cells BAM15 actually INCREASED mitochondrial ROS (Tai 2018), and at higher doses it drove ROS accumulation and apoptosis in cancer cells (Zunica 2021). So 'lowers ROS' holds for mild exposure in the metabolic models but reverses with cell type and dose. Alexopoulos 2020 Nat Commun PMID 32409697; Tsuji 2023 J Clin Invest PMID 36757801; Tai 2018 PMID 30505660; Zunica 2021 PMID 34627389

  6. Falling ATP / rising AMP:ATP activates AMPK — the metabolic 'low-fuel' switch Evidence-rodent (in vivo); Evidence-in vitro (cell potency)

    Because ATP output per unit fuel drops, the cell's AMP:ATP ratio rises, switching on AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK). AMPK ramps up glucose uptake and fat oxidation and dials down anabolism. In vascular smooth-muscle cells BAM15 activated AMPK more potently than CCCP, niclosamide, metformin, or AICAR; in obese/diabetic mice sustained AMPK activation tracked with improved insulin action and glucose control. Tai 2018 Acta Pharm Sin B PMID 30505660; Axelrod 2020 PMID 32519812

  7. Increased energy expenditure and nutrient 'burn' → less fat, better insulin sensitivity (in rodents) Evidence-rodent

    At the whole-body level in mice, the futile proton cycling raises energy expenditure and forces tissues to oxidize more glucose and fat. Across independent labs BAM15 reduced fat mass, hepatic fat and insulin resistance, largely WITHOUT reducing food intake or lean mass and, in the diet-induced-obesity work, without raising core body temperature at effective doses — a profile deliberately contrasted with DNP. Alexopoulos 2020 PMID 32409697; Axelrod 2020 PMID 32519812; Shah 2025 Mol Metab PMID 40639664

  8. Secondary adaptive signaling: mitophagy/quality control (aged mice), plus lifespan/neuroprotection (mostly C. elegans) Evidence-rodent (mouse mitophagy); Evidence-invertebrate (C. elegans lifespan)

    Mild uncoupling doubles as a mitochondrial-stress signal: in aged obese mice BAM15 activated PINK1-driven mitochondrial quality control (mitophagy), increased muscle mass/strength and limited ER stress and apoptosis in muscle (Dantas 2022, rodent). In the invertebrate C. elegans it reduced age-related mechanosensory neurodegeneration and modestly extended MEAN lifespan (mirroring older DNP data; Cho 2022). These are the findings the longevity/biohacker community leans on — but they are invertebrate and rodent, not human, and mechanistically downstream/indirect. Dantas 2022 J Cachexia Sarcopenia Muscle PMID 35304976; Cho 2022 Metabolites PMID 36422268

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. BAM15 = (2-fluorophenyl){6-[(2-fluorophenyl)amino](1,2,5-oxadiazolo[3,4-e]pyrazin-5-yl)}amine; a furazano[3,4-b]pyrazine diarylamine. It has no classical protein receptor — its 'target' is the lipid inner mitochondrial membrane, where it functions as a mobile anionic protonophore. It is a lipophilic weak acid: the neutral (protonated) form crosses the membrane, deprotonates on the matrix side, and the delocalized-charge anion returns, completing a proton-carrying cycle. Biophysically validated on planar bilayers and liposomes with proton conductance comparable to CCCP, and activity suppressed by lowering membrane dipole potential (definitive protonophore signature; Firsov 2021). A partial dependence on the adenine nucleotide translocase (ANT) was revealed in that same study because carboxyatractyloside partially reversed BAM15 uncoupling in isolated rat-liver mitochondria — suggesting ANT participates in proton conductance and helps make the uncoupling self-limiting ('mild'). This ANT/carboxyatractyloside finding is from Firsov 2021, not the original Kenwood 2014 paper.

Signalling cascade. Membrane proton leak -> collapse of proton-motive force / mitochondrial depolarization -> ATP synthase bypassed (oxidation uncoupled from phosphorylation) -> compensatory rise in electron transport / O2 consumption with heat dissipation, and (in mild metabolic exposure) lower ROS -> falling ATP raises AMP:ATP -> AMPK activation -> increased glucose uptake, fatty-acid oxidation, and (as a stress signal) PINK1-mediated mitophagy / mitochondrial quality control. Whole-organism readouts (rodent): higher energy expenditure, fat and hepatic-lipid loss, improved insulin sensitivity.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (orally bioavailable in mice; used as chow admixture ~0.1-0.2% w/w or by gavage in studies). No human route established.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally bioavailable in mice/rats; exact %F not defined in humans (no human data). Highly lipophilic, which aids absorption but complicates formulation.
Half-life
Not established in humans. Preclinical work implies limited exposure requiring repeated or dietary dosing to sustain effect; medicinal-chemistry follow-ups (imidazo[4,5-b]pyridine analogs derived from BAM15, PMID 39406121) were made specifically to improve PK, implying BAM15's own PK is suboptimal.
Metabolism
Detailed human metabolic pathways not characterized. Tissue-distribution work (LC-MS/MS) in tumor-bearing mice (Zunica 2021) shows BAM15 accumulating in adipose, liver and tumor tissue with notably LOW abundance in skeletal muscle — a distribution that may favor metabolic-tissue targeting.
Excretion
Not characterized in humans; no clinical clearance/half-life data.
Notes
Key translational caveat: rodent PK does not predict human PK. High lipophilicity and formulation difficulty are cited as barriers to clinical development. Because effect is driven by concentration at the mitochondrial membrane, PK variability directly translates into variability in the benefit-vs-toxicity window — a core reason grey-market use is hazardous.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Uncoupling is inherently dose-dependent and biphasic in consequence: mild uncoupling is beneficial (respiration up, ROS generally down, AMPK on, metabolic improvement), whereas excessive uncoupling fully collapses the membrane potential, crashes ATP, drives ROS/apoptosis, and causes hyperthermia and cell death. BAM15's selling point is a claimed WIDER window than DNP/FCCP. In cells, BAM15 stimulated higher maximal respiration than the equally-potent FCCP with less cytotoxicity (Kenwood 2014). In a head-to-head of 15 uncouplers in CHO-K1 cells and db/db mice, BAM15 had among the best in-vitro dose-tolerance ranges and, in vivo, dose-dependently improved body weight and metabolic parameters (0.2% > 0.15% w/w chow), with 0.2% giving significant gains in body weight, fat-pad weight, glucose tolerance, blood glucose, HbA1c, liver weight and triglyceride content — while several other uncouplers showed off-target mitochondrial inhibition (Shah 2025 PMID 40639664). CRITICAL: these are rodent dietary exposures; no human dose-response, no established human therapeutic window, and no safety margin exists. The comparison-paper authors explicitly warn uncouplers must NOT be generalized — each behaves differently.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs 2,4-Dinitrophenol (DNP)
    same: Both are lipophilic weak-acid protonophore uncouplers that dissipate the proton-motive force and increase energy expenditure/fat loss; both reduce neurodegeneration/extend lifespan in C. elegans.
    different: DNP also depolarizes the PLASMA membrane and has an extremely narrow therapeutic window — it causes fatal hyperthermia via 'runaway uncoupling' and continues to kill grey-market users (PMID 41487961). BAM15 is mitochondria-selective at uncoupling concentrations, partly self-limiting (ANT-dependent per Firsov 2021), and did not raise body temperature at effective rodent doses — a wider window in RODENTS. This does NOT make BAM15 proven-safe in humans.
  • vs FCCP / CCCP (lab protonophores)
    same: Comparable protonophoric potency; used as bioenergetic tools to measure maximal respiration.
    different: FCCP/CCCP depolarize the plasma membrane, inhibit mitochondria off-target, and are markedly more cytotoxic; BAM15 gives higher maximal respiration with less cytotoxicity and is orally bioavailable in vivo, whereas FCCP/CCCP are essentially lab-only reagents. (FCCP itself causes hepatic, pancreatic and reproductive toxicity in repeated-dose rat studies — PMID 37779591.)
  • vs OPC-163493 (Otsuka, liver-localized uncoupler) / TLC-6740 (OrsoBio)
    same: Same 'next-gen safer uncoupler' rationale for metabolic disease; all aim to widen the window vs DNP.
    different: OPC-163493 and TLC-6740 are engineered/liver-targeted, and TLC-6740 has entered human clinical development (NCT05822544, Phase 1/1b in healthy subjects and obesity, including a tirzepatide combination arm), whereas BAM15 has NO clinical program and remains a research tool / grey-market chemical. In head-to-head rodent work (Shah 2025) BAM15 gave the best overall metabolic outcomes among the tested uncouplers (including outperforming OPC-163493 doses), but OPC-163493/TLC-6740 are further along the regulatory path.
  • vs Endogenous UCP1 (brown-fat uncoupling protein)
    same: Both uncouple respiration to generate heat and can improve systemic glucose/lipid metabolism.
    different: UCP1 is a regulated, tissue-restricted protein channel switched on physiologically; BAM15 is an unregulated chemical protonophore acting on ALL mitochondria it reaches — no physiological on/off control, so the safety burden falls entirely on dose and PK. BAM15's C. elegans lifespan effect persisted in ucp-4 mutants, confirming it works independently of endogenous UCP machinery.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

BAM15 was discovered in 2013-2014 by Kenwood, Hoehn, Santos and colleagues (University of Virginia / Virginia Tech) in a screen explicitly seeking a mitochondrial uncoupler lacking the plasma-membrane off-target effects of FCCP/DNP (PMID 24634817). Metabolic proof-of-concept in obese mice followed in 2020 from two groups (UNSW/Sydney — Alexopoulos, Nat Commun; and Pennington Biomedical — Axelrod, EMBO Mol Med), driving interest in uncoupling as a metabolic-disease strategy. It has NEVER been approved by the FDA, EMA, or any regulator, has no INN, and is not catalogued in ChEMBL as a clinical-phase drug (ChEMBL v34 returns no BAM15 record). No registered human clinical trial of BAM15 exists (ClinicalTrials.gov has no BAM15 interventional study; the sole search hit, NCT04634409, is an unrelated Lilly COVID monoclonal-antibody trial). It is sold internationally as a 'research chemical' and adopted in biohacker/PED/longevity circles as a putatively safer successor to DNP — positioning motivated by rodent data but legally and clinically unvalidated. Medicinal-chemistry successors (imidazo[4,5-b]pyridine and other BAM15-derived scaffolds, PMID 39406121) are being pursued for MASH/metabolic disease, and the broader class has clinical torch-bearers in liver-targeted uncouplers (OPC-163493; TLC-6740, NCT05822544).

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Human pharmacokinetics: bioavailability, half-life, tissue distribution, metabolism and clearance are all uncharacterized in people.
  • Human therapeutic window: the dose separating beneficial mild uncoupling from dangerous hyperthermia/ATP crisis is unknown; rodent margins may not translate.
  • Chronic-exposure safety: effects of months-to-years of mild uncoupling on heart, brain, reproductive function, and mitochondrial adaptation are unstudied.
  • Whether the rodent metabolic and lifespan benefits reproduce in humans at all (most preclinical metabolic candidates fail).
  • Direction of the ROS effect in humans: BAM15 lowers ROS in some models but RAISES it in others (Tai 2018; Zunica 2021) — net human redox effect unknown.
  • Drug and supplement interactions — especially with other stimulants, thermogenics, or drugs affecting mitochondrial function, hepatic metabolism, or thermoregulation.
  • Real-world product identity/purity of grey-market 'BAM15', and whether sold material is even the correct compound.
  • Quantitative contribution and physiological consequence of the ANT-dependence of uncoupling in intact humans.
  • Long-term consequences of sustained AMPK activation and forced mitophagy in healthy (non-diseased) tissue.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

HARM-REDUCTION MONITORING ONLY — this is NOT a protocol and NOT an endorsement of use; no doses are provided. Because BAM15 is an unapproved uncoupler with no human safety data, the only responsible stance is medical involvement and awareness of class red-flags. EMERGENCY red flags of uncoupler toxicity (seek urgent medical care — this can progress to death rapidly and sometimes with delay): rising body temperature/feeling overheated, profuse unexplained sweating, fast heart rate, rapid breathing, flushing, agitation, dark urine or muscle pain (rhabdomyolysis), nausea/vomiting, confusion. These mirror documented DNP poisoning (PMID 41487961). General clinical monitoring a physician would consider if exposure is disclosed: core temperature, heart rate and hydration/electrolytes; liver function tests (uncouplers concentrate in and can injure the liver); renal function and creatine kinase (rhabdomyolysis risk); acid-base status (metabolic acidosis). Contextual cautions: heat, exertion, dehydration, fever, and other thermogenic/stimulant drugs all narrow the safety window and compound risk. There is no antidote for uncoupler overdose — management is supportive (aggressive cooling, fluids, glucose, ICU care), which is why prevention and clinician involvement matter far more than any monitoring checklist. Anyone who has used a research-chemical uncoupler and feels unwell should treat it as a medical emergency and disclose the exposure.

Caveats

CRITICAL SAFETY CONTEXT — this is a mechanism-library entry, not an endorsement or a how-to. (1) NO HUMAN DATA: every benefit listed is from cells, worms, or rodents; none has been reproduced in people, and preclinical metabolic wins routinely fail to translate. (2) 'SAFER THAN DNP' IS UNPROVEN IN HUMANS: BAM15's better therapeutic index vs DNP/FCCP is real in rodents and mechanistically plausible (plasma-membrane sparing at uncoupling concentrations, partly self-limiting/ANT-dependent uncoupling), but it is still a mitochondrial uncoupler — the same drug class that killed people as a 1930s diet pill and continues to cause fatal poisonings today (DNP). Any uncoupler, if the dose exceeds the window, can cause runaway hypermetabolism: dangerous hyperthermia, tachycardia, profuse sweating, metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, and multi-organ failure — sometimes with a delayed, irreversible crash (documented DNP 'runaway uncoupling', PMID 41487961). (3) GREY-MARKET PRODUCT RISK: material sold online as 'BAM15' has no pharmaceutical quality control — identity, purity, and dose accuracy are unverified, compounding an already narrow-window drug. (4) UNKNOWN CHRONIC EFFECTS: sustained uncoupling could impair processes that need a strong membrane potential (e.g., reproductive, cardiac, or neuronal function under stress); long-term human consequences are simply not characterized. (5) ROS effects are not uniformly beneficial — BAM15 lowered ROS in metabolic models but RAISED mitochondrial ROS in some cells (Tai 2018) and at higher/cancer doses (Zunica 2021). (6) This entry gives NO dosing and NO protocol — see the monitoring section for harm-reduction red flags only. Anyone considering or having used research-chemical uncouplers should involve a clinician; acute uncoupler toxicity is a medical emergency.

Weak-acid protonophore inserts into the inner mitochondrial membrane. BAM15 is a lipophilic weak acid. In its protonated form it dissolves into the lipid bilayer of the inner mitochondrial membrane, then releases its proton on the matrix side and shuttles back as the anion — cycling protons across the membrane. Direct planar-bilayer and liposome electrophysiology confirmed it behaves as a classical anionic protonophore, with proton-carrying potency close to the reference uncoupler CCCP, and its activity drops when membrane dipole potential is lowered (the hallmark of a true protonophore). Firsov 2021 Bioelectrochemistry PMID 32971482; Kenwood 2014 Mol Metab PMID 24634817
Mild, mitochondria-selective uncoupling: raises respiration and heat production while lowering ATP-coupling efficiency (Established, cell/isolated-mito data)
