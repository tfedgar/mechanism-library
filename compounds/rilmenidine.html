Rilmenidine

A decades-old, well-tolerated blood-pressure pill that a 2023 worm study flagged as an oral "caloric-restriction mimetic" — it extends lifespan in C. elegans through the nischarin/I1-imidazoline receptor, but there is zero human longevity data.

Rodent / preclinical 16 citations
Class
Centrally-acting antihypertensive; second-generation I1-imidazoline receptor agonist (oxazoline class)
Primary target
Nischarin (the mammalian I1-imidazoline receptor / IRAS; worm ortholog nish-1)
Human status
APPROVED as an antihypertensive (marketed in Europe/Australia/parts of Asia since ~1988 as Hyperium/Albarel; never FDA-approved in the US). Human safety and blood-pressure efficacy at antihypertensive doses are well established over decades of use. However, its geroprotective / lifespan-extending activity is documented ONLY in C. elegans, with a supporting caloric-restriction-like transcriptional signature in mouse liver and kidney (Bennett et al. 2023, PMID 36670049). There are NO human trials of rilmenidine for longevity, healthspan, or aging endpoints (a ClinicalTrials.gov search for rilmenidine in aging/longevity/healthspan returns zero studies), no mammalian survival data, and no evidence that any human dose reproduces the worm lifespan effect. Treat the anti-aging claim as an intriguing preclinical hypothesis, not a demonstrated human benefit.

At a glance

  • Lowers blood pressure by reducing central sympathetic outflow from the rostral ventrolateral medulla (Established, human)
  • Extends both mean and maximum lifespan in C. elegans (an invertebrate) when started at young OR old age — nematode-only finding (Speculative for humans)
  • Improves healthspan and stress resilience (heat, oxidative) in C. elegans — nematode-only finding (Speculative for humans)
  • Induces a gene-expression signature overlapping caloric restriction in worms, and separately in mouse liver/kidney tissue (mouse data are transcriptomic only) (Evidence-rodent, transcriptomic surrogate — not survival)
  • In worms, the longevity benefit requires autophagy plus the FOXO/DAF-16 and NRF/SKN-1 transcription factors, and is NOT additive with dietary restriction, reduced TORC1, or rapamycin — nematode genetics (Speculative for humans)
  • Fewer sedation/dry-mouth effects than clonidine due to relative I1 (vs alpha-2) selectivity (Established, human)
  • No demonstrated human anti-aging, healthspan, or metabolic-longevity effect (key unknown)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Drug binds the I1-imidazoline receptor (nischarin/IRAS), not primarily alpha-2 Established

    Rilmenidine is an agonist at the I1-imidazoline binding site. Unlike clonidine, it has substantially higher affinity for I1 sites than for alpha-2 adrenoceptors (roughly 30-fold I1 selectivity vs clonidine's ~4-fold), which is why it causes less sedation and dry mouth. The molecular I1 receptor is now identified as nischarin (also called IRAS, imidazoline-receptor antisera-selected protein). PMID 29962350 (Cobos-Puc & Aguayo-Morales 2019, Cardiovasc Hematol Disord Drug Targets); PMID 10984201 (Prichard & Graham 2000, Drugs Aging); PMID 10595861 (Bousquet & Feldman 1999, Drugs)

  2. In the brainstem, I1 agonism reduces sympathetic outflow Established

    Acting on I1 receptors in the rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM), rilmenidine dampens the sympathetic nervous system's drive to the heart and blood vessels. Agmatine is regarded as the endogenous ligand for these receptors, and the net effect is lowered peripheral vascular resistance and blood pressure. This is the classic, human-validated antihypertensive mechanism. PMID 29962350 (Cobos-Puc & Aguayo-Morales 2019); PMID 10984201 (Prichard & Graham 2000, Drugs Aging)

  3. Nischarin is a real signaling scaffold, independent of blood pressure Established

    Beyond the brainstem, nischarin is a cytoplasmic scaffold protein that binds integrins (alpha-5) and PAK1 and interacts with the tumor-suppressor kinase LKB1, influencing cell migration, adhesion and downstream signaling. This established cell-biology role (characterized mainly in breast-cancer cell lines and mouse xenografts) gives the receptor a plausible route to influence metabolism and stress pathways — the bridge the geroscience hypothesis rests on. Note this is a separate literature from the longevity claim. PMID 21917605 (Baranwal et al. 2011, JNCI); PMID 23572524 (Jain et al. 2013, J Biol Chem); PMID 26392073 (Maziveyi & Alahari 2015, Cancer Res)

  4. Rilmenidine produces a caloric-restriction-like transcriptional signature Evidence-rodent

    Researchers first screened for approved drugs whose gene-expression fingerprint resembled that of caloric restriction, using a signature-matching framework built from mouse longevity-intervention data. Rilmenidine scored as a match. When given to C. elegans, and separately when given to mice, it shifted gene expression in a direction overlapping the CR program — in mice this was seen in liver and kidney tissue only. PMID 36670049 (Bennett et al. 2023, Aging Cell); PMID 31353263 (Tyshkovskiy et al. 2019, Cell Metab — the signature framework)

  5. In worms, this extends lifespan via the nischarin ortholog nish-1 Speculative

    Treating C. elegans with rilmenidine at either young or older ages increased lifespan. Deleting nish-1 (the worm nischarin/I1 receptor) abolished the lifespan, healthspan and stress-resilience benefits — genetically pinning the effect in the worm to that receptor rather than an off-target action. This is an invertebrate result; whether the same receptor-dependence holds in mammals is untested. PMID 36670049 (Bennett et al. 2023, Aging Cell)

  6. The worm longevity effect runs through FOXO/DAF-16, NRF/SKN-1 and autophagy Speculative

    Rilmenidine's lifespan extension in worms required the transcription factors DAF-16 (FOXO) and SKN-1 (NRF1/2/3) and depended on functional autophagy — the same machinery central to dietary restriction. AMPK, by contrast, was NOT required, which distinguishes it from metformin-type mechanisms. All of this is C. elegans genetics; it has not been shown to govern any mammalian aging outcome. PMID 36670049 (Bennett et al. 2023, Aging Cell)

  7. In worms it converges on the same pathway as dietary restriction / TOR inhibition Speculative

    Adding rilmenidine on top of already calorically-restricted worms, or worms with reduced TORC1 function, or worms treated with rapamycin, gave NO further lifespan gain. This 'non-additivity' is the strongest argument that in the worm rilmenidine acts as a genuine CR/mTOR-axis mimetic rather than through a separate route — but it also implies it is unlikely to stack benefits on top of those interventions. Again, invertebrate-only. PMID 36670049 (Bennett et al. 2023, Aging Cell)

  8. Whether any of this translates to mammalian lifespan is unproven Speculative

    The mouse data show only a CR-like transcriptional signature in two organs, NOT extended survival or healthspan. No mammalian lifespan study, and no human aging trial, has been done. The leap from a worm lifespan curve to a human anti-aging drug is exactly where most geroscience candidates fail; the authors themselves frame the findings as warranting 'fresh lines of inquiry.' PMID 36670049 (Bennett et al. 2023, Aging Cell)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Agonist at the I1-imidazoline receptor, molecularly identified as nischarin/IRAS (worm ortholog nish-1). Key feature vs clonidine: markedly higher affinity for I1-imidazoline sites than for alpha-2 adrenoceptors (~30-fold I1 selectivity vs clonidine's ~4-fold), giving a cleaner sympatholytic profile with less sedation/dry mouth. Nischarin is also a cytoplasmic scaffold binding alpha-5 integrin, PAK1 and LKB1, linking it to cell-migration/adhesion and tumor-suppressor signaling independent of blood pressure (characterized in breast-cancer models).

Signalling cascade. Antihypertensive arm (human-established): I1 agonism in the rostral ventrolateral medulla (RVLM) reduces central sympathetic outflow -> lower peripheral vascular resistance -> lower BP (agmatine is the putative endogenous I1 ligand). Geroscience arm (C. elegans, with mouse transcriptomic support): nischarin/nish-1 engagement is associated with a caloric-restriction-like transcriptional program that in worms requires FOXO/DAF-16 and NRF/SKN-1 and is executed via autophagy; it is non-additive with dietary restriction, reduced TORC1, or rapamycin, and does NOT require AMPK. The pathway dependence is demonstrated only in the nematode.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapidly and nearly completely absorbed after oral dosing; absolute oral bioavailability close to 1 (~100%), with time-to-peak about 2 hours and no meaningful presystemic (first-pass) metabolism.
Half-life
Approximately 8 hours in healthy adults (supporting once- to twice-daily antihypertensive dosing); prolonged (~30-42 h) in renal insufficiency and modestly prolonged in the elderly.
Metabolism
Minimally metabolized — largely not dependent on hepatic cytochrome P450; the parent drug predominates and few/no metabolites are detected in plasma, so few CYP-mediated drug interactions are expected.
Excretion
Predominantly renal; roughly two-thirds of clearance is renal and about 65% of a dose is excreted as unchanged drug in urine. Clearance falls and half-life lengthens markedly with renal impairment (terminal half-life ~30-42 h in chronic renal failure vs ~8 h in health), with accumulation in chronic kidney disease and dialysis patients, so caution is warranted in renal impairment.
Notes
Low plasma protein binding (<10%); apparent volume of distribution ~5 L/kg (~315 L). These PK parameters are established from 1980s-90s human studies (single 1 mg doses). NONE of these PK studies address the exposures needed for any geroprotective effect — the worm/mouse work did not define a human-equivalent geroprotective dose.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

For BLOOD PRESSURE (established, human): a dose-effect relationship exists across the licensed antihypertensive range, with the antihypertensive effect near-maximal at the low end and tolerability favoring the lower dose (PMID 8223838, Luccioni 1993; PMID 2894164, Ostermann 1988 — best efficacy/acceptability at the lowest effective dose). For LONGEVITY: dose-response is characterized only in C. elegans, where rilmenidine extended lifespan when begun at young or old age (PMID 36670049). No human dose-response for any aging endpoint exists, and the concentrations effective in worms cannot be assumed to map onto safe human plasma levels. No dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs Clonidine
    same: Both are centrally-acting sympatholytic antihypertensives acting in the brainstem
    different: Clonidine is a mixed alpha-2/I1 agonist (~4-fold I1 selectivity) with prominent sedation, dry mouth and marked rebound hypertension; rilmenidine is I1-selective (~30-fold), giving fewer CNS side effects. Only rilmenidine carries the CR-mimetic/worm-lifespan signal.
  • vs Moxonidine
    same: Same second-generation I1-imidazoline class, similar BP mechanism and tolerability advantage over clonidine
    different: Moxonidine carries a direct human harm signal (increased mortality in heart failure, MOXCON, PMID 14607206) and has a much shorter half-life (~2 h); the geroscience worm-lifespan data are specific to rilmenidine, not moxonidine.
  • vs Rapamycin (mTOR inhibitor)
    same: Both act as CR-axis geroprotectors in model organisms; both extend C. elegans lifespan
    different: Rapamycin has robust, replicated mammalian lifespan data (e.g., ITP mouse survival); rilmenidine has NONE. In worms rilmenidine's effect was non-additive with rapamycin, implying a shared TOR/CR pathway rather than a distinct one.
  • vs Metformin
    same: Both are approved drugs repurposed as candidate geroprotectors / CR-mimetics
    different: Metformin's proposed longevity mechanism is heavily AMPK-linked; rilmenidine's worm lifespan effect explicitly did NOT require AMPK, and unlike metformin rilmenidine lowers blood pressure and sympathetic tone.
  • vs Caloric restriction itself
    same: Rilmenidine reproduces a CR-like transcriptional signature and, in worms, shares FOXO/autophagy dependence
    different: CR has broad, cross-species healthspan evidence; rilmenidine is non-additive with CR in worms (no extra benefit when stacked) and has no human aging data.

History & legal status

Rilmenidine (oxazoline chemistry; brand names Hyperium, Albarel) was developed by Servier and introduced for essential hypertension in Europe around 1988, later marketed in Australia and parts of Asia. It was designed as a second-generation imidazoline-class agent with improved tolerability over clonidine. It has NEVER been FDA-approved in the United States. The molecular I1 receptor it targets was subsequently identified as nischarin/IRAS. In 2023, Bennett, Goyala, Statzer, Ewald, Gladyshev, de Magalhães and colleagues (University of Liverpool / ETH Zürich / Harvard–Brigham & Women's) reported rilmenidine as a caloric-restriction-mimetic that extends lifespan in C. elegans (PMID 36670049), reframing this old antihypertensive as a geroscience repurposing candidate. No regulatory body has approved or is known to be evaluating rilmenidine for any aging/longevity indication, and no clinical trial for such an endpoint is registered on ClinicalTrials.gov.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether rilmenidine extends lifespan or healthspan in ANY mammal — the mouse data are transcriptomic only, with no survival endpoint.
  • What human exposure (if any) would reproduce the CR-like effect, and whether it is achievable without unacceptable hypotension/sympatholysis.
  • Whether the lifespan benefit is truly nischarin-specific in mammals as it is in worms (nish-1), given nischarin's many other signaling roles.
  • Whether chronic geroprotective dosing is safe given the class mortality signal (moxonidine/MOXCON) in cardiovascular disease.
  • Whether normotensive or athletic individuals derive any benefit, or only cardiovascular risk, from off-label use.
  • Long-term effects on metabolism, glucose handling, exercise capacity, and body composition in the fitness/longevity population — entirely unstudied.
  • Replication: the core longevity finding rests on a single 2023 primary paper and awaits independent confirmation.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

General principle — this is a hypothesis-stage geroprotector and an active blood-pressure drug; any use should be clinician-supervised, and this monograph gives NO dosing. Parameters a clinician would MONITOR if the drug is used: blood pressure and heart rate (risk of hypotension, dizziness, bradycardia, especially in normotensive or volume-depleted people); orthostatic symptoms and fall risk (particularly in older adults); renal function/eGFR, because clearance is renal and the drug accumulates in kidney impairment; signs of excessive sedation or fatigue; and — critically — avoidance of abrupt discontinuation to prevent rebound hypertension (any stopping should be gradual and supervised). Extra caution in anyone with heart failure or significant cardiovascular disease given the class-level MOXCON mortality signal. Watch for additive hypotension/CNS depression when combined with other antihypertensives, sedatives, or alcohol. Report any syncope, marked bradycardia, or worsening cardiac symptoms promptly.

Caveats

The entire anti-aging story rests on ONE primary paper (Bennett et al. 2023, Aging Cell, PMID 36670049) whose survival data are in C. elegans, a nematode with a ~3-week lifespan and no cardiovascular system, brain, or adaptive metabolism resembling ours. The mouse component is transcriptomic only — a CR-like gene signature in liver/kidney, NOT longer-lived mice. Worm-to-mammal translation of longevity effects is notoriously unreliable, and this finding awaits independent replication. Critically, the doses/exposures producing worm lifespan extension are not established as achievable or safe for chronic geroprotective use in humans; the licensed human dose is set for blood-pressure lowering. Because rilmenidine lowers blood pressure and suppresses sympathetic tone, off-label use by normotensive or fit individuals risks hypotension, dizziness, fatigue, and bradycardia — and abrupt discontinuation of centrally-acting sympatholytics can cause rebound hypertension. A major class-level warning: the closely related I1 agonist moxonidine INCREASED mortality (excess early deaths and heart-failure hospitalizations) in the MOXCON heart-failure trial (PMID 14607206), so this drug class is not benign in cardiovascular disease. No dosing or "how to run it" guidance is given here; this is a mechanism monograph, not a protocol.
