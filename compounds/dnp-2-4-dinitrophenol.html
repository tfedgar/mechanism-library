DNP

The mitochondrial "short circuit" that turns your food into pure body heat instead of usable energy — and can cook you from the inside out with no antidote.

Limited human data 24 citations
Class
Mitochondrial uncoupler / protonophore (chemical, lipophilic weak acid)
Primary target
The inner mitochondrial membrane — specifically the proton (H+) gradient that ATP synthase normally uses to make ATP
Human status
UNAPPROVED and effectively banned for human consumption. DNP was sold as a diet drug in the early 1930s, then pulled by the U.S. FDA in 1938 after reports of cataracts and deaths; it has NEVER been approved as a modern medicine. It is not investigational as a weight-loss drug in any legitimate program — it resurfaces only as an illegally-sold 'fat-burner' bought online, and fatalities have been rising since ~2000. Human data is therefore a mix of: (1) 1930s clinical use, (2) modern poisoning case reports and post-mortem/poison-control series (biased toward severe outcomes), and (3) self-reported online use for sublethal effects. There is real interest in DESIGNING safer, controlled-release or liver-targeted uncoupler derivatives as experimental drugs (e.g. for metabolic disease, neuroprotection) — but plain DNP itself has no safe therapeutic window and no antidote. (Geisler 2019 PMID 30909602; Grundlingh 2011 PMID 21739343; PMID 37703241; PMID 41487961)

At a glance

  • Sharp rise in metabolic rate and body heat (thermogenesis) — the basis of its fat-loss effect
  • Real, rapid fat/weight loss in humans (documented since the 1930s) — but at unpredictable, unsafe cost
  • Profuse sweating, flushing, rapid heartbeat, rapid breathing — signs of the runaway furnace
  • Life-threatening hyperthermia that can climb past 40-41.5°C and resist cooling
  • Cataracts, skin reactions, and agranulocytosis reported with repeated/longer exposure historically
  • Death — the effective dose and the lethal dose are perilously close; deaths continue today from illicit 'fat-burner' use

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Your cells normally bank energy as a proton gradient Established

    Inside every cell, mitochondria burn food (the electron transport chain) to pump protons (H+) out across their inner membrane. This builds up a charge difference — like water held behind a dam. The cell then lets that 'water' flow back through a turbine called ATP synthase to make ATP, the energy currency that powers everything. This is the baseline biochemistry DNP hijacks. Standard bioenergetics (chemiosmotic theory); reviewed in context of DNP by Grundlingh 2011 PMID 21739343 and Geisler 2019 PMID 30909602

  2. DNP punches a hole in the dam Established

    DNP is a small fat-soluble weak acid. It can grab a proton on the outside of the membrane, slip through the fatty membrane carrying that proton, and release it on the inside — then shuttle back for another. This 'protonophore' shuttling lets protons leak back across WITHOUT going through the ATP synthase turbine. The gradient is short-circuited. Blaikie 2006 PMID 16850251; Žuna 2021 PMID 34439844; Samartsev 2020 PMID 32367259

  3. Energy that should become ATP is released as raw heat Established

    Because the protons bypass the turbine, the energy from burning food no longer gets captured as ATP — it's dissipated directly as heat. This is the literal meaning of 'uncoupling': food oxidation is decoupled from ATP production. Body temperature climbs. (Carriers like UCP1, the ANT, and other mitochondrial transporters can amplify DNP's proton-leak, but DNP also works on its own.) Žuna 2021 PMID 34439844; Shabalina 2025 PMID 39357779 (DNP uncouples even without UCP1); Geisler 2019 PMID 30909602

  4. The cell burns more fuel trying to keep up Established

    Sensing it can't make enough ATP, the cell ramps up fuel-burning — oxygen consumption and metabolic rate shoot up, and the body taps fat and other stores to feed the now-wasteful furnace. This higher 'idle speed' is why DNP causes weight loss: calories are wasted as heat rather than stored. Metabolic rate can rise dramatically. Grundlingh 2011 PMID 21739343; Geisler 2019 PMID 30909602; Schönfeld 1989 PMID 2556180 (protonophoric uncoupling raises respiration)

  5. There is no off-switch — heat production runs away Evidence-human

    Unlike the body's own controlled uncoupling (e.g. UCP1 in brown fat, which is regulated), DNP's effect is unregulated and dose-dependent. As dose rises, heat output rises without a ceiling — a 'runaway uncoupling' where core temperature spirals into life-threatening hyperthermia. The lethal dose sits dangerously close to the 'effective' dose, so there is no safe margin. Runaway uncoupling case series PMID 41487961; Grundlingh 2011 PMID 21739343; dantrolene-failure fatal case PMID 30573533

  6. An ATP crisis plus extreme heat shuts the body down Evidence-human

    With ATP starved and temperature soaring, multiple systems fail together: severe hyperthermia, profuse sweating, racing heart, rapid breathing, muscle rigidity, and eventually multi-organ failure and cardiac arrest. Standard treatments (cooling, the muscle relaxant dantrolene) often fail because the problem is metabolic at the level of every mitochondrion, not a single organ. There is no specific antidote. Grundlingh 2011 PMID 21739343; fatal cases PMID 16803658, PMID 7058360; dantrolene ineffective PMID 30573533; PMID 41487961

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No high-affinity protein target / no published Ki or EC50 at a receptor. ChEMBL (CHEMBL273386) lists max clinical phase 2 but NO curated mechanism-of-action target entry — consistent with a physicochemical (membrane lipid) rather than receptor mechanism. Selectivity is essentially nil at the molecular level: any energy-transducing membrane (mitochondria of ALL tissues, and bacterial/chloroplast membranes) is a substrate, which is why toxicity is whole-body. ALogP ~1.2, PSA ~106 Å2, 1 H-bond donor — lipophilic enough to cross membranes and the blood–brain barrier but polar enough to be a mobile weak acid. Antonenko/Kotova (PMID 35441048) note the simple lipid-protonophore picture is incomplete: some uncoupler activity may also involve the ATP/ADP antiporter (ANT), dicarboxylate carrier, and direct ATPase interaction — i.e. DNP partly works THROUGH inner-membrane carrier proteins, not purely through naked lipid.

Signalling cascade. Cascade (Established): (1) Δp collapses → (2) ATP synthase can no longer use the gradient, so ATP output per O2 consumed plummets (oxidative phosphorylation is 'uncoupled' from electron transport). (3) The cell senses falling ATP / rising ADP and AMP. (4) The electron-transport chain runs FASTER to try to rebuild the gradient → O2 consumption and substrate oxidation (fat, glucose) surge — basal metabolic rate rises (Tainter's classic human observation: ~+11% at low chronic doses in animals up to +50% or more at higher doses). (5) The energy that would have become ATP is released as HEAT → thermogenesis/hyperthermia. (6) AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) is activated by the rising AMP:ATP ratio (Evidence-rodent/in vitro), driving fatty-acid oxidation and glucose uptake — this underlies the antidiabetic/anti-steatosis effects seen in rodents (Perry/Shulman CRMP, PMID 25721504). (7) At low/controlled exposure, mild uncoupling also LOWERS mitochondrial reactive-oxygen-species (ROS) production (because a slightly depolarised membrane leaks fewer electrons), the basis of the neuroprotective and 'metabesity' research interest (Evidence-rodent: PMID 20143955, 33422552). (8) At high exposure the same cascade becomes catastrophic: ATP cannot be made anywhere, the SERCA/Na-K-ATPase ion pumps fail, calcium floods the cytosol, and heat production outruns the body's ability to dissipate it ('runaway uncoupling', PMID 41487961).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsules/powder of the free phenol or its sodium salt) is the near-universal route in illicit weight-loss use; historically also oral. Dermal and inhalational absorption are also significant (industrial/occupational exposure) because DNP is lipophilic and was used as a dye/pesticide intermediate.
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapidly and well absorbed orally and across skin (Established, human case/occupational data). Onset of toxic symptoms is fast — fatal cases describe collapse within hours of ingestion (e.g. death ~4.5 h after ingestion in PMID 33706128). No formal human oral bioavailability fraction is published; the practical assumption is high oral absorption. Note: experimental CONTROLLED-RELEASE oral formulations (CRMP, PMID 25721504) and liver-targeted ester/liposome prodrugs (PMID 35808003) were specifically designed to BLUNT the rapid absorption and confine uncoupling to the liver — implying the parent drug's fast, systemic absorption is central to its danger.
Half-life
Long and, importantly, NON-standard. The plasma chemical half-life in humans has historically been cited as ~5–14 days (slow). A 2026 serial-respirometry study (PMID 41487961) measured a FUNCTIONAL (pharmacodynamic) half-life of uncoupled respiration of ~4.9 days in a surviving human — i.e. the metabolic derangement persists for many days after a single exposure, with no antidote to accelerate clearance. This long half-life means DNP ACCUMULATES with repeated daily dosing, so toxicity can appear after days of apparently tolerated use (chronic-poisoning deaths: PMID 39659715).
Metabolism
Reduced and conjugated. The nitro groups are reduced (sequentially) to amino groups, giving the main metabolites 2-amino-4-nitrophenol (2A-4NP) and, less so, 4-amino-2-nitrophenol (4A-2NP); the parent and 2A-4NP are then glucuronidated (DNP-glucuronide and 2A-4NP-glucuronide are the dominant urinary species). No sulfate conjugates were detected in the most recent human case work-up (PMID 41530400). Nitroreduction is carried out by hepatic/gut nitroreductases and is partly oxygen- and NADPH-dependent; glucuronidation is by UDP-glucuronosyltransferases (UGTs). Reduction can be slower/variable, contributing to inter-individual unpredictability.
Excretion
Renal — parent DNP and its amino/glucuronide metabolites are excreted in urine; this is the basis of LC-MS/MS detection in plasma and urine and the long detection window. DNP also distributes into hair (segmental hair analysis can reconstruct chronic exposure, PMID 37844362; 39659715) and is detectable in vitreous and bile. Forensically, DNP partitions into AQUEOUS tissues (blood, vitreous, bile) more than solid tissues (PMID 33706128), and shows post-mortem redistribution and chemical instability in stored specimens.
Notes
Crosses the blood–brain barrier (CNS toxicity: agitation, delirium, seizures). Lipophilic weak acid → uptake is pH-dependent: local acidosis (driven by DNP's own CO2 overproduction) increases the protonated fraction and drives MORE drug into mitochondria, a proposed self-amplifying feedback loop ('runaway uncoupling', PMID 41487961). The historically narrow therapeutic-to-toxic window is largely a PK/PD problem (long half-life + accumulation + no organ selectivity) — hence the prodrug/controlled-release and liposomal strategies (PMID 25721504, 35808003).

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Human dose-response is only crudely characterised and was never established by modern controlled trials — a central evidence gap. HISTORICAL HUMAN (Tainter/Cutting, Stanford, 1933–1934, summarised in Grundlingh review PMID 21739343): roughly 3–5 mg/kg/day raised basal metabolic rate by ~20–40% and produced weight loss of ~1–1.5 kg/week; Tainter estimated >100,000 Americans took it within ~1 year. The commonly cited estimated LETHAL oral dose is ~4.3 mg/kg (about 430 mg for a 100 kg adult) — note this is dangerously CLOSE to the 'effective' weight-loss dose, i.e. the therapeutic index is roughly ~1, the core reason it is unsafe (Grundlingh PMID 21739343). EPA/ATSDR set an oral reference dose of 0.002 mg/kg/day using a LOAEL of 2 mg/kg/day for human CATARACTS (a 1,000-fold safety factor) — so chronic harm appears at doses well below the lethal range. ILLICIT SELF-REPORT (PMID 37703241, ~1,630 online posts): most-reported doses 150–300 then 300–450 mg/day; thermoregulatory, fatigue and neurological symptoms rose significantly above ~150 mg/day. There is NO safe characterised dose: fatalities have followed single ingestions as small as ~'two tablets' (PMID 30573533) up to multi-gram suicidal/accidental ingestions; blood levels in fatalities cluster around tens of mg/L (e.g. 88 mg/L, PMID 39659715; ante-mortem 110 mg/kg, PMID 33706128). RODENT/CONTROLLED (CRMP, PMID 25721504): a controlled-release formulation giving only MILD hepatic uncoupling reversed diabetes/NASH in rats with no systemic toxicity — proving dose-response is steep and that benefit vs lethality is separated only by tight kinetic control, which illicit powder does not provide. BIRD chronic ~4 mg/kg/day shortened median lifespan ~21% (PMID 33278594). Bottom line: in humans the gap between a dose that burns fat and a dose that kills is small, individually unpredictable, and worsened by accumulation over days.

How it compares

  • vs FCCP / CCCP (carbonyl cyanide phenylhydrazone protonophores)
    same: Same fundamental mechanism — lipophilic weak-acid protonophores that dissipate the IMM proton gradient and uncouple oxidative phosphorylation; used as the standard 'uncoupler' tool compounds in respirometry.
    different: FCCP/CCCP are markedly MORE potent (active at sub-micromolar vs DNP's micromolar–high range), strictly research reagents — never marketed for humans — and even more acutely toxic; comparative rat studies group them with DNP as systemically toxic uncouplers (PMID 37779591, 35441048).
  • vs BAM15 / OPC-163493 / next-gen 'safer' uncouplers
    same: Same proton-shuttling endpoint and same therapeutic goal (mild uncoupling for obesity/diabetes/NASH/'metabesity').
    different: Engineered for a WIDER therapeutic window — more mitochondria-selective, less plasma-membrane depolarisation, self-limiting or tissue-targeted kinetics — explicitly designed to avoid DNP's near-1 therapeutic index and hyperthermia risk (PMID 33545391; OPC-163493 in PMID 37779591).
  • vs CRMP / DNP liver-targeted prodrugs & liposomal esters (controlled-release DNP)
    same: Deliver the IDENTICAL active species (DNP) and mechanism.
    different: Pharmaceutical kinetic re-engineering: controlled release or liver-confined delivery produces only mild hepatic uncoupling and reverses rodent diabetes/NASH with NO systemic toxicity — directly demonstrating that DNP's danger is a delivery/kinetics problem, not an intrinsic-mechanism dead end (PMID 25721504, 35808003).
  • vs Clenbuterol / T3 (thyroid hormone) — other illicit 'fat burners'
    same: Also raise metabolic rate / promote fat loss and are co-abused WITH DNP in the bodybuilding scene (PMID 37703241); both can cause tachycardia and hyperthermia-adjacent effects.
    different: Different mechanisms — clenbuterol is a β2-adrenergic agonist (receptor-mediated lipolysis/thermogenesis), T3 acts via nuclear thyroid receptors to raise basal metabolism and (indirectly) mitochondrial uncoupling. Neither directly short-circuits the proton gradient, and neither carries DNP's signature near-1 therapeutic index / runaway-hyperthermia lethality.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Clenbuterol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Two thermogenic agents acting through different mechanisms that converge on heat production and cardiac demand. DNP uncouples mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, dissipating the proton gradient as heat and forcing a compensatory rise in metabolic rate, oxygen demand and heart rate; clenbuterol independently raises resting energy expenditure (~+21%) and thermogenesis via beta-2/cAMP while directly stimulating the heart and lowering potassium. Combined, the extra heat load (DNP) plus adrenergic tachycardia and hypokalemia (clenbuterol) compound the risk of dangerous hyperthermia and tachyarrhythmia, with clenbuterol's long half-life meaning the adrenergic contribution cannot be quickly withdrawn if DNP-driven hyperthermia develops. clenbuterol.json (thermogenesis +21%, tachycardia, hypokalemia; PMID 31887249); dnp monograph (mitochondrial uncoupling -> heat, life-threatening hyperthermia) Watch: Core temperature and any sign of hyperthermia (profuse sweating, flushing, agitation) — this is the priority with DNP; plus heart rate, blood pressure, ECG, and serum potassium. Rising temperature or tachycardia warrants stopping and urgent medical review, not 'waiting it out' given clenbuterol's long persistence.
  • Ephedrine compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Two different heat-generating mechanisms whose harms stack. DNP is a mitochondrial protonophore that collapses the proton gradient, dissipating substrate energy directly as heat and producing life-threatening, cooling-resistant hyperthermia. Ephedrine independently raises metabolic rate and heat production via beta-adrenergic cAMP thermogenesis (a modest effect in humans), while its alpha-1 vasoconstriction can impair the cutaneous vasodilation and sweating needed to shed heat, and its beta-1 tachycardia adds cardiovascular strain to an already hypermetabolic state. Combined, they push core temperature and cardiac demand higher and blunt thermoregulatory compensation. The individual thermogenic/thermoregulatory effects are human-documented, but the specific stacking is a mechanistic inference (no human study of the combination). PMID 33945051; PMID 16212144 Watch: Core body temperature, signs of overheating (profuse sweating, flushing, rapid breathing), heart rate, hydration/electrolytes; escalating temperature or confusion is a medical emergency (DNP hyperthermia can be fatal and resists cooling).
  • Insulin compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    DNP creates a state of high glucose demand and impaired oxidative ATP production: with oxidative phosphorylation uncoupled, cells fall back on glycolysis and burn through glucose, which is why glucose supplementation is a rational supportive measure in DNP poisoning (DNP monograph pharmacology cascade + Lindeman 2026). Insulin independently drives glucose out of the blood into cells via GLUT4 and carries an open-ended, ceiling-less hypoglycaemia risk. Layering insulin's glucose-lowering on top of DNP's hypermetabolic glucose consumption can produce abrupt, severe hypoglycaemia - and the danger is amplified because hypoglycaemic sweating/tremor/tachycardia/confusion are nearly identical to early DNP toxicity, so a crashing blood sugar can be misread as (or hidden by) DNP toxicity, and vice versa. Both agents also drive potassium into cells (insulin via Na+/K+-ATPase), a shared additional electrolyte effect early on. DNP monograph (glucose supplementation as supportive care; glycolytic shift; hypermetabolic glucose demand) + Lindeman 2026 PMID 41487961; Insulin monograph (GLUT4, steep open-ended hypoglycaemia, late-arriving warning symptoms; PMID 3403713) Watch: Blood glucose (frequent objective checks - insulin's warning symptoms lag brain impairment, so 'feeling fine' is unreliable), and awareness that sweating, tremor, palpitations and confusion overlap between a hypo and early DNP toxicity. Any of these plus feeling hot is an emergency; carbohydrate/glucose support is part of DNP supportive care. Serum potassium.
  • MK-677 compounds risk ⚠ Speculative
    MK-677 raises GH and IGF-1, and because GH opposes insulin it consistently raises fasting glucose and reduces insulin sensitivity (an Established human downside). DNP creates the opposite acute demand - a hypermetabolic, glucose-consuming, glycolysis-dependent state in which glucose support is protective. The two push glucose handling in opposing directions (MK-677 impairs glucose disposal and raises glucose; DNP burns through it), destabilising glycaemic control and making blood-glucose behaviour during a DNP hypermetabolic episode less predictable. This is a glucose-axis complication (opposing effects producing an unstable, hard-to-interpret picture) rather than a shared-target interaction; the clinical magnitude is uncertain. MK-677 monograph (raised fasting glucose/insulin resistance Evidence-human; PMID 18981485) + DNP monograph (hypermetabolic glucose demand, glucose supplementation as supportive care; PMID 41487961) Watch: Blood glucose (both MK-677-driven fasting hyperglycaemia/insulin resistance and the risk of rapid glucose consumption during a DNP hypermetabolic state - watch for swinging, hard-to-interpret glucose). MK-677 fluid retention plus DNP dehydration is also worth tracking.
  • Metformin compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both compounds independently impair mitochondrial oxidative ATP production and push cells toward glycolysis/lactate, converging on the same bioenergetic failure point. Metformin partially inhibits mitochondrial respiratory-chain Complex I; DNP collapses the proton gradient so oxidative phosphorylation is uncoupled. DNP already causes documented lactic/metabolic acidosis (glycolytic shift, lactate accumulation), and metformin's rare-but-serious signature toxicity is lactic acidosis (MALA). Combining a Complex I inhibitor with a protonophore uncoupler is additive pressure toward lactic acidosis, and the risk is compounded if renal function drops - DNP can cause rhabdomyolysis/AKI, and metformin is renally cleared and accumulates in AKI, a specific trigger for MALA. Both also raise the AMP:ATP ratio and activate AMPK. DNP monograph (lactic/metabolic acidosis mechanism; AMPK activation; rhabdomyolysis/AKI) + PMID 41487961, 21739343; Metformin monograph (Complex I inhibition, AMPK, MALA tied to impaired respiration/renal clearance; PMID 20393934, 21241070) Watch: Venous/arterial blood gas, lactate and bicarbonate, renal function/eGFR (metformin accumulates in AKI), creatine kinase (rhabdomyolysis as an AKI trigger), and signs of acidosis (deep/rapid breathing, confusion, severe nausea/abdominal pain, malaise). A DNP-driven fall in renal function is a specific red flag for metformin-associated lactic acidosis.
  • Superdrol compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Superdrol is a 17-alpha-methylated oral steroid whose survive-first-pass chemistry makes it markedly hepatotoxic - classically cholestatic (jaundice, high bilirubin, pruritus) with hepatocellular injury and, at the severe end, structural liver damage. DNP delivers high first-pass hepatic exposure and energy-starves hepatocytes (impaired ATP supply), and human DNP poisoning includes transaminase elevation and hepatic injury. Combining a directly hepatotoxic 17aa oral with an uncoupler that ATP-depletes the same organ compounds drug-induced liver injury via two independent hepatotoxic mechanisms (steroid cholestasis/oxidative stress + uncoupler ATP depletion) hitting the liver simultaneously. Both are also independently linked to renal involvement/AKI at the severe end. Superdrol monograph (17aa cholestatic/hepatocellular DILI; PMID 23750872, 30934130, 18720005) + DNP monograph side_effect_mechanisms (hepatic ATP depletion, transaminase elevation, liver as high-exposure organ; PMID 39659715) Watch: Liver enzymes/transaminases, bilirubin (direct/total), and signs of cholestasis (jaundice, dark urine, itching, pale stools); renal function. Any rise on a 17aa oral plus DNP warrants stopping and clinical review.
  • T3 compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both drive up whole-body heat production and metabolic rate, but by different routes that add together. DNP mechanically short-circuits the mitochondrial proton gradient so food energy is dumped as raw heat and O2 consumption/basal metabolic rate surge (DNP monograph steps 3-4). T3 acts through nuclear thyroid receptors (TRalpha in heart, TRbeta in metabolic tissue) to raise basal metabolic rate and mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation/thermogenesis. Layering a receptor-driven rise in metabolic rate on top of DNP's unregulated, ceiling-less uncoupling pushes core temperature and cardiac oxygen demand higher than either alone - and DNP's hyperthermia has no off-switch and no antidote (dantrolene fails), so any added heat load moves toward the runaway-uncoupling threshold. T3 also independently raises tachyarrhythmia/atrial-fibrillation risk (its monograph's dominant harm), compounding the tachycardia DNP already causes. DNP monograph steps 3-4 (uncoupling->heat/BMR surge) + comparisons (T3 as co-abused fat-burner, distinct mechanism); T3 monograph (TRalpha cardiac/AF, calorigenesis); Grundlingh 2011 PMID 21739343; DNP-T3 co-use documented in Abdelati 2023 PMID 37703241 Watch: Core temperature (the single most dangerous sign - any sustained rise or feeling intensely hot is a medical emergency; dantrolene does NOT work for DNP hyperthermia), resting heart rate and rhythm/ECG (T3 adds atrial-fibrillation/tachyarrhythmia risk, steepest when TSH is suppressed), heat intolerance and profuse sweating. Free T3/free T4 and TSH characterise the thyroid drive.
  • Testosterone compounds risk ⚠ Speculative
    Testosterone reliably raises haematocrit/haemoglobin (erythrocytosis, via raised EPO and suppressed hepcidin - an Established dose-dependent human effect), thickening the blood and raising viscosity. DNP causes profuse sweating and hyperthermia, driving fluid loss and dehydration that concentrate the blood and push haematocrit/viscosity higher still. On an already androgen-elevated haematocrit, DNP-induced dehydration compounds blood viscosity and cardiovascular strain at exactly the moment cardiac oxygen demand is maximal from the hypermetabolic state - a plausible additive thrombotic and cardiac-strain risk. The individual mechanisms are human-established; the combined clinical outcome is inferred, not directly documented. Testosterone monograph (erythrocytosis Established via EPO up/hepcidin down; PMID 24158761) + DNP monograph (profuse sweating, dehydration, hyperthermia, cardiovascular strain/tachycardia); PMID 21739343 Watch: Haematocrit/haemoglobin, hydration/fluid status, blood pressure and heart rate; symptoms of hyperviscosity (headache, visual changes) or clot (leg swelling, breathlessness). Recognise heavy DNP sweating as a haematocrit-raising, viscosity-raising, cardiac-strain event layered on the androgen effect.
  • GHK-Cu opposes Speculative
    DNP uncouples oxidative phosphorylation, driving heat plus a large rise in mitochondrial reactive-oxygen-species and oxidative stress. GHK-Cu is antioxidant on paper - it lowers ROS and raises superoxide dismutase (NF-kB/Nrf2 modulation), so it nominally opposes DNP's oxidative burden. The caveat that makes this an interaction rather than a clean rescue: GHK-Cu delivers redox-active copper that, under high oxidative/glutathione-depleting load, can itself flip to pro-oxidant Fenton chemistry - so pairing a copper-carrying antioxidant peptide with a severe ROS generator is mechanistically uncertain, not reliably protective. PMID 27517151 (GHK-Cu lowers ROS, raises SOD, suppresses NF-kB); PMID 34781677 (GHK copper is redox-active, GSH-reducible - pro-oxidant potential under load) Watch: DNP is potentially lethal (hyperthermia, tachycardia) and dominates risk - this is a harm-reduction red flag, not a stack to optimize; watch core temperature and heart rate and for any hyperthermia. Do not assume GHK-Cu meaningfully offsets DNP toxicity.

History &amp; legal status

Industrial origin: 2,4-dinitrophenol was an industrial chemical (dye intermediate, wood preservative, herbicide/pesticide and explosives feedstock) before any medical use; munitions-factory workers in WWI were noted to lose weight and run hot. Cutting and Tainter at Stanford University reported its metabolic-rate-boosting effect in humans in 1933 and it was rapidly commercialised as an OTC diet drug (sold as Alpha-Dinitrophenol, Dinitrenal, etc.); Tainter estimated >100,000 US users within ~1 year. Cataracts, agranulocytosis, neuropathy and deaths followed. With the 1938 US Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, the FDA effectively removed it from the market and it was declared 'unfit for human consumption'; it has NEVER been an approved human medicine since. Regulatory status today: not approved for human use anywhere as a drug; it remains a regulated industrial chemical. EPA/ATSDR set an oral reference dose of 0.002 mg/kg/day (LOAEL 2 mg/kg/day for human cataracts, 1,000× safety factor). In the UK the Food Standards Agency treats DNP sold for consumption as illegal/'not fit for human consumption' and has pursued prosecutions; Interpol has issued global alerts after clusters of weight-loss deaths. Resurgence: since ~2001 it has re-emerged as an illicit internet 'fat-burner', with rising fatalities. Separately, controlled-release/liver-targeted DNP (e.g. Mitochon-type programmes; ChEMBL lists DNP at max clinical phase 2) is being investigated for metabolic and neurodegenerative disease — a legitimate research revival distinct from the illicit powder.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No modern controlled human dose-response exists: the boundary between a fat-loss dose and a lethal dose has never been mapped in a trial, and individual susceptibility (genetics of nitroreductase/UGT metabolism, baseline thermoregulation) is essentially uncharacterised.
  • The 'runaway uncoupling' acidosis→mitochondrial-uptake feedback loop is proposed from a 2-patient series (PMID 41487961) — it needs confirmation and could explain why some users die abruptly while others tolerate similar doses.
  • The exact molecular mechanism of DNP cataract and agranulocytosis is unproven (sulfhydryl/glutathione disruption is hypothesised but not demonstrated), and it is unknown why humans are far more cataract-susceptible than most mammals.
  • Whether truly safe chronic exposure exists at any dose is open: a captive-bird study (PMID 33278594) found even ~4 mg/kg/day shortened lifespan ~21% despite only mild metabolic effect — long-term safety of 'low' uncoupling in humans is unknown.
  • No antidote and no clearance accelerator is established; given the multi-day functional half-life, whether any intervention (beyond cooling, ventilation, glucose, K+ control) changes survival is untested.
  • Can pharmaceutical re-engineering (controlled-release/liver-targeted/next-gen uncouplers) deliver the metabolic and neuroprotective benefits without lethality in HUMANS? Proven in rodents (PMID 25721504) but not yet in adequately powered human trials.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring framing only — there is NO safe self-administration protocol, and no dosing guidance is given. Red-flag clinical signs that signal emerging/serious toxicity and warrant emergency care: rising core temperature (the single most dangerous sign — fever, feeling intensely hot, sweating profusely/diaphoresis), tachycardia and palpitations, fast/laboured breathing (tachypnoea), flushing, agitation/confusion/restlessness, nausea, and yellow staining of skin/sweat. Because DNP accumulates over days, symptoms can escalate after the dose that 'felt fine'. Laboratory/clinical parameters a clinician would watch in a known or suspected exposure: core temperature (continuous), serum potassium (severe HYPERkalaemia is a late ominous marker), arterial/venous blood gas and lactate (metabolic/lactic acidosis), blood glucose (hypermetabolism can drive demand — glucose support is part of supportive care), creatine kinase and renal function (rhabdomyolysis/AKI risk), liver enzymes/transaminases (hepatic injury), and full blood count (agranulocytosis risk with chronic exposure). Ophthalmologic review for cataract and neurological exam for peripheral neuropathy are relevant for any repeated/chronic exposure. Critical safety note: in DNP hyperthermia, dantrolene does NOT work (PMID 30573533) and the mainstay is aggressive external/internal cooling plus supportive care — this is a medical emergency, not something to manage at home.

Caveats

HARM REDUCTION — this matters: DNP is genuinely lethal and there is NO safe dose and NO antidote. The gap between a 'weight-loss' dose and a fatal dose is razor-thin and varies between people and even batch to batch (illicit DNP is unregulated powder). Because it acts on every mitochondrion at once, once core temperature starts running away, hospitals frequently CANNOT save the person — aggressive cooling and dantrolene have failed in fatal cases (PMID 30573533). Symptoms can appear deceptively mild then crash rapidly. Heat, exercise, and hot weather worsen the danger. This package explains the mechanism for understanding only; it is not a how-to and contains no dosing. Anyone who has taken DNP and feels hot, sweaty, or unwell needs emergency care immediately and should tell clinicians it is DNP, because the presentation mimics other causes of fever and can be missed (PMID 7058360).
