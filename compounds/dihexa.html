Dihexa

A lab-built "super-peptide" descended from a brain blood-pressure hormone, designed to switch on the brain's wiring-and-repair growth factor (HGF) and grow new synapses — spectacular in rodents, but with zero human data and a retracted keystone paper that should make you cautious.

Rodent / preclinical 14 citations
Class
Angiotensin IV (AngIV)-derived oligopeptide analog / proposed small-molecule hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) mimetic ("nootropic"/procognitive research peptide)
Primary target
The hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) / c-Met receptor tyrosine kinase system in the brain (claimed)
Human status
NO human data. There are zero clinical trials of dihexa itself (ClinicalTrials.gov: 0 results for dihexa). Every efficacy and mechanism claim comes from rodent, zebrafish, or cell-culture work, much of it from one lab (Washington State / M3 Biotechnology). Crucially, the single keystone paper that defined the mechanism — Benoist et al. 2014, J Pharmacol Exp Ther (PMID 25187433), which reported dihexa binds HGF and activates c-Met — has been RETRACTED, and the foundational synthesis/efficacy paper (McCoy et al. 2013, PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN) carries a publisher Expression of Concern. The closest thing to human evidence is a DIFFERENT, structurally distinct HGF/MET positive modulator, fosgonimeton (ATH-1017, Athira Pharma), which did reach a completed Phase 2/3 Alzheimer's trial (NCT04488419, n=554) — but it is not dihexa, and that Alzheimer's program did not deliver a clear cognitive win. Bottom line for a viewer: dihexa is a preclinical research chemical with a damaged evidence base, taken by biohackers on faith and forum anecdote, not on human trials.

At a glance

  • Reported in rodents: reverses scopolamine- and aging-induced memory deficits in the Morris water maze (spatial learning) — orally active and brain-penetrant, unlike the parent peptide [PMID 23055539, under Expression of Concern]
  • Reported in rodents/cells: drives dendritic spine formation and new synapse assembly in hippocampal neurons — the central 'synaptogenesis' claim [PMID 23055539; review PMID 25455861]
  • Rescued memory and reduced neuroinflammation in APP/PS1 Alzheimer's-model mice, linked to PI3K/AKT signaling [PMID 34827486]
  • Protected sensory hair cells from antibiotic toxicity in zebrafish via the HGF cascade (Akt/mTOR/MEK) — evidence the effect runs through growth-factor signaling [PMID 25674052]
  • Systematic review of AngIV-family peptides confirms a consistent pro-cognitive signal across many rodent studies — but explicitly in animals only [PMID 29733881]
  • Anecdotal human reports (Reddit/YouTube, commercial-interest-heavy): heightened mental clarity/focus, sometimes 'overstimulating'; users self-limit to 2-3x/week. This is uncontrolled anecdote, not evidence, and includes risky improvised DMSO/PEG injections.

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Born from a brain hormone fragment Established

    Your body has a renin-angiotensin system — best known for blood pressure. One downstream fragment, angiotensin IV (AngIV), turned out to improve memory when injected into rodent brains decades ago. But raw AngIV is useless as a drug: it's chewed up by enzymes and can't cross from blood into the brain. Chemists at Washington State stripped it to its 3 essential amino acids and bolted on fatty (hexanoic) groups to make it stable, fat-soluble, orally active, and brain-penetrant. That engineered molecule is dihexa. McCoy et al. 2013, J Pharmacol Exp Ther, PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN (NOTE: Expression of Concern); review Wright et al. 2015, Prog Neurobiol, PMID 25455861

  2. Claimed to grab onto HGF, the body's repair growth factor Speculative (key source retracted)

    Hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) is a master 'grow and repair' signal used all over the body and brain. The proposed mechanism is that dihexa physically binds HGF and helps it dock onto its receptor, c-Met — acting like a molecular matchmaker that amplifies a weak HGF signal. IMPORTANT: the one paper that actually showed this binding and c-Met activation has been RETRACTED, so this central step is no longer backed by valid published evidence even though it's repeated everywhere online. ⚠ RETRACTED: PMID 25187433 (Benoist 2014, later retracted) — do not rely on; treat the synaptogenesis claim as Speculative

  3. Switching on the c-Met receptor Evidence-in vitro

    c-Met is a receptor tyrosine kinase — a switch on the cell surface. When HGF activates it, the two halves pair up and tag each other with phosphate groups (phosphorylation), turning the switch 'on'. The model says dihexa lets even tiny, subthreshold amounts of HGF flip this switch. The zebrafish hair-cell study supports the idea that dihexa's benefits genuinely run through HGF/c-Met, because blocking HGF or the downstream kinases removed the effect. Uribe et al. 2015, Front Cell Neurosci, PMID 25674052 (HGF-antagonist and Akt/mTOR/MEK-inhibitor blockade); receptor biology in review PMID 25649658

  4. Firing the cell's growth and survival pathways Evidence-rodent

    Once c-Met is on, it lights up the classic intracellular growth cascades: PI3K/AKT (cell survival, anti-apoptosis) and MAPK/MEK and mTOR (protein-building for growth). These are the same pathways many growth factors use. In the Alzheimer's-mouse study, dihexa's memory rescue and reduced brain inflammation tracked with PI3K/AKT activation, and a PI3K blocker reversed the benefit. Sun et al. 2021, Brain Sci, PMID 34827486 (PI3K/AKT, wortmannin reversal); pathway dependence also in PMID 25674052

  5. Building new dendritic spines and synapses Evidence-rodent

    Activated growth pathways tell neurons to sprout new dendritic spines — the little knobs where one neuron receives input from another — and to assemble functional synapses. This is the headline 'synaptogenesis' claim: dihexa doesn't just tweak chemistry, it's proposed to physically add new connection points in the hippocampus, the brain's memory hub. McCoy et al. 2013, PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN (hippocampal spinogenesis/synaptogenesis, in vitro + rodent); review PMID 25455861

  6. More connections → better learning and memory (in animals) Evidence-rodent

    More working synapses should mean better information storage. In rodents, dihexa given by mouth reversed drug-induced and age-related memory loss in the water-maze test, and improved memory in an Alzheimer's mouse model. This is the cellular story turned into behavior — but only ever measured in animals, never in people. McCoy et al. 2013, PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN (under EoC); Sun et al. 2021, PMID 34827486; rodent-wide signal in systematic review Ho & Nation 2018, PMID 29733881

  7. The human gap — and why the c-Met angle cuts both ways Established

    Nobody has run a controlled trial of dihexa in humans, so we don't know if any of this transfers, what a safe dose is, or the long-term risks. And there's a built-in concern: c-Met is a proto-oncogene — overactive HGF/c-Met signaling is a known driver of many cancers and tumor spread. A compound whose entire selling point is amplifying that exact pathway across the whole body, taken chronically with no safety data, is a real theoretical risk, not just hype-skepticism. c-Met oncogenic role: corpus PMID 39853766 (HGF/c-Met in cancer); human-trial absence: ClinicalTrials.gov 0 results for dihexa (this search)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Dihexa (N-hexanoic-Tyr-Ile-(6)-aminohexanoic amide; also called PNB-0408) is a synthetic tripeptide-derived oligopeptide built from the three N-terminal residues (Nle-Tyr-Ile) of Norleucine1-angiotensin IV, with an N-terminal hexanoyl cap and a C-terminal 6-aminohexanoic amide added to increase hydrophobicity, reduce hydrogen bonding and block peptidase cleavage (Evidence-human/in vitro; McCoy 2013, PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN, under Expression of Concern). The ORIGINAL claimed primary target was the HGF/c-Met system: the keystone paper reported that dihexa 'binds with high affinity to hepatocyte growth factor (HGF)' and acts as an allosteric HGF/c-Met agonist/mimetic, augmenting c-Met (MET receptor tyrosine kinase) autophosphorylation in the presence of sub-threshold HGF and enhancing HGF-dependent cell scattering (CONTESTED — the source, Benoist 2014 PMID 25187433, has been RETRACTED and carries an Expression of Concern; do not treat its affinity/binding claims as established). A follow-up framed the mechanism as dihexa 'dimerizing with endogenous HGF to form a functional ligand' that activates c-Met (Evidence-in vitro, zebrafish; Uribe 2015 PMID 25674052, NOT retracted). No quantitative Ki/Kd/EC50 for a dihexa-HGF or dihexa-c-Met interaction has been independently reproduced and published with a verifiable number; the affinity is best labeled Speculative/Contested given the retraction. The ancestral pharmacophore (AngIV) also inhibits insulin-regulated aminopeptidase (IRAP/AT4 receptor), and whether dihexa's procognitive activity runs through HGF/c-Met versus IRAP is genuinely unresolved (Contested; Wright 2015 review PMID 25455861; Ho & Nation systematic review PMID 29733881).

Signalling cascade. Proposed canonical cascade (label: mechanism downstream of c-Met is Established for HGF biology generally, but dihexa's engagement of it is Contested because the anchoring paper is retracted): if dihexa potentiates c-Met, ligand-induced c-Met dimerization triggers trans-autophosphorylation of tyrosines in the kinase activation loop and the C-terminal multisubstrate docking site, recruiting GAB1/GRB2 and activating two principal limbs — (1) PI3K -> AKT -> mTOR, driving cell survival, anti-apoptotic signaling and protein synthesis for dendritic-spine/synapse formation, and (2) RAS -> RAF -> MEK -> ERK1/2 (MAPK), driving proliferation/scattering and CREB-dependent transcription. An independent replication in APP/PS1 Alzheimer mice showed dihexa engaging the PI3K/AKT axis: benefit was reversed by the PI3K inhibitor wortmannin, alongside reduced IL-1beta/TNF-alpha, increased IL-10, decreased astrocyte/microglial activation and increased synaptophysin (SYP) (Evidence-rodent; Sun 2021 PMID 34827486, NOT retracted). In the zebrafish lateral line, dihexa's hair-cell protection was attenuated by inhibitors of AKT, TOR and MEK and by the HGF antagonist 6-AH, consistent with HGF-pathway dependence (Evidence-in vitro/zebrafish; Uribe 2015 PMID 25674052). Net claimed downstream output: spinogenesis and functional synaptogenesis in hippocampal neurons (originally reported in the now-retracted Benoist 2014 and the precursor Benoist 2011 PMID 21719467, which is not retracted).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral and parenteral (i.p./i.v./local hydrogel in animal studies); marketed in the gray market as oral capsules, sublingual/transdermal creams and injectable solution. No human PK study exists. (Evidence-rodent; route claims abstract-level)
Absorption / bioavailability
Designed for oral activity and gut/BBB permeability by adding lipophilic caps and reducing hydrogen-bond donors; described as 'orally active, blood-brain-barrier permeant' (Evidence-rodent, abstract only — full text not retrieved; McCoy 2013 PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN, under Expression of Concern). No quantitative %F (oral bioavailability) in humans or animals has been published with a verifiable number. Claimed CNS penetrance is Evidence-rodent and not independently quantified.
Half-life
Engineered for metabolic stability; the SAR paper reports dramatically improved stability over parent AngIV analogs in liver microsomes and plasma (the precursor peptides have half-lives of seconds-to-minutes). A specific human half-life is UNKNOWN; commonly circulated 'long half-life' figures are not anchored to a verifiable published human value (Evidence-rodent/in vitro; McCoy 2013 PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN, abstract only, EoC). Treat any specific hour-figure as Speculative.
Metabolism
Intended to resist aminopeptidase/peptidase degradation that destroys native AngIV peptides; stabilized against liver microsomal metabolism in vitro. Specific CYP enzymes or esterase pathways have not been characterized in a verifiable publication (Evidence-in vitro; McCoy 2013 PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN, EoC).
Excretion
Not characterized in any verifiable human or animal publication (Unknown).
Notes
Dihexa is itself the stabilized analog (not a classic ester prodrug), built specifically to survive the peptidase and barrier liabilities that sank earlier AngIV peptides. A reported active-pharmacophore detail: even a minor N-terminal amino-group addition abolished much of its activity, indicating tight structure-activity dependence (Evidence-in vitro; Uribe 2015 PMID 25674052). The overarching PK caveat: ALL pharmacokinetic claims derive from preclinical/rodent work, mostly at abstract level, and the foundational mechanism paper is retracted — there is NO human PK data.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Human dose-response is entirely UNKNOWN — there are no published human pharmacokinetic, efficacy or dose-finding data for dihexa (no completed/published clinical trial). Rodent oral/behavioral dose ranges from the original developers: dihexa reversed scopolamine-induced and aged-rat Morris-water-maze deficits and increased hippocampal synaptogenesis at oral doses reported in the low mg/kg range (Evidence-rodent; McCoy 2013 PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN, abstract only, under Expression of Concern). In the APP/PS1 Alzheimer mouse, multiple oral doses of dihexa restored spatial learning, raised tissue AngIV, increased neuronal counts and SYP, and shifted cytokines via PI3K/AKT (Evidence-rodent; Sun 2021 PMID 34827486). In peripheral-nerve repair, dihexa was dosed at 2-4 mg/kg body weight (i.v./i.p./local) and improved motor recovery when combined with stem cells/G-CSF (Evidence-rodent; Weiss 2021 PMID 34703584). In zebrafish lateral-line hair cells, ~1 microM conferred optimal protection against aminoglycoside ototoxicity, an explicit in-vitro dose-response (Evidence-in vitro; Uribe 2015 PMID 25674052). CRITICAL gap: no validated human-equivalent dose, no ceiling/toxic dose, and at least one negative rodent study (Huntington's 3-NP model) found NO benefit (Evidence-rodent; Wells 2024 PMID 38489193) — so even animal efficacy is model-dependent. Any human 'protocol' circulating online is unvalidated extrapolation.

How it compares

  • vs Norleucine1-Angiotensin IV (Nle1-AngIV, the parent compound)
    same: Shares the Nle-Tyr-Ile N-terminal pharmacophore; both are procognitive/synaptogenic in rodent memory models and were proposed to act via the same HGF/c-Met (or IRAP/AT4) target
    different: Nle1-AngIV is a hexapeptide rapidly degraded by peptidases, poorly gut/BBB permeable, and effectively only centrally active; dihexa is the lipophilic, metabolically stabilized, orally active, claimed BBB-penetrant successor engineered from its first three residues (PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN, 21719467)
  • vs Native HGF (hepatocyte growth factor, the endogenous c-Met ligand)
    same: Both claimed to converge on c-Met activation and downstream PI3K/AKT and ERK signaling driving survival/plasticity; dihexa described as an HGF 'mimetic'
    different: HGF is a large, unstable protein that does not cross the BBB and cannot be given orally; dihexa is a small, stable, orally/BBB-deliverable molecule claimed to act allosterically/by dimerizing with HGF rather than being a full independent agonist (PMID 25674052) — but dihexa's affinity claim rests on a RETRACTED paper
  • vs Angiotensin IV-derived IRAP inhibitors (e.g., HFI-419, aryl-sulfonamide IRAP inhibitors)
    same: Same AngIV ancestry and procognitive rationale; both lines emerged from trying to drug the 'AT4 receptor' for memory
    different: These compounds are framed as insulin-regulated aminopeptidase (IRAP) enzyme inhibitors, the competing mechanistic hypothesis for AngIV cognition; dihexa's developers argued for HGF/c-Met instead — the two camps remain unreconciled, and dihexa's target assignment is now weakened by the retraction (PMID 33178027, 30023895, 29733881)
  • vs Cerebrolysin / BDNF-pathway nootropics (functional comparison users make)
    same: Marketed/used for neurotrophic, pro-plasticity, pro-cognitive and neuroregenerative goals
    different: Cerebrolysin is a regulated peptide preparation with human trial data; dihexa has zero human trials, an unregulated gray-market supply, and a retracted foundational mechanism — the evidence floor is far lower

History &amp; legal status

Dihexa was designed in the early 2010s by Joseph Harding, John Wright, Leen Kawas and colleagues at Washington State University as the orally active, BBB-permeant culmination of a decades-long program to drug the procognitive properties of angiotensin IV (first reported ~1988-2001) without its peptidase liabilities (PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN, 25455861). It was licensed to the spin-out M3 Biotechnology (later Athira Pharma); Athira's lead clinical asset became the related small molecule ATH-1017/fosgonimeton (an HGF-positive modulator) rather than dihexa itself — and Athira was subsequently embroiled in scientific-integrity issues when its founder/CEO (Leen Kawas) was found to have altered images in foundational papers, leading to her departure and to retractions/expressions of concern in the dihexa/HGF-c-Met literature. The KEYSTONE mechanism paper (Benoist 2014, PMID 25187433) is now RETRACTED and additionally carries an Expression of Concern (confirmed live across OpenAlex, Crossref and NCBI/PubMed); the SAR paper (McCoy 2013, PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN) carries an Expression of Concern. Dihexa itself has NEVER been approved by any regulator, has no completed/published human clinical trial, and is not a recognized medicine anywhere. Legally it exists only as an unscheduled 'research chemical'/gray-market peptide sold for 'not for human consumption' research use; it is widely marketed in nootropic and bodybuilding/PED circles. It is not an approved supplement and would not lawfully be sold as a dietary supplement or medicine.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does dihexa actually bind HGF or c-Met, and with what affinity? The high-affinity binding claim rests on a RETRACTED paper (PMID 25187433); no independently reproduced Ki/Kd/EC50 exists, so the entire 'HGF/c-Met mimetic' identity is unproven.
  • Is the real target HGF/c-Met or IRAP (insulin-regulated aminopeptidase)/AT4? This decades-old controversy is unresolved and the retraction reopens it (PMID 29744... see Ho & Nation PMID 29733881).
  • What are dihexa's human pharmacokinetics — oral bioavailability, true half-life, metabolizing enzymes, CNS penetrance, and excretion? None are established in humans; circulating 'long half-life' figures are unsupported.
  • What is the real-world oncologic risk of chronically agonizing a proto-oncogenic (c-Met) pathway in humans? No carcinogenicity or long-term safety data exist.
  • Does dihexa produce any cognitive benefit in humans at all, and at what dose? No human efficacy data exist, and rodent efficacy is model-dependent (negative in the 3-NP Huntington's model, PMID 38489193).
  • What is the identity, purity and content of gray-market dihexa products, and how much of reported user experience is the molecule versus contaminants, mislabeling, or placebo?

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring only (NOT a recommendation to use; no dosing guidance). Given the proto-oncogenic c-Met pathway it claims to activate, the priority is cancer-surveillance vigilance: anyone using it should have age/risk-appropriate cancer screening and promptly report new lumps, unexplained weight loss, persistent cough, changed moles or abnormal bleeding to a clinician; there is no validated tumor-marker panel for this exposure. Baseline and periodic bloodwork a clinician might reasonably track: complete blood count (CBC), comprehensive metabolic panel including liver enzymes (ALT/AST, since HGF/c-Met is active in liver) and kidney function (eGFR/creatinine). Watch for CNS overstimulation signs reported anecdotally — insomnia, headache, irritability, anxiety, or any new seizure-like activity — and stop on neurological red flags. Because supply is unregulated, source/lot quality is itself a hazard: third-party identity/purity testing reduces (does not eliminate) contaminant risk. Critically, ALL of this is precautionary extrapolation from mechanism and animal data — there is no human safety dataset, the foundational mechanism paper is retracted, and the only fully responsible framing is that the risk-benefit in humans is unknown. Anyone with a personal or family cancer history, or who is pregnant/breastfeeding, has the strongest reason to avoid it. Disclose any use to your physician.

Caveats

The foundational 2013 synthesis/efficacy paper (McCoy et al., PMID 23055539-EXPRESSION-OF-CONCERN) carries a publisher Expression of Concern. So the two papers that built dihexa's entire 'HGF mimetic / synaptogenesis' story are both compromised.
