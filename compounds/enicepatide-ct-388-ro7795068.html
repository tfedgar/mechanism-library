Enicepatide

Roche's once-weekly, cAMP signaling-biased GLP-1/GIP dual agonist — the incretin "backbone" it plans to pair with the amylin analog petrelintide. Early human data show tirzepatide-like weight loss, but efficacy rests on one small Phase 1; Phase 2/3 readouts are still pending.

Limited human data 5 citations
Class
Unimolecular dual incretin receptor agonist (peptide) — GLP-1 receptor + GIP receptor co-agonist, engineered as a cAMP signaling-biased agonist
Primary target
Dual agonism of the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) and the GIP receptor (GIPR), both class B G-protein-coupled receptors
Human status
PIPELINE / EMERGING. Enicepatide is investigational and NOT approved by any regulator for any indication (as of mid-2026). Only one peer-reviewed human dataset exists: the Phase 1 trial (NCT04838405) reported inside the CT-388 characterization paper (Chakravarthy et al., Mol Metab 2025; PMID 41319798), where single doses (0.5-7.5 mg) or 4 once-weekly doses (5-12 mg) in adults with overweight/obesity produced roughly -4.7% to -8.0% mean body-weight change at day 29 vs -0.5% placebo, with tolerability consistent with other incretins. Larger trials are done or ongoing but their results are NOT yet peer-reviewed or posted: a completed 48-week Phase 2 in obesity/overweight (NCT06525935, n=469, primary completion Dec 2025, results not yet posted), a 48-week Phase 2 in overweight/obese T2DM (NCT06628362, n=447, active/not recruiting, primary completion ~May 2026), a Roche Phase 2 combination study with petrelintide (NCT07589686, ZYNERGY, n=486, not yet recruiting), and a large Phase 3 program under the Roche code RO7795068 (NCT07351045 'Enith1', n=2000, obesity without T2DM; NCT07351058 'Enith2', n=1600, obesity with T2DM; NCT07670416, n=300, Chinese patients ± T2DM — all recruiting). Origin: developed by Carmot Therapeutics (Berkeley, CA), acquired by Roche/Genentech in early 2024. What is NOT yet known: any Phase 2/3 efficacy at 48+ weeks, head-to-head data vs tirzepatide/semaglutide, cardiovascular or long-term safety outcomes, and whether biased agonism gives any incremental benefit in humans (the "bias improves efficacy" claim is preclinical).

At a glance

  • Appetite suppression and reduced food intake, driving weight loss (clinically meaningful at 4 weeks in Phase 1; long-term magnitude still unproven in humans)
  • Weight loss: mean ~-4.7% to -8.0% at day 29 across doses in the Phase 1 study vs -0.5% placebo — broadly comparable to early tirzepatide, but from a single small trial
  • Improved glycemic control — improved fasting glucose and oral glucose tolerance in Phase 1; being formally tested in a T2DM Phase 2
  • Delayed gastric emptying (GLP-1R-mediated) — the likely driver of the GI side-effect profile
  • Preclinical: reduced MASH/steatohepatitis pathology and improved metabolic dysfunction in mice (not yet shown in humans)
  • Designed to be combined with the amylin analog petrelintide for potentially additive weight loss (combination only now entering Phase 2)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds and activates two incretin receptors at once (GLP-1R + GIPR) Evidence-in vitro

    Enicepatide is a single engineered peptide that switches on both the GLP-1 receptor and the GIP receptor — the same two gut-hormone receptors tirzepatide hits. In cell assays it activated both receptors. Chakravarthy et al., Mol Metab 2025, PMID 41319798 (DOI 10.1016/j.molmet.2025.102291)

  2. Biased signaling: drives cAMP with minimal receptor internalization Evidence-in vitro

    The engineered twist is 'signaling bias' — in cell assays CT-388 activates GLP-1R and GIPR while causing much less receptor internalization than the native hormones (GLP-1, GIP). The idea is that keeping receptors on the cell surface avoids desensitization so the signal stays strong over repeated weekly dosing. In humans this specific advantage is unproven. Chakravarthy et al., Mol Metab 2025, PMID 41319798; biased-agonism rationale established preclinically for the sibling molecule CT-859 in Rodriguez et al., Cell Rep Med 2025, PMID 40460831 (DOI 10.1016/j.xcrm.2025.102156)

  3. GLP-1R activation in brain and gut suppresses appetite and slows gastric emptying Established

    GLP-1 receptor signaling in appetite centers of the brain (and via gut-brain vagal pathways) reduces hunger and food intake, and slows gastric emptying. This is the best-established mechanism behind incretin weight loss and also behind the nausea/GI effects. Class effect reviewed in Müller et al., Mol Metab 2025, PMID 40024571 (DOI 10.1016/j.molmet.2025.102118); drug-specific appetite suppression in Chakravarthy et al., PMID 41319798

  4. GIPR activation adds complementary metabolic effects (glucose handling, adipose, possible nausea buffering) Contested

    Adding GIP-receptor activation is thought to complement GLP-1: it enhances glucose-dependent insulin secretion, acts on fat tissue and the brain, and may improve tolerability (some data suggest GIPR signaling can blunt nausea). Whether GIPR agonism vs antagonism is optimal is genuinely debated in the field. GIP biology and the agonism-vs-antagonism paradox reviewed in Müller et al., Mol Metab 2025, PMID 40024571; combined incretin action in Chakravarthy et al., PMID 41319798

  5. Glucose-dependent insulin secretion and improved glucose handling Evidence-human

    Incretin signaling increases insulin release from pancreatic beta cells in a glucose-dependent way (so hypoglycemia risk is low on its own). In the Phase 1 study, fasting glucose and oral-glucose-tolerance responses improved. Phase 1 glycemic results in Chakravarthy et al., Mol Metab 2025, PMID 41319798 (NCT04838405)

  6. Net result in humans: caloric deficit → weight loss + better glucose control Evidence-human

    The combined appetite suppression, slowed gastric emptying, and improved insulin/glucose handling produce a caloric deficit and measurable weight loss. In the small Phase 1, 4 weekly doses gave about -4.7% to -8.0% body weight vs -0.5% for placebo — promising but from a single short study. Chakravarthy et al., Mol Metab 2025, PMID 41319798 (Phase 1, NCT04838405)

  7. Preclinical-only: reduced steatohepatitis (MASH) and broader metabolic improvement Evidence-rodent

    In mice, CT-388 improved metabolic-dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH/MASLD) pathology and metabolic dysfunction. This liver benefit is rodent-only and has not been demonstrated in people. Chakravarthy et al., Mol Metab 2025, PMID 41319798 (preclinical section)

  8. Intended combination with amylin analog petrelintide for additive effect Speculative

    Roche's strategy pairs enicepatide (incretin backbone) with petrelintide, a long-acting amylin analog that suppresses appetite through a separate (amylin/calcitonin-receptor) pathway. The hope is additive or synergistic weight loss. This combination is only now entering Phase 2 and has no efficacy readout yet. Petrelintide characterization in Fischer Munch et al., J Med Chem 2025, PMID 41217931 (DOI 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.5c01185); combination tested in NCT07589686 (Roche/Zealand, Phase 2, not yet recruiting)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Unimolecular peptide dual agonist of the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) and GIP receptor (GIPR), both class B (secretin-family) GPCRs. In cell-based assays CT-388 activated both GLP-1R and GIPR. Its defining property is cAMP signaling bias: it evokes cAMP signaling with minimal receptor internalization relative to the native ligands (GLP-1, GIP), a design intended to reduce receptor desensitization/tachyphylaxis and sustain the metabolic signal.

Signalling cascade. Receptor activation → Gs → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA and downstream metabolic signaling. In beta cells this potentiates glucose-dependent insulin secretion; in CNS appetite circuits and via gut-brain vagal signaling it suppresses appetite; GLP-1R signaling in the gut slows gastric emptying. Reduced internalization is proposed to keep more receptor at the cell surface, maintaining responsiveness over repeated weekly dosing (a preclinical hypothesis, not yet demonstrated in humans).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection, once weekly.
Absorption / bioavailability
Subcutaneous peptide; specific human bioavailability figures not reported in the peer-reviewed literature. PK in the Phase 1 study supported once-weekly dosing.
Half-life
Not precisely quantified in the peer-reviewed record; the reported PK were consistent across species (mice, monkeys, humans) and adequate to support a once-weekly regimen. Exact human t1/2 not published (a Chinese Phase 1, NCT07626515, is designed to characterize Cmax/Tmax/AUC).
Metabolism
As an engineered peptide, expected to undergo proteolytic catabolism to amino acids/small peptides rather than CYP-mediated metabolism (class-typical for incretin peptides); drug-specific data not published.
Excretion
Not specifically reported; peptide catabolism expected, class-typical.
Notes
Phase 1 (NCT04838405) tested subcutaneous single doses of 0.5-7.5 mg and 4 once-weekly doses of 5-12 mg. The company reported 'consistent pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics across multiple species.' Formal human PK parameters (Cmax, AUC, Tmax, t1/2) are not detailed in the peer-reviewed record available here.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

In the Phase 1 study, mean percent change in body weight from baseline to day 29 ranged from about -4.7% to -8.0% across CT-388 doses versus -0.5% with placebo, indicating a dose-related weight-loss signal over 4 weekly doses (5-12 mg cohorts). Glycemic parameters improved during fasting and oral glucose tolerance testing. This is short-term (28-day) dose-response only; no validated long-term dose-response curve exists in humans yet. The completed Phase 2 (NCT06525935) was a dose-finding study across low/middle/high doses over 48 weeks, but its results are not yet available. NOTE: per harm-reduction scope, no dosing protocol is provided — these figures describe trial arms, not a usage regimen.

How it compares

  • vs Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound)
    same: Both are unimolecular once-weekly subcutaneous dual GLP-1R/GIPR agonists producing appetite suppression, weight loss, and glycemic improvement
    different: Tirzepatide is FDA/EMA-approved with large Phase 3 outcomes; enicepatide is investigational with only Phase 1 human data. Enicepatide is engineered for cAMP signaling bias with minimal receptor internalization (a mechanistic differentiator), whereas tirzepatide is an 'imbalanced' (GIP-favoring) dual agonist and it remains unclear whether that imbalance is optimal. Whether enicepatide's bias translates to a real clinical advantage is unproven.
  • vs Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy)
    same: Both cause GLP-1R-mediated appetite suppression and weight loss
    different: Semaglutide is a GLP-1-only agonist (no GIP arm) and is approved with cardiovascular outcome data (SELECT); enicepatide adds GIPR agonism and is far earlier in development with no CV data
  • vs Retatrutide (Lilly triple agonist)
    same: Both are next-generation incretin-based obesity peptides in the competitive obesity-drug race
    different: Retatrutide is a GLP-1/GIP/glucagon TRIPLE agonist with published Phase 2 weight-loss data; enicepatide is a DUAL (GLP-1/GIP) agonist with only Phase 1 data. Different mechanism (no glucagon arm) and a much earlier evidence base
  • vs Petrelintide (its intended partner)
    same: Both are obesity peptides intended to be used together in Roche's ZYNERGY program
    different: Petrelintide is a long-acting AMYLIN analog (acts via amylin/calcitonin-receptor pathway), not an incretin. Enicepatide is the incretin backbone; the two are complementary mechanisms, and the combination is only entering Phase 2 (NCT07589686)

History & legal status

Enicepatide originated as CT-388 at Carmot Therapeutics (Berkeley, CA), a fragment-based drug-discovery company. Roche acquired Carmot in early 2024 (deal announced December 2023) largely for this incretin portfolio, giving enicepatide the Roche development code RO7795068. The USAN/INN 'enicepatide' was assigned as it advanced. It has NO marketing approval anywhere. Development pathway to date: Phase 1 (NCT04838405, completed 2024) → Phase 2 obesity dose-finding (NCT06525935, completed Dec 2025) and Phase 2 T2DM (NCT06628362, active) → Phase 3 program under RO7795068 (NCT07351045, NCT07351058, NCT07670416, recruiting from early-to-mid 2026), plus a Chinese Phase 1 (NCT07626515, not yet recruiting) and the petrelintide combination Phase 2 (NCT07589686, ZYNERGY, not yet recruiting). Regulatory status: investigational new drug in clinical trials only.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does the completed 48-week Phase 2 (NCT06525935) confirm durable, clinically meaningful weight loss? Results are not yet public.
  • Does cAMP signaling bias / reduced receptor internalization actually give humans better efficacy, durability, or tolerability than existing dual agonists — or is it a mechanistic story that doesn't change outcomes? (Currently preclinical only.)
  • How does enicepatide compare head-to-head with tirzepatide, semaglutide, and retatrutide on weight loss and glycemia? No comparative trials exist.
  • What is the long-term safety and cardiovascular outcome profile? No CVOT or long-term data.
  • What are the precise human PK parameters (t1/2, Cmax, exposure-response) beyond 'supports weekly dosing'?
  • Body composition: how much of the weight lost is fat vs lean mass, and does the GIP arm mitigate this? (Phase 3 trials include MRI lean/adipose tissue measures.)
  • Does the petrelintide + enicepatide combination deliver additive/synergistic weight loss with acceptable tolerability? (Phase 2 not yet recruiting.)
  • Immunogenicity / anti-drug antibodies over long-term weekly dosing (being assessed in NCT07626515).
  • Efficacy and safety in T2DM specifically (Phase 2 NCT06628362 pending).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is an investigational drug used only within clinical trials; there is no approved-use monitoring protocol. Based on the trial designs and incretin class, the clinically relevant things to MONITOR (not a usage guide) include: GI tolerability (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation), especially early; hydration and signs of dehydration from GI losses; body weight and body composition (watch for excessive lean-mass loss — supported by adequate protein and resistance exercise as a general principle); glycemic parameters (fasting glucose, HbA1c; hypoglycemia risk is low with incretins alone but rises if combined with insulin/sulfonylureas); signs of acute pancreatitis (severe persistent abdominal pain) — trials exclude recent/chronic pancreatitis; gallbladder symptoms with rapid weight loss; and, per class labeling caution, thyroid — trials exclude personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma and screen serum calcitonin. Trials also screen out significant gastroparesis/GI-motility disorders and unstable severe psychiatric illness (with suicidality monitoring, e.g. C-SSRS/PHQ-9, built into the Phase 3 program). Any real-world use outside a trial is unstudied and carries unknown risk.

Caveats

EVIDENCE IS THIN AND EARLY. The only peer-reviewed human data for enicepatide come from one small Phase 1 trial (NCT04838405, n=129 randomized across dosing cohorts) reported inside a single 2025 Molecular Metabolism paper (PMID 41319798) authored almost entirely by the sponsor (Carmot/Genentech). The completed 48-week Phase 2 in obesity (NCT06525935, n=469) had primary completion in December 2025 but results are NOT yet posted or peer-reviewed, and the large Roche Phase 3 program (RO7795068) is ongoing. Treat any claim about durable, real-world weight loss as UNPROVEN. The headline "signaling bias improves efficacy" narrative is preclinical/in-vitro (mouse, monkey, cell assays and the sibling molecule CT-859); it has NOT been shown to give humans an advantage over existing dual agonists. There are NO head-to-head trials vs tirzepatide or semaglutide, NO cardiovascular outcome data, and NO long-term safety data. GI adverse events (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation) are expected as a class effect. The intended petrelintide combination is aspirational — it is only now entering Phase 2 with no efficacy readout. This is an investigational drug: it is not available by prescription, and no legitimate "protocol" for using it exists outside a clinical trial. This monograph is a mechanism/evidence summary, not medical advice.
