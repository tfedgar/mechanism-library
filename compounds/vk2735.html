VK2735

Viking's dual GLP-1/GIP receptor agonist for obesity — the most-watched non-Big-Pharma weight-loss asset, with both weekly injectable and daily oral versions, now in Phase 3 but not yet FDA-approved.

Limited human data 17 citations
Class
Dual incretin receptor agonist (GLP-1 receptor + GIP receptor co-agonist), peptide therapeutic
Primary target
Co-agonist at the GLP-1 receptor (GLP1R) and the glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptor (GIPR) — both class-B G-protein-coupled receptors that signal predominantly through Gs/adenylyl-cyclase/cAMP
Human status
PIPELINE / not approved. As of mid-2026, VK2735 (Viking Therapeutics) is investigational and has NOT been approved by the FDA or any regulator. Human evidence to date: a completed Phase 1 single/multiple ascending dose study (NCT05203237, n=92, healthy/overweight adults; run in Australia; included both subcutaneous SAD/MAD cohorts and an oral MAD-PO arm), a positive completed 13-week Phase 2 subcutaneous dose-ranging study (VENTURE, NCT06068946, n=176; mean weight loss up to ~14.7% at 15 mg vs ~1.7% placebo; published in Obesity 2026, PMID 41508550, DOI 10.1002/oby.70106), and a completed 13-week Phase 2 dose-finding study of the ORAL formulation (NCT06828055, n=280). Two large Phase 3 subcutaneous trials began in June 2025 and are ongoing (VANQUISH 1 in non-diabetic obesity, NCT07104500, ~4,500 participants; VANQUISH 2 in obesity with type 2 diabetes, NCT07104383, ~1,100), both with primary completion targeted for July 2027. No long-term (>78-week) efficacy, no cardiovascular-outcome, and no head-to-head-vs-tirzepatide data yet exist. Diabetics were EXCLUDED from the pivotal Phase 2 SC and oral studies (confirmed in the VENTURE report and both Phase 2 trial registrations).

At a glance

  • Substantial, dose-dependent body-weight reduction: up to ~14.6 kg / ~14.7% at the 15 mg weekly SC dose over just 13 weeks in Phase 2 (VENTURE), vs ~1.7% (1.8 kg) on placebo — a steep, not-yet-plateaued curve (Evidence-human)
  • High responder rate: 93% (130/140) of active-treated participants lost >=5% of body weight vs 12% (4/34) on placebo at 13 weeks (Evidence-human)
  • Appetite suppression / increased satiety and reduced food intake, driven mainly by central (hypothalamic/hindbrain) GLP-1R action (Established for the class)
  • Slowed gastric emptying, contributing to early satiety and to the dominant GI side-effect profile (Established for the class)
  • Glucose-dependent improvement in insulin secretion and glycemic control (Established for the class; VK2735-specific glycemic data limited because diabetics were excluded from pivotal Phase 2)
  • A daily ORAL option in development — a differentiator versus most injectable-only competitors (Evidence-human, early; oral arm tested in Phase 1 and in a completed n=280 Phase 2, detailed peer-reviewed oral results still pending)
  • Predominantly gastrointestinal adverse events (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) that were mostly mild-moderate and declined in frequency after dose titration to steady state (Evidence-human)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds and activates two incretin receptors at once Established

    VK2735 is an engineered peptide designed to activate both the GLP-1 receptor (GLP1R) and the GIP receptor (GIPR) — the same two-receptor strategy used by tirzepatide. Both are class-B GPCRs on pancreatic, gut and brain cells. This dual-agonist identity is stated in the trial registration and the peer-reviewed VENTURE report. PMID 41508550 (VENTURE, DOI 10.1002/oby.70106); NCT05203237 (Phase 1, VK2735 described as dual GLP-1/GIP receptor agonist)

  2. Triggers cAMP signaling inside target cells Established

    When agonists occupy GLP1R and GIPR, the receptors couple to Gs proteins and raise intracellular cyclic AMP (cAMP). This second-messenger surge is the shared molecular switch for downstream incretin effects. (Established for the receptor class; VK2735's specific signaling-bias fingerprint has not been published in peer-reviewed form.) MoA card GLP-1/GIP receptors [GLP1R/GIPR]; PMID 41931049 (NEJM, GLP-1 Receptor Agonists review)

  3. Acts on brain appetite circuits to cut hunger Established

    GLP-1R agonism reaches hypothalamic and hindbrain neurons that govern appetite — including rapid inhibition of AgRP 'hunger' neurons shown in mice — increasing satiety and lowering food intake. This central effect is the main engine of weight loss for the class (Established). GIPR signaling in the brain is PROPOSED to add to food-intake reduction and to blunt the nausea/aversion caused by GLP-1R activation, but that specific GIP contribution rests on rodent work (GIPR in GABAergic neurons) rather than human proof. PMID 39892489 (mechanisms review, human/class); PMID 40857106 (AgRP neuron inhibition, mice — Evidence-rodent); PMID 39612941 (GIPR in GABAergic neurons attenuating aversion, mice — Evidence-rodent)

  4. Slows gastric emptying Established

    Incretin-receptor activation slows the stomach's emptying, so food stays longer and people feel full sooner and eat less. This peripheral effect helps weight loss but is also the direct cause of most of the drug's GI side effects (nausea, vomiting, fullness) and of the perioperative 'retained gastric contents' concern. PMID 39418085 (delayed gastric emptying consequences, class); PMID 41931049 (NEJM review)

  5. Enhances glucose-dependent insulin secretion and lowers glucagon Established

    On pancreatic beta cells the cAMP rise amplifies insulin release, but only when blood glucose is elevated ('glucose-dependent') — which keeps hypoglycemia risk low — while also suppressing glucagon. GIP co-agonism adds insulinotropic and possibly favorable adipose-tissue effects on top of GLP-1. Note: VK2735's own glycemic data are limited because people with diabetes were excluded from the pivotal Phase 2 studies. PMID 39139038 (incretin-based therapies review, class); PMID 41312047 (GLP-1RA/dual-agonist pharmacology review, class)

  6. Net result: large, dose-dependent weight loss over months Evidence-human

    Combined appetite suppression, slowed gastric emptying and improved metabolic signaling produce sustained calorie deficit and progressive weight loss. In the 13-week VENTURE Phase 2 study, weekly SC VK2735 produced mean reductions from 9.1% (9.2 kg, 2.5 mg) up to 14.7% (14.6 kg, 15 mg) versus 1.7% (1.8 kg) on placebo — a curve still descending at week 13, implying more loss with longer treatment (being tested in Phase 3). PMID 41508550 (VENTURE Phase 2, abstract-confirmed figures, DOI 10.1002/oby.70106); NCT06068946

  7. Why add GIP to GLP-1 (the dual-agonist rationale) Contested

    The theory is that GIPR agonism enhances GLP-1-driven food-intake reduction and reduces the aversive/nausea effects, potentially giving better weight loss and tolerability than a GLP-1-only drug. This rationale rests largely on rodent mechanistic work and on tirzepatide clinical data; VK2735 has NOT been tested head-to-head against a GLP-1 mono-agonist or against tirzepatide. PMID 39592891 (why dual > mono review); PMID 39612941 (GIPR-in-GABAergic-neurons, rodent)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Engineered peptide co-agonist that binds and activates both the GLP-1 receptor (GLP1R) and the GIP receptor (GIPR), two class-B secretin-family GPCRs. Same dual-incretin target set as tirzepatide. VK2735's precise receptor potency ratio, signaling bias (e.g., cAMP vs beta-arrestin/internalization) and full amino-acid sequence are proprietary and not yet in a peer-reviewed structure/pharmacology paper; class behavior is used to infer the cascade.

Signalling cascade. Agonist occupancy -> Gs coupling -> adenylyl cyclase -> rising intracellular cAMP -> PKA/Epac signaling. Tissue-specific outputs: pancreatic beta cells (glucose-dependent insulin secretion up), alpha cells (glucagon down), CNS appetite centers (hypothalamus/area postrema/NTS -> satiety, incl. rapid AgRP-neuron inhibition shown in rodents), and GI tract (slowed gastric emptying and motility). GIP co-agonism is proposed to enhance food-intake reduction and reduce GLP-1-mediated aversion/nausea, partly via GIPR on GABAergic neurons (rodent evidence).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Two formulations under development: (a) once-WEEKLY subcutaneous injection; (b) once-DAILY oral formulation (an oral arm was tested as early as the Phase 1 MAD-PO cohorts). Phase 2 SC dosing was weekly with dose titration to steady state; oral Phase 2 dosing was once daily.
Absorption / bioavailability
SC: peptide absorbed from subcutaneous depot (typical for weekly incretin peptides). Oral: an orally administered formulation of the peptide — exact bioavailability, food effect, and absorption-enhancer details are not publicly detailed in peer-reviewed form; oral peptide incretins generally have low single-digit bioavailability requiring higher/daily dosing.
Half-life
Consistent with once-weekly SC dosing (i.e., a multi-day effective half-life for the SC form) and once-daily dosing for the oral form; exact human t1/2 values are not published in the peer-reviewed VENTURE report.
Metabolism
Not disclosed in peer-reviewed literature; incretin peptides of this class are generally cleared by proteolytic degradation (peptidases) rather than CYP metabolism, limiting classic drug-drug interactions.
Excretion
Not publicly specified; peptide catabolism to amino-acid fragments is expected for the class rather than intact renal/biliary excretion.
Notes
Because diabetics were excluded and PK detail is thin in the public record, treat all PK statements as class-inferred except the dosing frequency (weekly SC / daily oral), which is documented in the trial registrations.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Clear, monotonic dose-response over 13 weeks in the Phase 2 SC VENTURE study: mean weight reduction rose from 9.2 kg (9.1%) at 2.5 mg weekly to 14.6 kg (14.7%) at 15 mg weekly, versus 1.8 kg (1.7%) on placebo; 93% of active participants (130/140) reached >=5% loss, vs 12% (4/34) on placebo (figures confirmed against the VENTURE abstract). GI adverse events were also dose/titration-related and declined after titration to steady state. Importantly the weight curve had not plateaued at 13 weeks, so higher cumulative loss is expected with the 78-week Phase 3 exposure. The oral Phase 2 (NCT06828055, n=280) was an explicit dose-finding study; topline results were reported by the sponsor but detailed peer-reviewed dose-response for the oral form is pending.

How it compares

  • vs Tirzepatide (Zepbound/Mounjaro, approved dual GLP-1/GIP agonist)
    same: Both activate GLP1R + GIPR; both work via appetite suppression, slowed gastric emptying and glucose-dependent insulin secretion; both share the GI-dominant side-effect profile and class risks.
    different: Same dual-agonist class and mechanism, but tirzepatide is FDA-approved with a large 72-week efficacy dataset (roughly ~20% mean weight loss at the top dose in SURMOUNT-1, an established benchmark not derived from the VK2735 program) and an outcomes program; VK2735 is investigational with only 13-week Phase 2 data (~14.7% at 15 mg, curve not plateaued) and no head-to-head. VK2735's potential differentiator is a daily ORAL formulation in parallel development.
  • vs Semaglutide (Wegovy/Ozempic, GLP-1 mono-agonist)
    same: Shared GLP-1R-driven appetite/satiety and gastric-emptying effects; overlapping GI side effects and monitoring needs.
    different: Semaglutide hits only GLP-1R and is approved with cardiovascular-outcome benefit shown; VK2735 adds GIP co-agonism and is unapproved with no CV-outcome data. Dual-agonism is theorized to give more weight loss and better GI tolerability, but that advantage is unproven for VK2735 specifically.
  • vs Oral semaglutide (Rybelsus) / oral obesity incretins
    same: Both aim to deliver incretin pharmacology without an injection, and both face the intrinsic challenge of low oral peptide bioavailability.
    different: VK2735's oral form is a dual agonist (vs mono-agonist oral semaglutide) and is earlier-stage. Head-to-head oral efficacy/bioavailability comparisons are not yet published.
  • vs Retatrutide (triple GLP-1/GIP/glucagon agonist, investigational)
    same: Both are next-generation multi-incretin peptides targeting obesity with overlapping GLP-1/GIP biology.
    different: Retatrutide adds glucagon-receptor agonism (a third target) and has shown even larger weight loss in trials; VK2735 is a two-receptor agonist. Both are unapproved, but they occupy different points on the mono->dual->triple agonist spectrum.

History &amp; legal status

VK2735 is developed by Viking Therapeutics (San Diego). It is widely followed as the leading obesity asset from a non-Big-Pharma biotech. Development timeline per the trial registry: Phase 1 SAD/MAD/MAD-PO begun Dec 2021 (NCT05203237, conducted in Australia, completed Dec 2024); Phase 2 SC VENTURE conducted Aug 2023-Feb 2024 with positive topline and full publication in the journal Obesity in 2026 (PMID 41508550, DOI 10.1002/oby.70106); Phase 2 ORAL dose-finding run Dec 2024-Aug 2025 (NCT06828055, n=280); two Phase 3 SC studies (VANQUISH 1 non-diabetic obesity NCT07104500 and VANQUISH 2 diabetic-population NCT07104383) initiated June 2025, both active/not-recruiting with primary completion targeted mid-2027. As of this monograph (2026) there is NO FDA or other regulatory approval, no boxed-label of its own, and no marketed product; grey-market 'VK2735' sold as a research chemical is unregulated and not the clinical product.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Long-term (>78-week) efficacy and durability — does weight loss keep going and then plateau where? Phase 2 curve had not flattened at 13 weeks.
  • Head-to-head efficacy/tolerability vs approved tirzepatide and semaglutide — currently none.
  • Cardiovascular, renal and other hard-outcome benefits — no outcomes trial exists.
  • Efficacy and safety in people with type 2 diabetes — only now being tested (NCT07104383 / VANQUISH 2).
  • Oral formulation's real-world bioavailability, food effect, dose reproducibility, and whether it matches injectable efficacy; detailed oral Phase 2 results not yet peer-reviewed.
  • Magnitude of lean/muscle-mass loss specifically with VK2735 and whether the GIP component mitigates it.
  • Full PK (human half-life, metabolism, excretion), signaling-bias profile, and receptor potency ratio — not yet peer-reviewed.
  • Rare-event safety (pancreatitis, gallbladder, thyroid C-cell relevance) at population scale — requires the large Phase 3 datasets.
  • Weight regain / metabolic adaptation after discontinuation.
  • Safety in special populations (adolescents, pregnancy, elderly, renal/hepatic impairment).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

WHAT TO MONITOR (harm-reduction framing — not a dosing guide, and this is an unapproved investigational drug): (1) GI tolerability — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration; persistent/severe symptoms warrant medical review. (2) Severe abdominal pain radiating to the back (possible pancreatitis) — seek urgent care. (3) Right-upper-quadrant pain, fever, jaundice (gallbladder disease), especially with rapid weight loss. (4) Signs of dehydration and, in anyone on it, kidney function if vomiting/diarrhea is significant. (5) BEFORE any surgery, endoscopy, or sedation/anesthesia, disclose incretin use — delayed gastric emptying raises aspiration risk and may require holding the drug and extended fasting per anesthesia guidance. (6) Muscle/lean-mass and physical function — resistance exercise and adequate protein are the evidence-based mitigations against sarcopenia during rapid loss; consider body-composition tracking rather than scale weight alone. (7) Thyroid: personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2 is a contraindication for the class; any new neck mass/hoarseness/dysphagia should be evaluated. (8) Hypoglycemia risk is low alone but rises if combined with insulin/sulfonylureas. (9) Mental-health/behavioral changes have been discussed for the class and warrant attention. (10) Because grey-market 'VK2735' is unverified, purity/identity/dose cannot be trusted — the clinical trial safety data do NOT apply to such products. This section describes what to watch for and does NOT provide any dose, titration schedule, or administration instructions.

Caveats

PIPELINE ASSET — DO NOT TREAT AS PROVEN. (1) VK2735 is NOT approved anywhere; Phase 3 (VANQUISH 1/2) will not read out until ~2027. All efficacy claims rest on ONE 13-week Phase 2 SC study (n=176) plus a completed but not-yet-peer-reviewed Phase 2 oral study (n=280) — short duration, modest size, single sponsor (Viking), U.S.-only Phase 2 SC sites. (2) The headline 14.7% figure is at 13 weeks and the weight curve had NOT plateaued, so it is not directly comparable to the 68-72-week numbers quoted for approved drugs like tirzepatide/semaglutide — cross-trial comparisons are apples-to-oranges. (3) People with diabetes were EXCLUDED from the pivotal Phase 2 trials, so glycemic efficacy and safety in T2D remain unproven (being tested only now in NCT07104383). (4) No cardiovascular-outcome trial, no long-term safety, no data in adolescents/pregnancy, no head-to-head vs tirzepatide/semaglutide. (5) 'Grey-market' or research-chemical 'VK2735' sold online is unregulated, of unknown identity/purity/dose, and carries all the class risks plus contamination and dosing-error risk — the trial data do NOT transfer to such products. (6) Muscle/lean-mass loss with rapid weight loss is a class-wide concern not specifically quantified for VK2735 yet. (7) The GIP-attenuates-nausea / GIP-boosts-food-intake-reduction rationale is largely rodent-derived and not proven in humans for VK2735. This monograph explains mechanism and what to monitor; it is NOT medical advice and contains NO dosing protocol.
