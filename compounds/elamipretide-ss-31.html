Elamipretide

A cardiolipin-binding tetrapeptide that re-stabilizes the mitochondrial inner membrane to shore up flagging cellular energy — genuinely first-in-class biology. Its clinical record is mostly negative primary endpoints, with one real bright spot: in September 2025 it became the first FDA-approved therapy for the ultra-rare Barth syndrome (accelerated approval, muscle-strength endpoint).

Human status
Approved for ONE ultra-rare indication; investigational for everything else. In September 2025 the FDA granted elamipretide (Forzinity) ACCELERATED approval to improve muscle strength in adult and pediatric Barth syndrome patients weighing >=30 kg — the first disease-specific treatment ever approved for Barth syndrome (PMID 41335372; PMID 41260682). Accelerated approval was based on an intermediate clinical endpoint (knee-extensor muscle strength) and a confirmatory post-marketing Phase 3b/4 trial (4TAZPower, NCT07531251, not yet recruiting as of mid-2026, ~72-week composite functional endpoint) is required. This is a small, narrow approval, not broad validation. Elsewhere the record is humbling: Elamipretide has one of the largest clinical programs of any 'mitochondrial peptide' (Stealth BioTherapeutics), letting community hype be checked against real Phase 2/3 data. Barth data: TAZPOWER (NCT03098797) 168-week open-label extension in ~8-10 patients showed trends/gains in 6-minute walk, lower fatigue, and improved 3D LV volumes (PMID 38602181) — but it is tiny and open-label. Primary mitochondrial myopathy: MMPOWER-3 (NCT03323749; trial status TERMINATED) MISSED its primary 6-minute-walk endpoint overall; a genotype analysis found benefit in the nuclear-DNA (nDNA) subgroup (which includes mtDNA-maintenance/replisome genes such as POLG/TWNK) while the mtDNA-variant cohort showed no difference, with a significant CPEO signal (37.3 vs -8.0 m, p=0.0024) (PMID 39574155). This motivated NuPower (NCT05162768), a Phase 3 restricted to nuclear-DNA mitochondrial disease, now COMPLETED (Dec 2024; results not yet in the registry as of this review). Heart failure (HFrEF): PROGRESS-HF (NCT02788747) found NO improvement in LV end-systolic volume at 4 weeks for either 4 mg or 40 mg SC (P=0.90 and P=0.28) (PMID 32068002). Dry AMD/geographic atrophy: ReCLAIM-2 (NCT03891875) MISSED both co-primary endpoints (LL-BCVA and GA area); a surrogate ellipsoid-zone signal is being carried forward as the primary endpoint in the ongoing Phase 3 dry-AMD program (ReNEW, NCT06373731, active/not recruiting) (PMID 39605874). Route is subcutaneous daily injection (earlier studies used IV or topical ophthalmic); it is not oral. Anything sold as 'SS-31' in the peptide/longevity gray market is research-grade material of unverified identity/purity — NOT the approved Forzinity medicine — and no healthy-aging/longevity use is supported by a completed positive human trial.

At a glance

  • Restores mitochondrial ATP production and improves oxidative-phosphorylation coupling in energy-starved/aged tissue (rodent in vivo, rapid onset)
  • Stabilizes inner-membrane cristae and cardiolipin-dependent respiratory organization; modulates membrane surface electrostatics (biophysical)
  • Reduces mitochondrial reactive-oxygen-species output and improves redox state (mostly rodent/in vitro)
  • Proposed to limit the mitochondrial apoptosis pathway by keeping cytochrome c as an electron carrier (in vitro)
  • In Barth syndrome: sustained improvements in walk distance, fatigue and LV volumes in a very small open-label extension — and, on a muscle-strength endpoint, the basis for FDA accelerated approval (Forzinity, Sept 2025)
  • In primary mitochondrial myopathy: NO overall benefit on the primary endpoint, but a genotype-specific signal favoring nuclear-DNA (e.g., replisome / CPEO) patients
  • In heart failure (HFrEF) and dry AMD: primary endpoints NOT met, with only secondary/surrogate signals
  • Best-supported human safety signal: generally well tolerated, with injection-site reactions the dominant adverse event

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Selective uptake and mitochondrial targeting Established

    Elamipretide is a synthetic 4-amino-acid peptide (D-Arg-2',6'-dimethyl-Tyr-Lys-Phe-NH2) with alternating positive-charge and aromatic residues. This design lets it cross cell membranes without a transporter and then concentrate at the inner mitochondrial membrane, largely independent of the membrane potential — so it does not need a healthy, well-polarized mitochondrion to get in, which is part of why it can reach damaged ones. (Note: the often-quoted '1000-fold+' enrichment figure traces to early cell work and is best treated as an approximate, order-of-magnitude claim rather than a precise constant.) PMID 24117165; PMID 39940712

  2. Binds cardiolipin at the inner membrane Established

    Once inside the mitochondrion it binds cardiolipin, a signature lipid found almost exclusively in the inner membrane, through a mix of electrostatic (charge) and hydrophobic (aromatic) interactions. Biophysical studies on model and mitochondrial membranes confirm it partitions into the membrane interfacial region, with binding density directly related to membrane surface charge. This binding is the anchor for everything downstream. PMID 32273339; PMID 24117165

  3. Modulates membrane surface electrostatics and stabilizes cristae Established

    By binding cardiolipin, SS-31 tunes the electrostatic surface of the inner membrane and alters lipid packing without lysing the bilayer. Contemporary biophysical work reframes the core mechanism away from simple antioxidant scavenging and toward this membrane-electrostatic effect, which is proposed to help preserve the tightly folded cristae where the respiratory machinery lives and to change how ions (including calcium) distribute at the membrane surface. PMID 32273339; PMID 40294492

  4. Proposed to preserve cytochrome c as an electron carrier (not a peroxidase) Evidence-in vitro

    When cardiolipin binds cytochrome c, cytochrome c can switch into a peroxidase that generates reactive oxygen species and helps trigger apoptosis. By occupying/protecting cardiolipin, SS-31 is proposed to keep cytochrome c doing its normal job — carrying electrons in the respiratory chain — rather than becoming a damaging peroxidase. This is the proposed molecular basis for both the bioenergetic and anti-apoptotic effects; it rests largely on in-vitro biochemistry and mechanistic review rather than direct human data. PMID 24117165; PMID 40294492

  5. Improves respiratory-chain organization, coupling and ATP output Evidence-rodent

    Stabilized cristae and cardiolipin are thought to support better organization of the electron-transport complexes, improving oxidative-phosphorylation coupling (P/O) and restoring ATP production. In aged mouse skeletal muscle, a single dose restored in-vivo mitochondrial ATP production, P/O coupling and cell energy state to young levels within one hour, with no effect in young muscle — a fast, functional readout of this step. PMID 23692570; PMID 40294492

  6. Lowers oxidative stress and reduces apoptotic signaling Evidence-rodent

    With the electron chain running more cleanly and cytochrome c held in its electron-carrier role, mitochondrial reactive-oxygen-species output falls and redox state improves; downstream this is reported to limit lipid peroxidation and blunt the mitochondrial (intrinsic) apoptosis pathway in stressed tissue. Most of this is inferred from redox and cell-survival readouts in animal and cell models rather than direct human tissue. PMID 23692570; PMID 38237649

  7. Translates to organ-level functional benefit in some models Contested

    In disease models — heart failure, skeletal/mitochondrial myopathy, ischemia-reperfusion, kidney lipotoxicity, retinal degeneration — restoring mitochondrial bioenergetics translates into measurable functional improvement (e.g., fatigue resistance, preserved cardiac and renal structure). Whether this reliably reproduces in humans is mixed: it does in the ultra-rare Barth setting (basis of the 2025 approval) but repeatedly failed the primary endpoint in larger heart-failure, general mitochondrial-myopathy, and dry-AMD trials (see human_status). PMID 27519664; PMID 40294492

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Aromatic-cationic tetrapeptide (D-Arg-2',6'-dimethylTyr-Lys-Phe-NH2). Binds cardiolipin in the inner mitochondrial membrane via combined electrostatic (cationic Arg/Lys with anionic cardiolipin head groups) and hydrophobic (dimethyl-Tyr/Phe insertion) interactions; partitions into the membrane interfacial region with lipid binding density directly related to membrane surface charge. It is not a classic receptor ligand — the 'target' is a lipid microenvironment, not a protein pocket (no mechanism-of-action entry is curated for it in ChEMBL, consistent with a lipid-directed rather than receptor-directed agent).

Signalling cascade. Cardiolipin binding -> altered inner-membrane surface electrostatics and lipid packing -> stabilized cristae -> protected cardiolipin-cytochrome c interaction (proposed to keep cytochrome c as electron carrier, not peroxidase) -> improved respiratory-complex organization and OXPHOS coupling -> restored ATP, lower ROS, reduced apoptosis. Newer models emphasize the electrostatic/structural mechanism over direct free-radical scavenging.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Subcutaneous once-daily injection is the modern clinical/approved route (40 mg SC in the Barth, AMD and HF trials; the approved Barth indication is for patients >=30 kg). Also studied historically as IV infusion (Bendavia/MTP-131) and as topical ophthalmic solution (Ocuvia). Not oral.
Absorption / bioavailability
Not usefully orally bioavailable (a peptide, degraded/poorly absorbed by the gut), which is why it is injected; SC dosing gives systemic exposure adequate for once-daily regimens.
Half-life
Short plasma half-life (order of hours), consistent with once-daily dosing; exact human t1/2 values are sparsely published. A dedicated impaired-renal-function PK study was conducted with IV MTP-131 (NCT02436447, Phase 1, n=23).
Metabolism
As a small D-amino-acid-containing synthetic peptide it resists ordinary aminopeptidase degradation better than a natural peptide; detailed human metabolic fate is not well characterized in the public literature.
Excretion
Renal handling was considered relevant enough to justify the dedicated renal-impairment PK study (NCT02436447); the precise fraction excreted unchanged is not clearly established publicly.
Notes
An exposure-response point from the MMPOWER-3 genotype analysis: within the responsive nuclear-DNA cohort there was a suggestion of a plasma-concentration-to-6MWT relationship, hinting at (but not proving) exposure-response; this did not hold across the overall trial population.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

No safe/effective dose can be stated for community use, and this entry intentionally gives no protocol. What the trials show about dose is instructive: in HFrEF (PROGRESS-HF) both 4 mg and 40 mg SC daily failed to beat placebo on LV end-systolic volume (P=0.90 and P=0.28), i.e., raising the dose did not rescue efficacy (PMID 32068002). The Barth and AMD trials standardized on 40 mg SC daily. Any exposure-response signal appeared only within the responsive nuclear-DNA myopathy subgroup, not across the board (PMID 39574155). Bottom line: efficacy has tracked with the RIGHT patient (genotype/disease) far more than with dose, and higher exposure has not converted negative trials into positive ones.

How it compares

  • vs MitoQ / SkQ1 (mitochondria-targeted antioxidants)
    same: Both aim to protect mitochondria and reduce oxidative damage; both are 'mito-targeted' molecules popular in longevity circles.
    different: MitoQ/SkQ1 use a lipophilic cation (TPP+) to accumulate via membrane potential and act chiefly as redox-cycling antioxidants; elamipretide targets a LIPID (cardiolipin), works largely potential-INDEPENDENTLY, and its dominant mechanism is now considered structural/electrostatic membrane stabilization rather than direct radical scavenging.
  • vs Other Szeto-Schiller peptides (e.g., SS-20)
    same: Same aromatic-cationic peptide family, same mitoprotective goal, shared Szeto/Weill Cornell origin.
    different: SS-31 (elamipretide) is the lead clinical candidate with the cardiolipin-binding pharmacophore and is now FDA-approved for Barth syndrome; other SS peptides differ in residue composition and remain largely preclinical.
  • vs Idebenone / CoQ10 analogs (classic 'mito' supplements)
    same: Both marketed toward mitochondrial disease and cellular energy; both engage the respiratory chain broadly.
    different: Idebenone/CoQ analogs act as electron carriers/antioxidants within the chain; elamipretide instead stabilizes the membrane scaffold (cardiolipin/cristae) that organizes the chain — an upstream, structural approach rather than substituting an electron carrier.

History & legal status

Discovered by Hazel Szeto and Peter Schiller (the 'Szeto-Schiller' peptides) at Weill Cornell; SS-31 was originally characterized as a mitochondria-targeted antioxidant and later reframed as a cardiolipin-protective, first-in-class agent (Szeto, Br J Pharmacol 2014, PMID 24117165). Developed clinically by Stealth BioTherapeutics under names MTP-131 / Bendavia (IV; cardiac ischemia-reperfusion and heart failure) and Ocuvia (ophthalmic), then standardized as elamipretide for SC dosing. The cardiac IR/HF programs (e.g., PROGRESS-HF) were negative; the pivotal PMM Phase 3 MMPOWER-3 missed its primary endpoint and was terminated; the AMD ReCLAIM-2 Phase 2 missed its co-primaries. The program then concentrated on (1) Barth syndrome, where in September 2025 the FDA granted ACCELERATED approval (brand Forzinity) to improve muscle strength in patients >=30 kg — the first disease-specific Barth therapy (PMID 41335372; PMID 41260682) — with a confirmatory post-marketing Phase 3b/4 trial required (4TAZPower, NCT07531251); (2) genotype-restricted PMM via the now-completed NuPower Phase 3 (NCT05162768); and (3) dry AMD using ellipsoid-zone preservation as the regulatory primary endpoint (Phase 3 ReNEW, NCT06373731). Beyond the narrow Barth indication elamipretide remains investigational. Note: gray-market 'SS-31' sold to the peptide/longevity community is unlicensed research chemical, not the approved Forzinity product.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether the required confirmatory Barth trial (4TAZPower) verifies clinical benefit — the 2025 approval is accelerated, on an intermediate muscle-strength endpoint, and could be withdrawn if not confirmed.
  • Whether the striking preclinical bioenergetic effects reproduce as durable CLINICAL benefit OUTSIDE ultra-rare disease — most primary endpoints in larger populations have been negative.
  • Whether the nuclear-DNA (replisome/CPEO) mitochondrial-myopathy signal confirms in NuPower (results not yet public as of this review).
  • Whether ellipsoid-zone preservation in AMD is a valid surrogate that translates into preserved vision long-term (the Phase 3 ReNEW bet).
  • Human pharmacokinetic detail (precise half-life, metabolism, fraction renally cleared) is thin in the public literature.
  • Long-term safety of chronic injectable use in healthy people (the longevity use-case) is essentially uncharacterized.
  • Whether any healthy-aging / sarcopenia / 'mito-repair' benefit exists in humans — no completed positive trial supports it.
  • Quality, identity and purity of gray-market 'SS-31' peptide products are unverified and unregulated, and they are not the approved medicine.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is education/harm-reduction, not a protocol or endorsement of non-clinical use, and it contains no dosing guidance. If elamipretide is being used — ideally only as the approved medicine under physician care for its labeled Barth indication, or within a supervised trial — reasonable things to WATCH (not a dosing guide) include: local injection sites for reactions or signs of infection (the most common documented adverse event); general tolerability and any systemic reaction after early doses; and, because the mechanism touches ion (calcium) handling and renal handling was a specific PK concern, attention to kidney function in anyone with renal impairment. In disease contexts, the objective functional measures used in trials (6-minute walk, timed sit-to-stand / timed up-and-go, fatigue and muscle-strength assessments for muscle indications; cardiac imaging for cardiac use; OCT ellipsoid-zone and visual-acuity measures for retinal use) are the trackable markers of whether it is doing anything. Seek medical review for: signs of injection-site infection, unexpected systemic symptoms, or any use of unverified gray-market 'SS-31' product (which is not the approved drug). There is no established biomarker for at-home self-monitoring, and no completed trial supports self-directed longevity dosing.

Caveats

Mechanistically this is a genuine first-in-class idea, but the certainty gradient is steep. Cardiolipin binding and membrane-electrostatic effects are well established biophysically; the cytochrome-c 'electron-carrier-not-peroxidase' step and the ROS/apoptosis story rest mainly on in-vitro and rodent work. The leap from 'better mitochondrial energetics in a dish or a mouse' to 'clinical benefit in people' has repeatedly failed at the primary-endpoint level (HFrEF, overall PMM, AMD co-primaries). The one regulatory success is narrow: FDA ACCELERATED (not full) approval in Barth syndrome (Sept 2025) on an intermediate muscle-strength endpoint, still requiring a confirmatory trial — a real milestone, but for an ultra-rare disease and not proof the drug works broadly. Early 'antioxidant/ROS-scavenger' framing is now considered incomplete and partly superseded by the membrane-electrostatics mechanism, so older secondary sources overstate the antioxidant story. Data-hygiene note: one widely propagated citation for elamipretide's anti-apoptotic claim, PMID 24831514, is actually a telmisartan/inflammatory-bowel-disease paper (verified) and does NOT support the claim — the apoptosis mechanism is better anchored to Szeto's cytochrome-c/cardiolipin work (PMID 24117165) and mechanistic reviews (PMID 40294492). Longevity/'mito-repair' community use runs far ahead of evidence: no completed trial supports healthy-human anti-aging benefit, gray-market 'SS-31' is unregulated and is not the approved product, and long-term injectable-peptide safety in healthy people is not established. This monograph is mechanism education and harm-reduction, not medical advice, and deliberately contains no dosing protocol.
