Canagliflozin

An approved diabetes/kidney drug that blocks sugar reabsorption in the kidney and, in the process, mimics parts of caloric restriction. It is a recent mouse-lifespan hit in the NIA Interventions Testing Program (ITP), extending median survival ~14% in male mice only. In humans it is well-studied for the heart and kidney, but no human longevity data exist.

Rodent / preclinical 15 citations
Class
SGLT2 inhibitor (gliflozin / phlorizin-derived C-glycoside phenolic glycoside); repurposed candidate "caloric-restriction mimetic" / geroprotector
Primary target
Sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2, gene SLC5A2) in the renal proximal tubule; blocks glucose (and Na+) reabsorption, causing glycosuria and a mild sustained energy/caloric deficit
Human status
FDA-approved March 2013 (first-in-class SGLT2 inhibitor in the US; trade name Invokana; ATC A10BK02; USAN 2009) for type 2 diabetes, with added indications for reducing cardiovascular events and for diabetic kidney disease. (Note: ChEMBL lists a 2011 first-approval reflecting non-US timing; the US FDA approval was 2013.) Human evidence for its APPROVED uses is strong and randomized: the CANVAS program (PMID 28605608, n=10,142) showed reduced major adverse cardiovascular events (HR 0.86), and CREDENCE (PMID 30990260, n=4,401) showed slowed kidney disease progression (primary composite HR 0.70, ~30% relative risk reduction); class-wide CV benefit is confirmed in the SMART-C meta-analysis (PMID 38583093, MACE HR 0.91). However, for LONGEVITY/anti-aging there are ZERO human outcome data - the geroprotective claim rests entirely on mouse ITP studies. Its safety history includes an amputation signal: CANVAS flagged increased lower-limb amputation (HR ~1.97) and fracture risk, leading to an FDA boxed warning for amputation in 2017; the FDA REMOVED that boxed amputation warning in August 2020 after further data (including CREDENCE, which did not show a significant amputation increase). Off-label use for aging is experimental and not evidence-based in humans.

At a glance

  • Insulin-independent lowering of blood glucose via urinary glucose loss (glycosuria) - established in humans
  • Mild sustained caloric deficit; modest weight/fat loss, lower fasting glucose, flattened glucose peaks
  • Extends MEDIAN lifespan ~14% in male mice (ITP), whether started at 7 months or 16 months; NO benefit and modest harm (~6% shorter lifespan) in female mice
  • Proven human cardiovascular benefit: fewer major adverse cardiovascular events and heart-failure hospitalizations in type 2 diabetes (CANVAS; class-confirmed in meta-analysis)
  • Proven human kidney protection: slows progression of diabetic kidney disease, reduces albuminuria (CREDENCE)
  • Mouse-only neuroprotection: improved brain insulin sensitivity, less neuroinflammation, better activity (male-predominant)
  • Known human harms: genital yeast infections, volume depletion, rare euglycemic ketoacidosis; amputation and fracture signals seen in the CANVAS program
  • NO human longevity, healthspan, or anti-aging outcome data exist - the geroprotection story is entirely preclinical

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Blocks kidney glucose reabsorption (SGLT2) Established

    Canagliflozin sits on SGLT2 in the proximal tubule of the kidney, the transporter that normally recaptures most filtered glucose back into the blood. Blocking it dumps glucose into the urine (glycosuria), lowering blood sugar in an insulin-independent way. This is the on-label, well-characterized action. PMID 26041408 (Devineni & Polidori, Clin Pharmacokinet 2015)

  2. Creates a mild, sustained calorie/energy deficit Evidence-rodent

    Losing glucose in the urine acts like a small daily calorie leak. In humans this lowers fasting glucose and blunts glucose peaks (well established). The idea that it shifts the body toward a fasting-like, fat-burning state and 'wastes calories out the urine' is the leading proposed explanation for its geroprotective signal - but that signal, and the fat-mass loss seen with it (which in the ITP mice occurred in FEMALES only), come from mouse studies, not human aging data. PMID 32990681 (Miller et al., JCI Insight 2020); PMID 26041408 (human glycosuria/PK)

  3. Blunts daily peak glucose (shared with acarbose) Evidence-rodent

    The ITP authors argue the lifespan benefit tracks with flattening daily glucose peaks, not just average glucose. Acarbose, a structurally unrelated drug that also blunts glucose surges (by slowing gut carbohydrate breakdown), extends male mouse lifespan too - convergent evidence that controlling glucose excursions, rather than any single molecular target, may be the throughline. PMID 32990681 (Miller et al., JCI Insight 2020); PMID 27312235 (Strong et al., Aging Cell 2016, acarbose ITP)

  4. Weakly inhibits intestinal SGLT1 -> more GLP-1, lower post-meal glucose Evidence-rodent

    Canagliflozin is not perfectly selective: at the high concentrations reached in the gut lumen after an oral dose it also partly blocks SGLT1 in the intestine. This delays glucose absorption, lowers post-meal spikes, and pushes glucose further down the gut where it triggers GLP-1 (an incretin/satiety hormone) release. This is a real but secondary contributor and distinguishes it somewhat from more SGLT2-selective gliflozins. PMID 26105952 (Oguma et al., JPET 2015); PMID 30240793 (Tsimihodimos et al., Eur J Pharmacol 2018, review)

  5. At higher exposure: inhibits mitochondrial complex I and activates AMPK Evidence-in vitro

    Above the concentrations needed to block SGLT2, canagliflozin partially inhibits mitochondrial complex I, which raises cellular AMP/ADP and switches on AMPK - the master 'low-energy' sensor. AMPK activation restrains the growth-promoting mTORC1 pathway and mimics fasting/metformin-like signaling. Hawley et al. showed this in cells/isolated mitochondria and also in mouse liver in vivo; whether these off-target effects contribute to whole-animal longevity (versus just the glycosuria) is unresolved. PMID 27381369 (Hawley et al., Diabetes 2016); PMID 38660713 (Albalawy et al., Am J Physiol Renal 2024)

  6. Dampens brain insulin resistance and neuroinflammation (male mice) Evidence-rodent

    In aged UM-HET3 mice, canagliflozin restored insulin sensitivity in the hypothalamus and hippocampus, reduced microglial/astrocyte activation, lowered inflammatory signaling (partly via reduced S6-kinase/mTOR signaling in microglia), and improved activity. Hippocampal and behavioral benefits were largely male-specific - mirroring the sex-specific lifespan pattern. This is a downstream tissue effect, shown only in mice. PMID 35707855 (Jayarathne et al., Aging Cell 2022)

  7. Net effect: less late-life disease burden, sex-dependent Evidence-rodent

    The integrated result in mice is delayed/lower burden of the fatal neoplastic and degenerative diseases of old age, extending MEDIAN (not maximum) lifespan in males. Crucially, the same intervention modestly SHORTENED female lifespan (~6%), and increased intestinal tumor burden in a female cancer-prone mouse model - so 'geroprotection' here is conditional, not universal. PMID 38753230 (Miller et al., GeroScience 2024); PMID 34448851 (Korfhage et al., J Gerontol A 2022)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Reversible, selective inhibitor of SGLT2 (SLC5A2), a Na+/glucose cotransporter on the apical membrane of the early (S1/S2) renal proximal tubule; canagliflozin is a C-glycoside phlorizin derivative (USAN stem -gliflozin, defined by ChEMBL as 'phlorizin derivatives, phenolic glycosides'). It is substantially more potent at SGLT2 than SGLT1 (rat/mouse SGLT1 IC50 ~0.55-0.61 uM in Oguma et al. versus low-nanomolar potency at SGLT2), but because luminal intestinal concentrations after an oral dose are high, it achieves clinically meaningful transient SGLT1 inhibition in the gut - more so than most other gliflozins. Above therapeutic target concentrations it also inhibits mitochondrial respiratory complex I, raising cellular AMP:ATP and activating AMPK (Hawley et al.).

Signalling cascade. SGLT2 block -> glycosuria + natriuresis -> lower plasma glucose, mild osmotic diuresis, restored tubuloglomerular feedback (afferent arteriole tone) -> lower intraglomerular pressure (proposed basis of kidney protection); systemically -> caloric deficit, ketone shift, weight loss, blunted glucose excursions; at higher exposure -> complex I inhibition -> AMPK up / mTORC1 down (nutrient-sensing shift). Canagliflozin also inhibits proximal-tubule NHE3 and endocytic albumin uptake independently of SGLT2 (Albalawy et al.).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (immediate-release tablet)
Absorption / bioavailability
Oral bioavailability ~65%; peak plasma (Tmax) ~1-2 h; dose-proportional exposure over a wide dose range; steady state in ~4 days with minimal accumulation; high plasma protein binding (mainly albumin)
Half-life
Terminal half-life ~10.6 h (100 mg) to ~13.1 h (300 mg) in healthy participants, supporting once-daily dosing
Metabolism
Primarily O-glucuronidation by UGT1A9 and UGT2B4 to two inactive glucuronides (M7, M5); minor CYP3A4 oxidative metabolism. Co-administration with the UGT inducer rifampin modestly lowers canagliflozin exposure.
Excretion
~60% excreted in feces and ~33% in urine, largely as inactive metabolites (glucuronides); <1% unchanged in urine
Notes
Molecular formula C24H27FO6S (hemihydrate form), full MW ~462.5 (freebase ~444.5); ALogP ~3.0; 0 Lipinski (rule-of-five) violations; ChEMBL CHEMBL4594217. Systemic exposure rises in renal impairment (though glucose-lowering efficacy falls as filtered glucose load drops). The mouse longevity finding of ~20-fold higher exposure in aged females vs young males (PMID 38753230) is a PK observation with direct relevance to the sex-specific effects.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Human diabetes doses are 100-300 mg once daily; glycosuria and HbA1c lowering are dose-related, with the 300 mg dose giving additional post-meal glucose lowering attributed partly to transient intestinal SGLT1 inhibition. In the ITP mouse work, canagliflozin was delivered in chow at 180 ppm; cross-species dose equivalence is not clean, and the same dietary concentration produced ~20-fold higher blood levels in aged females than young males (PMID 38753230) - a dose/exposure confound that plausibly drives the sex-divergent outcomes. NO human dose-response data exist for any aging endpoint. This entry intentionally gives no human dosing protocol for off-label use.

How it compares

  • vs Acarbose (another ITP lifespan hit)
    same: Both flatten post-meal glucose surges; both show larger male-mouse lifespan benefit.
    different: Acarbose blocks carbohydrate breakdown in the gut (alpha-glucosidase) rather than kidney glucose reabsorption, yet also extends male-biased mouse lifespan (Strong et al. 2016, PMID 27312235). This convergence is a core argument that blunting daily glucose PEAKS - not any one molecular target - may underlie the longevity effect.
  • vs Metformin
    same: Shared AMPK/complex-I off-target biology and 'caloric-restriction mimetic' framing.
    different: Both can activate AMPK, but metformin acts largely via gut/hepatic complex-I inhibition and has NOT robustly extended lifespan in healthy ITP mice, whereas canagliflozin has. Canagliflozin's dominant action is glycosuria/caloric loss; metformin's is hepatic glucose suppression.
  • vs Rapamycin
    same: Both are nutrient-sensing-pathway geroprotectors of interest to the longevity community.
    different: Rapamycin is a direct, potent mTORC1 inhibitor with robust male AND female lifespan extension including effects on maximum lifespan; canagliflozin only touches mTOR indirectly (via AMPK/insulin), extends median (not clearly maximum) lifespan, and only in males.
  • vs Empagliflozin / dapagliflozin (other gliflozins)
    same: Same core SGLT2 mechanism and class-wide human CV/renal protection.
    different: Canagliflozin is among the least SGLT2-selective gliflozins (most intestinal SGLT1 activity, hence extra post-meal glucose/GLP-1 effect), uniquely activates AMPK via complex-I inhibition at clinical concentrations (Hawley et al.), and is the one with ITP lifespan data and the CANVAS amputation/fracture signal. Head-to-head human comparisons show broadly comparable CV effectiveness across the class at effective doses (PMID 39836397).

History &amp; legal status

Canagliflozin (research code JNJ-28431754; Invokana, Janssen) received USAN in 2009 and US FDA approval in March 2013 as the first-in-class SGLT2 inhibitor in the US, from the phlorizin/gliflozin lineage of phenolic glycosides. Later label expansions added cardiovascular risk reduction (post-CANVAS) and diabetic kidney disease (post-CREDENCE, 2019). In 2017 the FDA added a boxed warning for lower-limb amputation based on CANVAS; in August 2020 the FDA REMOVED that boxed amputation warning after accumulated data (including CREDENCE) did not confirm the same magnitude of risk. It entered the geroprotection conversation via the NIA Interventions Testing Program: the 2020 lifespan hit (PMID 32990681) and 2024 mid-life-start replication (PMID 38753230) made it one of ~7 ITP-tested drugs to extend lifespan >=10% in at least one sex, and the ~4th with demonstrated mid-life benefit - which is why it is currently a prominent off-label longevity topic despite zero human aging data.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether ANY human longevity or healthspan benefit exists - never tested; all aging data are murine.
  • Why the effect is male-specific: is it purely a pharmacokinetic exposure difference (aged females ~20x higher blood levels), a hormonal interaction, or both?
  • How much of the mouse lifespan benefit is glycosuria/caloric restriction vs. off-target AMPK/complex-I signaling vs. blunted glucose peaks.
  • Whether the female harm signals (shortened lifespan, increased intestinal adenomas in mice) have any relevance to humans.
  • Optimal timing/context: mid-life start works in male mice, but healthy, non-diabetic human physiology may respond very differently (less baseline hyperglycemia to correct).
  • Long-term bone and fracture risk in non-diabetic, potentially leaner off-label users - the population most likely to self-experiment for aging.
  • Whether GLP-1 elevation via intestinal SGLT1 contributes meaningfully to any geroprotective effect.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For harm-reduction education only (not an endorsement of off-label use, and containing no dosing guidance): clinicians caring for anyone on canagliflozin generally watch for signs of volume depletion (dizziness, orthostatic symptoms, dehydration - higher risk in older people or those on diuretics); genital/urinary infections (glycosuria feeds yeast and bacteria); symptoms of ketoacidosis even with normal blood glucose (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, rapid breathing - warranting urgent evaluation, especially during illness, fasting, very-low-carbohydrate intake, surgery, or heavy alcohol use); foot integrity and perfusion given the CANVAS amputation signal; and bone health/fracture risk given CANVAS and mouse bone data. Labs commonly followed include renal function/eGFR and volume status, with ketone testing if symptomatic. Off-label longevity use adds unquantified risk because the balance of benefit vs. harm has never been established in non-diabetic or female humans, and mouse data explicitly show sex-dependent harm. This is not medical advice.

Caveats

STRONGEST caveat: the entire longevity case is preclinical. In mice the benefit is male-only, extends MEDIAN not maximum lifespan (a disease-delay / survival-curve rectangularization effect, not a demonstrated change in the maximum-lifespan ceiling), and is not universally benign - late-start canagliflozin modestly SHORTENED female lifespan (~6%, PMID 38753230), and it increased intestinal adenoma burden in female cancer-prone ApcMin/+ mice (PMID 34448851). A likely reason for the sex split: aged female mice had ~20-fold higher canagliflozin blood levels than young males (PMID 38753230), so 'the same dietary dose' is not the same exposure. Human extrapolation is unjustified: humans are not calorie-wasting the same fraction, dosing/exposure differ, and no human aging endpoint has ever been measured. Real human harms are documented (genital mycotic infections, volume depletion/hypotension, rare euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis, and the CANVAS amputation and fracture/bone-density signals - the latter mechanistically supported by mouse bone-quality data, PMID 37166526, which showed cortical bone thinning in both sexes and reduced bone mineralization in males). This monograph is mechanism education and harm-reduction only - it is NOT a recommendation to take canagliflozin for aging, and gives no dosing protocol.
