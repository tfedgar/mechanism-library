5-Amino-1MQ

Main target: Nicotinamide N-methyltransferase. Evidence is animal- and lab-only — no human trials yet.

Works in humans?Animal / lab only
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A small molecule that "unplugs the drain" on your cells' NAD+ and energy currency by blocking the enzyme NNMT — in obese and aged mice it shrinks fat and boosts muscle, but zero human studies exist yet.

Rodent / preclinical 8 citations
Class
Small-molecule NNMT enzyme inhibitor (preclinical research compound)
Primary target
Nicotinamide N-methyltransferase (NNMT) — a cytosolic enzyme, overexpressed in fat and liver in obesity and in muscle with aging, that methylates nicotinamide (vitamin B3) using SAM as the methyl donor
Human status
NO human data of any kind. There are no published clinical trials and none registered on ClinicalTrials.gov (BioMCP search returned 0 trials). Every efficacy claim — fat loss, muscle gain, NAD+ boosting — comes exclusively from mouse, rat, and cell-culture experiments, and most of those from a single research group (Watowich/UTMB and its spin-out Ridgeline Therapeutics). Human dosing, safety, oral bioavailability, and whether the mouse effects translate at all are entirely unknown. The compound sold by peptide vendors is an unregulated research chemical, not a tested drug. Online "results" are anecdote and marketing, not evidence.

At a glance

  • Reversed diet-induced obesity in mice: lower body weight, less white fat mass, smaller fat cells, lower plasma cholesterol — with NO change in food intake (so it is a metabolic/energy-expenditure effect, not appetite suppression). Evidence-rodent (PMID 29155147).
  • Raised intracellular NAD+ and SAM and suppressed fat-cell lipogenesis (fat building) in cultured adipocytes. Evidence-in vitro (PMID 29155147).
  • Increased ~40% grip strength in aged sedentary mice, additive with exercise (~60% combined), and improved muscle quality. Evidence-rodent (PMID 38969654).
  • Reactivated senescent muscle stem cells and roughly doubled regenerating muscle fiber size with ~70% greater contractile force after injury in 24-month-old mice. Evidence-rodent (PMID 30753815).
  • Mechanistically distinct from GLP-1 drugs (Ozempic etc.): works on cellular energy metabolism, not the appetite/gut axis. Established (mechanism); body-composition outcomes Evidence-rodent.
  • Selective: did not inhibit related methyltransferases or NAD+ salvage enzymes, and no observable adverse effects at tested doses in mice — but this is NOT human safety data. Evidence-rodent/in vitro (PMID 29155147).

Mechanism, step by step

7 steps1 human-evidenced 5 preclinical 1 speculative

  1. NNMT normally 'wastes' two valuable molecules Established

    NNMT takes nicotinamide (the active form of vitamin B3) and tags it with a methyl group, turning it into 1-methylnicotinamide (1-MNA), which is then excreted. Doing so burns through two of your cells' most precious resources at once: it consumes nicotinamide (the raw material your cells recycle back into NAD+) and it burns a molecule called SAM, the cell's universal methyl-group donor. PMID 24717514 (Kraus 2014, Nature); PMID 38919254 (review)

  2. 5-Amino-1MQ blocks the enzyme (plugs the drain) Evidence-in vitro

    5-Amino-1MQ is a small molecule shaped to slot into NNMT and shut it down. Crucially, lab tests showed it's selective — it doesn't hit related enzymes or the NAD+ salvage machinery — and it can cross cell membranes, so it actually gets inside fat cells to work. PMID 29155147 (Neelakantan 2018, Biochem Pharmacol)

  3. NAD+ and SAM levels rise inside the cell Evidence-in vitro

    With NNMT switched off, nicotinamide is no longer being thrown away — it gets recycled back into NAD+, the coenzyme that powers energy metabolism. SAM also accumulates. In cultured fat cells, blocking NNMT measurably raised both NAD+ and SAM. So the compound doesn't ADD NAD+ like an NMN supplement; it stops the cell from draining it. PMID 29155147; PMID 24717514

  4. Fat cells shift from storing to burning Evidence-rodent

    Higher NAD+ feeds energy-sensing pathways (including SIRT1) and, as the original Nature study showed, frees up the polyamine cycle — a futile loop that burns energy. The net effect in fat cells was suppressed lipogenesis (less fat building) and increased cellular energy expenditure (more fuel burned). This is the proposed 'fat-burning' mechanism the marketing points to — but it is shown in cells and mice, not people. PMID 24717514 (Kraus 2014); PMID 29155147

  5. In obese mice, fat mass and weight drop without eating less Evidence-rodent

    When diet-induced-obese mice were given a potent NNMT inhibitor, they lost body weight and white fat, their fat cells shrank, and cholesterol fell — while eating the same amount. That 'no change in appetite' detail is what makes it mechanistically different from GLP-1 drugs. PMID 29155147

  6. In aged muscle, the same NAD+ boost wakes up muscle stem cells Evidence-rodent

    NNMT climbs with age in muscle. Inhibiting it in old (24-month) mice restored the NAD+/NADH balance, reactivated dormant muscle stem cells, regenerated bigger, stronger muscle fibers after injury, and raised grip strength ~40% — an effect that stacked on top of exercise. This is why it's also pitched for sarcopenia and 'muscle anti-aging.' PMID 30753815 (Neelakantan 2019); PMID 38969654 (Dimet-Wiley 2024)

  7. The human gap Contested

    Every step above the molecular level is mouse or cell data, much of it from one lab and its commercial spin-out. No human has been studied in a published trial, no human dose is established, and the long-term safety of chronically blocking a methylation enzyme is unknown. Treat human fat-loss/muscle claims as hypotheses, not facts. ClinicalTrials.gov search: 0 trials (BioMCP, 2026); PMID 38919254 notes no clinical trials documented

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. 5-Amino-1MQ (5-amino-1-methylquinolinium) is a small-molecule INHIBITOR of nicotinamide N-methyltransferase (NNMT, EC 2.1.1.1) — a cytosolic SAM-dependent methyltransferase. It belongs to a methylquinolinium chemical scaffold deliberately built to mimic NNMT's natural substrate, nicotinamide, so it occupies the nicotinamide (acceptor) pocket of the enzyme. ACTION: enzyme inhibitor / substrate-site competitor (NOT a receptor agonist or antagonist — there is no receptor involved). SELECTIVITY (Evidence-in vitro): the parent methylquinolinium series was shown to be selective for NNMT and did NOT inhibit structurally related SAM-dependent methyltransferases nor enzymes of the NAD+ salvage pathway (Neelakantan 2018, PMID 29155147). PERMEABILITY (Evidence-in vitro): methylquinolinium analogues bearing a primary amine (the '5-amino' substituent) showed high membrane permeability in parallel artificial-membrane (PAMPA) and Caco-2 assays — important because NNMT is an intracellular enzyme (PMID 29155147). NUMERIC AFFINITY GAP: published Ki/IC50 values specifically for 5-amino-1MQ are NOT reported in the indexed peer-reviewed corpus; the Neelakantan paper characterised a 'potent' lead from the series without an indexed numeric IC50 for this exact analogue, and 5-amino-1MQ has NO ChEMBL entry (compound_search returned 0 hits). Treat any specific Ki number circulating in vendor/influencer material as unverified. Certainty: Established (it inhibits NNMT) / Evidence-in vitro (selectivity, permeability) / Contested-numeric (exact potency).

Signalling cascade. The cascade is built on NNMT's normal reaction: NNMT transfers a methyl group from S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) onto nicotinamide (the amide form of vitamin B3), producing 1-methylnicotinamide (1-MNA) plus S-adenosylhomocysteine (SAH). This single reaction sits at the crossroads of THREE pathways, and inhibiting it pushes on all three (Kraus 2014, PMID 24717514). (1) NAD+ AXIS: nicotinamide is the precursor that NAMPT uses to regenerate NAD+ via the salvage pathway. Every nicotinamide NNMT methylates is one fewer available for NAD+ resynthesis, so inhibiting NNMT spares nicotinamide and RAISES intracellular NAD+ (shown directly in adipocytes, PMID 29155147). Higher NAD+ increases the activity of NAD+-dependent sirtuins, especially SIRT1, which deacetylates and activates metabolic regulators (e.g., PGC-1α-type programs) favouring fatty-acid oxidation and mitochondrial activity (Established mechanistically; downstream activation is Evidence-rodent/in vitro). (2) METHYL / SAM AXIS: because the reaction also consumes SAM (the universal methyl donor), blocking NNMT RAISES intracellular SAM and lowers SAH (PMID 29155147). In adipose tissue this increased SAM availability changed histone methylation — specifically H3 lysine-4 methylation — which de-repressed the polyamine-flux enzymes ornithine decarboxylase (ODC) and spermidine/spermine N1-acetyltransferase (SSAT/Sat1) (Kraus 2014, PMID 24717514). (3) POLYAMINE FUTILE CYCLE / ENERGY EXPENDITURE: the up-regulated ODC–SSAT–polyamine-oxidase (PAO) cycle is energy-consuming; NNMT inhibition increased adipocyte oxygen consumption in an ODC-, SSAT- and PAO-dependent manner, and increased urinary/secreted diacetylspermine (a polyamine-catabolism marker), i.e. it raises cellular energy EXPENDITURE rather than suppressing appetite (Kraus 2014, PMID 24717514). NET PHENOTYPE in adipocytes: suppressed lipogenesis, increased NAD+/SAM, increased O2 consumption (PMID 29155147, 24717514). Falsifiable prediction: effects should disappear if NAD+/sirtuin and polyamine-flux nodes are independently blocked — consistent with the ODC/SSAT/PAO-dependence shown.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Preclinical/grey-market only. In published rodent studies the NNMT inhibitor (5-amino-1MQ / methylquinolinium lead) was given by systemic injection (intraperitoneal in the diet-induced-obesity and aged-muscle work). Human-market product is sold as injectable solution and oral capsules; NO human PK route data exist. Certainty: Evidence-rodent (parenteral); human routes Speculative.
Absorption / bioavailability
The defining medicinal-chemistry feature of the 5-amino-methylquinolinium scaffold is engineered membrane permeability: the primary-amine analogues showed high passive AND active transport across membranes in PAMPA and Caco-2 assays (Neelakantan 2018, PMID 29155147), which is why an otherwise charged quaternary-like quinolinium can reach an intracellular enzyme. However, note the molecule is a permanently cationic methylquinolinium — quantitative oral bioavailability (%F) in any species is NOT reported in the indexed literature. Certainty: Evidence-in vitro (permeability); oral %F Unknown.
Half-life
No peer-reviewed plasma half-life (t1/2) for 5-amino-1MQ in any species is present in the indexed corpus. Dosing schedules in the human/grey market (e.g., multiple times per day, per influencer/vendor content) imply a short functional duration but this is NOT supported by published PK. Certainty: Unknown / Speculative.
Metabolism
Not characterised in the peer-reviewed corpus. As an NNMT substrate-site inhibitor it is designed NOT to be turned over by NNMT itself; its own metabolic fate (phase I/II enzymes, e.g. CYPs, other methyltransferases) is unpublished. Certainty: Unknown.
Excretion
No published excretion/clearance data for 5-amino-1MQ. (By contrast the downstream pathway product diacetylspermine is renally excreted as a polyamine-flux marker — that is a biomarker of the drug's effect, not the drug's own excretion.) Certainty: Unknown.
Notes
5-Amino-1MQ is the small molecule itself, NOT an ester or peptide prodrug. The amine substituent is a permeability/affinity feature, not a cleavable promoiety. Blood-brain-barrier penetration is uncharacterised. The biggest honest gap in this compound is PK: essentially none of half-life, bioavailability, metabolism, or clearance has been published for the exact analogue in humans. Certainty: Established (chemical identity) / Unknown (all human PK).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

DOSE-RESPONSE IS RODENT-ONLY; HUMAN DOSE-RESPONSE IS ENTIRELY UNKNOWN. (1) Diet-induced obesity, mice: systemic administration of a potent methylquinolinium NNMT inhibitor to high-fat-diet mice significantly reduced body weight and white-adipose mass, decreased adipocyte size, and lowered plasma total cholesterol — WITHOUT changing total food intake and with no observable adverse effects reported over the study (Neelakantan 2018, PMID 29155147). The indexed abstract does not give an mg/kg dose-effect curve. (2) Microbiome/adiposity, DIO mice: 5-amino-1MQ COMBINED with a low-fat-diet switch produced 'dramatic' whole-body adiposity and weight loss that normalised obese mice to age-matched lean animals faster than diet switch alone (Dimet-Wiley 2022, PMID 35013352) — magnitude framed qualitatively, no dose-response table indexed. (3) Aged muscle, 24-month-old mice (22–24 mo treatment window): NNMTi alone raised grip strength ~40% vs sedentary controls; exercise alone ~20%; NNMTi + exercise additive to ~60%; plus improved intramyocellular lipid content and (with exercise) increased gastrocnemius fiber cross-sectional area (Dimet-Wiley 2024, PMID 38969654). These are the clearest quantified effect magnitudes available — but they are mouse, and the indexed abstract reports the intervention without an mg/kg potency curve. HUMAN: no dose, no dose-response, no efficacy data exist. Effect sizes seen in lean, dieting humans (the populations who use it) are entirely unstudied. Certainty: Evidence-rodent (effect magnitudes) / Unknown (human dose-response).

How it compares

  • vs NMN / NR (nicotinamide mononucleotide / nicotinamide riboside) — NAD+ precursors
    same: Both ultimately aim to raise intracellular NAD+ to support sirtuin activity and mitochondrial/metabolic function.
    different: Opposite strategy: NMN/NR ADD substrate to fill the NAD+ tank; 5-amino-1MQ BLOCKS the NNMT drain that depletes the nicotinamide precursor (and additionally raises SAM and drives the polyamine energy cycle). 5-amino-1MQ also actively suppresses lipogenesis/adiposity in rodents, which precursors do not do the same way. NMN/NR have far more human data; 5-amino-1MQ has none. The community framing 'block the drain vs fill the tank' is accurate. Certainty: Established (NAD+ axis) / Evidence-rodent (5-amino-1MQ effects).
  • vs Other NNMT inhibitors (e.g., bisubstrate / SAM-nicotinamide conjugate inhibitors, and academic NNMTi leads)
    same: Same molecular target (NNMT) and same intended NAD+/SAM-sparing mechanism.
    different: 5-amino-1MQ is a SMALL, membrane-permeable methylquinolinium occupying the nicotinamide pocket; many high-potency research NNMT inhibitors are larger bisubstrate analogues with poor cell permeability — the methylquinolinium series was specifically engineered to get inside cells (PMID 29155147). 5-amino-1MQ is the analogue that actually reached in-vivo rodent efficacy studies. Certainty: Evidence-in vitro/rodent.
  • vs GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide/tirzepatide) — the dominant fat-loss drugs
    same: Both pursued for weight/fat reduction and metabolic improvement.
    different: Completely different mechanism and evidence tier. GLP-1 agonists act on a receptor to suppress appetite and have large human RCTs and regulatory approval. 5-amino-1MQ works intracellularly via NNMT to raise energy expenditure WITHOUT changing food intake in mice (PMID 29155147), and has ZERO human trials or approvals. Users sometimes frame it as a 'stall-breaker' for already-lean dieters — that specific use is entirely unstudied. Certainty: GLP-1 Established-human; 5-amino-1MQ Evidence-rodent only.
  • vs 1-methylnicotinamide (1-MNA / MNAM) — NNMT's own product, sold as a supplement
    same: Both sit on the NNMT reaction and both are marketed for metabolic/anti-inflammatory benefit.
    different: They are on OPPOSITE sides of the enzyme: 1-MNA is the downstream PRODUCT of NNMT; 5-amino-1MQ INHIBITS NNMT and thereby LOWERS 1-MNA production (PMID 29155147, 30552109). Supplementing 1-MNA and inhibiting its synthesis are mechanistically contradictory interventions, which is an under-appreciated point. Certainty: Established (biochemical relationship).

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin opposes Evidence-rodent
    CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin raise GH and IGF-1 through the GHRH-R + GHS-R1a axis. Sustained GH elevation antagonizes insulin action at liver and muscle, tending toward insulin resistance and higher fasting glucose (the same class effect established for GH secretagogues like MK-677). 5-Amino-1MQ does not raise GH; it is a separate metabolic lever (NAD+/SIRT1 energy expenditure and fat oxidation) whose proposed insulin-sensitizing direction runs opposite to the GH-driven glucose burden, so on the glucose/insulin axis it partially opposes/offsets the secretagogue stack, milder in kind than but analogous to the MK-677 edge. As with MK-677, the offsetting is theoretical and rodent-grounded on the 5-Amino side (Nnmt-KO did not improve glucose tolerance), so it should not be assumed to neutralize the GH-induced glucose handling burden. On body composition (GH-driven lean mass + NNMT-driven fat loss) the two are complementary. GH secretagogue -> insulin resistance is Established for the class (analogous to MK-677 human data, PMID 9467542); 5-Amino-1MQ metabolic direction rodent (PMID 24717514), with Nnmt-KO glucose-tolerance null (PMID 30552109). Net partial-opposition on glucose inferred. Watch: Fasting glucose/insulin and HbA1c for GH-driven insulin resistance; IGF-1 if tracking the GH arm. No human data on the combination.
  • MK-677 opposes Evidence-rodent
    MK-677 (a GHSR-1a agonist that raises GH/IGF-1) reliably impairs glucose homeostasis in humans: it raises fasting glucose and reduces insulin sensitivity (a classic GH-driven insulin-resistance effect confirmed across RCTs). 5-Amino-1MQ is proposed to work in the opposite metabolic direction: by raising NAD+ and activating SIRT1-type programs it is expected to favor insulin sensitivity and fatty-acid oxidation (NNMT overexpression associates with insulin resistance, and inhibiting it is proposed to improve it). So on the glucose/insulin axis 5-Amino-1MQ partially opposes MK-677's diabetogenic tendency, while on body composition the two are complementary (GH-driven lean mass + NNMT-driven fat loss). IMPORTANT caveat on the opposition's strength: the 5-Amino-1MQ insulin-sensitizing direction is rodent/in vitro only, and Nnmt-knockout mice did NOT actually improve glucose tolerance (Brachs 2019), and human adipose NNMT rises during weight loss, so the counter-force may be weaker or more context-dependent than the clean rodent story implies. The insulin-resistance axis is the load-bearing edge to monitor. MK-677 raises fasting glucose/impairs OGTT and insulin sensitivity (human RCTs, PMID 9467542); 5-Amino-1MQ insulin-sensitivity direction from NNMT metabolic-target data (PMID 38919254, 24717514, rodent/in vitro), with the counter-nuance that Nnmt-KO did not improve glucose tolerance (PMID 30552109). Net partial-opposition inferred. Watch: Fasting glucose, fasting insulin, and OGTT/HbA1c: MK-677 reliably worsens glucose tolerance in humans, so track whether the combination trends toward hyperglycemia; watch for polyuria/thirst as insulin-resistance signs.
  • Cardarine shares target Speculative
    Cardarine (GW501516) is a PPARdelta agonist that upregulates fatty-acid oxidation and mitochondrial fuel-burning in muscle. 5-Amino-1MQ raises NAD+/SIRT1 activity, and SIRT1/PGC-1alpha coactivate PPARdelta-driven fatty-acid-oxidation gene programs, so the two converge on the same oxidative-metabolism transcriptional output from an upstream (NAD+/SIRT1) versus receptor (PPARdelta) entry point. Their fat-oxidation/endurance effects are thus mechanistically overlapping and potentially additive. Safety asymmetry worth flagging: Cardarine carries a rodent carcinogenicity signal, and NNMT is itself a cancer-relevant enzyme (overexpressed in many tumors, and the 5-Amino-1MQ monograph notes chronic NNMT inhibition in healthy people is unstudied) so stacking a compound with a carcinogenicity flag alongside one whose chronic effect on a cancer-linked pathway is unstudied is a compounding unknown, not a proven hazard. 5-Amino-1MQ NAD+/SIRT1/PGC-1alpha -> FAO (PMID 24717514); NNMT cancer-target caveat in monograph. Cardarine PPARdelta/FAO and rodent carcinogenicity are class facts. Convergence and compounded oncologic unknown inferred. Watch: No specific validated labs; be aware both compounds carry unresolved oncologic-safety questions (Cardarine rodent carcinogenicity; NNMT cancer biology). Track general health and avoid treating either as safe for chronic human use.
  • MOTS-c shares target Speculative
    Both push on the same cellular energy-sensing network from different entry points, but they do NOT share a single molecular target. 5-Amino-1MQ inhibits NNMT, sparing nicotinamide and raising intracellular NAD+ and SAM; higher NAD+ feeds NAD+-dependent SIRT1 and downstream PGC-1alpha/mitochondrial and fatty-acid-oxidation programs. MOTS-c acts through the folate-AICAR-AMPK axis (and CK2 in muscle) to raise metabolic flexibility and mitochondrial efficiency. AMPK and the NAD+/SIRT1/PGC-1alpha axis are mutually reinforcing nodes of one energy-stress network (AMPK can raise NAD+, and NAD+/SIRT1 feeds back on PGC-1alpha-driven mitochondrial biogenesis), so the two compounds converge on overlapping fat-oxidation and insulin-sensitivity machinery. This is convergent pathway stacking, not receptor competition, so effects on energy expenditure and insulin sensitivity are plausibly additive rather than antagonistic. MoA-gen MOTS-c (PMID 25738459 folate-AICAR-AMPK; PMID 36670507 review); 5-Amino-1MQ monograph (PMID 24717514 NAD+/SIRT1/PGC-1alpha axis; PMID 29155147). Convergence on the shared energy-sensing network inferred; no co-administration study exists. Watch: Fasting glucose and HbA1c (both nudge insulin sensitivity, so watch for lower-than-expected glucose), plus general markers of metabolic rate/body composition; there is no validated human protocol for either compound, so treat any observed change as an unstudied signal.
  • Metformin shares target Speculative
    Metformin partially inhibits mitochondrial Complex I, raising the AMP/ATP ratio and activating AMPK (LKB1-dependent), and secondarily shifts the NAD+/NADH ratio. 5-Amino-1MQ raises the NAD+ pool by blocking NNMT-mediated nicotinamide disposal. Both therefore act on the AMPK-NAD+/SIRT1 energy-sensing axis that drives fatty-acid oxidation and insulin sensitivity, so their metabolic/glucose-lowering effects can compound. Mechanistic subtlety: metformin's action partly depends on NAD+/NADH redox balance, and NNMT/NAD+ handling intersects with methyl-group (SAM) flux; chronically raising SAM while altering NAD+ could theoretically shift one-carbon/homocysteine metabolism in someone on metformin (which itself lowers B12 and can raise homocysteine). The dominant expected interaction is additive metabolic/glucose-lowering pressure. 5-Amino-1MQ monograph (PMID 24717514 NAD+/SAM axis); NNMT-as-metabolic-target review (PMID 38919254). Metformin AMPK/Complex-I mechanism is Established-human; the additive co-effect is inferred, no co-use study. Watch: Fasting glucose/HbA1c for additive lowering (hypoglycemia risk if combined with other glucose-lowering agents), plus B12/homocysteine given both compounds' theoretical footprint on one-carbon metabolism; no human data for the pairing.
  • SLU-PP-332 shares target Speculative
    SLU-PP-332 is a pan-ERR (alpha/beta/gamma) agonist 'exercise mimetic' that upregulates mitochondrial biogenesis, oxidative muscle fibers, fatty-acid oxidation, and energy expenditure (mice only). 5-Amino-1MQ raises NAD+/SIRT1 signaling, which drives the same PGC-1alpha-type mitochondrial-biogenesis and fatty-acid-oxidation programs, and ERRalpha is a canonical PGC-1alpha/SIRT1 transcriptional partner. The two therefore converge on overlapping mitochondrial/oxidative-metabolism transcription, so their exercise-mimetic and energy-expenditure effects could be additive. Both are rodent-only research chemicals; SLU-PP-332 also showed cardiac effects at high exposure (ERRs are central in heart), so the shared metabolic push is theoretical and the combined cardiac/metabolic load is unstudied. SLU-PP-332 ERR -> FAO/energy expenditure (rodent); 5-Amino-1MQ NAD+/SIRT1/PGC-1alpha/mitochondrial axis (PMID 24717514). Convergence on PGC-1alpha/ERR programs inferred; no co-use study. Watch: Resting heart rate and any exertional cardiac symptoms (SLU-PP-332 cardiac signal), plus body composition and metabolic markers; no human data for either compound alone or combined.
  • Semaglutide redundant Evidence-rodent
    Both reduce fat mass but by mechanistically DISTINCT routes, which is exactly why the 5-Amino-1MQ monograph flags it as 'mechanistically different from GLP-1 drugs.' Semaglutide drives weight loss centrally via GLP-1R agonism on hindbrain/area-postrema and hypothalamic neurons, suppressing appetite and food intake (Established-human). 5-Amino-1MQ reduces fat in rodents via increased adipocyte energy expenditure with NO change in food intake. Because the two lower fat through non-overlapping pathways (appetite vs. cellular energy burn), their fat-loss effects are additive/redundant toward the same endpoint rather than competing. The caution is that stacking two independent fat-loss drivers deepens the total caloric deficit. Semaglutide central appetite mechanism (STEP program, human); 5-Amino-1MQ fat loss without appetite change (PMID 29155147, rodent); distinctness stated in monograph. Additivity toward the shared endpoint inferred. Watch: Body weight and body composition for excessive or too-rapid loss (especially in already-lean users), plus lipid panel; watch nutritional adequacy given semaglutide-reduced intake layered on an energy-expenditure agent.

History & legal status

"Origin: NNMT emerged as an anti-obesity/metabolic target from Barbara Kahn's lab (Kraus et al., Nature 2014, PMID 24717514), which showed adipose/hepatic Nnmt knockdown protects mice from diet-induced obesity. The 5-amino-methylquinolinium small-molecule inhibitor scaffold was developed and characterised by the University of Texas group (Neelakantan/Watowich et al., Biochem Pharmacol 2018, PMID 29155147) and advanced by the spin-out Ridgeline Therapeutics (named as author affiliation in the 2022 and 2024 mouse studies, PMIDs 35013352, 38969654). REGULATORY STATUS: 5-amino-1MQ is an entirely PRECLINICAL, investigational compound. It has NO approval from FDA, EMA or any regulator for any indication; it is not an approved drug, not a dietary supplement, and not a recognised peptide. A ClinicalTrials.gov search returned ZERO registered human trials for NNMT inhibitors / 5-amino-1MQ, and PubMed shows no human clinical-trial literature. It has no ChEMBL drug record. It is sold online as a 'research chemical' (capsules and injectable vials), which places it outside any approved-use or pharmacopoeial quality framework; legality varies by jurisdiction and it is generally not approved for human consumption. No bans specific to the molecule are documented in the indexed sources, but its sale for human use is not sanctioned. Certainty: Established (preclinical-only, no approvals)."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No human pharmacokinetics at all: half-life, oral bioavailability (%F), metabolizing enzymes, and clearance/excretion route are entirely unpublished for 5-amino-1MQ in humans (or any species for the exact analogue). Dosing claims circulating online are not PK-grounded.
  • No human efficacy or safety data: zero registered clinical trials and zero human outcome studies. Whether ANY rodent fat-loss/muscle effect translates to humans — especially the lean, already-dieting users who actually take it — is unknown.
  • Does human NNMT biology even match the mouse model? Brachs 2019 (PMID 30552109) found adipose NNMT expression RISES during human weight loss while plasma MNAM falls, and Nnmt effects in mice were sex- and diet-dependent and did not improve glucose tolerance — so the clean mouse story may not hold in people.
  • Exact potency and selectivity margin of 5-amino-1MQ specifically: no indexed Ki/IC50 and no ChEMBL entry for this analogue; the dose at which selectivity over other methyltransferases is lost in vivo is undefined.
  • Long-term consequences of chronically perturbing the SAM/methyl pool and the polyamine (ODC/SSAT/PAO) futile cycle — including possible oxidative byproducts and epigenetic drift — have not been studied beyond short rodent windows.
  • Consequence of chronically suppressing 1-methylnicotinamide (1-MNA), a bioactive NNMT product with its own reported vascular/anti-inflammatory roles, is unknown.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only — NO dosing guidance, and there is no validated human monitoring protocol because the compound is unstudied in humans. Reasonable things a clinician could track, given the mechanism and the rodent data: (1) Body composition / weight and, in lean users, signs of EXCESSIVE fat or weight loss given the energy-expenditure mechanism. (2) Lipid panel — rodent data showed lowered total cholesterol (PMID 29155147); worth a baseline and follow-up to detect unexpected shifts. (3) Glucose / HbA1c and insulin sensitivity markers — NNMT modulation affects glucose/insulin handling in mice, though glucose tolerance was NOT improved in knockout mice (PMID 30552109), so changes could go either way. (4) Liver function tests (NNMT is highly expressed in liver and the target was modulated there in Kraus 2014, PMID 24717514). (5) General signs of methylation/metabolic disturbance and any new GI symptoms (microbiome shifts seen in mice, PMID 35013352). (6) For INJECTABLE grey-market product specifically: watch for injection-site reactions, infection, or signs consistent with contaminated/non-sterile material — arguably the highest near-term real-world risk given unverified product quality. Emphasise: absence of human safety data means 'normal labs' cannot be reassuring about long-term safety. Certainty: monitoring rationale is mechanism-based (Evidence-rodent); no human-validated thresholds exist."

Caveats

NO HUMAN DATA — this is the single most important caveat. All fat-loss and muscle benefits are from mice/rats/cells. | SINGLE-GROUP RISK: the direct 5-Amino-1MQ in-vivo work comes overwhelmingly from one research group (Watowich, UTMB) and its commercial spin-out Ridgeline Therapeutics, which has a financial interest in the compound — independent replication is thin. | 'NAD+ booster' framing is indirect: it does NOT add NAD+; it reduces the enzyme that depletes nicotinamide. The leaky-bucket/'plug the drain' metaphor used by sellers is roughly accurate but oversells certainty. | NNMT is also a cancer-biology target: it's overexpressed in many tumors and inhibitors show anti-proliferative effects in cancer cells (PMID 33645410, 39067875). That cuts both ways — it means NNMT does a lot in the body, and chronically inhibiting it in healthy people has unstudied consequences. | Selectivity and 'no adverse effects' were shown only in short mouse studies, not human safety testing. | Sold as an unregulated research chemical by peptide vendors — purity, dose, and even identity of what's in the vial are not guaranteed. | Most online claims trace to commercial/anecdotal sources (the corpus hits were almost entirely low-credibility, commercially-interested social posts), not peer-reviewed human evidence.

Nicotinamide N-methyltransferase.
No — not FDA-approved.
Evidence is animal- and lab-only — no human trials yet.
NNMT normally 'wastes' two valuable molecules. NNMT takes nicotinamide (the active form of vitamin B3) and tags it with a methyl group, turning it into 1-methylnicotinamide (1-MNA), which is then excreted. Doing so burns through two of your cells' most precious resources at once: it consumes nicotinamide (the raw material your cells recycle back into NAD+) and it burns a molecule called SAM, the cell's universal methyl-group donor. PMID 24717514 (Kraus 2014, Nature); PMID 38919254 (review)
Reversed diet-induced obesity in mice: lower body weight, less white fat mass, smaller fat cells, lower plasma cholesterol — with NO change in food intake (so it is a metabolic/energy-expenditure effect, not appetite suppression). Evidence-rodent (PMID 29155147).
