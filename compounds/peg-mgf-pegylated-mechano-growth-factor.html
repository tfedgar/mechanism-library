PEG-MGF

A synthetic copy of the C-terminal tail of a muscle-specific IGF-1 splice variant, PEGylated to last longer in blood and injected after training for muscle repair — but the human evidence is essentially nonexistent, the muscle-cell effect was NOT reproduced by two pharma labs, and no interventional clinical trial of the peptide exists.

Rodent / preclinical 9 citations
Class
IGF-1 splice-variant (E-domain) peptide / putative muscle-repair signalling peptide
Primary target
The 24-amino-acid C-terminal E-domain peptide (MGF-Ct24E) of the IGF-1Ec splice variant (rodent IGF-1Eb)
Human status
No approved indication and no completed INTERVENTIONAL clinical trial of MGF or PEG-MGF as a muscle-growth or repair therapy exists (a ClinicalTrials.gov search returns IGF-1-family trials but no trial administering the MGF/PEG-MGF peptide; the only MGF-specific registration is an OBSERVATIONAL study measuring endogenous serum IGF-1Ec/MGF levels versus tumour burden, not an intervention). Human evidence is limited to (a) descriptive work showing the endogenous IGF-1Ec/MGF splice variant is mechanically induced in muscle, and (b) in-vitro experiments on human muscle cells — not on people receiving the injected peptide. The clinically most rigorous data are NEGATIVE/contested: two pharmaceutical companies (Novartis, GSK) could not reproduce the muscle-cell effects (Fornaro 2014, PMID 24253050). Human use is confined to unregulated grey-market bodybuilding, where reported results are anecdotal and heavily confounded by concurrent anabolic steroids, GH, and IGF-1/IGF-1 LR3. MGF and analogues have been banned in sport by WADA since 2005. As a therapeutic, the honest evidence tier is Contested-to-Speculative; any 'established' framing refers only to the compound's long-standing PRESENCE in the peptide-using community, NOT to established efficacy.

At a glance

  • CLAIMED (marketing): post-workout muscle repair, satellite-cell proliferation, muscle-fibre hyperplasia (more fibres), and localized growth in the injected muscle — run 'PEG' for a longer duration than plain MGF.
  • SUPPORTED IN CELLS ONLY: the synthetic E-peptide can extend proliferative lifespan and promote proliferation/migration of muscle progenitor cells in some in-vitro human/mouse studies — reportedly independent of the IGF-1 receptor.
  • NOT REPRODUCED: two pharma labs (Novartis, GSK) found NO effect of MGF peptide on myoblast or muscle-stem-cell proliferation, no block of differentiation, and no cardiomyocyte ERK activation — directly contradicting the core mechanism.
  • IN LIVE MICE: direct muscle injection reduced fibrosis and inflammation but did NOT improve fibre regeneration; separately, MGF OVEREXPRESSION delayed macrophage resolution and was pro-inflammatory.
  • HUMAN DATA: essentially zero for the injected peptide. No interventional clinical trial of MGF/PEG-MGF for muscle growth exists (only observational studies measuring endogenous IGF-1Ec/MGF levels); reported benefits in athletes are anecdote, often confounded by concurrent anabolic steroids, IGF-1/IGF-1 LR3, GH and training.
  • REGULATORY: MGF and its analogues have been on the WADA Prohibited List (banned in sport) since 2005.

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Mechanical load splices the IGF-1 gene toward the 'MGF' isoform Established

    When muscle is stretched or damaged (e.g. resistance exercise), the IGF-1 gene is alternatively spliced to a variant with a different 3' exon, called IGF-1Ec in humans (IGF-1Eb in rodents), a.k.a. 'mechano growth factor.' In rabbit muscle, stretch plus electrical stimulation upregulated this MGF/autocrine-IGF-1 splice variant, whereas stimulation without stretch did not — linking the mechanical signal to this specific splice form. This is the biological event PEG-MGF is meant to imitate. McKoy et al. 1999, J Physiol, PMID 10087355, DOI 10.1111/j.1469-7793.1999.0583v.x

  2. The distinctive C-terminal E-peptide is the 'active' fragment injected as MGF Established

    The reading-frame shift from splicing produces a unique 24-amino-acid C-terminal 'E-domain' peptide (MGF-Ct24E), cleaved from the mature IGF-1 portion. Bodybuilding 'MGF'/'PEG-MGF' products are synthetic copies of this ~24aa tail (and, in at least one characterized illicit product, a fuller IGF-1Ec-like protein). Note this peptide is a fragment of a pro-hormone that is normally processed within the IGF-1 system; whether the free synthetic peptide is a genuine physiological signalling molecule is itself contested. Splice/frame-shift biology: McKoy 1999 PMID 10087355; illicit-product characterization: Thevis et al. 2014, Growth Horm IGF Res, PMID 25466910, DOI 10.1016/j.ghir.2014.10.004

  3. In some models, the E-peptide pushes satellite cells to proliferate/migrate and delay fusion — reportedly via a non-IGF-1R route Evidence-in vitro

    In cultured human myogenic precursor cells, the synthetic MGF-Ct24E peptide acted as a pro-migration/pro-proliferation signal (modulating u-PA, u-PAR and MMP-7) and, per the authors, appeared to work through a mechanism that does NOT involve the IGF-1 receptor (unlike mature IGF-1). The proposed logic: MGF expands the satellite-cell pool and delays fusion, so more nuclei are available before differentiation — a plausible route to repair. Importantly, the receptor MGF supposedly binds has never been identified. Mills et al. 2007, Exp Cell Res, PMID 17156777, DOI 10.1016/j.yexcr.2006.10.032

  4. On aging human muscle cells, the E-peptide extended proliferative lifespan and later increased fusion — but not in old-adult cells Evidence-in vitro

    In primary human muscle-cell cultures from donors of different ages, the MGF-24aa-E peptide significantly increased the proliferative lifespan and delayed senescence, and later increased fusion, of satellite cells from neonatal and young-adult muscle — but NOT from old-adult muscle. The authors framed this as a possible anti-sarcopenia strategy 'without the oncogenic side effects observed for IGF-1.' This is the single strongest pro-MGF cell paper, but it is still in-vitro human cells, not people. Kandalla et al. 2011, Mech Ageing Dev, PMID 21354439, DOI 10.1016/j.mad.2011.02.007

  5. Structure-function work splits the effects: the N-terminal drives proliferation, the C-terminal (MGF-E) drives differentiation/migration Evidence-in vitro

    A systematic C2C12 muscle-cell study found the IGF-1-homologous N-terminal part of IGF-1Ec promotes proliferation, while the C-terminal MGF-E part promotes differentiation and migration — and concluded the isolated MGF-E peptide 'cannot completely replace all the functions of IGF-1Ec' in muscle repair. This undercuts the marketing claim that the injected 24aa E-peptide alone reproduces the muscle-building biology. Yi et al. 2017, Cell Adh Migr, PMID 28471324, DOI 10.1080/19336918.2017.1318240

  6. CRITICAL: two pharmaceutical companies could not reproduce ANY muscle-cell effect of the MGF peptide Contested

    Scientists at Novartis and GSK independently tried to reproduce the headline claims. MGF peptide up to 500 ng/mL failed to increase proliferation of mouse C2C12 cells, primary human myoblasts, OR primary mouse muscle stem cells; it failed to inhibit myoblast differentiation into myotubes; and neither native nor a stabilized MGF peptide activated p-ERK in cardiac myocytes. Mature IGF-1 (and full-length IGF-1Eb) worked robustly in the same assays. Their conclusion 'call[s] into question whether there is a physiological role for MGF.' This is the most important single fact about PEG-MGF: the core mechanism is contested and failed independent replication. Fornaro et al. 2014, Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab, PMID 24253050, DOI 10.1152/ajpendo.00408.2013

  7. Even in a live-injection mouse model, MGF did not regenerate muscle fibres — it mainly cut fibrosis/inflammation Evidence-rodent

    When MGF was injected directly into contused mouse muscle with regeneration impaired by macrophage depletion, it did NOT improve muscle-fibre regeneration; it reduced fibrosis and dampened inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IFN-gamma, IL-1beta, TGF-beta), chemokines, an oxidative-stress marker and matrix-metalloproteinase markers. So the closest in-vivo analogue to how people use PEG-MGF shows an anti-inflammatory/anti-fibrotic signal, not the hyperplasia/repair growth story that is marketed. Liu et al. 2019, Front Physiol, PMID 31164836, DOI 10.3389/fphys.2019.00601

  8. PEGylation is the only part with solid pharmacology — it extends half-life; it does nothing for efficacy Established

    Native MGF/E-peptide is degraded quickly. Attaching a polyethylene-glycol (PEG) chain shields a peptide from proteolysis and extends circulating half-life — a well-established principle across PEGylated peptides/proteins (e.g. PEGylated GHRH is long-acting and stimulates GH in humans). PEGylation is why 'PEG-MGF' is claimed to be run less frequently than plain MGF. Crucially, PEGylation only changes how long the molecule lasts; it cannot create biological activity the underlying peptide does not have. Munafo et al. 2005 (PEGylated GHRH), Eur J Endocrinol, PMID 16061831, DOI 10.1530/eje.1.01965

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Proposed active moiety is the 24-amino-acid C-terminal E-domain peptide of IGF-1Ec (MGF-Ct24E). Unlike mature IGF-1, its reported in-vitro bioactivity was described as NOT mediated by the type-1 IGF receptor (IGF-1R); the actual receptor/binding partner has never been identified. Structure-function work indicates the IGF-homologous N-terminal portion of IGF-1Ec drives proliferation while the MGF C-terminal E-peptide drives differentiation/migration, so the isolated injected E-peptide does not equal whole IGF-1Ec. PEGylation adds a polyethylene-glycol chain that is pharmacodynamically inert — it alters exposure time, not receptor engagement. Note that the pivotal Novartis/GSK study failed to reproduce ANY of these binding-linked cellular effects.

Signalling cascade. Hypothesized (in-vitro-supported but contested): E-peptide -> unidentified receptor -> pro-migratory/pro-proliferative signalling with delayed differentiation/fusion, plus modulation of the urokinase (u-PA/u-PAR) and MMP-7 systems in myogenic precursors. Critically, the Novartis/GSK replication found NO ERK activation by native or stabilized MGF in cardiomyocytes, so even the signalling node is disputed.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Subcutaneous or intramuscular injection (grey-market users inject IM into the trained muscle post-workout on the theory of local/paracrine action). No approved formulation or human PK study exists.
Absorption / bioavailability
Peptide; oral bioavailability is negligible (digested), hence injection. Local IM injection is presumed to create a high local tissue concentration, but no human data quantify local vs systemic exposure.
Half-life
Native MGF: short (order of minutes, from peptide biology, not a measured PEG-MGF value). PEG-MGF: claimed hours (marketing), NOT established by any published PK study.
Metabolism
Proteolytic degradation by peptidases. Native MGF/E-peptide is short-lived. PEGylation shields the peptide from proteolysis; by analogy to other PEGylated peptides (e.g. PEGylated GHRH, which is long-acting in humans; Munafo 2005), this extends functional half-life — the basis for less-frequent dosing claims vs non-PEG MGF. No published half-life value exists for PEG-MGF specifically.
Excretion
Peptide fragments cleared by proteolysis and renal routes; PEG components are cleared renally/hepatically depending on PEG size. No compound-specific human data.
Notes
All PK statements for PEG-MGF are inferential. The only hard analytical characterization of an illicit MGF product (Thevis 2014) found a 'full-length' MGF (12264.9 Da) with elimination of the terminal lysine and an R109H substitution — i.e. real-world products may differ chemically from the labelled peptide, invalidating any assumed PK.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

No validated human dose-response exists — this document intentionally gives no dosing protocol. In-vitro, pro-proliferative/pro-migratory effects in permissive assays were reported at low (nanogram-per-mL) peptide concentrations, but the pivotal replication tested up to 500 ng/mL with NO proliferative effect, so even the cell-level dose-response is contested. There is no human exposure-response relationship, no threshold, and no ceiling established. Users' regimens are folklore, not pharmacology.

How it compares

  • vs IGF-1 / IGF-1 LR3
    same: Both derive from the IGF-1 gene, are injected grey-market peptides, and are WADA-prohibited; both are frequently stacked, confounding attribution.
    different: IGF-1 and its long-acting LR3 analogue are potent, well-characterized IGF-1R agonists with robust proliferative effects in the same assays where MGF failed (Fornaro 2014). MGF is proposed to act via a different, unidentified receptor and to delay fusion rather than drive full differentiation. IGF-1 has clear hypoglycemia risk and stronger theoretical oncogenic signalling; MGF's proponents argued it lacks these — but MGF also lacks IGF-1's reproducible activity.
  • vs Plain (non-PEGylated) MGF
    same: Same putative active E-peptide and same unresolved efficacy/mechanism problems.
    different: Non-PEG MGF is degraded quickly, so users inject it close to training for a brief local effect; PEG-MGF's PEG chain extends half-life, allowing less-frequent dosing. PEGylation changes duration only, not intrinsic (contested) activity.
  • vs Follistatin / myostatin inhibitors
    same: Both are muscle-growth-targeted grey-market biologics with limited or no controlled human efficacy data for physique enhancement.
    different: Myostatin-pathway agents act through a distinct, better-mapped signalling axis (myostatin/activin -> ActRIIB -> SMAD) with clearer preclinical hypertrophy data; MGF's pathway and receptor remain undefined.

History &amp; legal status

MGF originates from Geoffrey Goldspink's group in the 1990s, which identified a mechanically-induced IGF-1 splice variant in stretched/overloaded muscle (McKoy 1999). The synthetic 24aa E-peptide was explored preclinically for muscular dystrophy and sarcopenia. WADA has prohibited MGF and its analogues in sport since 2005; anti-doping labs later characterized an illicit 'full-length MGF' product and built detection assays (Thevis 2014). It has NEVER been an approved drug; there is no FDA/EMA approval and no completed interventional clinical trial of MGF/PEG-MGF as a muscle therapy. It circulates as a 'research chemical'/grey-market bodybuilding peptide. Around 2013-2014 the muscle-cell mechanism was seriously challenged when Novartis and GSK failed to reproduce it (Fornaro 2014).

Key unknowns / open questions

  • The identity of the receptor MGF supposedly binds — the entire 'IGF-1R-independent' mechanism rests on an unidentified target.
  • Whether the isolated injected 24aa E-peptide has any real effect in living human muscle (no interventional human efficacy trial exists).
  • Why two pharmaceutical companies could not reproduce the core cell effects — assay differences, peptide stability, or absence of a true effect.
  • Real human PK of PEG-MGF: half-life, local vs systemic exposure, and how PEG size/site changes it — all unpublished.
  • Whether marketed 'PEG-MGF' vials actually contain the correct, correctly-PEGylated peptide at the labeled dose (documented sequence modifications in illicit products).
  • Long-term safety, immunogenicity (anti-PEG antibodies), and any oncologic risk from chronic stem-cell-proliferative signalling — essentially unstudied in humans.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Framed strictly as harm-reduction (NOT an endorsement or protocol; no doses, frequencies, or 'how to run it' are given). If someone has used or is considering these grey-market peptides, reasonable things to MONITOR and discuss with a clinician include: injection-site integrity (watch for spreading redness, warmth, worsening pain, fever — signs of infection/abscess needing medical care); any allergic/hypersensitivity symptoms after injection (rash, wheeze, swelling — seek urgent care); if IGF-1/IGF-1 LR3 is being co-used or contamination is suspected, symptoms of hypoglycemia (shakiness, sweating, confusion) and blood-glucose awareness; and general labs a physician might follow when growth-factor peptides are involved (e.g. fasting glucose/HbA1c, and clinician-guided IGF-1 levels). Because product identity and purity are unverifiable, the single highest-yield 'monitoring' step is recognizing that the vial's contents and dose are unknown. Age-appropriate cancer screening should not be skipped given the theoretical proliferative concern. None of this constitutes medical advice for a specific person — anyone using these agents should be candid with a qualified clinician.

Caveats

This document must not be read as an endorsement. Key honesty points: (1) The central mechanism failed independent replication — two pharma labs found NO effect of MGF peptide on muscle-cell proliferation, no block of differentiation, and no cardiomyocyte ERK signalling, and questioned whether MGF has any physiological role (Fornaro 2014, PMID 24253050). (2) The receptor MGF supposedly acts through has never been identified; the 'IGF-1R-independent' claim rests on a handful of in-vitro papers (e.g. Mills 2007). (3) There are ZERO interventional human efficacy trials for muscle growth; every human 'result' is anecdote, and users almost always stack it with steroids/GH/IGF-1, making attribution impossible. (4) The isolated 24aa E-peptide may not reproduce the whole IGF-1Ec protein's biology (Yi 2017), and even direct muscle injection in mice failed to regenerate fibres (Liu 2019); MGF overexpression was actually pro-inflammatory and delayed macrophage resolution (Sun 2018). (5) Grey-market 'PEG-MGF' vials have unverified identity, purity, PEGylation site and dose; the 2014 doping-control paper characterized a supplied 'full-length MGF' product (~12.26 kDa) with a truncated terminal lysine and an R109H substitution — you cannot assume the vial contains what the label says (Thevis 2014). (6) Because the whole premise is stimulating stem/progenitor-cell proliferation within an IGF-1-family growth system, a theoretical concern about promoting growth of pre-existing neoplastic or premalignant cells cannot be excluded, even though direct evidence is lacking. This is a harm-reduction/mechanism document and contains no dosing protocol.
