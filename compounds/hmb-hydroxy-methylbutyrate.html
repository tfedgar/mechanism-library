HMB

The classic anti-catabolic: a leucine metabolite that reliably slows muscle breakdown, with a smaller and more context-dependent push on muscle building — helpful when muscle is under threat, unremarkable in well-fed trained lifters.

Human / approved 13 citations
Class
Leucine metabolite / anti-catabolic amino-acid derivative (dietary supplement)
Primary target
No single receptor
Human status
Widely sold and consumed dietary supplement (not a drug), with decades of human use. It has an ISSN position stand (2024, PMID 39699070) endorsing ~38 mg/kg/day with exercise, and human isotope-tracer data (PMID 23551944) showing a single dose of the free-acid form acutely raises muscle protein synthesis and lowers breakdown. Human efficacy is tier-dependent: most convincing where muscle is catabolically stressed (aging, disuse/bed rest, critical illness, malnutrition); weaker and inconsistent in already-trained, well-fed athletes. Some influential positive resistance-training trials are industry-linked with implausibly large effects and are contested. Reported safe up to ~1 year of oral use in the ISSN stand.

At a glance

  • Attenuates muscle protein breakdown — the most robust, reproducible effect (human tracer + in-vitro proteasome data)
  • Modestly increases muscle protein synthesis acutely (~+70% in fed young men after a single HMB-FA dose; less than an equivalent dose of leucine)
  • May preserve lean mass and function during catabolic threat: aging/sarcopenia, disuse/bed rest, critical illness, malnutrition
  • Small and inconsistent gains when added to resistance training in older adults — recent meta-analyses find at most modest gains in physical function (e.g. handgrip) with no significant effect on body composition or muscle quality
  • May reduce exercise-induced muscle damage and speed recovery (proposed driver of any performance benefit)
  • Effect on trained, well-nourished athletes is small to negligible
  • Reported anti-inflammatory / mitochondrial and lipid-handling effects (mechanistically interesting, clinically unproven)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Leucine → KIC → HMB (endogenous origin) Established

    HMB is a natural downstream product of the essential amino acid leucine, formed via α-ketoisocaproate (KIC). Only a small fraction (~5%) of dietary leucine is converted to HMB in the body, so supplementing HMB directly delivers far more than you could get from food — that is the whole rationale. PMID 39699070

  2. Rapid absorption; free-acid form clears/peaks differently Evidence-human

    Oral HMB is absorbed quickly and appears in plasma and muscle within about an hour. The free-acid gel form (HMB-FA) reaches higher and faster peak plasma concentrations than the older calcium salt in human pharmacokinetic studies (Fuller 2010, PMID 21134325), though later comparative data are more mixed (Fuller 2015, PMID 26373270) and a rat study found the calcium salt more bioavailable overall (PMID 25143371). PMID 21134325

  3. Suppresses the ubiquitin-proteasome breakdown machinery Evidence-in vitro

    HMB's signature action: it dials down the ubiquitin-proteasome system, the cell's main protein-shredding pathway. In murine myotubes exposed to a cancer-cachexia breakdown signal (proteolysis-inducing factor), HMB attenuated proteasome activation and protein loss. This anti-catabolic effect is the most consistent thing HMB does, though the pivotal demonstration is in a cell model. PMID 15574784

  4. Lowers net muscle protein breakdown in humans Evidence-human

    In young men, a single dose of HMB-FA cut muscle protein breakdown by about 57% measured with amino-acid tracers, and did so without needing an insulin spike. This is the cleanest evidence that the anti-catabolic mechanism operates in living human muscle, not just cell culture (single acute study, abstract-level). PMID 23551944

  5. Activates mTORC1 to raise protein synthesis Evidence-human

    HMB also nudges muscle-building upward by activating mTORC1, the master growth switch. The single acute human tracer study showed MPS rose ~70% (vs ~110% for an equivalent dose of leucine). The 2024 ISSN stand reports this mTORC1 activation is largely independent of the normal leucine sensor (Sestrin2-GATOR2), implying HMB works through a partly separate route than leucine itself. PMID 23551944

  6. Modulates autophagy and mitochondrial/lipid handling Evidence-rodent

    Beyond the proteasome, HMB is reported to modulate autophagy (the other major protein-recycling route) and to influence mitochondrial content/dynamics and fat metabolism in muscle — proposed as why it may protect muscle during disuse. A human bed-rest study found HMB affected muscle mitochondrial content/dynamics and lipids, but the deeper autophagy mechanisms lean on rodent/in-vitro work. PMID 28705993

  7. Net result: shifted protein balance toward muscle preservation Evidence-human

    Adding a lowered breakdown rate to a modestly raised synthesis rate tips the muscle's protein turnover balance toward preservation/gain. This matters most when breakdown is pathologically high (aging, illness, immobilization) and matters least when a person is already young, well-fed and training — where the breakdown signal HMB blunts is already low. PMID 23551944

  8. Transcriptional footprint supports a proteostasis role Evidence-human

    A single oral dose of HMB shifts expression of muscle genes tied to protein turnover and — notably — up-regulates amino-acid transporters (LAT1/SLC7A5, PAT1/SLC36A1), a pattern resembling the muscle's response to eating protein. This gives a molecular fingerprint consistent with HMB acting as a proteostasis/anabolic-support signal. PMID 41683256

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. HMB has no known single high-affinity receptor. It acts as a metabolic signaling molecule. Anti-catabolic arm: down-regulates the ubiquitin-proteasome proteolytic system and blunts proteolysis-inducing-factor-driven protein degradation (in-vitro myotube data); also implicated in autophagy modulation. Anabolic arm: activates mTORC1 and downstream effectors (p70S6K1/S6), reported by the 2024 ISSN stand to be independent of the canonical leucine sensor Sestrin2-GATOR2. Acute human transcriptomics show up-regulation of amino-acid transporters (SLC7A5/LAT1, SLC36A1/PAT1), a signature resembling protein feeding.

Signalling cascade. HMB (from diet/supplement or ~5% endogenous leucine conversion via KIC) → rapid plasma/muscle rise → simultaneously (a) suppresses ubiquitin-proteasome + modulates autophagy → ↓ muscle protein breakdown, and (b) activates mTORC1 → ↑ muscle protein synthesis + ↑ amino-acid transporter expression → net protein balance shifted toward preservation/accretion. Magnitude of net benefit scales with how catabolic the baseline state is.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (calcium salt HMB-Ca as powder/capsule; free-acid HMB-FA as gel/liquid).
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapidly absorbed; appears in plasma within ~30-60 min. HMB-FA gel reached higher/faster peak plasma concentrations (+185%) and improved clearance vs HMB-Ca in one human study (Fuller 2010, PMID 21134325), but comparative data are mixed (Fuller 2015, PMID 26373270 found smaller differences; a rat study, PMID 25143371, found HMB-Ca more bioavailable overall). Typical studied intake ~3 g/day (~38 mg/kg).
Half-life
Short — plasma HMB is cleared over a few hours; the free-acid form shows faster plasma clearance than the calcium salt in human PK studies, which underpins interest in more frequent or pre-exercise timing.
Metabolism
Endogenously derived from leucine via α-ketoisocaproate (KIC). A fraction of absorbed HMB is further metabolized (e.g., toward HMG-CoA / cholesterol-synthesis intermediates); the rest circulates as free HMB.
Excretion
Partly excreted unchanged in urine; remainder metabolized. Urinary loss patterns differ between the free-acid and calcium-salt forms.
Notes
Because clearance is rapid, plasma exposure is transient; this underpins interest in the faster-appearing free-acid form and in dosing near exercise.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

The consistently studied intake is ~3 g/day, framed by the 2024 ISSN stand as ~38 mg/kg body weight/day taken with exercise training; this is the dose associated with body-composition benefit in the stand's pooled analyses. Below this, effects are inconsistent; there is no established benefit to markedly higher doses, and no dosing/titration protocol is provided here (harm-reduction scope). Response is strongly modulated by state rather than dose: the same intake produces a larger measurable effect in catabolically stressed populations (aging, disuse, illness) than in young trained lifters. Timing near exercise is proposed to matter for the acute MPS/anti-inflammatory response, especially for the faster-clearing free-acid form.

How it compares

  • vs Leucine
    same: Both activate mTORC1 and stimulate muscle protein synthesis; HMB is leucine's downstream metabolite.
    different: An equivalent dose of leucine produced a larger acute MPS spike (~110% vs HMB ~70%) in the pivotal human tracer study and is cheaper; HMB is distinctly stronger at suppressing muscle protein BREAKDOWN and its mTORC1 action is reported (ISSN stand) to be independent of the leucine Sestrin2-GATOR2 sensor. HMB supplementation delivers far more HMB than diet, since only ~5% of leucine converts.
  • vs Whey protein / EAAs
    same: Both support net muscle protein balance and are used around training and in aging.
    different: Whey/EAAs supply substrate and drive synthesis strongly; HMB adds little on top when protein intake is already adequate, but its anti-catabolic action may matter when intake is low or breakdown is high. HMB is not a protein source.
  • vs Creatine monohydrate
    same: Both are well-studied, safe muscle-support supplements with ISSN backing.
    different: Creatine has far more robust, reproducible performance and lean-mass benefits in healthy trained people via a bioenergetic (phosphocreatine) mechanism; HMB's better-supported strength is anti-catabolic muscle PRESERVATION in stressed states, not performance in the trained and well-fed.
  • vs HMB-FA vs HMB-Ca (the two forms)
    same: Same active molecule and mechanisms.
    different: HMB-FA (free acid gel/liquid) peaked faster/higher in one human PK study (Fuller 2010); but comparative human data are mixed (Fuller 2015) and a rat study found HMB-Ca more bioavailable overall (Shreeram 2014). The 'FA is clearly better' marketing claim is not settled, and no outcome (not just PK) superiority is established.

History & legal status

HMB was popularized in the mid-1990s (work associated with Steven Nissen and Iowa State University) as a leucine-metabolite anti-catabolic and entered the sports-supplement market in both calcium-salt and later free-acid forms. It is regulated as a dietary supplement (e.g., under DSHEA in the US), not as a drug, and is not prohibited by WADA. Commercially it is associated with MTI Biotech/Metabolic Technologies (multiple ISSN-stand authors are affiliated), relevant context when weighing the literature. It has since been incorporated into clinical oral nutritional supplements (e.g., Abbott's Ensure/Juven line) for malnutrition and muscle wasting, where combination-product trials like NOURISH were run.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether isolated HMB (not combined with protein/vitamin D) meaningfully changes hard clinical outcomes in aging/illness — most positive outcome data come from combination products.
  • The true magnitude of benefit in trained, well-fed athletes once industry-linked outlier trials (e.g., Wilson 2014) are set aside; recent meta-analyses in older adults find no significant body-composition/muscle-quality effect and only modest functional gains.
  • Whether HMB-FA is genuinely superior to HMB-Ca for outcomes (not just plasma kinetics) — human head-to-head data are mixed.
  • The precise molecular target(s): how HMB activates mTORC1 independently of the leucine sensor, and how it suppresses the proteasome, are not fully mapped in humans.
  • Long-term (multi-year) effects and effects in younger metabolically healthy people beyond ~1 year of safety data.
  • Human relevance of rodent GH/IGF-I-axis and hyperinsulinemia findings, and of autophagy/mitochondrial mechanisms.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

HMB is low-risk, so monitoring is light and mostly about honest expectation-setting. What to watch/track (no dosing advice): (1) Adequacy of the fundamentals first — total protein intake and a real training/rehab stimulus; HMB adds little if these are lacking. (2) GI tolerance and palatability, especially with the acidic free-acid gel/liquid. (3) In older, ill, or malnourished users, track functional outcomes (grip strength, gait speed, lean mass) rather than assuming benefit. (4) Because a rodent signal exists for insulin/glucose changes at high doses, individuals with diabetes or on glucose-lowering therapy can reasonably keep an eye on glucose control, though human data are reassuring. (5) Anyone with liver or kidney disease, pregnancy/lactation, or on complex medication regimens should involve a clinician, as with any supplement. Stop and reassess if you notice unexpected GI, metabolic, or other symptoms. This is monitoring guidance, not a treatment or dosing protocol.

Caveats

Efficacy is highly context-dependent: HMB is best supported when muscle breakdown is pathologically elevated (aging/sarcopenia, bed rest/disuse, critical illness, malnutrition) and is small-to-negligible in young, well-nourished, resistance-trained people — because there is little excess breakdown left to suppress. Several of the most cited positive resistance-training trials (notably Wilson 2014, PMID 24599749) reported effect sizes far larger than any other muscle intervention and have industry ties and methodological criticism; treat headline 'HMB builds tons of muscle' claims as contested, not established. Notably, recent independent meta-analyses in older adults (e.g., García-Alonso 2025, PMID 41305674) find at most modest functional gains and NO significant effect on body composition or muscle quality. The HMB-FA-is-superior narrative is oversold: free-acid peaks faster in some human PK studies (Fuller 2010, PMID 21134325), but head-to-head data are mixed (Fuller 2015, PMID 26373270) and a rat study found the calcium salt more bioavailable overall (Shreeram 2014, PMID 25143371). Much positive human outcome data comes from combination products (e.g., HMB + vitamin D + protein, as in NOURISH), so isolated-HMB effects are hard to separate. Mechanistic depth (autophagy, mitochondria, anti-inflammatory) leans heavily on rodent/in-vitro work, and the pivotal human MPS/MPB numbers come from a single acute tracer study. HMB is a supplement, not a drug: it is not a substitute for adequate protein and training, and not banned in sport. This monograph describes mechanism and evidence, not a protocol.
