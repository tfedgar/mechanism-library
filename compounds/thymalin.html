Thymalin

Main target: No single receptor. Evidence is animal- and lab-only — no human trials yet.

EvidenceRodent / preclinical
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A Soviet-era calf-thymus peptide complex that pushes immature blood cells toward mature T-cells and dampens inflammation — with intriguing but low-quality human data and an unproven "epigenetic reprogramming" story.

Human status
Not FDA-approved and not evaluated by EMA. Thymalin is a legacy drug developed in the USSR (Khavinson group, St. Petersburg) in the 1970s-80s and registered/marketed in Russia and some former-Soviet states as an injectable immunomodulator [PMID 9637345]. A ClinicalTrials.gov search for Thymalin returns zero registered trials. The modern human data are almost entirely single-center, non-randomized COVID-19 "add-on" reports from the originating group and collaborators [PMID 33575961, 36169363], plus an old long-term geroprotection cohort from the same group that is PubMed-indexed as a randomized/clinical trial but is described by its authors as an open, poorly-controlled study and does not meet modern blinded-placebo standards [PMID 14523363, 12577695] — signal-generating at best, not confirmatory. In Western longevity/peptide circles it is sold gray-market as a research chemical with no pharmaceutical-grade oversight. Treat all efficacy claims as unproven pending independent, blinded, placebo-controlled trials.

At a glance

  • Pushes CD44+/CD117+ hematopoietic progenitors toward mature CD28+ T-lymphocytes in human cell culture (a differentiation, not proliferation, effect) [PMID 33237528]
  • Raises circulating lymphocyte and monocyte counts in severe COVID-19 (human observational) and restores depressed T-/B-cell numbers in models of hematopoietic depression (rodent) [PMID 36169363, 2629662]
  • Dampens pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α) in LPS-stimulated human cells and, for related Khavinson peptides, induces a TNF-tolerant, anti-inflammatory macrophage state in vitro [PMID 37686182, 35408963]
  • Proposed epigenetic gene regulation: constituent short peptides may enter the nucleus and bind DNA/histones to alter methylation and transcription — mechanistically plausible but mostly in silico/in vitro [PMID 34834147, 37686182]
  • Claimed geroprotective / mortality-reducing and homeostasis-normalizing effects in elderly people — from low-quality, effectively open-label studies by the developers [PMID 14523363, 12577695]
  • Generally reported as low-toxicity in its historical clinical use, with the main plausible risk being immune/allergic reactions to a foreign animal-derived peptide mixture (see caveats)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps1 established 3 rodent / preclinical 3 contested / speculative

  1. It's a mixture, not a single molecule Established

    Thymalin is not one defined drug — it is a complex of polypeptides and short peptides pulled out of calf thymus glands by mild acid extraction. From this mixture the developers isolated active small peptides, including the dipeptide Glu-Trp (EW, later marketed as Thymogen) and Lys-Glu (KE, Vilon). So 'how Thymalin works' really means 'how this cocktail of thymus-derived peptides works,' and different fractions do different things. PMID 9637345

  2. Signals immature immune cells to grow up into T-cells Evidence-in vitro

    In human cell culture, Thymalin acted on hematopoietic stem/progenitor cells and shifted them toward mature T-lymphocytes: it lowered the stem-cell marker CD44 and the intermediate marker CD117 (c-kit) about 2-3 fold and raised the mature T-cell marker CD28 roughly 6.8 fold. In plain terms, it nudges 'blank' precursor cells to finish differentiating into working T-cells, rather than simply multiplying cells. This is the core proposed mechanism behind 'restoring T-cell differentiation.' Note this is a single in vitro study from the originating group. PMID 33237528

  3. Restores T-cell numbers when the immune system is depleted Evidence-rodent

    In a rodent model of induced hematopoietic/immune depression, thymalin stimulated lymphopoiesis and helped restore both T- and B-lymphocyte numbers in immune organs. Separately, in humans with severe COVID-19, adding Thymalin was associated with a roughly 2-fold rise in lymphocyte and monocyte counts [PMID 36169363] — a single-center, non-randomized observation, not proof. The consistent theme is a 'top-up when low' effect on immune-cell populations rather than blanket stimulation. PMID 2629662

  4. Turns down the inflammatory 'cytokine storm' signals Evidence-in vitro

    In inflamed human immune cells (LPS-stimulated PBMCs), Thymalin and its active dipeptides EW and KE cut production of the pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-1β, IL-6 and TNF-α by roughly 1.4-6 fold. A separate in vitro study (independent Italian group, THP-1 monocyte/macrophage line) showed the related Khavinson peptides push macrophages into a 'TNF-tolerant,' anti-inflammatory state and reduce adhesion to endothelial cells. So beyond building T-cells, it also acts as an inflammation brake in cell models. PMID 37686182

  5. Proposed epigenetic step: short peptides enter the nucleus and touch DNA Contested

    The signature (and most speculative) part of the story: very short peptides (2-7 amino acids) like KE and EW are argued to be small enough to slip into the cell nucleus, bind specific DNA sequences in gene promoters and interact with histones, and thereby change DNA methylation and which genes are switched on or off. This is the 'epigenetic bioregulation' claim. It is biologically plausible and supported by binding/modeling and some cell work, but it is NOT established that this is how Thymalin produces its clinical effects in a living person, and most of the primary literature is from the originating group. PMID 34834147

  6. Computationally predicted gene targets tie the two effects together Speculative

    Molecular docking predicted that EW prefers to bind a GGAG double-stranded DNA promoter motif (B-form) and KE a GCGC motif (curved nucleosomal form), and that their likely gene targets include AKT1/AKT2 (central to the cytokine-storm pathway), plus ACE2 and CYSLTR1 (EW) and CHUK/IKK-α (KE) — genes functionally tied to IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, IL-4 and IL-10. This offers a candidate molecular route from 'peptide binds DNA' to 'less inflammation,' but it is an in silico prediction with only limited in vitro cytokine confirmation, not a proven in vivo pathway. PMID 37686182

  7. Net effect claimed: rebalanced immunity ('immunocorrection') Contested

    Putting the pieces together, the developers frame Thymalin as an 'immunocorrector' — it raises low immune function and lowers overactive inflammation toward a normal set-point, rather than only stimulating. The downstream clinical claims (fewer respiratory infections, geroprotection, lower mortality) rest on this rebalancing idea, but the human evidence for those endpoints is old, effectively open-label and from the originating group, so the mechanism-to-outcome link is not confirmed. PMID 14523363

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. No defined single-receptor pharmacology. Thymalin is an acid-extracted complex of thymic polypeptides and oligopeptides; its biological activity is attributed to short peptide fragments, chiefly the dipeptides Glu-Trp (EW; the active principle of the derivative drug Thymogen) and Lys-Glu (KE; the peptide Vilon) [PMID 9637345]. The proposed molecular mechanism is not classic ligand-receptor signaling but (a) surface/paracrine action on thymocytes and hematopoietic progenitors driving T-cell differentiation, and (b) a proposed direct intranuclear action in which short peptides penetrate the nucleus and bind sequence-specific sites in gene promoters and histone proteins, modulating DNA methylation and transcription [PMID 34834147]. Molecular docking predicts EW favors a B-form dsDNA GGAG motif and KE a curved nucleosomal-form GCGC motif, with candidate downstream targets AKT1/AKT2, ACE2, CYSLTR1 (EW) and CHUK/IKK-α (KE) [PMID 37686182].

Signalling cascade. Two convergent arms. Immune-cell arm: acts on CD44+/CD117+ progenitors → downregulates CD44/CD117, upregulates CD28 → maturation into functional T-lymphocytes (human in vitro) [PMID 33237528]; restores T/B-lymphocyte numbers in depleted states (rodent) [PMID 2629662]. Anti-inflammatory arm: in monocytes/macrophages and PBMCs (in vitro), drives a TNF-tolerant phenotype and lowers IL-1β/IL-6/TNF-α, reduces endothelial adhesion [PMID 35408963, 37686182]. The proposed epigenetic step (peptide→promoter DNA→methylation/transcription change) is offered as the upstream unifying mechanism but is not confirmed in vivo [PMID 34834147].

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Historically administered as an intramuscular injection (lyophilized powder reconstituted); the classic Russian clinical courses used repeated IM dosing. No oral formulation with established bioavailability.
Absorption / bioavailability
Not formally characterized in modern PK terms. As a peptide mixture it would not survive oral digestion intact, consistent with parenteral use. No human bioavailability data located.
Half-life
Not established in published human PK. Peptide constituents are expected to have very short plasma half-lives (minutes), which is difficult to reconcile with the claimed long-lasting/persistent effects — a genuine open question.
Metabolism
Presumed rapid proteolytic degradation to constituent amino acids/short peptides, as expected for peptides; the very short active fragments (KE, EW) are the proposed bioactive species. No formal metabolism study identified.
Excretion
Not characterized; presumed via normal peptide/amino-acid catabolism and renal handling of fragments. No data located.
Notes
The mismatch between an expected minutes-long peptide half-life and claims of effects persisting years after a 2-3 year treatment course [PMID 14523363] is a major unresolved PK/mechanism gap and a reason to be skeptical of durable-benefit claims.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

No rigorous human dose-response data exist. Historical clinical use employed fixed short IM courses; modern reports (e.g., COVID-19 add-on) used the manufacturer's standard regimen rather than exploring dose-response [PMID 33575961, 36169363]. In vitro, defined concentrations of Thymalin and its dipeptides produced graded effects on differentiation markers and cytokine output [PMID 33237528, 37686182], but these cell-culture concentrations cannot be translated to human dosing. Because the product is a non-standardized extract, 'dose' is not even uniformly defined across sources. No dosing protocol is provided here by design.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Thymosin alpha-1 (thymalfasin, Zadaxin)
    same: Both are thymus-derived immunomodulatory peptides that promote T-cell differentiation/thymic output and are used to correct depressed immunity.
    different: Thymosin alpha-1 is a single, chemically defined 28-amino-acid synthetic peptide studied in multiple randomized trials and approved/marketed in many countries; Thymalin is an undefined multi-peptide calf-thymus EXTRACT with no independent RCTs, no ClinicalTrials.gov footprint, and no Western regulatory approval. Thymalin's signature (unproven) angle is the intranuclear/epigenetic dipeptide mechanism.
  • vs Thymogen (Glu-Trp / EW) and Vilon (Lys-Glu / KE)
    same: These are the isolated/synthesized short-peptide 'active principles' derived from or related to Thymalin, sharing the immunomodulatory and cytokine-lowering activity.
    different: They are single defined dipeptides (more standardizable) whereas Thymalin is the parent whole-extract complex; the dipeptides are the main vehicle for the epigenetic DNA-binding hypothesis.
  • vs Epithalamin/Epitalon (pineal bioregulator, same Khavinson family)
    same: Same developer, same 'peptide bioregulator/geroprotector' framework, frequently co-administered in the mortality-reduction cohort.
    different: Epithalamin is pineal-derived and aimed at neuroendocrine/aging endpoints, not thymic immune reconstitution; combining the two is where the largest (and least rigorous) mortality claims come from [PMID 14523363].

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Thymalin was developed in the USSR by V. Kh. Khavinson and V. G. Morozov (St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology) beginning in the 1970s, isolated from calf thymus by mild acid extraction and introduced clinically as an 'immunocorrector' [PMID 9637345]. It is registered and marketed in Russia and some post-Soviet states but has never been approved by the FDA or EMA and has no ClinicalTrials.gov registrations (a Thymalin search returns zero trials). It re-emerged in Western longevity/biohacker and PED-adjacent communities in the 2020s (partly on the back of the COVID-19 add-on papers and the old Kiev geroprotection cohort), where it circulates as a gray-market research chemical. The bulk of the literature originates from the founding group; independent replication is scarce (the THP-1 in vitro study by an Italian group [PMID 35408963] is a notable exception).

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Whether ANY of the human clinical benefits (mortality reduction, geroprotection, fewer infections) survive an independent, randomized, blinded, placebo-controlled trial — none exists.
  • Whether the proposed epigenetic mechanism (peptides entering the nucleus and reprogramming gene expression via DNA/histone binding) actually operates in vivo at achievable exposures, or is an in silico/in vitro artifact.
  • Basic human pharmacokinetics: absorption, half-life, distribution, and how minutes-scale peptide half-lives could produce years-long effects.
  • The exact, standardized composition and potency of 'Thymalin' — and how much gray-market product matches the historical pharmaceutical preparation.
  • Long-term safety, immunogenicity, and any autoimmune or over-immunomodulation risk with repeated dosing in humans.
  • Whether effects are meaningfully different from (or additive to) the better-characterized single peptide Thymosin alpha-1.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is educational, not a protocol, and not a recommendation to use. IF someone were using Thymalin under medical supervision, sensible things a clinician would watch (given the mechanism and evidence gaps) include: signs of allergic/hypersensitivity or injection-site reactions after dosing (mechanistically the most plausible acute harm from a foreign animal-peptide injectable); a complete blood count with lymphocyte/monocyte differential to track the claimed immune-cell changes; inflammatory markers (CRP, and where relevant IL-6, ferritin, D-dimer) that were the reported readouts in the COVID studies [PMID 33575961, 36169363]; caution and medical oversight in anyone with autoimmune disease, on immunosuppression, or post-transplant, since altering T-cell differentiation and cytokine balance could theoretically destabilize immune homeostasis; and rigorous attention to product source/sterility/pharmaceutical grade because gray-market animal-tissue extracts carry contamination risk unrelated to the drug's intrinsic pharmacology. Any new or worsening symptom after injection warrants stopping and seeking medical evaluation. No dosing, cycling, or 'how to run it' guidance is provided.

Caveats

Read this as an emerging/legacy compound with a thin, biased evidence base, not a proven therapy. (1) Identity/standardization: Thymalin is an animal-tissue extract, not a defined single molecule — potency and exact composition vary by manufacturer, and gray-market "Thymalin" is not pharmaceutical-grade or purity-verified. (2) Evidence quality: the striking human claims — ~40%-to-~20% mortality drop in one COVID series, 2-4x mortality reduction in the geroprotection cohort, fewer infections — trace to single-center, non-randomized or effectively open-label studies run by the developers (Khavinson group) and their collaborators [PMID 14523363, 12577695, 33575961, 36169363]; note the 2003 geroprotection paper is PubMed-tagged as a randomized/clinical trial but is described by its own authors as open and poorly controlled, and there are no independent RCTs and nothing on ClinicalTrials.gov. Expect strong publication and allegiance bias. (3) The headline "epigenetic reprogramming" mechanism is largely in silico (molecular docking) plus in vitro binding/cytokine studies [PMID 34834147, 37686182] — plausible but not shown to drive real clinical effects in people. (4) Species/route gaps: much mechanistic work is in cell lines or rodents; human data are correlational. (5) Safety data are historical and low-rigor — "well tolerated" claims come from the same low-quality literature, and being a foreign bovine-peptide mixture given by injection it carries real (if under-quantified) risks of allergic/hypersensitivity reactions and, in theory, contamination if not sterile/pharmaceutical-grade. (6) This is not medical advice and contains no dosing guidance; it summarizes published evidence and its serious limitations.
