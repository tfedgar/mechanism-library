S-23

A high-affinity, fully agonist nonsteroidal SARM originally built as a male birth-control pill — it shuts down your own testosterone and sperm production hard, while pushing muscle and bone like a steroid would.

Limited human data 23 citations
Class
Nonsteroidal selective androgen receptor modulator (SARM), arylpropionamide chemotype
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR / NR3C4) — binds the ligand-binding pocket as a near-full agonist with very high (sub-nanomolar to low-nanomolar) affinity, among the strongest-binding SARMs characterized
Human status
No approved human use and no published human clinical trials. Evidence is preclinical (rodent) plus in-vitro AR assays; the bulk of recent human-relevant literature is anti-doping metabolite/detection work (it is WADA-prohibited). Sold only on the gray/research-chemical market, frequently mislabeled or contaminated. Certainty for all physiological effects in people: Evidence-rodent / Speculative for humans.

At a glance

  • Anabolic in muscle and bone in castrated rodents — restores/increases muscle and bone mass comparable to testosterone (Evidence-rodent)
  • Strong suppression of the HPG axis: lowers LH/FSH and reversibly halts spermatogenesis — the property that made it a male-contraceptive candidate (Evidence-rodent)
  • Relatively spares the prostate vs testosterone at contraceptive-equivalent doses — the 'selective' part of SARM (Evidence-rodent)
  • Increases sexual motivation in female rodents and restores sexual/motor behavior in castrated males (Evidence-rodent)
  • Expected human harms by SARM class: HDL cholesterol suppression and natural-testosterone shutdown; liver injury (DILI) reported with SARM products generally (Evidence-human, class-level)
  • Cardiovascular concern flagged for SARM-containing supplements broadly (Contested / Evidence-human class-level)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds the androgen receptor with very high affinity Evidence-rodent / Evidence-in vitro

    S-23 is a small non-steroid molecule that slots into the same pocket of the androgen receptor (AR) that testosterone and DHT use. It binds tightly — reported as one of the higher-affinity SARMs — so it competes effectively with your own androgens. PMID 18772237 (Jones 2009, Endocrinology); corpus MoA-gen S-23 (PMID )

  2. Acts as a near-full agonist, triggering a specific receptor shape Evidence-in vitro

    Once bound, S-23 bends the receptor into an active shape and recruits a particular set of helper proteins (cofactors) — a pattern that differs from the one testosterone produces. This ligand-specific cofactor recruitment is the leading explanation for why SARMs hit muscle and bone but go easier on the prostate. PMID 18801930 (Bohl/Narayanan, S-22 cofactor study); corpus MoA-gen S-23 (PMID , )

  3. Drives androgen-responsive genes in muscle and bone (genomic action) Evidence-rodent

    The activated receptor moves into the cell nucleus and switches on androgen target genes — the same genomic program that builds skeletal muscle and increases bone mineral density. In castrated rats this rebuilds muscle and bone toward intact levels. corpus MoA-gen S-23 (PMID 18801930, 37113659, 23649620); class context PMID 19079612

  4. Suppresses the brain–testis axis (negative feedback) Evidence-rodent

    Because the brain reads S-23 as an androgen, it dials down LH and FSH from the pituitary. With those signals gone, the testes stop making testosterone and stop producing sperm — reversible infertility. This was the intended contraceptive effect, and it is also why recreational users crash their own hormones. PMID 18772237 (Jones 2009 — characterized S-23 for hormonal male contraception)

  5. Tissue selectivity: muscle/bone gain with relative prostate sparing Evidence-rodent

    At doses that fully suppressed fertility, S-23 produced strong muscle and bone effects while having a smaller effect on prostate weight than equivalent testosterone — the defining 'selective' behavior. Note: 'selective' does not mean 'side-effect-free.' PMID 18772237 (Jones 2009)

  6. Why it is NOT a 17-alpha-alkyl oral steroid (the key differentiator) Established (chemistry) / Evidence-human (SARM-class DILI & lipids)

    Oral 17-alpha-alkylated anabolic steroids (e.g. methandrostenolone, stanozolol) are toxic to the liver and crush HDL partly because the 17-alpha methyl group makes them survive liver first-pass and stresses hepatocytes. S-23 is a non-steroidal arylpropionamide — a different scaffold — so that specific 17-alpha hepatotoxic/lipid pathway does not apply. However, SARMs as a class still show drug-induced liver injury and HDL suppression in human case reports, so 'not a steroid' is not 'safe for the liver or lipids.' PMID 36479151 (SARMs as emerging liver toxin); PMID 38059982 (SARM adverse events incl. DILI); PMID 39755947 (athlete SARM abuse systematic review)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. S-23 = (S)-N-(4-cyano-3-trifluoromethyl-phenyl)-3-(3-fluoro,4-chlorophenoxy)-2-hydroxy-2-methyl-propanamide, an arylpropionamide nonsteroidal SARM from the Dalton/Miller (Ohio State / GTx) series. It binds the androgen receptor (AR) ligand-binding domain (LBD) with HIGH affinity: competitive inhibitory constant Ki = 1.7 +/- 0.2 nM in a rat-AR radioligand displacement assay [Evidence-rodent/in vitro; PMID 18772237]. This is roughly an order of magnitude tighter than first-generation arylpropionamide SARMs (e.g. S-1, S-4) and approaches DHT-like affinity. In the same series, S-23's AR relative binding affinity was reported near the high end of the analog set (the series spanned ~0.1% to 26.5% of DHT) [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 20444881]. In a cell-based AR transactivation (luciferase) reporter assay S-23 behaved as a FULL agonist, reaching efficacy comparable to DHT/testosterone rather than the partial-agonist profile of most prostate-sparing SARMs [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 18772237]. Uniquely within its series, S-23 was a FULL agonist in BOTH androgenic (prostate, seminal vesicle) and anabolic (levator ani muscle) tissues in vivo, i.e. it is comparatively NON-tissue-selective relative to sibling SARMs that spare the prostate [Evidence-rodent; PMID 20444881, 18772237]. As a nonsteroidal arylpropionamide it is non-aromatizable (cannot be converted to estradiol) and is not a 5-alpha-reductase substrate. No published human AR-affinity or off-target (PR/GR/ER/MR, hERG) selectivity panel exists for S-23 [Speculative for off-target].

Signalling cascade. S-23 acts through the canonical genomic AR pathway [Established for the receptor; class mechanism]. (1) Lipophilic S-23 diffuses across the plasma membrane and binds the AR LBD (activation-function-2 pocket), as for DHT [Established]. (2) Ligand binding triggers dissociation of chaperone heat-shock proteins (HSP90 and co-chaperones) from cytoplasmic AR and a conformational change in the LBD that repositions helix 12/AF-2 [Established]. (3) The liganded AR homodimerizes and translocates to the nucleus via its nuclear localization signal [Established]. (4) The AR dimer binds androgen response elements (AREs) in promoters/enhancers of androgen-responsive genes [Established]. (5) Coactivator complexes (e.g. SRC/p160 family, CBP/p300) are recruited and corepressors (N-CoR/SMRT) released, assembling a transcriptionally active complex that drives expression of genes such as those for muscle hypertrophy and (in androgenic tissue) prostate/seminal-vesicle programs [Established; PMID 16492776 for SARM coactivator handling]. A central feature of SARM tissue selectivity is that the SARM-bound AR recruits a ligand-specific subset of cofactors and engages distinct upstream kinase signalling versus DHT; for the closely related arylpropionamide S-22, the nonsteroidal ligand drove AR action through DISTINCT genomic and NON-genomic pathways from DHT, including rapid phosphorylation of kinases (MAPK/ERK, and Src/Akt-type cascades) and altered cofactor recruitment to the PSA enhancer [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 18801930]. S-23's defining behavior is upstream of transcription: by mimicking testosterone's androgenic signal at the hypothalamus/pituitary it suppresses GnRH-driven LH and FSH release, collapsing intratesticular testosterone and spermatogenesis (the basis of its male-contraceptive effect) [Evidence-rodent; PMID 18772237].

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Orally active small molecule (MW ~ 416, arylpropionamide). In rodent contraception studies given by daily oral/subcutaneous dosing; on the consumer 'research-chemical' market sold as oral liquids/capsules and increasingly as transdermal preparations [Evidence-rodent for oral activity, PMID 18772237; human exposure data PMID 35182830, transdermal PMID 40632609].
Absorption / bioavailability
Designed for oral bioavailability as part of the 'male pill' program; S-23 was described as having favourable PK and oral activity in male rats [Evidence-rodent; PMID 18772237]. No quantitative human bioavailability (F%) value has been published. For orientation, the sibling arylpropionamide SARM C-6 from the same lab had ~76% oral bioavailability and a ~6.3 h half-life in rats [Evidence-rodent, analog; PMID 15347734] — S-23's own numbers are not separately reported.
Half-life
No formally published human elimination half-life. Indirect human pharmacokinetic signal: after a single ~8 mg oral dose in one volunteer, parent S-23 was detectable in (hydrolyzed) urine from 2 h out to 28 days at 0.5-93 ng/mL, implying a long terminal elimination/very deep detection window [Evidence-human, n=1; PMID 35182830]. In horses, parent S-23 was detectable in plasma up to 143 h and in urine (as the hydroxylated benzonitrile metabolite) up to 360 h after oral dosing [Evidence-equine; PMID 38982651].
Metabolism
Hepatic phase I + phase II, characterized with human liver microsomes: oxidative metabolism generates hydroxy-S-23 and an O-dephenylated (B-ring-cleaved) metabolite, plus dihydroxy-S-23 seen in vivo; phase II yields S-23-glucuronide and hydroxy-S-23-glucuronide [Evidence-human in vitro + in vivo; PMID 35182830]. Pathways mirror other arylpropionamide SARMs (hydroxylation, hydrolytic B-ring cleavage, glucuronidation, sulfation) implicating CYP-mediated oxidation (CYP class) and UGT-mediated glucuronidation; the specific CYP/UGT isoforms were inferred from HLM/UGT incubations rather than mapped enzyme-by-enzyme [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 35182830, 20967890].
Excretion
Renal excretion of conjugated metabolites predominates for detection purposes; the dominant urinary analyte is the hydroxylated 4-amino-2-(trifluoromethyl)benzonitrile metabolite, highly conjugated with both sulfate and glucuronide, giving the longest urinary detection [Evidence-equine/human; PMID 38982651, 35182830]. Hydroxy-S-23 did not extend the detection window beyond parent (metabolite:parent ratio <1) [Evidence-human; PMID 35182830].
Notes
Not an ester or prodrug — administered as the active molecule. Non-aromatizable and not 5-alpha-reduced, so it cannot generate estradiol or DHT. It is small and lipophilic and therefore expected to cross the blood-brain barrier (consistent with central LH/FSH suppression and with in-vitro effects on human neural progenitor cells [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 40931400]); no direct human CNS PK is published. A single S-23 hair test was negative one month after one tablet (LOQ 0.1 pg/mg), so it is not a reliable long-term hair marker [Evidence-human n=1; PMID 35182830].

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

DOSE-RESPONSE IS DEFINED ONLY IN RODENTS; there is NO human dose-response data and no human efficacy/safety trial of S-23 exists (zero registered clinical trials). In castrated male rats the ED50 (dose to restore tissue to half-maximal size) was 0.43 mg/day for the prostate and 0.079 mg/day for the levator ani muscle — i.e. it is potent and, unusually for this series, fully active in androgenic as well as anabolic tissue [Evidence-rodent; PMID 18772237]. In INTACT male rats, S-23 alone suppressed serum LH by >50% at doses >0.1 mg/day, with dose-dependent prostate shrinkage but levator ani growth, and dose-dependent increases in bone mineral density and lean mass with reduced fat mass [Evidence-rodent; PMID 18772237]. As a contraceptive: S-23 0.1 mg/day combined with estradiol benzoate produced azoospermia in 4/6 rats and 0/6 pregnancies in mating trials; after stopping, fertility returned to a 100% pregnancy rate by ~100 days, i.e. fully reversible [Evidence-rodent; PMID 18772237]. In ovariectomized female rats, S-23 increased sexual motivation with potency/efficacy comparable to testosterone propionate [Evidence-rodent; PMID 20444881]. Translation of these milligram/day rat doses to humans is UNKNOWN; consumer products are dosed empirically without any clinical pharmacology basis [Speculative for humans].

How it compares

  • vs S-22 (enobosarm / ostarine, MK-2866)
    same: Same arylpropionamide chemotype and parent research series (Dalton/Miller, Ohio State/GTx); same canonical AR genomic + nongenomic signalling; both orally active, non-aromatizable, anabolic in muscle; both WADA-prohibited and sold illicitly.
    different: S-22/ostarine is the prototypical TISSUE-SELECTIVE (prostate-sparing) partial agonist in androgenic tissue and reached human Phase II/III oncology-cachexia trials; S-23 is a FULL agonist in androgenic AND anabolic tissue (NOT prostate-sparing), more potent at AR (Ki 1.7 nM), and was developed specifically as a male CONTRACEPTIVE with stronger HPG/spermatogenesis suppression. S-23 has essentially no human trial program.
  • vs C-6 (sibling arylpropionamide, same lab)
    same: Same series and male-contraception program; AR agonist; oral; suppresses LH/FSH, testosterone and spermatogenesis in rats.
    different: C-6 has lower AR affinity (Ki 4.9 nM vs S-23's 1.7 nM) and is comparatively MORE anabolic-selective (higher anabolic-than-androgenic ratio), with published rat PK (76% oral bioavailability, 6.3 h half-life). S-23 is higher-affinity and less tissue-selective. [PMID 15347734]
  • vs RAD-140 (testolone)
    same: Nonsteroidal SARM, full/near-full AR agonist, orally active, anabolic, non-aromatizable, WADA-banned, widely sold illicitly, and associated with cholestatic liver injury as a class.
    different: RAD-140 is a different chemotype (not arylpropionamide) developed as an anabolic/anti-cachexia and breast-cancer candidate, not a contraceptive; it has more documented human DILI case reports. S-23 is distinguished by its high AR affinity and its explicit contraceptive (spermatogenesis-suppressing) intent.
  • vs Testosterone / DHT (endogenous androgens)
    same: Bind the same AR LBD and drive the same genomic ARE-mediated transcription; cause HPG suppression, erythrocytosis and androgenic effects.
    different: S-23 is nonsteroidal, orally active without 17-alkylation, NON-aromatizable (no estradiol) and NOT 5-alpha-reduced; so it lacks estrogenic feedback/benefits and DHT-amplification, and unlike testosterone has no approved therapeutic use or established human dosing/monitoring framework.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Ligandrol compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-rodent
    S-23 is a near-full-agonist SARM with very high AR affinity and one of the strongest HPTA-suppression reputations in the class (it has been studied as a male-contraceptive candidate precisely because it shuts down gonadotropins in rodents). Combined with Ligandrol at the same androgen receptor, the dominant compounded harm is profound suppression of LH/FSH and endogenous testosterone — a deeper, potentially slower-to-recover secondary hypogonadism than either alone, plus additive HDL suppression common to the SARM class. s-23.json (near-full AR agonist, sub-nM affinity, strong suppression, male-contraceptive candidate — rodent); PMID 22459616 (LGD suppression, human) Watch: LH, FSH, total/free testosterone, SHBG (expect marked suppression); fasting HDL/lipids; symptoms of hypogonadism and, in women, virilization. Confirm post-exposure axis recovery with repeat gonadotropin/testosterone bloodwork.
  • Clomiphene citrate interacts Evidence-rodent
    S-23 is a high-affinity androgen-receptor agonist noted for STRONG HPTA suppression — it drives androgen negative feedback that switches off endogenous LH/FSH and testosterone (studied as a male-contraceptive concept for exactly this shutdown). Clomiphene is the upstream SERM used to restart the axis after such suppression; it can only raise LH/FSH/T once the suppressing androgen is gone and the pituitary/testes are free to respond. While S-23 is present, the axis stays clamped and clomiphene's central disinhibition is overridden by the ongoing androgen feedback. Note: S-23's suppression is characterized in rodents (no human data), while the clomiphene-restart side is human — hence the mixed certainty. S-23 monograph (strong AR agonist, strong HPG suppression, male-contraceptive candidate — Evidence-rodent) + clomiphene monograph caveats (needs intact, un-suppressed axis); PMC OA AAS/PCT review (androgen suppression of HPTA; SERM restart) [corpus textbook] Watch: LH/FSH and total/free testosterone across cessation and recovery — gonadotropins should climb as S-23 clears if the axis is intact; flat LH/FSH signals continued suppression. Objective before/after bloodwork rather than symptoms alone.

History & legal status

"Origin: S-23 emerged in the mid-2000s from the arylpropionamide SARM program of James T. Dalton and Duane D. Miller (University of Tennessee / The Ohio State University, commercialized via GTx, Inc.), the same medicinal-chemistry lineage that produced S-1, S-4 (andarine) and S-22 (ostarine/enobosarm). It was characterized specifically as a candidate for hormonal male contraception ('the male pill') in combination with estradiol, first reported in detail in 2009 [PMID 18772237]. Regulatory status: S-23 has NEVER been approved by any regulator (FDA/EMA), never advanced to human clinical trials (zero registered trials), and remains an investigational/preclinical compound. Like all SARMs it is prohibited at all times (in and out of competition) by the World Anti-Doping Agency under class S1.2 (other anabolic agents) and has been on doping-control radar since SARMs were banned in January 2008 [PMID 20967890]; it has been identified in multiple human athlete doping cases and in horse-racing doping control [PMID 38982651]. In the US it is not a controlled substance per se but is not a lawful dietary-supplement ingredient — the FDA has warned that SARMs are unapproved drugs and illegal to market in supplements; it is nonetheless sold online as a grey-market 'research chemical' (liquids, capsules, transdermals), often mislabeled or adulterated [PMID 38517236, 40632609]."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No human pharmacokinetics beyond a single-volunteer detection study (PMID 35182830) — true human bioavailability, half-life, clearance and steady-state exposure are unknown; rat/horse PK cannot be reliably extrapolated.
  • No human dose-response or efficacy/safety data at all — the milligram/day doses that work in rats have never been validated in humans, and consumer dosing is empirical and unverified.
  • Liver-injury risk is inferred from the SARM class (RAD-140, LGD-4033, ostarine, stenabolic case reports); the specific incidence, threshold dose and biopsy pattern of S-23-induced DILI in humans are uncharacterized.
  • Reversibility and recovery time of HPG-axis/spermatogenesis suppression in humans is unknown — rat fertility recovered by ~100 days, but human recovery (and risk of persistent hypogonadism) has never been studied.
  • The neuropsychiatric/depression signal is only an in-vitro proteomic finding in neural progenitor cells (PMID 40931400) with no systemic context — whether S-23 affects human mood is entirely unestablished.
  • Long-term cardiovascular, prostate, and erythrocytosis outcomes, off-target receptor selectivity (PR/GR/ER/MR, hERG), and drug-interaction profile (CYP/UGT inhibition/induction) for S-23 are unmeasured.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (this is not an endorsement of use, and no dosing guidance is given). Given the AR-agonist and HPG-suppressing pharmacology and the SARM-class adverse-effect pattern, the bloodwork and signs reasonably watched are: (1) Liver function — ALT, AST, total/direct bilirubin, ALP and GGT at baseline and periodically; jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, right-upper-quadrant pain, or pruritus are red flags for the cholestatic DILI seen across SARMs [PMID 36945289, 39421081]. (2) Hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis — LH, FSH, total and free testosterone, and (if fertility matters) a semen analysis; expect suppression, and watch for failure to recover after stopping [PMID 18772237]. (3) Lipids — fasting lipid panel for the expected drop in HDL-C and adverse lipid shift [PMID 39421081]. (4) Hematocrit/hemoglobin — for androgen-driven erythrocytosis and the attendant thrombotic risk [PMID 32257854]. (5) In women, signs of virilization (acne, hirsutism, voice change, menstrual disruption) — S-23 is a full, non-prostate-sparing androgen, so virilizing potential is high. (6) Mood/affective changes, given the in-vitro depression-pathway signal [PMID 40931400]. Because grey-market S-23 is frequently mislabeled or adulterated, product identity itself is a safety variable [PMID 38517236]. Any concerning result warrants discontinuation and evaluation by a clinician."

Caveats

No human trials of S-23 exist; every physiological claim above is rodent or in-vitro and may not translate. S-23 is WADA-prohibited and most published recent work is anti-doping detection/metabolism, not efficacy or safety. Gray-market 'S-23' is frequently mislabeled, underdosed, or contaminated with other SARMs/steroids (NMR/LC-MS surveys), so what users take is often not what the label says. Liver-injury and HDL/testosterone-suppression evidence is class-level (other SARMs, human case reports), extrapolated to S-23 — direct S-23 human safety data do not exist. The 17-alpha-alkyl hepatotoxicity mechanism is included strictly as a contrast: S-23 does not share that scaffold. Harm-reduction note: this profile (potent HPG shutdown, lipid and liver concerns, unregulated supply) carries real risk; no dosing guidance is provided here.
