Tesofensine

A brain "triple reuptake" blocker that turns up dopamine, noradrenaline and serotonin all at once to crush appetite — a failed Parkinson's/Alzheimer's drug that stalled in obesity, never approved, and carrying a real human-data integrity asterisk.

Human / approved 14 citations
Class
Centrally-acting triple monoamine reuptake inhibitor (DAT + NET + SERT blocker); small-molecule appetite suppressant
Primary target
Presynaptic monoamine transporters — dopamine transporter (DAT), noradrenaline transporter (NET) and serotonin transporter (SERT) — blocking reuptake so all three neurotransmitters linger longer in the synapse
Human status
NOT approved anywhere. Tesofensine reached phase II for obesity (the Danish TIPO-1 trial, NCT00394667), showing strong weight loss, but development by NeuroSearch did not yield a marketed obesity drug; rights later moved on and it remained in limbo for years. It earlier FAILED its original indications (Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease), where the weight loss was first noticed as an incidental finding. Key human data carry an integrity asterisk: the pivotal Lancet phase II trial received a formal Expression of Concern (PMID 23561987, 2013) and a published letter alleged under-reporting of adverse effects (PMID 23849924) — so the headline efficacy/safety numbers should be read with caution. There is no large phase III efficacy/safety dataset and no regulatory approval. Despite this, it circulates in the fitness/peptide-and-research-chemical gray market; that real-world use is NOT backed by approval, long-term safety data, or quality control.

At a glance

  • Pronounced appetite suppression — higher satiety/fullness, lower food intake — is the dominant weight-loss mechanism in humans (PMID 20479765)
  • Robust dose-dependent weight loss in phase II: roughly 9.2% placebo-subtracted at 0.5 mg over 24 weeks, ~double what then-approved drugs achieved (PMID 18950853)
  • Restores low reward-circuit dopamine in obese rodent brains, countering the dopamine deficit linked to overeating (PMID 23932919)
  • Increases overnight energy expenditure (~4.6%) and fat oxidation modestly, but no meaningful rise in total 24-h calorie burn (PMID 20479765)
  • Sympathomimetic cardiovascular effects: elevated heart rate (~7.4 bpm at 0.5 mg) and blood-pressure concerns (PMID 18950853)
  • Low abuse potential in humans — stimulant/liking effects no greater than placebo, below amphetamine (PMID 20520602)
  • Blocked post-weight-loss rebound and synergized with a serotonin precursor in rodents (PMID 38656972)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It jams three reuptake pumps at once Established (in vitro / pharmacology)

    Normally, after a brain cell fires off dopamine, noradrenaline or serotonin, little molecular 'vacuum cleaners' (the transporters DAT, NET and SERT) suck the neurotransmitter back up to switch the signal off. Tesofensine plugs all three pumps, so the chemicals stay in the gap between neurons longer and the signal stays louder. This 'triple' action is its defining feature. PMID 19777399 (Bello & Zahner, Curr Opin Investig Drugs 2009)

  2. In living human brains it actually occupies the dopamine transporter Evidence-human

    This isn't just a test-tube claim. A PET imaging study in people showed tesofensine binds the dopamine transporter in a dose-dependent way — roughly 18% blockade at low doses up to about 77%, maxing out near 80%. That confirms the drug reaches its target in the human brain and that dopamine is a central player, not an afterthought. PMID 24239329 (Appel et al., Eur Neuropsychopharmacol 2014)

  3. More dopamine 'refills' a tank that's low in obesity Evidence-rodent

    Obese brains tend to run low on dopamine signalling in reward areas (nucleus accumbens, prefrontal cortex), which may push compensatory overeating. In diet-induced obese rats, tesofensine restored those lowered dopamine levels back toward normal — it topped up an empty tank rather than just flooding a normal one. This is a leading explanation for why it blunts the drive to eat. PMID 23932919 (Hansen et al., Pharmacol Biochem Behav 2013)

  4. The 'eat less' signal runs through α1-adrenoceptor and D1 receptor pathways Evidence-rodent

    Raising the neurotransmitters is upstream; the appetite effect needs a downstream receptor. In obese rats, tesofensine's food-cutting effect was almost completely abolished by blocking α1-adrenergic receptors (with prazosin) and partly blocked by blocking dopamine D1 receptors (with SCH23390) — but NOT by blocking α2, D2, D3 or 5-HT2 receptors. So the noradrenaline→α1 and dopamine→D1 arms are the key wiring for appetite suppression. PMID 20200509 (Axel et al., Neuropsychopharmacology 2010)

  5. It quiets the hypothalamus's 'keep eating' neurons Evidence-rodent

    Newer mouse/rat work shows tesofensine silences a specific set of GABAergic neurons in the lateral hypothalamus that normally promote feeding. Artificially silencing those same neurons made the drug work even better, and tesofensine also blocked the weight 'rebound' that usually follows weight loss. This adds a circuit-level picture on top of the neurotransmitter story. PMID 38656972 (Perez et al., PLoS One 2024)

  6. In humans the weight loss is mostly 'eat less,' with only a small metabolism bump Evidence-human

    When researchers actually measured this in overweight/obese men, tesofensine raised feelings of satiety and fullness and lowered planned food intake — appetite suppression was the main driver. It nudged up overnight energy expenditure (~4.6%) and fat burning slightly, but did NOT meaningfully raise total 24-hour calorie burn. So it's primarily an appetite drug, not a metabolism furnace. PMID 20479765 (Sjödin et al., Int J Obes 2010)

  7. Same wiring drives the side effects — and the abuse risk looks low Evidence-human

    The very noradrenaline/sympathetic boost that helps appetite also raises heart rate and blood pressure: in the phase II trial, 0.5 mg lifted heart rate by ~7.4 beats/min. On the upside, a controlled human study found tesofensine's subjective 'liking'/stimulant effects were no greater than placebo and below amphetamine — so despite touching dopamine, its abuse potential appears low, similar to bupropion/atomoxetine. PMID 18950853 (Astrup et al., Lancet 2008); PMID 20520602 (Schoedel et al., Clin Pharmacol Ther 2010)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Tesofensine (NS 2330) is a small-molecule nortropane/phenyltropane-class TRIPLE MONOAMINE REUPTAKE INHIBITOR — it blocks the three presynaptic monoamine transporters DAT (dopamine), NET (norepinephrine) and SERT (serotonin). It is an INHIBITOR (transporter blocker), NOT a releasing agent: unlike amphetamine or phentermine it does not reverse the transporter to dump monoamine into the cleft; it sits on the transporter and stops reuptake, so the effect depends on ongoing physiological neuronal firing. CERTAINTY: Established that it blocks all three transporters (ChEMBL mechanism record, action_type INHIBITOR, target = monoamine transporter, direct_interaction=true, disease_efficacy=true; PMID 19777399 review 'in vitro potently blocked dopamine, norepinephrine and serotonin reuptake'; USAN stem '-fensine' = norepinephrine/serotonin/dopamine reuptake inhibitor). NUMBERS / SELECTIVITY: The widely-cited in vitro uptake-inhibition potencies from the original NeuroSearch characterization are approximately IC50 DAT ~6.5 nM, NET ~1.7 nM, SERT ~11 nM in rat synaptosome/cell assays — i.e. roughly balanced across all three with a modest NET > DAT > SERT rank order (CERTAINTY: Evidence-in vitro; these specific nM figures come from secondary/review reporting of NeuroSearch preclinical data and an audit should treat the exact numbers as approximate — the corpus confirms the qualitative 'potent triple block' but the original primary in vitro paper was not directly retrieved this run). Functionally the in-human consequence is dominated by DAT in striatum: PET shows clear dose-dependent striatal DAT occupancy (PMID 24239329). An active human metabolite, M1 (desethyl-tesofensine), is itself a DAT inhibitor; in mice M1's EC50 for DAT inhibition was 4-5x HIGHER (i.e. ~4-5x less potent) than parent, but circulates at ~8x higher steady-state concentration, so it contributes materially to overall activity (PMID 17982477, Evidence-rodent + in vitro).

Signalling cascade. Step 1 — transporter blockade: tesofensine occupies DAT/NET/SERT on presynaptic terminals, preventing clearance of dopamine, norepinephrine and serotonin from the synaptic cleft, so each monoamine's dwell time and effective concentration rise. Step 2 — amplified post-synaptic receptor signalling: the surplus monoamine drives downstream receptors. Rodent antagonist-dissection studies show the APPETITE-SUPPRESSING signal flows mainly through (a) alpha-1 adrenoceptors and (b) dopamine D1 receptors: tesofensine's hypophagia was almost completely reversed by the alpha-1 blocker prazosin and partially by the D1 antagonist SCH23390, but was NOT blocked by alpha-2, D2, D3, or 5-HT2A/C antagonists (PMID 20200509, Evidence-rodent). Step 3 — circuit level: in the lateral hypothalamus tesofensine SILENCES a subset of GABAergic (Vgat+) feeding-promoting neurons; chemogenetically silencing those same neurons enhances its food-suppression, placing LH GABA neurons downstream of the monoamine surge (PMID 38656972, Evidence-rodent/mouse). Step 4 — striatal dopamine tone: chronic DAT blockade raises synaptic dopamine and, in diet-induced obese rats, reverses the low forebrain dopamine and altered striatal D2/D3 receptor availability associated with obesity (PMIDs 23932919, 21889317; PET-confirmed DAT occupancy 18-77% in humans, PMID 24239329). Net behavioural output: reduced food intake / enhanced satiety plus a modest sympathomimetic rise in energy expenditure and fat oxidation (PMID 20479765, Evidence-human). The same noradrenergic/dopaminergic drive that suppresses appetite also raises heart rate and blood pressure (PMID 23784901, Evidence-rodent).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral, once daily. Free base; small lipophilic molecule (MW 328.3, C17H23Cl2NO; ALogP ~4.6, 0 Rule-of-Five violations, polar surface area ~12.5; ChEMBL CHEMBL3989690). High lipophilicity and low PSA are consistent with good CNS penetration (BBB-crossing centrally-acting drug; PMID 17982477 describes it as 'centrally acting').
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally absorbed and centrally active at sub-milligram doses (clinical doses 0.25-1.0 mg/day; PMID 18950853). Exact human oral bioavailability and Tmax were not retrieved in primary form this run (CERTAINTY: human absolute bioavailability = data-gap from the sources pulled).
Half-life
VERY LONG human elimination half-life — on the order of ~8 days (~200+ hours) is the figure reported in the tesofensine clinical literature, which is why it is dosed once daily and reaches steady state slowly and why the PET occupancy study held 'constant plasma levels' across multi-day dosing (PMID 24239329 design implies long, stable exposure). CERTAINTY: the qualitative long half-life / once-daily, slow-steady-state behaviour is Established; the precise ~220 h number is from secondary clinical-pharmacology reporting and an auditor should treat the exact hours as approximate since the primary single-dose PK paper was not directly retrieved this run. In MICE, PK of parent and metabolite M1 fit one-compartment models with NONLINEAR (saturable) elimination and metabolism at higher doses (PMID 17982477, Evidence-rodent).
Metabolism
Hepatic. Tesofensine is metabolized to an active N-desethyl metabolite, M1, which is itself a DAT inhibitor (slightly lower intrinsic potency than parent in mice but ~8x higher steady-state levels, so it contributes to net effect) (PMID 17982477). The specific CYP isoenzymes were not identified in the sources retrieved this run (CERTAINTY: identity of metabolizing CYPs = data-gap).
Excretion
Not characterized in the primary sources retrieved this run (CERTAINTY: route/fraction of renal vs biliary excretion = data-gap). Saturable/nonlinear elimination kinetics were observed preclinically (PMID 17982477).
Notes
Not an ester, not a prodrug (ChEMBL prodrug flag = 0). Crosses the blood-brain barrier (centrally acting). The combination of (a) a very long half-life and (b) an active long-lived metabolite means exposure accumulates and washes out slowly — relevant to both efficacy persistence and to how long sympathomimetic/CNS adverse effects can linger after stopping. Marketed/grey-market 'research' tesofensine is the same free base; quality and true content of non-pharmacy material is unverified.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

How it compares

  • vs Sibutramine (Meridia)
    same: Both are monoamine reuptake inhibitors used/developed for obesity that suppress appetite and produce sympathomimetic effects (raised HR/BP); sibutramine is itself often described as a triple reuptake inhibitor. In the DIO-rat head-to-head, tesofensine matched/exceeded sibutramine on weight loss.
    different: Tesofensine showed roughly twice the human weight loss of approved drugs including sibutramine in phase II. Crucially, sibutramine was WITHDRAWN worldwide (2010) after the SCOUT trial showed excess cardiovascular events — a fate that hangs over tesofensine because both share the HR/BP-raising sympathomimetic mechanism; tesofensine's long half-life and active metabolite differentiate its PK.
  • vs Phentermine
    same: Both raise central catecholamines to suppress appetite and both increase heart rate; both act largely via noradrenergic/dopaminergic drive.
    different: Phentermine is principally a norepinephrine/dopamine RELEASING AGENT (reverses transporters to expel monoamine), whereas tesofensine is a reuptake BLOCKER that depends on neuronal firing. Mechanistically this gives tesofensine less rodent stereotypy (lower stimulant-like motor signature) at therapeutic doses (PMID 38656972), and a much longer half-life vs phentermine's short action.
  • vs Bupropion (and the bupropion/naltrexone combo, Contrave)
    same: Both inhibit dopamine and norepinephrine reuptake (NDRI) and both reduce appetite via catecholaminergic mechanisms.
    different: Bupropion is an NDRI with essentially NO serotonin reuptake activity, whereas tesofensine adds potent SERT inhibition (true triple). Bupropion is FDA-approved (for depression/smoking, and with naltrexone for obesity); tesofensine is not approved anywhere. Tesofensine's three-transporter load gives larger phase-II weight loss but also a broader sympathomimetic/serotonergic side-effect surface.
  • vs Brasofensine (NS 2214) and other phenyltropane reuptake inhibitors
    same: Structural/mechanistic cousin from the same NeuroSearch/Parkinson's-era program — a monoamine (esp. dopamine) reuptake inhibitor also trialled in Parkinson's disease.
    different: Brasofensine likewise failed to show benefit in PD; tesofensine is the member of this chemical lineage that was repurposed to obesity after the incidental weight-loss observation, and is the one with the substantial human obesity dataset.

History &amp; legal status

Origin: developed by NeuroSearch A/S (Denmark), code NS 2330, initially with Boehringer Ingelheim, as a CNS triple monoamine reuptake inhibitor for NEURODEGENERATIVE disease (Alzheimer's and Parkinson's), exploiting its pro-dopaminergic action. It FAILED those indications — the phase II Parkinson's trial (n=261) did not beat placebo despite up to 77% DAT occupancy (PMID 17149725), and Alzheimer's signals were weak. The pivotal serendipity: unintended dose-dependent WEIGHT LOSS in those neurology trials redirected the molecule to obesity (PMID 19777399). NeuroSearch ran the TIPO program; TIPO-1 (Astrup, Lancet 2008, PMID 18950853) showed ~2x the weight loss of then-approved drugs and the FDA endorsed advancing to phase III. Regulatory status: tesofensine has NEVER been approved by the FDA or EMA for any indication; large phase III obesity trials were not completed under NeuroSearch (the program stalled, partly amid the cardiovascular-safety chill cast over appetite suppressants after sibutramine's 2010 withdrawal). Rights later passed to Saniona, which has pursued tesofensine (and a tesofensine+metoprolol combination, 'Tesomet,' explicitly pairing it with a beta-blocker to blunt the HR/BP signal) in conditions such as hypothalamic obesity and Prader-Willi syndrome; some regional development (e.g. via Medix in Mexico/Latin America) has been reported. As of this synthesis it remains an INVESTIGATIONAL drug (ChEMBL max_phase 2, no first_approval), not a licensed medicine, and any 'research chemical' / grey-market supply is unapproved and unverified. CERTAINTY: Established for the NeuroSearch origin, PD/AD failure, obesity repurposing, Lancet phase II, and non-approval; the Saniona/Tesomet/Medix corporate and later-stage trial details are from background knowledge and were not re-verified against primary sources this run.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No completed phase III: human efficacy AND hard cardiovascular-outcome safety beyond 24 weeks are UNKNOWN — the entire human case rests on phase II. Given the mechanistic kinship to withdrawn sibutramine, long-term CV safety is the central open question.
  • Durability of appetite suppression: appetite/satiety effect attenuated over 24 weeks even as weight fell (PMID 21720440) — whether weight loss plateaus/regains long-term, and whether tolerance develops at the receptor level, is unresolved.
  • Exact human in vitro selectivity and abuse liability: the precise DAT/NET/SERT Ki ratios in human tissue and the real-world abuse potential of a long-half-life DAT blocker with ~77% striatal occupancy have not been firmly established in humans.
  • Human PK details: the exact human half-life, oral bioavailability, the CYP enzymes responsible for M1 formation, and the excretion route were not pinned down in the retrieved primary literature — these are gaps for drug-interaction prediction.
  • Cardiovascular mechanism mitigation in humans: rodent data show beta-blockade (metoprolol) abolishes the tachycardia while sparing weight loss (PMID 23784901, basis of 'Tesomet'), but whether co-administered beta-blockade safely preserves efficacy in humans long-term is untested at outcome level.
  • Special populations: efficacy/safety in the genetic/hypothalamic obesity syndromes now being targeted (e.g. Prader-Willi, hypothalamic obesity) versus common polygenic obesity, and any interaction with serotonergic medications, remain open.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

HARM-REDUCTION MONITORING ONLY — not a protocol, no dosing implied. Given the sympathomimetic triple-reuptake mechanism, the highest-yield things to watch are: (1) RESTING HEART RATE — tesofensine reliably raises HR (~+7 bpm even at the mid dose in trials); a sustained climb or palpitations is a red flag. (2) BLOOD PRESSURE — track systolic/diastolic; clinically meaningful rises appeared at higher doses. Pre-existing hypertension, arrhythmia, or known cardiovascular disease massively raises the stakes (this is the exact mechanism that got sibutramine pulled). (3) SLEEP and MOOD/MENTAL STATE — insomnia, anxiety, agitation, or any mood instability reflect the central noradrenergic/dopaminergic load; the long half-life means these can persist for days after stopping. (4) GI/autonomic signs — dry mouth, constipation/hard stools, nausea are the common, mechanism-consistent effects. (5) DRUG INTERACTIONS — because it blocks SERT, combining with SSRIs/SNRIs, MAO inhibitors, triptans, or other serotonergic agents is a serotonin-excess concern; combining with other stimulants/sympathomimetics compounds the cardiovascular load — these warrant clinician review, not self-experiment. (6) Baseline + periodic cardiovascular check (HR, BP, and an ECG if any palpitations/arrhythmia history) is the sensible bloodwork-adjacent surveillance, plus weight and a check that appetite suppression isn't producing inadequate intake of protein/micronutrients during rapid loss. Anyone with cardiac disease, uncontrolled hypertension, an eating disorder, on serotonergic/MAOI medication, or pregnant/breastfeeding should treat this as off-limits. Because it is unapproved and often grey-market, product identity/purity itself is an unmonitored hazard.

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP & INTEGRITY: The most-cited efficacy figures come from a single phase II trial that carries a Lancet Expression of Concern (PMID 23561987) plus a formal allegation of adverse-event under-reporting (PMID 23849924) — treat the famous '~10% weight loss, twice other drugs' claim as promising but not confirmed, and never present it as settled. No phase III, no approval. PRECLINICAL-HEAVY MECHANISM: The detailed circuit story (α1/D1 dependence, dopamine restoration, lateral-hypothalamus GABA silencing, rebound prevention) is largely RODENT data (PMIDs 20200509, 23932919, 38656972) — biologically plausible and consistent with the human PET DAT-occupancy finding (24239329), but the receptor-level wiring has not been proven to drive weight loss in people. SAFETY: It's a sympathomimetic — expect raised heart rate and blood pressure; cardiovascular risk is the main concern and a likely reason it never crossed the approval finish line. Do not frame as benign. CONTEXT: Originally a neurodegeneration drug that failed; weight loss was a side effect, not a designed feature. Forum/marketing hype ('most powerful fat-loss compound') outruns the evidence — there is no long-term human safety record and no regulated supply.
