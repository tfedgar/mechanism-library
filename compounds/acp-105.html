ACP-105

Main target: Androgen receptor. Evidence is animal- and lab-only — no human trials yet.

EvidenceRodent / preclinical
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A potent non-steroidal SARM that never left the animal lab — now sold as a research chemical on the strength of rodent muscle, bone, and cognition data that has never been tested in a single human.

Rodent / preclinical 17 citations
Class
Non-steroidal selective androgen receptor modulator (SARM); aryl-nitrile / 8-azabicyclo[3
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR, gene NR3C4) — tissue-selective partial agonist relative to testosterone
Human status
NO human data of any kind. There are zero registered clinical trials for ACP-105 on ClinicalTrials.gov (confirmed: intervention search returns 0 studies), no ChEMBL clinical-phase record (name search returns no compound entry), and no published human pharmacokinetic, efficacy, or safety study. ACP-105 was discovered by ACADIA Pharmaceuticals AB (Malmö, Sweden) and first described in 2009 (Schlienger et al., J Med Chem; PMID 19856921) and remained a preclinical (rodent/in-vitro) research compound; it was never advanced into human development. Essentially all recent literature treats it as a doping agent and analytical-chemistry target (metabolite identification in horses and human microsomes for anti-doping detection) or as an in-silico toxicology subject — not as a therapeutic in trials. It is prohibited by WADA (SARMs banned in sport since 2008) yet sold online as a 'research chemical,' frequently alongside RAD-140 and LGD-4033. Any human use is unapproved, unmonitored self-experimentation. Human safety statements in this monograph are therefore either computational predictions specific to ACP-105 or extrapolations from the broader SARM class, explicitly flagged as such.

At a glance

  • Anabolic signaling in muscle/bone (increased anabolic parameters) — demonstrated only in castrated rats, not humans (PMID 19856921)
  • Tissue selectivity: partial agonist/weaker action in prostate vs muscle-bone — the basis of the 'strong but selective' pitch (PMID 19856921; class support PMID 15308613)
  • CNS effects: reduced anxiety-like behavior; anti-amyloid and pro-cognitive effects only when combined with an ERβ agonist (rodent AD model) (PMID 24020966)
  • Improved motor coordination and fear conditioning in a separate rodent model (PMID 21219889)
  • Not aromatized and not 5α-reduced (non-steroidal), so no estrogen/DHT conversion from the parent molecule (PMID 19856921)
  • Predicted oral bioavailability, CYP3A4-dominant metabolism, short half-life, and brain penetration — in-silico only (PMID 40928534)
  • Expected class effect (UNPROVEN for ACP-105 specifically): suppression of the body's own testosterone via HPG-axis negative feedback, as documented for other AR-active SARMs in humans (PMID 22459616) and rodents (PMID 16099859)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps1 human / established 6 rodent / preclinical

  1. Binds the androgen receptor as a non-steroidal ligand Established

    ACP-105 is a small non-steroidal molecule (an aryl-nitrile built on an 8-azabicyclo[3.2.1]octane 'tropane' scaffold, CAS 1048998-11-3) that fits into the ligand-binding pocket of the androgen receptor — the same protein testosterone and DHT normally activate. It was found by screening compounds in ACADIA's R-SAT functional receptor assay, not derived from a steroid. Because it is not a steroid, it is not a substrate for 5-alpha-reductase or aromatase, so it cannot be converted to DHT or to estrogen. PMID 19856921

  2. Acts as a PARTIAL agonist, not a full one Evidence-in vitro

    When ACP-105 binds the AR it only partially switches it on compared with testosterone — it is characterized as a potent partial agonist. A partial agonist produces a submaximal signal and can even blunt a stronger natural androgen if both are present. This partial-agonist character is the pharmacological basis for the 'strong but selective' marketing, but the intrinsic ceiling of effect is lower than a full androgen. PMID 19856921

  3. Ligand binding drives a tissue-selective (SARM) response Evidence-rodent

    The core SARM idea is that a ligand changes the AR's shape so it recruits different coactivator/corepressor proteins in different tissues — behaving more like a full agonist in muscle and bone but like a weak partial agonist (or antagonist) in the prostate. ACP-105 is explicitly characterized as tissue-selective; its own chemical series also yielded pure antiandrogens, one of which blocked the AR T877A mutant found in prostate cancer, underscoring that small changes on this scaffold flip agonist-to-antagonist behavior. Note: the detailed coactivator mechanism is generalized from the SARM field (e.g., S-4/S-1 rodent work), not mapped residue-by-residue for ACP-105 specifically. PMID 19856921; PMID 15308613

  4. Activated AR turns on anabolic gene programs in muscle and bone Evidence-rodent

    Once switched on, the AR moves into the cell nucleus and acts as a transcription factor. In androgen-responsive tissues this drives protein synthesis and satellite-cell/osteoblast activity that increase muscle mass and bone density. In a 2-week study in castrated (testosterone-depleted) male rats, ACP-105 improved anabolic parameters — the direct in-vivo readout that it engages this pathway. This is rodent, short-term, and in castrated animals — not a demonstration of muscle growth in intact humans. PMID 19856921

  5. Crosses into the brain and engages neural AR Evidence-rodent

    ACP-105 acts as an AR agonist in the central nervous system. In gonadectomized triple-transgenic Alzheimer's-model mice it reduced anxiety-like behavior on its own, and — combined with a selective estrogen-receptor-beta agonist (AC-186) — increased the amyloid-degrading enzymes neprilysin and insulin-degrading enzyme, lowered brain amyloid-beta, and improved spatial memory. In-silico ADME modelling likewise predicts blood-brain-barrier penetration. This is the origin of the 'investigated for cognitive decline' claim, but the pro-cognitive/anti-amyloid effect in that study REQUIRED the ERβ co-agonist, not ACP-105 alone. PMID 24020966

  6. Separately shown to affect motor/fear behavior in another rodent model Evidence-rodent

    In a distinct study, ACP-105 improved rotorod (motor coordination) performance and enhanced cued fear conditioning in female mice, including after cranial irradiation, alongside changes in cortical MAP-2 (a structural neuron protein). This is a second, independent rodent CNS signal — but it is a small behavioral study in a niche radiation model, not evidence of a benefit in humans. PMID 21219889

  7. Predicted to be metabolized mainly by liver CYP3A4 to multiple metabolites Evidence-in vitro

    In-silico ADME work predicts ACP-105 is well absorbed orally, is a consistent CYP3A4 substrate (with minor CYP2C9/2C19/2D6 input), forms roughly six phase-I metabolites plus glucuronide (UGT) conjugates, and has a short predicted half-life (~1.2 h). Equine in-vivo and human-microsome studies confirm it undergoes extensive metabolism, which is why anti-doping labs target its metabolites rather than the parent. Heavy predicted CYP3A4 handling also flags drug-interaction potential — but these are computational and in-vitro findings, not measured human pharmacokinetics. PMID 40928534

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Non-steroidal small-molecule ligand of the androgen receptor (AR, NR3C4). Chemically 2-chloro-4-[(3-endo)-3-hydroxy-3-methyl-8-azabicyclo[3.2.1]oct-8-yl]-3-methylbenzonitrile (CAS 1048998-11-3) — an aryl-nitrile on an 8-azabicyclo[3.2.1]octane (tropane) core, structurally unrelated to steroids and to the aryl-propionamide SARMs (S-4/ostarine class). Characterized as a POTENT partial agonist at AR relative to testosterone; identified through ACADIA's R-SAT (receptor selection and amplification technology) functional screen. Sister compounds from the same series were pure antiandrogens, one active against the AR T877A prostate-cancer mutant, illustrating how narrowly agonist vs antagonist behavior is tuned on this scaffold.

Signalling cascade. Ligand binding → conformational change in AR ligand-binding domain → altered surface for coregulator binding → tissue-dependent recruitment of coactivators (agonist-like in muscle/bone) vs corepressors/partial signaling (prostate) → AR nuclear translocation and binding to androgen-response elements → transcription of anabolic and CNS gene programs. Because ACP-105 is not a steroid, it is not aromatized to estrogen or 5α-reduced to a more potent androgen, removing those amplification/feedback arms present with testosterone. The tissue-selective coregulator mechanism is generalized from the SARM class (rodent S-1/S-4 data), not mapped for ACP-105 specifically.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (used orally in rodent and equine studies; grey-market products are oral). No human route data.
Absorption / bioavailability
In-silico ADME predicts high gastrointestinal absorption (up to ~100%), moderate lipophilicity (predicted LogP ~3.0–3.5), and low aqueous solubility. NO measured human bioavailability exists.
Half-life
Predicted short elimination half-life ~1.18 h (in-silico). No measured human half-life. (Predicted clearance values varied widely across models.)
Metabolism
Extensive hepatic metabolism. In-silico work identifies ~6 phase-I metabolites (M1–M6) formed predominantly by CYP3A4 (82–100% contribution) with minor CYP2C9/2C19/2D6 input, via oxygenation, N-dealkylation, and UGT (glucuronide) conjugation. Equine in-vivo and human-microsome studies confirm extensive metabolism, driving anti-doping assays to target metabolites rather than parent drug.
Excretion
Predicted renal/biliary elimination of conjugated metabolites; no OCT2-mediated active renal secretion predicted. Metabolites are the analytical markers in urine/hair doping tests.
Notes
Blood-brain-barrier penetration predicted by most models (consistent with the observed rodent CNS activity). Strong plasma protein binding (77–99%) with <1% free fraction predicted. Heavy predicted CYP3A4 dependence flags potential for drug–drug interactions with CYP3A4 inhibitors/inducers. ALL pharmacokinetic values here are computational or animal-derived — none are measured human data.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

No human dose-response data exist — there are no human studies, so no dose can be stated and none is provided here. In rodent proof-of-concept, ACP-105 improved anabolic parameters over a 2-week course in castrated male rats (PMID 19856921); Alzheimer's-model and irradiation mouse studies used chronic dosing in the low mg/kg range typical for rodent SARM work (PMID 24020966; PMID 21219889), but these regimens do not translate to human dosing. Conceptually, as a PARTIAL agonist, ACP-105 has a lower maximal (ceiling) effect than a full androgen: increasing the dose cannot exceed that submaximal plateau, and in the presence of endogenous testosterone a partial agonist can even reduce net AR signaling. This is a key reason 'more is better' logic fails for this compound. No 'how to run it' guidance is given by design.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs RAD-140 (testolone)
    same: Both non-steroidal SARMs, WADA-banned, sold together as research chemicals, marketed as 'strong but selective', and both lack any completed efficacy trial for the physique use case.
    different: RAD-140 has documented human DILI case reports (PMID 36561105); ACP-105 has NO human data at all and is an explicit partial agonist, so its intrinsic anabolic ceiling is expected to be lower.
  • vs LGD-4033 (ligandrol)
    same: Both non-steroidal SARMs, both WADA-prohibited, both expected to suppress endogenous testosterone.
    different: LGD-4033 has actual human Phase 1 pharmacokinetic/safety data with documented dose-dependent testosterone/SHBG/HDL suppression (PMID 22459616) plus human hepatotoxicity reports (PMID 39421081); ACP-105 has none — its 'human' data are entirely computational.
  • vs Ostarine / S-4 (aryl-propionamide SARMs)
    same: Shared SARM concept: fuller agonist in muscle/bone, partial agonist in prostate.
    different: Ostarine/S-4 are a different chemical class (aryl-propionamides) with substantial rodent AND human data; ACP-105's distinct tropane/aryl-nitrile chemistry has only rodent + in-silico support.
  • vs Testosterone / DHT (natural androgens)
    same: Act through the same androgen receptor to drive anabolic and CNS effects.
    different: Testosterone is a full agonist that is aromatized to estrogen and 5α-reduced to DHT; ACP-105 is a non-steroidal PARTIAL agonist that undergoes neither conversion, giving a lower efficacy ceiling but also removing the estrogenic arm — with the tradeoff of essentially no human safety record.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

ACP-105 was discovered by ACADIA Pharmaceuticals AB (Malmö, Sweden) and first described in 2009 (Schlienger et al., J Med Chem; PMID 19856921), emerging from an R-SAT high-throughput screen. It was explored preclinically for anabolic (muscle/bone) and CNS/cognitive indications (Alzheimer's amyloid, radiation-related cognitive decline) in the early 2010s but was never advanced into human clinical trials and appears to have been shelved as a therapeutic. From ~2013 onward the literature shifts almost entirely to anti-doping analytical chemistry (metabolite identification in horses and human microsomes; e.g., PMID 24881457, PMID 32852865, PMID 33535528) — reflecting that ACP-105 leaked into the grey market as a 'research chemical' sold alongside RAD-140 and LGD-4033. As a SARM it falls under the WADA Prohibited List (SARMs banned in sport since 2008) and is not approved by any drug regulator for human use anywhere; it has no FDA/EMA status and no ClinicalTrials.gov or ChEMBL clinical record. Recent (2025) interest is from forensic/clinical toxicologists building in-silico ADME and toxicity profiles precisely because empirical human data are absent (PMID 40928534, PMID 40064700).

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Human pharmacokinetics: real bioavailability, half-life, and metabolite exposure are all unmeasured (only in-silico predictions exist).
  • Whether the rodent anabolic effect produces meaningful, safe muscle/bone gains in intact humans at any tolerable exposure.
  • Degree and reversibility of HPG-axis / testosterone suppression in humans — never measured for ACP-105 (only class evidence from other SARMs).
  • Real human hepatotoxicity, lipid, and cardiovascular risk (only class evidence and in-silico hERG/genotoxicity flags exist).
  • Whether the in-silico alerts (hERG cardiotoxicity, genotoxicity, nitrile-derived cyanide) correspond to any real-world toxicity.
  • Long-term safety, carcinogenicity, and fertility effects — entirely unstudied.
  • Whether the 'cognitive' benefit is reproducible or requires the ERβ co-agonist that was needed in the one supportive Alzheimer's study.
  • Actual identity/purity of grey-market 'ACP-105' — mislabeling, substitution with other SARMs, and contamination are documented across the SARM market (PMID 38560649).

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction information, NOT an endorsement or a protocol, and it contains no dosing. For anyone who has used or is exposed to grey-market ACP-105, clinically relevant things to MONITOR (ideally with a physician) include: (1) Liver — baseline and follow-up ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, and bilirubin; seek urgent care for jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, right-upper-quadrant pain, or unexplained itching (SARM-class DILI is well documented). (2) Lipids/cardiometabolic — a full lipid panel watching for falling HDL-C; blood pressure. (3) Hormonal axis — total/free testosterone, LH, FSH, and (if relevant) estradiol to detect suppression; symptoms of low testosterone (low libido, fatigue, mood change, testicular changes). (4) General — any palpitations or syncope warrant medical review including an ECG given the theoretical hERG signal. (5) Product-integrity risk — because mislabeling/substitution/contamination is common, unexpected or steroid-like effects (severe acne, aggression, virilization, gynecomastia) may indicate the product is not what the label claims. (6) Athletes — ACP-105 and its metabolites are detectable in urine and hair anti-doping assays and it is WADA-prohibited, so use carries a near-certain sanction. Discontinuation and medical evaluation are the appropriate responses to any abnormal finding; there is no established safe monitoring regimen because the compound has never been studied in humans.

Caveats

CRITICAL EVIDENCE GAP: everything positive said about ACP-105 comes from short rodent studies (mostly in castrated/gonadectomized animals) and in-vitro/in-silico modelling. There is not one human study — no dose, no efficacy readout, no measured half-life, no safety follow-up. Marketing that presents it as a proven 'strong but selective' muscle builder is extrapolating from 2-week castrated-rat anabolic data (PMID 19856921), which does not predict effect size, safety, or suppression in intact humans. The headline 'cognitive decline' story rests on a single Alzheimer's-mouse study (PMID 24020966) in which the anti-amyloid/pro-memory benefit needed a SECOND drug (an ERβ agonist, AC-186) — ACP-105 alone mainly reduced anxiety-like behavior. 'Partial agonist' means an intrinsically lower efficacy ceiling than testosterone, not a free-lunch version of it. Purity/identity is a major real-world hazard: chemical analysis of online SARM products found only ~70% contained the labeled SARM, ~23% contained a DIFFERENT SARM, ~30% contained undeclared drugs (e.g., tamoxifen, clomifene, testosterone, tadalafil), and measured content ranged from 30-90% of the label claim (PMID 38560649). The compound is WADA-prohibited — a near-certain sanction for any tested athlete. In-silico toxicology flags (predicted hERG interaction/cardiotoxicity potential, genotoxicity/DNA-interaction alerts, possible cyanide release from the nitrile group on metabolism; PMID 40064700, PMID 40928534) are unvalidated computational predictions but are reasons for caution, not reassurance. This entry is educational harm-reduction, not endorsement; it contains no dosing or 'how to run it' guidance by design.
