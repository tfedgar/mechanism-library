Calcium-D-glucarate

An OTC "estrogen detox" salt that inhibits gut beta-glucuronidase to keep already-inactivated estrogens from being un-inactivated and reabsorbed — a clearance play, not an aromatase blocker. The enzyme-level mechanism is real and the compound is non-toxic, but essentially all of the estrogen-lowering and anti-cancer data is in rats; controlled human trials on estrogen levels do not exist.

Rodent / preclinical 9 citations
Class
Beta-glucuronidase inhibitor / calcium salt of a natural dietary acid (estrogen-clearance nutraceutical)

Intestinal (and to a lesser extent serum/tissue) beta-glucuronidase
Human status
Sold widely as an OTC dietary supplement (commonly 500 mg tablets), typically stacked with DIM or I3C and marketed to lifters, TRT/PED users, and the longevity crowd as an 'estrogen detox.' The compound is real, cheap, food-derived, and has an excellent safety record. BUT the human evidence for the marketed use is essentially absent: there are NO controlled human trials showing calcium-D-glucarate lowers serum estradiol, alters estrogen metabolite ratios, or improves any clinical outcome. A ClinicalTrials.gov search returns only false-positive hits — the one study actually tagged with 'calcium glucarate' (NCT00416715) is a letrozole/vitamin-D musculoskeletal trial where calcium glucarate is merely one of several calcium-supplement arms, not a CDG estrogen study; the remaining hits are mis-tagged iron-sucrose studies. All estrogen-lowering and anti-tumor data come from rat carcinogenesis models (chiefly the Walaszek/Hanausek/Slaga group) and in-vitro work. The mechanism (gut beta-glucuronidase inhibition reducing enterohepatic estrogen recycling) is biologically sound and established at the enzyme level, but the leap to 'meaningfully lowers estrogen in a human, especially on cycle' is unproven. Honest tier: established MECHANISM, speculative CLINICAL EFFECT in humans.

At a glance

  • Inhibits gut (and serum/tissue) beta-glucuronidase, reducing deconjugation and enterohepatic reabsorption of glucuronidated estrogens and other toxins (enzyme inhibition rodent-established; human estrogen effect unquantified)
  • Acts on estrogen CLEARANCE/elimination — not synthesis and not aromatase; it cannot 'crash' estradiol the way an aromatase inhibitor can
  • Reduced estrogen-driven mammary tumorigenesis and reported lower estradiol in carcinogen rat models (rodent only)
  • Non-hormonal; a food-derived compound repeatedly described as non-toxic and well tolerated in the preclinical literature
  • Marketed and stacked with DIM/I3C as the OTC 'estrogen detox' — but the two act at different points (DIM shifts hepatic estrogen metabolism; CDG protects the downstream conjugates from being recycled)
  • Claimed lipid-lowering and general carcinogen-detox effects, both weakly supported and mostly preclinical/aspirational

Mechanism, step by step

  1. CDG is a sustained-release source of the real inhibitor Established

    Calcium-D-glucarate is the calcium salt of D-glucaric acid, a compound the body already makes in small amounts and that occurs in oranges, apples, grapefruit, and cruciferous vegetables. In the acidic stomach and in tissues, glucarate equilibrates with D-glucaro-1,4-lactone, which is the molecule that actually inhibits beta-glucuronidase. The calcium salt acts as a slow-release depot, so the lactone is generated gradually rather than in one spike. PMID 12197785; PMID 18772850; PMID 3091283

  2. The lactone inhibits beta-glucuronidase Evidence-rodent

    D-glucaro-1,4-lactone binds and inhibits beta-glucuronidase, the enzyme that cleaves glucuronic-acid groups off conjugated molecules. In rats, a single oral dose of calcium glucarate inhibited beta-glucuronidase activity by roughly 37-57% across serum, liver, lung, and intestinal microsomes, and chronic feeding suppressed intestinal/hepatic enzyme activity and gut-bacterial enzyme activity by 54-70%. PMID 2346674; PMID 2925160

  3. Phase II detox tags estrogens with glucuronic acid for excretion Established

    The liver inactivates estrogens (and many drugs and carcinogens) by attaching glucuronic acid — a Phase II reaction called glucuronidation. This makes them water-soluble and marks them for excretion into bile and out through the gut. This step is normal biology and happens with or without CDG; CDG does not increase this conjugation, it protects it downstream. PMID 12197785; PMID 15035900

  4. Normally, gut beta-glucuronidase un-does that tagging and estrogen is reabsorbed Established

    Once glucuronidated estrogens reach the intestine in bile, bacterial and tissue beta-glucuronidase can cleave off the glucuronic acid, regenerating free, fat-soluble estrogen that gets reabsorbed back into the bloodstream — the enterohepatic recirculation loop. High beta-glucuronidase activity (e.g. from gut dysbiosis) means more estrogen is recycled rather than eliminated. PMID 12197785; PMID 18772850

  5. CDG blocks that reactivation, so conjugated estrogen leaves the body Evidence-rodent

    By inhibiting gut beta-glucuronidase, CDG keeps estrogens (and glucuronidated carcinogens) in their conjugated, water-soluble form. They can no longer be cleaved and reabsorbed, so a larger fraction is carried out in the feces. The net effect is enhanced elimination of estrogen — it acts on clearance, not on synthesis or aromatization. Direct estrogen-excretion data are rodent; the enzyme-inhibition step is established. PMID 3091283; PMID 6202433

  6. In rodents this lowered circulating estradiol and blocked hormone-driven tumors Evidence-rodent

    In the DMBA rat mammary-tumor model, feeding calcium-D-glucarate after the carcinogen reduced tumor development by over 70%, and the authors reported lower endogenous estradiol and precursors of 17-ketosteroids. A slow-release lactone derivative (DAGDL) gave similar ~70% tumor reductions and reduced carcinogen-DNA binding. This rat literature is the basis of the 'estrogen detox' claim. PMID 3091283; PMID 6202433; PMID 2208084

  7. Whether any of this meaningfully lowers estrogen in humans is unproven Speculative

    The human evidence is essentially a leap from rat data. There are no controlled human trials showing calcium-D-glucarate lowers serum estradiol, changes estrogen ratios, or affects clinical outcomes. The mechanism is plausible and the compound is safe, but the magnitude of any effect in people — especially against the strong estrogen drive of a testosterone/AAS cycle — has not been demonstrated. PMID 12197785; PMID 15035900

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Calcium-D-glucarate (ChEMBL CHEMBL2104217, preferred name 'Calcium saccharate'; molecular formula C6H8CaO8; MW 248.2; natural product; NSC-122011) is the calcium salt of D-glucaric acid. It is not the pharmacophore. In the acidic stomach and in tissues it equilibrates with D-glucaro-1,4-lactone, which is the reversible inhibitor of beta-glucuronidase (a glycosidase that hydrolyzes glucuronide conjugates). The calcium salt behaves as a sustained-release reservoir that keeps generating the lactone, giving a longer, flatter inhibition of the enzyme than free lactone alone.

Signalling cascade. Oral CDG -> gastric/tissue conversion to D-glucaro-1,4-lactone -> inhibition of beta-glucuronidase in gut lumen, gut wall, serum, and tissues -> glucuronide-conjugated estrogens (and glucuronidated carcinogens/xenobiotics) delivered in bile are NOT deconjugated -> they remain water-soluble -> excreted in feces instead of being reabsorbed via enterohepatic recirculation -> net increase in estrogen/toxin elimination. Downstream (rodent) consequence: lower circulating estradiol and precursors of 17-ketosteroids, and reduced estrogen-driven mammary tumor promotion.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (tablets, commonly 500 mg). No parenteral use.
Absorption / bioavailability
CDG dissociates and is converted to D-glucaro-1,4-lactone in the stomach; the lactone is absorbed from the intestinal tract and distributed via blood to internal organs. Quantitative human oral bioavailability is not well characterized. Conversion to the active lactone is incomplete and pH/context dependent.
Half-life
Not formally established in humans. Practical duration of enzyme inhibition is extended by the slow-release salt behavior; rat data show diurnal variation in the depth of serum/tissue enzyme inhibition after dosing.
Metabolism
D-glucaric acid and D-glucaro-1,4-lactone are natural end-products of the D-glucuronic acid pathway in mammals (D-glucuronolactone dehydrogenase oxidizes the lactone of glucuronic acid to the dilactone, which hydrolyzes spontaneously to D-glucaro-1,4-lactone). The lactone and free acid interconvert in aqueous solution.
Excretion
In rats given oral D-glucaro-1,4-lactone, ~11% was excreted in urine and only ~0.2% in bile; urinary (not biliary) excretion was dose-proportional. Mean biliary lactone concentration reached ~0.06 mM, sufficient to suppress ~75% of biliary beta-glucuronidase activity. In humans, urinary D-glucaric acid is a recognized (indirect) biomarker of hepatic enzyme induction.
Notes
Because the salt is a slow-release depot, steady enzyme inhibition depends on continued intake; a single rat dose gives transient, partial (roughly 40-60% in tissues) inhibition that recovers.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human dose-response for estrogen endpoints has not been established — no such trials exist. In rodents, effect scaled with dose/exposure: a single oral 4.5 mmol/kg dose of calcium glucarate inhibited beta-glucuronidase by ~57% (serum), ~44% (liver), and ~37-39% (lung, intestine); chronic 4% dietary supplementation suppressed intestinal/hepatic and gut-bacterial enzyme activity by 54-70%. Biliary lactone of ~0.06 mM suppressed ~75% of enzyme activity. The commonly marketed human intake (hundreds of mg to ~1-2 g/day) is far below the weight-adjusted rodent chemoprevention exposures, which is one reason human translation is uncertain. NOTE: this is mechanism/exposure context, not a dosing recommendation.

How it compares

  • vs Aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole, exemestane)
    same: Both are used by lifters/PED users to manage estrogen.
    different: AIs block estrogen SYNTHESIS (aromatase) and can rapidly, deeply lower estradiol with proven human effect and real crash risk. CDG acts only on CLEARANCE of already-made, already-conjugated estrogen and has no proven human estradiol-lowering effect. They are not interchangeable; CDG cannot substitute for an AI when synthesis drive is high.
  • vs DIM / indole-3-carbinol (the usual stack partner)
    same: Both are OTC 'estrogen detox' supplements marketed together.
    different: DIM/I3C act UPSTREAM, shifting hepatic estrogen metabolism (favoring 2-hydroxy over 16-hydroxy metabolites) and inducing enzymes; CDG acts DOWNSTREAM, protecting the glucuronide conjugates from being recycled in the gut. Complementary mechanisms in theory; neither has strong human estrogen-outcome data in this context.
  • vs SERMs (tamoxifen, raloxifene)
    same: All discussed in breast-cancer / estrogen contexts.
    different: SERMs block the estrogen receptor directly with robust clinical trial evidence; CDG does not touch the receptor and has no clinical outcome evidence. Entirely different mechanism and evidence tier.
  • vs Dietary D-glucaric acid (oranges, apples, cruciferous veg)
    same: Same active molecule family; the supplement is just a concentrated calcium salt.
    different: Food amounts are small (~1-5 mg/100 g; e.g. ~4.5 mg/100 g in oranges); the supplement delivers much more. Whether the supplement dose crosses a threshold food doesn't reach — for a real human effect — is exactly the unproven question.

History & legal status

D-glucaric acid and its lactone were characterized as natural mammalian metabolites and beta-glucuronidase inhibitors decades ago. The cancer-prevention program was driven largely by Walaszek, Hanausek and Slaga (South Dakota State / MD Anderson / AMC / UT), producing the rat chemoprevention literature from the 1980s onward and positioning calcium-D-glucarate as a slow-release delivery form. In ChEMBL the compound appears as CHEMBL2104217 (preferred name 'Calcium saccharate'; NSC-122011), flagged as a natural product with a maximum clinical phase of 2 — reflecting historical exploratory human study, NOT an approved estrogen or oncology indication. It is sold in the US as a dietary supplement (not FDA-approved as a drug for any condition) and has no approved therapeutic claim for estrogen management. Note: calcium saccharate also has a separate, unrelated pharmaceutical role as a stabilizer/excipient in some calcium products, which contributes to registry name collisions.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does oral CDG measurably lower serum estradiol or shift estrogen metabolite ratios in humans? No controlled data.
  • What human dose (if any) achieves enough gut beta-glucuronidase inhibition to matter, given incomplete salt-to-lactone conversion and sub-rodent dosing?
  • Can it meaningfully counter estrogen elevation on an aromatizing testosterone/AAS cycle, where synthesis drive is high? Unstudied.
  • Real-world interaction risk: does it alter clearance of co-administered glucuronidated drugs/steroids in people?
  • Is the DIM+CDG stack additive, redundant, or neither in humans? No head-to-head or combination human data.
  • Long-term consequences (if any) of chronically suppressing gut beta-glucuronidase on the microbiome and on recycling of beneficial conjugates.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For harm-reduction only (no dosing/protocol guidance): (1) Do not treat CDG as an aromatase inhibitor — if managing estrogen on cycle, track serum estradiol (sensitive/LC-MS assay) and symptoms rather than assuming CDG is controlling it. (2) Watch for LOW-estrogen symptoms (joint aches, low libido, low mood, worsened lipids) if CDG is layered on top of an actual AI or SERM. (3) If on other medications, be aware of the theoretical interaction with glucuronidated drugs and review with a clinician/pharmacist rather than self-managing. (4) Account for the calcium contribution in total daily calcium if stacking calcium sources. (5) Prefer single-ingredient products over proprietary DIM blends so effects/side effects are attributable. (6) Persistent GI symptoms, jaundice, or unexplained lab abnormalities warrant stopping and clinical evaluation — not more supplement.

Caveats

Read this as: good mechanism, thin human data, oversold as a product. (1) The active inhibitor is D-glucaro-1,4-lactone, not the salt itself — potency depends on in-vivo conversion, which is incomplete and variable. (2) Every estrogen-lowering and anti-cancer result cited is rodent or in-vitro; the widely repeated 'lowers estradiol' claim traces to rat mammary-tumor studies, not human data. (3) It acts on CLEARANCE (elimination of already-conjugated estrogen), not on synthesis or aromatization — so it is mechanistically incapable of the fast, deep estradiol suppression an aromatase inhibitor produces. It will NOT rescue a high-E2 problem on a heavy aromatizing AAS cycle the way anastrozole would, and marketing that implies otherwise is wrong. (4) Because beta-glucuronidase also deconjugates many drugs and other steroids, there is a theoretical (not documented) potential to alter clearance of co-administered glucuronidated drugs — relevant to polypharmacy PED users. (5) Supplement quality/dose is unregulated; 'proprietary blends' with DIM make it impossible to attribute any effect. (6) None of this is dosing or protocol advice — it is mechanism and evidence only.
