UBX1325

The most clinically advanced industry senolytic — an eye-injected BCL-xL inhibitor meant to kill off "zombie" senescent cells in the diabetic retina. Early human data (through Phase 2) are encouraging on safety and hint at a visual-acuity benefit, but no trial has yet delivered a statistically decisive efficacy result.

PIPELINE / clinical-stage (not approved anywhere). UBX1325/foselutoclax is Unity Biotechnology's lead senolytic and the most clinically advanced industry senolytic. NOT available as a prescribable therapy; NOT a supplement or research chemical you can obtain — it is an in-eye injectable studied only in monitored trials.

At a glance

  • Senolysis: selective apoptosis of senescent vascular/retinal cells via BCL-xL inhibition (rodent + in-vitro human-cell evidence)
  • Reduced retinal vascular leakage / edema in diabetic mouse models (preclinical)
  • Human safety/tolerability demonstrated for a single intravitreal injection in Phase 1 and Phase 2a (advanced/anti-VEGF-refractory DME)
  • A suggestive but NOT statistically significant visual-acuity benefit vs sham in Phase 2a DME (BEHOLD: +5.6 ETDRS letters at wk48, 95% CI -1.5 to 12.7)
  • Potential for durable, disease-modifying effect from intermittent dosing rather than continuous suppression (hypothesis; not yet proven in humans)
  • Being tested head-to-head vs aflibercept (anti-VEGF standard of care) in later trials (wet AMD ENVISION, DME ASPIRE) — positioned as a mechanistically distinct complement/alternative, not a proven replacement

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Chronic hyperglycemia drives retinal cells into senescence Evidence-human (p16INK4A+ cells in patient retinas) / Evidence-in vitro (hyperglycemia-induced senescence in culture)

    In diabetes, sustained high glucose pushes subsets of retinal vascular endothelial cells into cellular senescence — a stress state where cells stop dividing but do not die, accumulating and expressing markers like p16INK4A. Two lines of evidence support this: in cell culture, sustained hyperglycemia produced senescent endothelial cells with disrupted cell-cell junctions and leaky barrier function (in vitro); and p16INK4A+ senescent cells are found in the retinas of diabetic-retinopathy patients (human tissue observation). PMID 38321220 (Crespo-Garcia, Nat Med 2024); PMID 33548171 (Crespo-Garcia, Cell Metab 2021)

  2. Senescent cells secrete inflammatory signals (SASP) and break the blood-retinal barrier Evidence-rodent (causal) / Evidence-human (tissue correlation)

    Senescent retinal cells adopt a senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP), releasing inflammatory and pro-angiogenic factors. This micro-inflammation plus the leaky, poorly-junctioned endothelium is proposed to drive the fluid leakage (edema) and pathological new-vessel growth seen in diabetic macular edema and diabetic retinopathy. The causal 'senescence drives edema/angiogenesis' step rests mainly on mouse models plus human-tissue correlation, not on a human interventional proof. PMID 33548171 (Cell Metab 2021); PMID 38321220 (Nat Med 2024); review PMID 36091769 (Hassan & Bhatwadekar, Front Pharmacol 2022)

  3. Senescent cells survive by depending on BCL-xL Evidence-in vitro (human and mouse cultured cells)

    Senescent cells resist apoptosis by upregulating pro-survival BCL-2-family proteins. Screening of senolytics showed that certain senescent cell types (notably vascular endothelial cells) specifically depend on BCL-xL to stay alive — silencing BCL-xL, or inhibiting it with navitoclax, selectively kills them, whereas some other senescent cell types (e.g. preadipocytes) are not killed. This selectivity was shown in cultured human/mouse cells, not in humans in vivo. It is the vulnerability UBX1325 is designed to exploit. PMID 26711051 (Zhu/Kirkland, Aging Cell 2016 — navitoclax senolytic, BCL-xL dependence in senescent HUVECs); PMID 33548171 (Cell Metab 2021)

  4. UBX1325 blocks BCL-xL, releasing the apoptotic brake Evidence-rodent (in the retina) / Established (BH3-mimetic mechanism)

    Foselutoclax is a prodrug converted in the eye to the active molecule UBX0601, a small-molecule (BH3-mimetic-type) inhibitor that binds BCL-xL and prevents it from sequestering pro-apoptotic BH3 proteins. In a cell already 'primed to die' — like a senescent cell — removing the BCL-xL brake tips the balance toward mitochondrial (BAX/BAK) apoptosis. The BH3-mimetic mechanism of BCL-xL-family inhibition is well established; that UBX1325 specifically does this in the diseased retina is inferred from preclinical models. PMID 38321220 (Nat Med 2024); BCL-2-family inhibition / on-target platelet effect PMID 23291630 (Souers, Nat Med 2013)

  5. Senolysis: selective elimination of senescent (not healthy) vessels Evidence-rodent

    Because healthy quiescent cells are not 'primed' the same way, BCL-xL inhibition preferentially removes senescent, disease-associated cells. In mouse retinopathy, BCL-xL inhibitors cleared endothelial subsets carrying senescence signatures (including Col1a1-expressing cells) on single-cell RNA-seq while leaving healthy vasculature, yielding a retina more conducive to normal vascular repair. This selectivity is demonstrated in rodents, not confirmed in humans. PMID 33548171 (Cell Metab 2021 — single-cell analysis of BCL-xL-inhibitor-treated retinas)

  6. Clearing the senescent burden reduces leakage and preserves retinal function (preclinical) Evidence-rodent

    In a mouse model of DME, pharmacologically eliminating senescent cells reduced diabetes-induced retinal vascular leakage and preserved retinal function. The hypothesis is that a course of injections could 'reset' the diseased tissue, giving a longer-lasting, disease-modifying effect than anti-VEGF drugs that only suppress a downstream signal. This remains a hypothesis for humans. PMID 38321220 (Nat Med 2024)

  7. In humans: safe/tolerable so far; efficacy is trending but not statistically decisive Evidence-human (safety established; efficacy trend, not statistically significant)

    A Phase 1 single-ascending-dose study in advanced DME/nAMD patients (no longer benefiting from anti-VEGF) met its primary goal of safety/tolerability with a single intravitreal injection (n=19). A subsequent randomized Phase 2a (BEHOLD, NCT04857996; n=65, single injection vs sham) again met safety as its primary endpoint and, as a secondary efficacy readout, showed a between-group difference in best-corrected visual acuity at week 48 of +5.6 ETDRS letters — but the 95% CI was −1.5 to 12.7, i.e. it crossed zero and did NOT reach statistical significance. So the human data establish proof-of-safety and a suggestive trend, NOT proof of durable vision benefit; larger trials are needed. PMID 38321220 (Nat Med 2024; NCT04537884); PMID 40261111 (Klier/Dananberg, NEJM Evid 2025 — BEHOLD Phase 2a, NCT04857996); editorial PMID 40261115 (Chew, NEJM Evid 2025)

  8. Local delivery is deliberate — to avoid the systemic toxicity of BCL-xL inhibition Established

    Systemic BCL-xL inhibition (e.g. navitoclax) causes dose-limiting thrombocytopenia because platelets depend on BCL-xL for their lifespan. UBX1325 is injected directly into the eye and formulated as a prodrug to concentrate action locally and minimize systemic exposure — a design choice made specifically to sidestep the platelet toxicity that limits systemic BCL-xL drugs. PMID 23291630 (Souers, Nat Med 2013 — on-target thrombocytopenia from BCL-xL inhibition; ABT-199/venetoclax engineered to be BCL-2-selective and spare platelets); route/prodrug design NCT06011798, PMID 38321220

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. UBX1325 is administered as foselutoclax, a phosphate prodrug cleaved in the eye to the active molecule UBX0601, a small-molecule inhibitor of BCL-xL (BCL2L1). It is a BH3-mimetic-type inhibitor: it occupies the hydrophobic BH3-binding groove of BCL-xL, preventing BCL-xL from sequestering pro-apoptotic BCL-2-family effectors/activators. In cells already 'primed' for death (senescent cells), neutralizing BCL-xL permits BAX/BAK-mediated mitochondrial outer-membrane permeabilization and apoptosis. Selectivity for BCL-xL (vs BCL-2) is the intended profile; senolytic selectivity is conferred not by the target alone but by the biology — senescent vascular endothelial cells are disproportionately BCL-xL-dependent (shown in vitro).

Signalling cascade. BCL-xL inhibition → freed pro-apoptotic BH3 proteins → BAX/BAK activation → mitochondrial outer-membrane permeabilization → cytochrome c release → caspase cascade → apoptosis of the senescent cell. Net tissue effect (preclinical): loss of SASP-secreting, barrier-disrupting senescent endothelial cells → reduced micro-inflammation and vascular leakage → restored conditions for physiological vascular repair.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Intravitreal (IVT) injection into the affected eye. In the repeat-dose Phase 2b ASPIRE trial (NCT06011798): 10 µg per injection, given 8 weeks apart.
Absorption / bioavailability
Delivered as a phosphate prodrug (foselutoclax) to improve local handling/solubility; converted in situ to the active BCL-xL inhibitor UBX0601. Systemic bioavailability is intentionally minimized by the local ocular route to avoid platelet toxicity. In Phase 1 (NCT04537884) a secondary objective measured plasma UBX1325 up to 24 h post-dose, consistent with the intent to characterize/limit systemic exposure.
Half-life
Not reliably established in the peer-reviewed sources reviewed. The clinical dosing rationale (single injection in Phase 1/2a, then ~8-week dosing intervals in Phase 2b) reflects a senolytic 'hit-and-clear' concept rather than continuous receptor occupancy — the premise is a durable biological effect from intermittent clearance, not a long plasma/vitreous half-life. Treat any specific half-life figure as not verified here.
Metabolism
Prodrug-to-active conversion (dephosphorylation) to UBX0601 in ocular tissue. Detailed human ocular/systemic metabolism parameters are not established in the peer-reviewed literature reviewed here.
Excretion
Not characterized in the reviewed peer-reviewed sources.
Notes
PK is deliberately compartmentalized to the eye; the key PK/PD idea is that a pulse of senolysis may produce effects outlasting drug presence. Quantitative human ocular PK has not been published in the sources reviewed.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

No dosing protocol is provided here (harm-reduction). For context only: the program moved from a single-ascending-dose Phase 1 (NCT04537884, establishing tolerated single IVT doses) and a single-injection Phase 2a vs sham (BEHOLD, NCT04857996) to fixed repeat regimens in later trials — e.g. ASPIRE (NCT06011798) used foselutoclax 10 µg IVT every 8 weeks vs aflibercept 2 mg every 8 weeks, and ENVISION (NCT05275205) used repeat IVT injections in wet AMD vs aflibercept. The underlying pharmacology is threshold/senolysis-driven (clear the primed senescent population) rather than classically titrated; higher/more frequent exposure raises the theoretical risk of hitting BCL-xL in non-target cells. A clean, statistically decisive human dose-response for efficacy has not been established.

How it compares

  • vs Aflibercept / anti-VEGF (standard of care for DME & wet AMD)
    same: Both delivered by intravitreal injection for the same retinal diseases; both aim to reduce edema and preserve vision.
    different: Anti-VEGF suppresses a downstream growth-factor signal and requires frequent, often lifelong injections; UBX1325 attacks an upstream driver (senescent cells) and is hypothesized to give more durable, disease-modifying benefit from intermittent dosing. Crucially, aflibercept has proven Phase 3 efficacy and approval; UBX1325 does not — it is the experimental challenger, tested head-to-head vs aflibercept in ENVISION (wet AMD) and ASPIRE (DME), and has not shown a statistically decisive advantage in any published trial.
  • vs Navitoclax (ABT-263), a systemic BCL-2/BCL-xL senolytic
    same: Same core molecular target class (BCL-xL inhibition → senolysis); navitoclax is in fact the tool compound used in the foundational senolytic screens for this pathway.
    different: Navitoclax is oral/systemic and hits platelets (dose-limiting thrombocytopenia); it is used mainly in oncology/research. UBX1325 is engineered as a localized intravitreal prodrug to get senolysis in the eye without systemic platelet toxicity.
  • vs Dasatinib + Quercetin (D+Q) / fisetin (systemic senolytics popular in longevity circles)
    same: All are senolytics that aim to clear senescent cells.
    different: D+Q and fisetin act via different/broader pro-survival pathways and are taken systemically (often self-experimented in longevity communities); UBX1325 is a target-selective BCL-xL inhibitor delivered surgically into one organ (the eye) for a specific disease. UBX1325 is NOT a systemic anti-aging senolytic and cannot be repurposed as one.
  • vs UBX0101 (Unity's earlier intra-articular knee-OA senolytic)
    same: Same company, same senolytic thesis (clear senescent cells to treat age-related disease), same 'local injection into the affected joint/organ' delivery strategy.
    different: UBX0101 was an MDM2/p53-interaction senolytic injected into the knee for osteoarthritis; its pivotal Phase 2 (NCT04129944) did not beat placebo and the program was halted (2020). UBX1325 is a different molecule (BCL-xL inhibitor) for a different organ (eye) and remains in development — the OA failure is why the company pivoted to ophthalmology.

History &amp; legal status

Developed by Unity Biotechnology (UNITY, South San Francisco), founded on the thesis that clearing senescent cells can treat age-related disease. Its earlier senolytic UBX0101 (an MDM2/p53-interaction inhibitor) was delivered as a single INTRA-ARTICULAR (in-the-knee) injection for osteoarthritis — NOT a systemic drug — and its pivotal Phase 2 knee-OA trial (NCT04129944, single dose vs placebo, completed May 2020) did not separate from placebo on pain; UNITY discontinued the OA program in 2020 and pivoted to localized ophthalmology with UBX1325. UBX1325 entered the clinic with a Phase 1 SAD study (NCT04537884, from 2020), then Phase 2a single-injection DME vs sham (BEHOLD, NCT04857996; peer-reviewed 2025, PMID 40261111), Phase 2 repeat-dose wet AMD vs aflibercept (ENVISION, NCT05275205), and Phase 2b ASPIRE in DME vs aflibercept (NCT06011798, completed 2025). The generic/INN name foselutoclax reflects the phosphate-prodrug form; the active molecule is UBX0601. As of the January 2026 cutoff: no marketing approval in any jurisdiction, no registered Phase 3, and the best published efficacy signal (Phase 2a) is a non-significant trend. It remains the furthest-along industry senolytic but has not delivered a decisive positive pivotal result. Company financing/strategic status has been precarious, adding program-continuity risk independent of the science.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does UBX1325 produce clinically meaningful, statistically significant, durable vision gains in humans — and is it non-inferior or superior to aflibercept? (Phase 2a vs sham showed only a non-significant trend, +5.6 letters, 95% CI -1.5 to 12.7; head-to-head-vs-aflibercept efficacy from ENVISION/ASPIRE was not found peer-reviewed in the sources reviewed.)
  • How long does a single senolytic 'clearance' effect actually last in the human retina, and how often must it be repeated?
  • Human ocular and systemic PK/half-life of foselutoclax/UBX0601 are not well characterized in the peer-reviewed literature reviewed.
  • Long-term safety of repeat intravitreal BCL-xL inhibition — including any impact on healthy retinal vasculature or repair over years.
  • Whether senescent-cell burden is truly causal (vs a marker) of DME in the broad patient population, and which patients respond.
  • Whether any systemic exposure over repeated dosing is enough to affect platelets in real-world use (no systemic signal seen so far in Phase 2a).
  • Durability of the company/program itself given financing and the prior UBX0101 osteoarthritis setback.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is an investigational intravitreal drug administered only by clinicians in trials — there is no at-home use. In the trial setting, what is monitored: (1) Ocular safety — best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA, ETDRS letters), central subfield/retinal thickness by OCT, intraocular pressure, and slit-lamp/fundus exams to catch intraocular inflammation (uveitis), endophthalmitis (a medical emergency: pain, redness, sudden vision drop after injection), retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, or cataract. (2) Efficacy/disease-activity — in ASPIRE, protocol-defined worsening (e.g. CST worsening ≥75 µm from baseline, a ≥10-letter BCVA drop, or new clinically significant hemorrhage) could trigger rescue with aflibercept; these are disease-activity safeguards, not a drug-dosing scheme. (3) Systemic — because BCL-xL inhibition can cause thrombocytopenia if it reaches the circulation, platelet counts are a logical safety parameter, though the local route is designed to keep systemic exposure minimal and no systemic safety signal was seen in Phase 2a. Red flags any patient should report after an eye injection: increasing eye pain, worsening redness, light sensitivity, floaters/flashes, or sudden vision loss. No dosing or self-administration guidance is provided — this compound cannot be safely or legally self-run.

Caveats

Evidence-tier honesty: The disease-modifying story (senescent cells cause DME; clearing them helps) is strongly supported in cell culture and mouse models (PMID 33548171, 38321220), plus human-tissue correlation (p16INK4A+ cells in patient retinas), but human EFFICACY is not established. The best human efficacy data to date is the peer-reviewed BEHOLD Phase 2a (PMID 40261111, NEJM Evid 2025): a single injection vs sham met its safety primary endpoint and showed a secondary between-group BCVA difference of +5.6 ETDRS letters at week 48 — but with a 95% CI of -1.5 to 12.7 that crosses zero, i.e. a trend, NOT a statistically significant benefit. The authors' own conclusion was 'trends suggestive of potential efficacy; larger trials are needed,' echoed by an accompanying editorial (PMID 40261115). NOTE: BEHOLD compared UBX1325 against SHAM, not against aflibercept — the head-to-head-vs-aflibercept trials are ENVISION (wet AMD, NCT05275205) and ASPIRE (DME, NCT06011798), whose full efficacy results were not found peer-reviewed in the sources reviewed and should be read cautiously. Treat 'senolytic that reverses eye disease' as a plausible hypothesis under test, not a proven fact. Mechanistic caveats: 'senolytic selectivity' is real but not absolute — BCL-xL is also essential for platelet survival, so any systemic exposure risks thrombocytopenia (PMID 23291630); the intravitreal prodrug design exists specifically to keep the drug local. This is an ophthalmic injectable with the usual intravitreal risks (see monitoring). Audience note for a fitness/longevity readership: UBX1325 is an EYE drug for diabetic retinal disease, not a systemic 'anti-aging senolytic' you take to clear senescent cells body-wide — do not extrapolate longevity claims from it, and there is no legitimate way to self-administer it. No dosing guidance is provided here by design.
