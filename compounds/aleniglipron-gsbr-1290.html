Human status
PIPELINE / CLINICAL-STAGE — not approved anywhere. Aleniglipron (GSBR-1290) is an investigational oral small-molecule GLP-1R agonist from Structure Therapeutics (via its wholly owned subsidiary Gasherbrum Bio, Inc.). Human evidence to date: completed Phase 1 single/multiple-ascending-dose studies in healthy and T2DM subjects (NCT05762471 Phase 1b/2a, COMPLETED, n=142; NCT05893043 Japanese/non-Japanese bridging, COMPLETED, n=18; NCT06139055 comparative-bioavailability/formulation, status UNKNOWN on ClinicalTrials.gov, n=70), and one peer-reviewed Phase 2b obesity trial — ACCESS (NCT06693843), published in Nature Medicine on 5 June 2026 (PMID 42249138; publisher correction PMID 42343118, 24 June 2026), which randomized 230 adults with obesity/overweight and met its primary weight-loss endpoint (up to -11.3% placebo-adjusted at week 36). Additional Phase 2 studies exist (NCT06703021 ACCESS II dose-optimization, COMPLETED; NCT07169942 body composition, ACTIVE_NOT_RECRUITING; NCT07400588 in T2DM, ACTIVE_NOT_RECRUITING). A pivotal Phase 3 obesity program is planned but NOT yet recruiting as of mid-2026 (NCT07654361, ~3,600 participants; NCT07654374 ACCOMPLISH-2 in obesity + T2DM, ~1,100). There are NO published long-term (>1 year), cardiovascular-outcome, or Phase 3 efficacy/safety data. It is behind orforglipron in development and should be understood as an early, promising, but unproven second entrant in the oral-small-molecule GLP-1 race.

Mechanism, step by step

7 steps6 human-evidenced 1 speculative

  1. A pill, not a peptide, binds the GLP-1 receptor Established

    Native GLP-1 and injectable drugs like semaglutide are peptides that wrap into the large extracellular mouth of the GLP-1 receptor. Aleniglipron is a small non-peptide molecule that instead slots into a pocket inside the receptor's transmembrane core. That small-molecule pocket is what makes an orally absorbed, once-daily pill possible — peptides are largely destroyed in the gut, small molecules can survive it. Aleniglipron is described by its developer (Structure Therapeutics / Gasherbrum Bio) as an orally bioavailable small-molecule GLP-1R agonist in the same design family as orforglipron. Class transmembrane/non-canonical binding mechanism: preprint DOI 10.20944/preprints202505.0972.v1 (NOT peer-reviewed); orforglipron nonpeptide binding PMID 39693407; orforglipron review PMID 41683830. Aleniglipron identity/route: PMID 42249138 (ACCESS Phase 2b).

  2. Binding switches on Gs and raises cAMP inside the cell Established

    Once the small molecule stabilizes the active shape of the receptor, the receptor couples to the Gs protein, which activates adenylyl cyclase and raises intracellular cyclic AMP (cAMP). cAMP is the core second messenger that drives the useful GLP-1 effects (insulin release, appetite signaling). This Gs-cAMP arm is the shared engine of every GLP-1R agonist, peptide or pill. PMID 40806371 (GLP-1R as functionally Gs-selective GPCR, Gs→adenylyl cyclase→cAMP); PMID 41683830 (orforglipron stimulates cAMP).

  3. The design aims to be 'Gs-biased' — heavy on cAMP, light on β-arrestin Contested

    After a receptor fires, β-arrestin normally docks onto it, pulls it inside the cell (internalization) and blunts further signaling (desensitization). Small-molecule GLP-1R agonists in this class are engineered to favor cAMP signaling while recruiting less β-arrestin — so-called G-protein-biased or β-arrestin-sparing agonism, a property discussed for orforglipron and tirzepatide. The theory is sustained signaling with potentially less receptor desensitization. Whether aleniglipron SPECIFICALLY is biased has not been published in a peer-reviewed pharmacology paper, so treat 'bias' as a class-level design hypothesis, not an established property of this exact molecule. Class biased-agonism concept: PMID 40806371, PMID 41570980 (C-tail phospho-mutation model of G-protein bias), PMID 39693407 (orforglipron pharmacology), preprint DOI 10.20944/preprints202505.0972.v1 (NOT peer-reviewed). Molecule-specific bias data for aleniglipron: not yet published.

  4. In the pancreas: glucose-dependent insulin release Established

    In pancreatic beta cells, the cAMP rise amplifies insulin secretion — but mainly when blood glucose is already elevated (glucose-dependent). That glucose-dependence is why GLP-1R agonists as monotherapy carry low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk. This is the mechanism behind the modest HbA1c and glucose improvements seen with the class; aleniglipron's own glycemic effects in people with type 2 diabetes are still being formally studied (Phase 2, NCT07400588). Class insulinotropic mechanism: PMID 40191699 (scoping review); PMID 37897727 (cAMP/PKA and β-arrestin in beta cells). Aleniglipron T2DM program: NCT07400588 (ongoing Phase 2).

  5. In the brain: appetite and food intake fall Evidence-human

    GLP-1 receptors in hypothalamic and hindbrain appetite centers, when activated, reduce hunger and food intake and increase satiety. Small molecules in this class are reported to have CNS activity, and the large weight loss seen with aleniglipron is consistent with central appetite suppression being the dominant driver (rather than glucose control) in people without diabetes. The precise central circuitry engaged by aleniglipron specifically has not been mapped in published work — this is inferred from the class and from the observed weight loss. Weight loss in humans: PMID 42249138 (ACCESS, up to -11.3% placebo-adjusted at wk36). Central appetite mechanism (class-level, not aleniglipron-specific): PMID 41683830; PMID 40191699.

  6. In the gut: slowed emptying — the double-edged effect Evidence-human

    GLP-1R activation slows gastric emptying, which prolongs fullness and contributes to reduced intake, but is also the direct cause of the class's signature nausea, vomiting and constipation. In ACCESS these GI events were mostly mild-to-moderate, decreased in frequency over time, and — notably — vomiting rarely recurred when dosing was restarted after a permitted interruption, which the authors read as tolerance developing to the gut effect. PMID 42249138 (GI events mild-moderate, decreased over time, little/no vomiting recurrence on reintroduction). Class GI mechanism: PMID 40191699.

  7. Net result: sustained weight loss with no plateau at 36 weeks Evidence-human

    Adding it up — less appetite, smaller meals, slower emptying, better glucose handling — produces meaningful weight loss. The clinically important observation from ACCESS is that weight was still falling at week 36 (no apparent plateau) at the end of the double-blind period, and continued to fall in the open-label extension interim analysis. That is a promising signal, but 36 weeks is short and durability, cardiovascular and body-composition questions are exactly what the Phase 3 program is designed to answer. PMID 42249138 (no weight-loss plateau at end of double-blind period; continued loss at OLE interim, median 20 weeks treatment).

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Non-peptide small-molecule agonist of the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R), a class B1 GPCR. Unlike peptide agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) that bind the large extracellular orthosteric domain, small-molecule GLP-1R agonists of this generation engage a pocket within the transmembrane bundle (a non-canonical orthosteric site), which is what enables oral bioavailability. Aleniglipron is publicly characterized as belonging to the same orally bioavailable small-molecule class as orforglipron; a peer-reviewed structural/affinity paper specific to aleniglipron has not been published, so exact binding mode and receptor affinity (Ki/EC50) are not established in the literature for this compound.

Signalling cascade. Agonist binding → stabilization of active GLP-1R conformation → coupling to Gs → adenylyl cyclase activation → ↑intracellular cAMP → PKA/EPAC signaling. In beta cells this amplifies glucose-dependent insulin secretion; in CNS appetite centers it reduces food intake; in the GI tract it slows gastric emptying. The class is described as biasing signaling toward the Gs/cAMP arm while limiting β-arrestin recruitment and receptor internalization/desensitization — a property demonstrated for orforglipron/tirzepatide but not published specifically for aleniglipron.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral, once daily (QD). ACCESS used once-daily dosing with 4-week titration steps. A food/water-restriction profile has not been published for aleniglipron the way it has for orforglipron.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally bioavailable small molecule (the defining rationale of the class). Compound-specific oral bioavailability figures for aleniglipron have NOT been published in the peer-reviewed literature; a dedicated Phase 1 comparative-bioavailability/formulation study (capsule vs tablet) was registered and started (NCT06139055), but its status is listed as UNKNOWN on ClinicalTrials.gov and results are not published.
Half-life
Not established in the published literature for aleniglipron; once-daily dosing implies a half-life compatible with QD administration, but the specific value is not peer-reviewed-confirmed.

Not publicly detailed in peer-reviewed sources for aleniglipron; small-molecule GLP-1R agonists are generally hepatically metabolized, which is one reason hepatic safety is monitored closely across the class.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

In ACCESS Phase 2b (PMID 42249138), participants were titrated every 4 weeks to final doses of 45, 90 or 120 mg once daily. A clear dose-response was seen in placebo-adjusted LS-mean weight change at week 36: -8.2% (95% CI -11.1 to -5.3) at the 45 mg arm, -9.8% (-12.5 to -7.2) at 90 mg, and -11.3% (-13.9 to -8.6) at 120 mg, all P<0.0001 vs placebo. Higher doses gave more weight loss, with the expected trade-off of GI tolerability. No apparent weight-loss plateau was seen by week 36, and weight continued to fall at the open-label extension interim analysis (median ~20 weeks of treatment). The dose is being further studied in additional Phase 2 work (ACCESS II, NCT06703021). This describes the published trial design only — it is NOT a dosing protocol or titration schedule for use.

How it compares

  • vs Orforglipron (Eli Lilly)
    same: Both oral once-daily small-molecule GLP-1R agonists engaging the transmembrane pocket; overlapping design rationale (Gs-biased signaling) and broadly similar magnitude of weight loss
    different: Orforglipron is well ahead — full Phase 3 obesity data (e.g., PMID 40960239, 72-week obesity trial) and a T2DM program; aleniglipron is a Phase-2b-stage second entrant with no Phase 3 data yet. Orforglipron has a published ~79% oral bioavailability with no food/water restriction; aleniglipron's PK specifics are not yet peer-reviewed.
  • vs Oral peptide GLP-1 (oral semaglutide)
    same: Oral, once-daily, GLP-1R-mediated weight/glucose effects
    different: Oral semaglutide is a peptide needing an absorption enhancer (SNAC) and strict fasting/water dosing rules with low, variable bioavailability; aleniglipron is a true small molecule that does not require an absorption enhancer
  • vs Danuglipron / lotiglipron (Pfizer small molecules)
    same: Same oral small-molecule GLP-1R class
    different: Danuglipron faced high GI adverse-event rates; lotiglipron showed transaminase (liver-enzyme) elevations in its Phase 2 study and was discontinued — a cautionary backdrop. ACCESS reported no drug-induced liver injury for aleniglipron, but at small scale / short duration
  • vs Injectable peptides (semaglutide, tirzepatide)
    same: Same GLP-1R target and downstream cAMP/appetite biology
    different: Injectables have years of large-scale outcome data (CV, renal, mortality) and larger weight loss (esp. tirzepatide, a dual GIP/GLP-1); aleniglipron offers oral convenience but has no outcome data and unproven long-term efficacy

History & legal status

Aleniglipron originates as GSBR-1290 from Structure Therapeutics (developed via its wholly owned subsidiary Gasherbrum Bio, Inc.), a company built on structure-based design of oral small-molecule agonists for metabolic disease. It entered clinical testing in early 2023 (Phase 1 studies NCT05762471 and NCT05893043 started January and April 2023). It received the INN 'aleniglipron.' The pivotal early efficacy readout is the ACCESS Phase 2b trial, published in Nature Medicine on 5 June 2026 (PMID 42249138) with a publisher correction on 24 June 2026 (PMID 42343118). As of mid-2026 it is NOT approved by any regulator; a Phase 3 chronic-weight-management program (NCT07654361; ACCOMPLISH-2 NCT07654374) is planned but NOT yet recruiting. It is positioned commercially as a fast-follower behind orforglipron in the oral-small-molecule GLP-1 race.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Long-term (>1 year) efficacy and weight-loss durability — ACCESS was only 36 weeks (double-blind) with an ongoing open-label extension; no plateau was seen but the true maintenance level is unknown
  • Cardiovascular, renal and mortality outcomes — no outcome trials exist for aleniglipron; any such benefit is extrapolated from the injectable peptide class
  • Whether aleniglipron is genuinely Gs-biased / β-arrestin-sparing — no molecule-specific signaling-bias data are published; the claim is inherited from the class
  • Hepatic safety at scale — ACCESS saw no DILI, but small-molecule GLP-1 agonists as a class carry a hepatic watch-item (lotiglipron showed transaminase elevations and was discontinued); large Phase 3 exposure is needed
  • Head-to-head efficacy vs orforglipron and vs injectable tirzepatide/semaglutide — no comparative trials
  • Detailed human PK (bioavailability, half-life, metabolism, drug interactions, food effect) — not yet in peer-reviewed publications
  • Efficacy specifically in type 2 diabetes (HbA1c) — still in Phase 2 (NCT07400588)
  • Effect on lean vs fat mass / body composition — being studied (NCT07169942) but unreported
  • Effects in special populations, pregnancy, and long-term muscle/bone consequences of rapid weight loss

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For an unapproved compound the only responsible framing is what a clinician WOULD monitor in a supervised trial, not a use protocol. Trial monitoring in ACCESS and the Phase 3 design tracks: (1) GI tolerability — nausea, vomiting, constipation, and hydration, since these drive most discontinuations; (2) liver function tests (ALT/AST/bilirubin) given the class hepatic watch-item — any transaminase rise warrants attention; (3) weight, waist circumference and BMI trajectory, plus (in the body-composition study) lean-vs-fat mass to watch for excessive muscle loss; (4) glucose/HbA1c and hypoglycemia risk, especially if ever combined with insulin/sulfonylureas; (5) gallbladder symptoms (right-upper-quadrant pain) during rapid weight loss; (6) heart rate, blood pressure and ECG (including QTcF) as standard cardiovascular safety; (7) signs of pancreatitis (severe abdominal pain); and (8) periprocedural aspiration risk from delayed gastric emptying if surgery/anesthesia is planned. None of this constitutes medical advice or a way to 'run' the drug — it describes what supervised research monitoring covers.

Caveats

Aleniglipron is investigational and unapproved — nothing here is a recommendation to obtain or use it, and no dosing guidance is provided. The strongest evidence is a single 230-person, 36-week Phase 2b trial (ACCESS, PMID 42249138) sponsored by the drug's developer; short duration and industry sponsorship both warrant caution, and a publisher correction was issued (PMID 42343118). The 'Gs-biased / β-arrestin-sparing' mechanism is a class-level design hypothesis extrapolated from orforglipron and receptor pharmacology (PMID 40806371, PMID 41570980, PMID 39693407) — molecule-specific biased-signaling data for aleniglipron have NOT been published, so claims that it is 'cleaner' than peptides or than other small molecules are not yet substantiated for this compound. Weight-loss percentages come from a population with high baseline BMI (mean 39.5) and may not generalize. Class-wide risks (gallbladder events, pancreatitis, aspiration under anesthesia due to delayed gastric emptying, and — for other small-molecule candidates like lotiglipron — transaminase elevations that contributed to ending their development, PMID 39415344) have not been excluded for aleniglipron at scale; ACCESS reported no drug-induced liver injury but was small and short. No cardiovascular, renal, or mortality outcome data exist. Do not equate 'no plateau at 36 weeks' with proven long-term efficacy. Fitness/PED/longevity communities sometimes source unapproved research chemicals sold as 'GSBR-1290' — purity, identity and dose of such material are unverifiable and potentially dangerous.
