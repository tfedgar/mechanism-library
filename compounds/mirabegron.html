Mirabegron

The overactive-bladder drug that became the clinical proof that switching on human brown fat can raise daily calorie burn — a real +203 kcal/day, but only at doses that also nudge the heart.

Human / approvedFDA-approved 7 citations
Class
Selective beta-3 adrenergic receptor (β3-AR) agonist (small-molecule, orally active)
Primary target
Beta-3 adrenoceptor (ADRB3), a Gs-coupled GPCR expressed on brown/beige adipocytes (and on bladder detrusor smooth muscle, gallbladder, and white adipocytes)
Human status
Approved and widely marketed — but NOT for fat loss. Mirabegron (Myrbetriq/Betmiga/Betanis, Astellas; ChEMBL CHEMBL2095212) is FDA-approved (2012) and EMA-approved for overactive bladder at 25–50 mg/day; it is oral, first-in-class, carries no black-box warning, and is not withdrawn. Its metabolic/BAT effects are established in humans as a research proof-of-concept, demonstrated in small (n=12–14) mechanistic trials in lean healthy volunteers using supratherapeutic doses (100–200 mg). It has never been approved, and is not in late-stage development, as an obesity or metabolic drug. The single most important honest caveat: at the bladder-approved dose the thermogenic effect is weak/inconsistent, and at the dose that reliably raises metabolic rate the drug also affects heart rate and blood pressure — and even 4 weeks of chronic (open-label) dosing produced measurable metabolic-marker improvements but ZERO weight loss. It is the reference compound that validated the β3→BAT→thermogenesis pathway in people, motivating the search for more selective next-generation β3 (and dual β2/β3) agonists.

At a glance

  • Activates brown adipose tissue thermogenesis in humans (visible on 18F-FDG-PET) — the landmark demonstration that a β3 agonist can do this in people, not just rodents
  • Raises resting metabolic rate by 203 ± 40 kcal/day (+13%) after a single 200 mg dose (Established, acute, lean healthy men)
  • Stimulates lipolysis: acute ~68% rise in circulating non-esterified (free) fatty acids at 200 mg
  • Chronic open-label 100 mg/day (4 wk, n=14 women) improves HDL, ApoA1, adiponectin (+35%), and insulin sensitivity — but produced NO weight or body-composition change
  • Relaxes bladder detrusor smooth muscle — the actual approved therapeutic use (overactive bladder)
  • Relaxes gallbladder (acute ~35% size increase) and alters bile-acid metabolism — an off-target β3 effect (Evidence-human, acute)
  • At the doses needed for metabolic effects (100–200 mg), loses enough β3-selectivity to raise heart rate and blood pressure

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds and activates the β3-adrenoceptor Established

    Mirabegron is a selective agonist at the human β3-adrenoceptor, a Gs-coupled GPCR, mimicking the natural catecholamine (noradrenaline) signal. β3 is the dominant β-receptor subtype on rodent brown fat and is also functionally present on human brown/beige adipocytes, bladder detrusor, gallbladder, and white fat — though in human BAT a β2 component also contributes. Cero 2021, JCI Insight, DOI 10.1172/jci.insight.139160 (PMID 34100382); Cypess 2015, Cell Metab, DOI 10.1016/j.cmet.2014.12.009 (PMID 25565203); ChEMBL CHEMBL2095212 (first-in-class β3 agonist)

  2. Raises cAMP and activates PKA inside the adipocyte Evidence-human

    Gs coupling activates adenylyl cyclase, raising cyclic AMP, which activates protein kinase A (PKA). In cultured human brown/beige adipocytes, mirabegron stimulates β3-dependent signaling, and silencing the β3 receptor abolishes the lipolytic and thermogenic response — showing the signal genuinely runs through β3. (The specific cAMP-loss-on-silencing detail is in the full paper; the abstract confirms lipolysis and thermogenesis are lost.) Cero 2021, JCI Insight, DOI 10.1172/jci.insight.139160 (PMID 34100382) — ex vivo human adipocytes

  3. Triggers lipolysis — fat droplets are broken down to free fatty acids Evidence-human

    PKA phosphorylates lipases (HSL/ATGL machinery), so stored triglycerides are hydrolysed to non-esterified (free) fatty acids. In humans, an acute 200 mg dose raised circulating non-esterified fatty acids ~68%, and silencing β3 in human brown/beige cells abolished mirabegron-driven lipolysis. Baskin 2018, Diabetes, DOI 10.2337/db18-0462 (PMID 29980535); Cero 2021, JCI Insight, DOI 10.1172/jci.insight.139160 (PMID 34100382)

  4. Free fatty acids switch on UCP1, uncoupling the mitochondria Evidence-human

    In brown/beige fat the liberated fatty acids both fuel and help activate uncoupling protein 1 (UCP1). UCP1 short-circuits the mitochondrial proton gradient so energy is released as heat instead of being captured as ATP — non-shivering thermogenesis. β3 silencing in human cells abolishes mirabegron-driven thermogenesis, tying the heat output to this receptor. Cero 2021, JCI Insight, DOI 10.1172/jci.insight.139160 (PMID 34100382) — ex vivo human adipocytes

  5. Brown adipose tissue lights up on imaging Established

    Activated BAT takes up more glucose, so it becomes visible on 18F-FDG PET/CT. All 12 healthy men showed higher BAT metabolic activity after a single 200 mg dose vs placebo, and a dose-ranging study showed BAT uptake rose more-than-dose-proportionally from placebo to 50 mg to 200 mg. Cypess 2015, Cell Metab, DOI 10.1016/j.cmet.2014.12.009 (PMID 25565203); Baskin 2018, Diabetes, DOI 10.2337/db18-0462 (PMID 29980535)

  6. Whole-body resting metabolic rate rises Established

    The extra heat generation shows up as measurably higher calorie burn at rest. A single 200 mg dose raised resting metabolic rate by 203 ± 40 kcal/day (about +13%), and the size of the BAT response predicted the size of the metabolic-rate increase. This is the classic β3/BAT proof-of-concept in humans. The approved bladder dose (25–50 mg) is far lower and does NOT reliably produce this effect — only the 200 mg dose did (50 mg raised neither REE nor NEFA in the dose-ranging study). Cypess 2015, Cell Metab, DOI 10.1016/j.cmet.2014.12.009 (PMID 25565203); Baskin 2018, Diabetes, DOI 10.2337/db18-0462 (PMID 29980535)

  7. With chronic dosing, broader metabolic markers improve — but not body weight Evidence-human

    Over 4 weeks of open-label 100 mg/day in 14 healthy women, BAT activity and resting energy expenditure stayed elevated, and HDL, ApoA1, adiponectin (+35%), and insulin sensitivity/secretion all improved — explicitly WITHOUT any change in body weight or composition. So the metabolic-marker benefits are real, but weight loss was not demonstrated. O'Mara 2020, J Clin Invest, DOI 10.1172/JCI131126 (PMID 31961826), NCT03049462 — open-label, n=14, no placebo arm

  8. At the high doses needed for fat effects, β3-selectivity fades and the heart is affected Established

    The 25–50 mg bladder dose is cardiovascularly quiet, but the 100–200 mg doses used to activate BAT partly spill over onto β1/β2 receptors, raising heart rate and blood pressure. This is the core reason mirabegron itself is a proof-of-concept, not a practical fat-loss drug — the thermogenic and cardiac effects are hard to separate at effective doses. Cypess 2015, Cell Metab, DOI 10.1016/j.cmet.2014.12.009 (PMID 25565203); Weber 2018 (SYNERGY), Blood Press Monit, DOI 10.1097/MBP.0000000000000320 (PMID 29578880) — CV-safety context at bladder doses

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Selective agonist at the human β3-adrenoceptor (ADRB3), a Gs-coupled 7-transmembrane GPCR. Selectivity over β1 and β2 is real but relative and dose-dependent — high concentrations recruit β1/β2. Functional β3 agonism has been directly confirmed in primary human brown/beige adipocytes, where receptor knockdown abolishes the drug's lipolytic and thermogenic responses.

Signalling cascade. β3 → Gs → adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → PKA → (1) phosphorylation of hormone-sensitive lipase/perilipin → lipolysis → free fatty acids; (2) FFA-driven activation of UCP1 → mitochondrial proton-gradient uncoupling → heat (non-shivering thermogenesis). In detrusor smooth muscle the same cAMP/PKA rise causes relaxation (the bladder indication).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (extended-release tablet). Approved bladder dose 25–50 mg once daily; metabolic-research studies used 100–200 mg.
Absorption / bioavailability
Absolute oral bioavailability is dose-dependent and modest (~29% at 25 mg, rising to ~35% at 50 mg); exposure increases more than dose-proportionally. A high-fat meal reduces Cmax/AUC for the tablet. Tmax roughly 3–4 h.
Half-life
Terminal half-life approximately 50 hours, supporting once-daily dosing; steady state reached in ~7 days.
Metabolism
Metabolized by multiple routes including dealkylation, oxidation (CYP3A4, and to a lesser extent CYP2D6), glucuronidation, and amide hydrolysis. Clinically important: mirabegron is a moderate CYP2D6 INHIBITOR, so it can raise plasma levels of CYP2D6 substrates (e.g., metoprolol, desipramine, some antidepressants/antipsychotics).
Excretion
Eliminated via both urine and feces, with substantial renal excretion of parent/metabolites. Dose adjustment/caution advised in severe renal and moderate hepatic impairment.
Notes
The ADMET constants in this section are from the drug's regulatory pharmacology (label/SmPC-level knowledge), NOT re-derived or independently citation-grounded here; the mechanism and clinical-effect claims elsewhere in this monograph are the citation-grounded, database-verified core.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Strongly dose-dependent and the crux of the whole compound. Placebo → 50 mg → 200 mg produced more-than-dose-proportional BAT 18F-FDG uptake; only the 200 mg dose acutely raised resting energy expenditure (~+5.8%) and non-esterified fatty acids (~+68%), while 50 mg (the top bladder dose) did neither. The landmark +203 kcal/day (+13%) RMR increase required 200 mg. Chronic metabolic benefit (HDL, adiponectin, insulin sensitivity) was shown at 100 mg/day. The problem: the same escalation that unlocks thermogenesis also erodes β3-selectivity, so heart-rate/blood-pressure effects grow with the metabolically effective doses.

How it compares

  • vs CL316,243 and other rodent β3 agonists
    same: All are β3-AR agonists exploiting the same cAMP→lipolysis→UCP1 thermogenic axis
    different: Those transformed rodent metabolism but repeatedly FAILED to reproduce the effect in humans, because rodent BAT is β3-dominant while human β3 expression is lower and human BAT signaling has a larger β2 component. Mirabegron is important precisely because it broke that translational deadlock — it actually activated human BAT.
  • vs Cold exposure
    same: Both raise BAT thermogenesis and energy expenditure and share overlapping lipid-mediator biomarkers (Walker 2024, PMID 38198796)
    different: Cold activates BAT via endogenous sympathetic noradrenaline (broad α/β signaling) and is uncomfortable/hard to standardize; mirabegron is an oral, pharmacological BAT stimulus. A human lipidomics study compared the two and found overlapping lipid-mediator (oxylipin) biomarker responses.
  • vs GLP-1 / incretin agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide)
    same: Both are discussed within obesity-pharmacology strategy
    different: Incretins produce large, proven weight loss mainly by reducing intake; mirabegron raises energy expenditure but produced NO weight loss over 4 weeks of open-label dosing. Thermogenesis-first strategies remain unproven for fat loss and are being explored as complements, not replacements.
  • vs Next-generation dual β2/β3 agonists (e.g., preclinical ATR-127, PMID 38796310)
    same: Share the goal of safely increasing energy expenditure via adrenergic thermogenesis
    different: Newer candidates aim to combine skeletal-muscle (β2) and BAT (β3) activation while sparing β1 to avoid the cardiac spillover that limits mirabegron at effective doses. ATR-127 is preclinical (rodent) only.

History & legal status

Developed by Astellas Pharma; first-in-class selective β3-adrenoceptor agonist (ChEMBL confirms first-in-class, first approval 2012, oral, no black-box warning, not withdrawn; USAN stem '-begron'). FDA-approved June 2012 and EMA-approved (as Betmiga) for overactive bladder; brand names Myrbetriq/Betmiga/Betanis. Approved doses are 25–50 mg once daily. Its metabolic story is entirely investigator-initiated/academic (largely NIH/NIDDK, Cypess group): the 2015 Cell Metabolism paper was the first demonstration that a β3 agonist could activate BAT and raise metabolic rate in humans, reviving a field repeatedly defeated by rodent-to-human translation failure. Subsequent NIH studies (Baskin 2018; O'Mara 2020, NCT03049462) extended this to dose-response and chronic dosing. Despite intense interest, mirabegron has never advanced to regulatory approval for obesity/metabolic disease, and there is no black-box warning, though its bladder label carries cardiovascular cautions (blood-pressure increase; avoid in severe uncontrolled hypertension) and CYP2D6-interaction warnings.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether β3-driven BAT thermogenesis can produce meaningful, durable fat loss in humans — the +203 kcal/day acute signal did NOT translate into weight change over 4 weeks
  • Whether the metabolic effect can be achieved at doses that DON'T raise heart rate/blood pressure — i.e., whether β3-selectivity can be preserved at effective exposure
  • Efficacy and safety in the target population (people with obesity/type 2 diabetes) rather than lean healthy volunteers — existing trials are tiny (n=12–14), mostly single-sex, short, and mostly without placebo control for the chronic data
  • Long-term cardiovascular safety at metabolic doses (100–200 mg), and long-term consequences of sustained lipolysis/high free-fatty-acid flux
  • How much of the human thermogenic response is truly β3 vs a β2 contribution in human BAT, and whether that changes with obesity or aging
  • Whether chronic dosing durably beiges white fat in humans or the response adapts/desensitizes over time

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction context, not endorsement of off-label use — mirabegron is approved only for overactive bladder and its metabolic use is unproven. If a clinician is overseeing mirabegron use, sensible things to MONITOR (not a protocol, and with no dosing guidance here) include: blood pressure and resting heart rate, and a firm avoid in severe or uncontrolled hypertension; a baseline and periodic blood-pressure check is the single most important safety item. Watch for palpitations, headache, and signs of urinary retention (particularly with bladder-outlet obstruction or when combined with antimuscarinic drugs). Review the full medication list for CYP2D6 substrates (e.g., metoprolol, some antidepressants/antipsychotics), whose levels mirabegron can raise. Use caution in significant renal or hepatic impairment. There is no validated need for routine BAT imaging or metabolic-rate testing outside research. Red flags warranting stopping and seeking care: chest pain, sustained fast heart rate, markedly elevated blood pressure, severe headache, or inability to urinate.

Caveats

Do not read this as a weight-loss drug. The headline "+203 kcal/day" is real but comes from a single 200 mg dose (4x the top bladder dose) in 12 lean young men, measured acutely — it is a physiology proof-of-concept, not a body-fat outcome. The best chronic human data (O'Mara 2020, open-label 100 mg x 4 weeks, n=14 women, no placebo arm) found improved HDL, adiponectin and insulin sensitivity but explicitly NO change in body weight or composition, so the calorie-burn signal did not translate into fat loss over a month. Effect sizes come from tiny, mostly single-sex, healthy-volunteer studies — not powered for clinical outcomes and not generalizable to people with obesity, whose BAT is typically lower and less responsive. Critically, β3-selectivity is dose-dependent: the metabolically active doses (100–200 mg) partially activate β1/β2 and raise heart rate and blood pressure, which is exactly why mirabegron is a research tool rather than a metabolic therapy. Mirabegron also carries genuine cardiovascular cautions in its bladder labeling (blood-pressure rise; not recommended in severe uncontrolled hypertension) and can raise levels of CYP2D6-metabolized drugs. This monograph is mechanism education and harm-reduction context — not a protocol; using a bladder drug off-label for metabolism is unproven and not risk-free.
