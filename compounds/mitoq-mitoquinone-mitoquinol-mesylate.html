MitoQ

Main target: The inner mitochondrial membrane. Human evidence is still limited.

EvidenceLimited human data
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A ubiquinone (CoQ10-like) antioxidant bolted to a positively charged "molecular missile" that drives it deep inside mitochondria — the best-studied consumer mito-targeted antioxidant, with real (if modest) human trials on blood-vessel function.

Limited human data 7 citations
Class
Mitochondria-targeted antioxidant; triphenylphosphonium (TPP+)-conjugated ubiquinone derivative
Primary target
The inner mitochondrial membrane
Human status
Sold globally as an over-the-counter dietary supplement, NOT an approved drug. As an investigational drug it has reached clinical phase 2 (ChEMBL CHEMBL1229093, max_phase 2). Human RCTs exist and are ongoing: the positive flagship is a small 6-week endothelial-function crossover analysis in 20 healthy older adults with impaired baseline endothelial function using 20 mg/day (Rossman 2018, part of NCT02597023, which is registered as a phase-NA study, total enrollment 55, now completed). A larger, NIA-funded phase 2 vascular-aging trial (NCT04851288; 20 mg/day, n=112) is active but no longer recruiting, with results not yet posted. A 128-patient Parkinson's RCT (PROTECT) was negative for disease progression (Snow 2010). Additional registered trials include human skeletal-muscle/PK studies (e.g., NCT04098510) and an early-phase-1 exercise-plus-MitoQ trial in postmenopausal women (NCT05686967); many registered studies across vascular aging, CKD/HFpEF, COPD, MS fatigue, ulcerative colitis, Long-COVID and others are small, mechanistic, or use surrogate endpoints, and several have been terminated or withdrawn. Bottom line: mechanism is well established; human efficacy evidence is early, largely on surrogate markers, and positive mainly for endothelial function.

At a glance

  • Reduces mitochondrial reactive oxygen species and lipid peroxidation inside cells (established mechanistically)
  • Improves vascular endothelial function (flow-mediated dilation) in healthy older adults with impaired baseline function over 6 weeks (single small human RCT, n=20)
  • Lowers circulating oxidized LDL, a marker of oxidative stress, in older adults
  • Reduces aortic stiffness in the subgroup with elevated baseline stiffness (human, secondary/subgroup finding only)
  • In animals/cells: protects the respiratory chain, cardiolipin and endothelial NO signaling; extends lifespan/healthspan in a C. elegans (worm) Alzheimer model — an invertebrate finding, NOT a mammalian longevity claim
  • Did NOT slow Parkinson's disease progression in a 128-patient RCT — a clear negative for a hard clinical endpoint
  • At high (low-micromolar) concentrations behaves as a respiratory-chain inhibitor independent of antioxidant activity in vitro (off-target; relevant to safety and to its exploratory anticancer signals)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps5 human / established 2 rodent / preclinical

  1. TPP+ cation carries a ubiquinone into mitochondria Established

    MitoQ is CoQ10's active ubiquinone head-group tethered by a 10-carbon chain to triphenylphosphonium (TPP+), a fat-soluble positive ion. Because it is lipophilic and charged, it crosses membranes easily and is pulled toward the mitochondrial matrix by the steep voltage across the inner membrane (negative inside), concentrating several-hundred-fold over the surrounding cytoplasm. This is the whole point: ordinary CoQ10 does not selectively load into mitochondria; the TPP+ 'address label' is what makes MitoQ mitochondria-targeted. Kelso 2001, J Biol Chem, PMID 11092892

  2. The respiratory chain switches it ON to the active antioxidant Established

    Once embedded in the inner-membrane lipid bilayer, the ubiquinone part is reduced by the respiratory chain (notably complex II) to ubiquinol — the chemically active, radical-scavenging form. So MitoQ is essentially a pro-form that the cell's own electron transport chain activates in situ. Kelso 2001, J Biol Chem, PMID 11092892

  3. Ubiquinol neutralizes membrane lipid radicals and is recycled Established

    The ubiquinol form donates electrons to quench lipid peroxyl radicals in the inner membrane, blocking the chain reaction of lipid peroxidation and protecting membrane lipids (including cardiolipin) from oxidative damage. After it is spent, the respiratory chain re-reduces it, so a single molecule can act repeatedly (catalytic recycling) rather than being consumed once. Kelso 2001, J Biol Chem, PMID 11092892

  4. Lower mitochondrial ROS restores nitric-oxide signaling in blood vessels Evidence-rodent

    In aged arteries, excess mitochondrial ROS soaks up and inactivates nitric oxide (NO), the molecule that tells vessels to relax. In old mice, MitoQ cut mitochondria-derived superoxide and oxidative-stress markers in the artery wall and restored NO-dependent widening of vessels (endothelium-dependent dilation) to youthful levels, without changing the response to a pure NO donor — pinpointing the endothelium/NO pathway as the site of action. Gioscia-Ryan 2014, J Physiol, PMID 24665093

  5. In humans, MitoQ improves endothelial function and lowers oxidized LDL Evidence-human

    A 6-week randomized, placebo-controlled crossover trial in 20 healthy older adults with impaired baseline endothelial function found brachial-artery flow-mediated dilation (a standard measure of endothelial function) was ~42% higher on MitoQ than placebo, an improvement tied to reduced mitochondrial-ROS suppression of the endothelium. Plasma oxidized LDL (an oxidative-stress marker) also fell. This is the flagship human result and the reason MitoQ is more than hype — but it is one small study of a surrogate marker, not hard outcomes. Rossman 2018, Hypertension, PMID 29661838

  6. Part of the benefit is systemic, via the 'circulating milieu' (lower oxLDL / LOX-1) Evidence-human

    An ancillary analysis of that trial's plasma showed that blood taken after MitoQ made cultured human endothelial cells produce ~25% more NO and carry ~25% less mitochondrial oxidative stress. Artificially adding oxLDL back to post-MitoQ levels, or blocking the oxLDL receptor LOX-1, erased the benefit — evidence that MitoQ works partly by changing circulating factors (notably reducing oxLDL/LOX-1 signaling), not only by acting inside vessel-wall mitochondria. Murray 2023, Am J Physiol Heart Circ Physiol, PMID 37326998

  7. Important caveat: at higher concentrations MitoQ also inhibits the respiratory chain (an OFF-target, antioxidant-independent effect) Evidence-in vitro

    MitoQ is not a pure antioxidant. A redox-'crippled' version (DM-MitoQ) that cannot scavenge radicals still inhibited cancer-cell growth (IC50 ~0.26 vs ~0.38 uM) and still blocked complex I-dependent oxygen consumption about as potently as normal MitoQ (IC50 ~0.17 vs ~0.52 uM). This shows a chunk of MitoQ's high-concentration biology is the TPP+ scaffold interfering with mitochondrial respiration/bioenergetics — not antioxidant action. It is why 'more is better' is wrong and why supratherapeutic exposure can shift MitoQ from protective toward disruptive. (Shown in cultured cancer cells at low-micromolar concentrations; relevance to whole-body human exposure is not established.) Cheng/Kalyanaraman 2023, Free Radic Biol Med, PMID 37321281

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. MitoQ has no classical single-protein receptor. Its 'target' is a physical/electrochemical compartment: the TPP+ lipophilic cation is driven across the plasma and inner mitochondrial membranes by membrane potentials (Nernstian accumulation), concentrating several-fold per few tens of mV, so the negative-inside inner mitochondrial membrane potential yields several-hundred-fold matrix-side accumulation and adsorption of the ubiquinone to the matrix-facing surface of the inner membrane. The ubiquinone moiety is then reduced by the respiratory chain (notably complex II) to the active ubiquinol antioxidant.

Signalling cascade. Membrane-potential-driven uptake -> reduction to ubiquinol by respiratory chain -> scavenging of lipid peroxyl radicals / interruption of lipid peroxidation -> re-reduction (recycling) by the chain -> net decrease in mitochondrial oxidative damage. Downstream (in vessels): preserved NO bioavailability, reduced oxLDL/LOX-1 signaling, improved endothelium-dependent dilation.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (supplement capsules/softgels); also used IV/experimentally in animals.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally absorbed but with low and variable systemic bioavailability; it is lipophilic and the bulky charged TPP+ moiety complicates absorption. Human plasma MitoQ is measurably higher on treatment vs placebo, confirming systemic exposure at 20 mg/day (Rossman 2018, PMID 29661838), but robust human bioavailability/tissue-distribution PK is not well established and should be treated as uncertain.
Half-life
Not reliably established in humans; the compound partitions into membranes/mitochondria, so plasma half-life underestimates tissue residence.
Metabolism
The ubiquinone/ubiquinol head-group is interconverted (oxidized <-> reduced) by the respiratory chain; the TPP+-alkyl scaffold is metabolically stable. Detailed human metabolic pathways are not well characterized in the public literature.
Excretion
Not well characterized in humans; preclinical work indicates tissue uptake into organs including heart, liver and brain after oral dosing in rodents.
Notes
Human single-dose skeletal-muscle and blood PK has been studied in a small registered trial (NCT04098510, n=10) but robust peer-reviewed human PK is limited; treat all human PK numbers as provisional.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Biphasic/hormetic in principle: at low-to-moderate concentrations MitoQ acts as a recycling antioxidant that reduces mitochondrial ROS; at higher (supratherapeutic) concentrations the TPP+ scaffold inhibits complex I-dependent respiration and can act as a bioenergetic disruptor/pro-oxidant independent of antioxidant chemistry (redox-crippled DM-MitoQ inhibits respiration and cell proliferation with low-micromolar IC50s in cultured cancer cells; Cheng/Kalyanaraman 2023, PMID 37321281). Human efficacy signals came from 20 mg/day; a higher acute exposure was used only as an in-trial mechanistic probe (Rossman 2018). This is a mechanistic explanation of why 'more is better' fails — NOT a dosing recommendation.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Plain CoQ10 / ubiquinol supplements
    same: Same active ubiquinone redox chemistry (electron donor/antioxidant)
    different: CoQ10 is not selectively concentrated in mitochondria and is poorly delivered there; MitoQ's TPP+ 'address label' produces several-hundred-fold mitochondrial accumulation, which is the entire rationale for the molecule (Kelso 2001, PMID 11092892).
  • vs SkQ1 (plastoquinonyl-TPP+, Skulachev ion)
    same:
    different: Also a TPP+-targeted antioxidant but uses a plastoquinone head-group; proponents claim activity at lower concentrations with a wider antioxidant/pro-oxidant window — but far less human data than MitoQ.
  • vs SS-31 / elamipretide (Szeto-Schiller peptide)
    same:
    different: A cardiolipin-binding peptide that targets mitochondria by binding cardiolipin rather than by membrane-potential-driven cation accumulation; stabilizes cristae/respiration rather than acting as a recycling ubiquinol antioxidant. Different mechanism, different (peptide) pharmacology.
  • vs MitoTEMPO / Mito-TEMPOL
    same:
    different: Another TPP+-targeted antioxidant but built on a nitroxide (SOD-mimetic) rather than a ubiquinone; used mainly as a research tool, minimal human data.
  • vs General/untargeted antioxidants (vitamin E, NAC)
    same:
    different: MitoQ concentrates the antioxidant precisely where much pathological ROS is generated (inner mitochondrial membrane), whereas untargeted antioxidants distribute broadly and have largely failed in cardiovascular/neurodegeneration outcome trials.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Invented by Michael Murphy, Robin Smith and colleagues at the University of Otago, New Zealand; the founding chemistry was published in 2001 (Kelso et al., J Biol Chem, PMID 11092892). Developed clinically as mitoquinone/mitoquinol mesylate, with early phase 2 trials in Parkinson's disease (negative, Snow 2010, PMID 20568096) and in hepatitis-C-related liver enzyme elevation. It never obtained drug approval and is now marketed worldwide as an over-the-counter dietary supplement. It remains an active academic research tool and investigational agent (ChEMBL CHEMBL1229093, max clinical phase 2). Not FDA-approved as a drug for any indication.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Whether the improvement in surrogate markers (FMD, oxLDL) translates into hard clinical outcomes (cardiovascular events, cognition, mortality) — untested.
  • Human pharmacokinetics: true oral bioavailability, tissue/mitochondrial delivery, half-life and inter-individual variability are poorly characterized.
  • The safe upper exposure limit and where the antioxidant-to-bioenergetic-inhibitor threshold sits in humans (the 'hormetic' dose-response is largely inferred from cell/animal data).
  • Long-term safety of chronic supplementation, and whether blunting mitochondrial ROS could impair beneficial redox signaling (e.g., exercise adaptation).
  • Reproducibility and generalizability: the positive vascular data come from a single research group and small samples; the larger phase 2 trial (NCT04851288) and others are needed and not yet reported.
  • Safety in pregnancy, and efficacy across the many exploratory indications (CKD/HFpEF, COPD, MS fatigue, ulcerative colitis, Long-COVID, cancer) where trials are small, terminated, withdrawn, or unpublished.
  • Whether the exploratory anticancer/antimetastatic signals reflect a genuinely useful antioxidant-independent OXPHOS-inhibitory action or a liability.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction guidance, NOT a protocol and not medical advice. Because MitoQ is an unapproved supplement with a real off-target (respiratory-chain inhibitory) profile at high exposure: (1) Avoid supratherapeutic 'megadosing' — more is not better and can shift the compound from antioxidant toward bioenergetic disruptor. (2) People with cardiovascular, liver, kidney or oncologic disease, or who are pregnant/breastfeeding, or on prescription medication, should involve a clinician before use, given unknown drug interactions and the absence of outcome data. (3) Watch for and stop with GI intolerance or any new symptoms; report adverse effects. (4) Do not treat MitoQ as a substitute for evidence-based cardiovascular therapy (blood pressure/lipid management, exercise). (5) If used, prefer characterized products and be aware supplement quality is not FDA-guaranteed. (6) Clinicians running or interpreting trials should track endothelial/vascular surrogates alongside safety labs (liver/renal panels) since chronic mitochondrial-targeted exposure lacks long-term human safety data.

Caveats

Evidence honesty: The strongest human data (Rossman 2018) is ONE small crossover RCT (n=20, in older adults specifically selected for impaired baseline endothelial function) measuring a surrogate (flow-mediated dilation), not clinical outcomes like heart attacks, cognition or lifespan — impressive mechanistically but not proof of long-term health benefit. Arterial-stiffness and oxLDL benefits were secondary/subgroup findings. The largest hard-endpoint trial (Parkinson's, n=128) was flatly negative. Human-vs-rodent gap: much of the vascular story rests on old mice (Gioscia-Ryan 2014); the widely cited lifespan extension is in C. elegans (worms), not mammals, and does not support a human longevity claim. Hype-vs-data: 'antioxidant = good' oversimplifies — a redox-crippled analog that cannot scavenge radicals still inhibits the respiratory chain in cultured cells, so at higher (low-micromolar) exposures MitoQ can act as a bioenergetic/OXPHOS inhibitor via its TPP+ scaffold, meaning 'more is better' is false and megadosing is not benign (this is in-vitro evidence; the exposure threshold in whole humans is unknown). Oral bioavailability is low and variable, and tissue delivery in humans is not well characterized. This is not medical advice and contains no dosing protocol; anyone with cardiovascular, hepatic, renal or oncologic disease, or on medication, should involve a clinician, and pregnancy/breastfeeding safety is not established.
