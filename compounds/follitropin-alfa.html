At a glance

  • Stimulates Sertoli cells to support and sustain spermatogenesis (the FSH arm of the gonadotropin toolkit; complements hCG's Leydig/testosterone arm)
  • Increases testicular volume — a marker of seminiferous-tubule/Sertoli-cell mass (roughly a doubling of testicular volume in combination trials)
  • Raises serum inhibin B, a Sertoli-cell product used to track spermatogenic activity
  • In combination with hCG, induces sperm in the ejaculate of most azoospermic HH men, enabling natural or assisted-reproduction pregnancies
  • No direct effect on testosterone or virilization — it is 'clean FSH' with no LH-like activity, so estradiol/androgen changes come indirectly via improved testicular function
  • Restores/expands the immature Sertoli-cell pool when given before or early in puberty (FSH 'priming'), which appears to improve later fertility potential

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps2 human 4 established 1 rodent / preclinical

  1. Recombinant FSH reaches the testis Established

    Follitropin alfa is an injected copy of natural human FSH — the same two-chain glycoprotein (alpha + beta subunits) made in CHO cells. Given under the skin, it circulates and reaches the testis, replacing the FSH a shut-down or deficient pituitary isn't making. PMID 27697201

  2. Binds the FSH receptor on Sertoli cells — and only there Established

    FSH binds the FSH receptor (FSHR), a G-protein-coupled receptor found in the testis almost exclusively on Sertoli cells. It does not meaningfully activate the LH/hCG receptor, so it acts as a 'clean' FSH signal with no built-in testosterone-driving activity. PMID 26236283

  3. Switches on cAMP / PKA signaling inside the Sertoli cell Established

    An activated FSHR couples to Gs protein, raising intracellular cAMP and activating protein kinase A (plus additional pathways). This is the core molecular switch that turns FSH binding into changed Sertoli-cell gene expression and function. PMID 30619090

  4. Sertoli cells mature, proliferate, and ramp up support functions Evidence-human

    FSH signaling drives immature Sertoli cells to proliferate (mainly before/around puberty) and mature Sertoli cells to produce the growth factors, transport proteins, aromatase and nutritional support that germ cells depend on. In gonadotropin-deficient boys, r-hFSH alone roughly doubled testicular volume and tripled inhibin B — a direct fingerprint of Sertoli-cell expansion. PMID 17218732

  5. Supported germ cells progress through spermatogenesis Evidence-rodent

    With the Sertoli-cell 'nurse' environment restored, spermatogonia proliferate and germ cells advance toward mature sperm. Rodent and in-vitro work confirm FSH (with testosterone) regulates Sertoli-cell support of germ-cell development, but FSH is a supporting, not sufficient, signal. PMID 40141344

  6. FSH cooperates with LH/hCG-driven testosterone — it does not replace it Established

    Full spermatogenesis needs high INTRA-testicular testosterone, which comes from LH (or hCG) acting on Leydig cells. In knockout mice, FSH-receptor-KO males stay fertile while LH-receptor-KO males are infertile — evidence that FSH optimizes and boosts output but the testosterone/LH arm is the non-negotiable driver. This is exactly why FSH is 'added to hCG,' not used alone, when sperm counts stall. (The KO evidence is rodent; the clinical corollary — FSH added to hCG — is established in human HH.) PMID 28100869

  7. Measurable spermatogenesis and pregnancies result — over months, not weeks Evidence-human

    In HH men, hCG+FSH induces sperm in the ejaculate in about three-quarters of patients and pregnancy in roughly half of couples, but it is slow: mean ~12 months to first sperm and ~19 months to pregnancy, and final sperm concentrations usually stay below the normal range. FSH improves the odds and the counts on top of hCG; it is not a fast switch. PMID 41987691

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Follitropin alfa is recombinant human FSH: a heterodimer of a non-covalently associated alpha-subunit (shared with LH, hCG, TSH) and an FSH-specific beta-subunit that confers receptor selectivity. It is a full agonist of the FSH receptor (FSHR), a class-A (rhodopsin-family) G-protein-coupled receptor with a large leucine-rich-repeat extracellular hormone-binding domain. In the testis FSHR is expressed essentially only on Sertoli cells; follitropin alfa has negligible affinity for the LH/hCG receptor (LHCGR), which is why it is 'clean FSH' with no direct Leydig/testosterone or virilizing action.

Signalling cascade. Hormone binding to the FSHR ectodomain triggers a conformational change transmitted through the transmembrane domain, coupling the receptor to Gs. This raises intracellular cAMP and activates protein kinase A (PKA), the canonical pathway; FSHR also engages additional/biased signaling (e.g., beta-arrestin, ERK, and other pathways in some contexts). In Sertoli cells this reprograms gene expression toward proliferation (pre-/peripubertally), maturation, aromatase (estradiol synthesis from testosterone), inhibin B secretion, and production of germ-cell support factors — together enabling the seminiferous epithelium to sustain spermatogenesis. Glycosylation of the recombinant hormone strongly influences half-life, receptor interaction, and which signaling arms are favored.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection (also historically intramuscular); it is a protein and is not orally bioavailable.
Absorption / bioavailability
Absorption is the rate-limiting step (slow, first-order). Absolute subcutaneous bioavailability of follitropin alfa is roughly 60-65% (reported 60-65% in a human head-to-head PK study).
Half-life
Terminal elimination half-life on the order of ~24 hours after repeated subcutaneous dosing (reported ~24 h for follitropin alfa vs ~30 h for a human-cell-line rFSH). This relatively short half-life is why r-hFSH is dosed several times weekly rather than as a single depot.
Metabolism
As a glycoprotein it is not metabolized by CYP enzymes; it is cleared by receptor-mediated uptake and by hepatic/renal handling of glycoproteins, with terminal sialic-acid content of the glycans a major determinant of circulating half-life.
Excretion
Cleared largely via metabolism/receptor uptake with a minor renal component; in the cited human PK study, follitropin alfa clearance was ~0.44 L/h after IV dosing and apparent clearance (CL/F) ~0.99 L/h after repeated subcutaneous dosing. Note most quantitative PK data derive from women (IVF/ovulation-induction studies).
Notes
Follitropin alfa is expressed in CHO (non-human) cells; the glycan pattern differs from urinary FSH and from human-cell-line rFSH, which measurably changes PK/PD — e.g., a human-cell-line rFSH (FE 999049) showed lower clearance, higher exposure and longer half-life than follitropin alfa at equal Steelman-Pohley IU. Corifollitropin alfa is a separate long-acting FSH analog (fused hCG beta C-terminal peptide) dosed every other week, not the same molecule as follitropin alfa.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Because FSHR occupancy drives a graded Sertoli-cell response, exposure (not a single threshold) governs the effect. Clinically, dosing is titrated to biological markers rather than a fixed target: testicular volume, and especially serum inhibin B, rise with adequate FSH exposure and are used to gauge Sertoli-cell response. Response is slow and ceilinged — even with adequate FSH added to hCG, mean time to first sperm is ~12 months and final sperm concentrations commonly remain below normal (~9.8 million/mL pooled), so 'more/longer' improves the odds but does not reliably normalize counts. NO dosing regimen is provided here; titration must be clinician-directed with biomarker monitoring. (Response magnitude also depends on baseline testicular tissue, prior puberty, and the concurrent hCG/testosterone arm.)

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs hCG / recombinant LH (the other gonadotropin arm)
    same: Both are glycoprotein gonadotropins used to restore male fertility in HH.
    different: hCG/LH act on the LH/hCG receptor on Leydig cells to drive intratesticular testosterone (the essential driver); follitropin alfa acts on FSHR on Sertoli cells to support/boost spermatogenesis. FSH does not raise testosterone; hCG does not directly do the Sertoli-cell job. They are complementary — hCG first, FSH added when counts stall.
  • vs Urinary/highly-purified FSH (e.g., menotropins/hMG-derived FSH)
    same: Same receptor target, comparable efficacy at inducing spermatogenesis in HH.
    different: Follitropin alfa is recombinant (CHO cells): unlimited supply, no urinary-derived contaminants, more consistent batch-to-batch, but different glycosylation and higher cost. hMG additionally carries LH activity; pure urinary FSH does not.
  • vs Follitropin beta (Puregon/Follistim)
    same: Both are recombinant human FSH made in CHO cells, FSHR agonists, therapeutically near-equivalent.
    different: Different manufacturers/cell clones and glycoform profiles; historically follitropin alfa is standardized by mass (fill-by-mass) while some formulations were IU-based. Clinically interchangeable in most settings.
  • vs Corifollitropin alfa (Elonva)
    same: Both deliver FSH-receptor agonism.
    different: Corifollitropin alfa is a long-acting FSH analog (FSH beta fused to the hCG beta C-terminal peptide) with a much longer half-life, dosed roughly every other week vs several times weekly for follitropin alfa. Different molecule, not a generic of follitropin alfa.
  • vs Clomiphene / enclomiphene / aromatase inhibitors (endogenous-axis stimulators)
    same: All aim to improve male fertility/testicular function.
    different: SERMs/AIs work upstream by increasing the body's OWN LH and FSH via the hypothalamic-pituitary axis — useless if the pituitary can't respond (HH). Follitropin alfa replaces FSH directly downstream, so it works even when the axis is deficient.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Follitropin alfa (Gonal-F/GONAL-f, originally Serono, now Merck KGaA/EMD Serono) was one of the first recombinant human gonadotropins, developed to replace urinary-derived FSH (extracted from postmenopausal urine). It is produced in genetically engineered Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells and reached the market in the mid-1990s. It is standardized by protein mass (fill-by-mass) rather than bioassay IU in modern formulations. Approved indications center on female ovarian stimulation (anovulation, ART/IVF) and, in men, stimulation of spermatogenesis in hypogonadotropic hypogonadism in combination with hCG. Its close analog follitropin beta (Puregon/Follistim) is also a marketed recombinant FSH. Use in anabolic-steroid 'restarts' is off-label and not part of any regulatory indication.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Efficacy and safety in the fitness/PED 'restart' scenario (recovering a previously normal axis after anabolic-steroid suppression) are essentially unstudied — all human data are in congenital/acquired HH or female ART, populations with fundamentally different testicular starting points.
  • Optimal timing, duration and biomarker targets for adding FSH to hCG are not standardized; the meta-analysis flagged most underlying studies as low methodological quality.
  • Why final sperm concentrations usually remain subnormal (~10 million/mL) even with successful induction — how much reflects irreversible tissue limits vs suboptimal/short treatment — is unresolved.
  • The clinical impact of FSHR genetic variants and of glycoform differences (CHO-derived vs human-cell-line rFSH; biased signaling) on individual male response is not well characterized.
  • Long-term safety of repeated exogenous FSH in men (beyond fertility windows) and any effect on testicular tumor risk are not established.
  • Head-to-head data specifically in men comparing follitropin alfa vs follitropin beta vs long-acting corifollitropin alfa for spermatogenesis are limited.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction and response monitoring (clinician-directed; not a protocol): track serum inhibin B and testicular volume as the primary markers of Sertoli-cell response and spermatogenic activity — a rise indicates the FSH is working. Follow semen analyses over time (expect months, not weeks, before sperm appear; counts often stay subnormal). Because r-hFSH is given alongside hCG, monitor total testosterone and estradiol and watch for gynecomastia/breast tenderness (largely from the hCG/aromatization arm), and consider whether estradiol control is needed. Watch for injection-site reactions and, rarely, signs of hypersensitivity. Immunogenicity (anti-FSH antibodies) is low-risk but consider it if response unexpectedly plateaus or fails. In women (the drug's main indication) the critical monitor is for ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome and multiple pregnancy via follicle counts and estradiol — not applicable to men but part of the drug's overall safety picture. Anyone considering this should do so under endocrinology/andrology supervision, not self-directed; this entry provides no dosing guidance.

Caveats

FSH is a booster, not the engine: high intratesticular testosterone (from hCG/LH acting on Leydig cells) is the non-negotiable driver of spermatogenesis, so follitropin alfa is used added to hCG, never as a standalone restart. The entire human evidence base is fertility induction in hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (congenital/acquired GnRH-gonadotropin deficiency) and female ovarian stimulation — NOT anabolic-steroid users trying to recover a previously normal axis, where r-hFSH is off-label and essentially unstudied for this purpose. It is slow and imperfect: mean ~12 months to first sperm, ~19 months to pregnancy, and post-treatment sperm concentrations frequently stay below normal thresholds. It is a large glycoprotein given by subcutaneous injection (not oral), is expensive, and — like any exogenous protein — carries a low but real risk of anti-drug antibodies. This monograph describes mechanism and what to monitor; it is not a protocol and gives no dosing.
