Survodutide

A once-weekly injection that hits two hunger-and-metabolism receptors at once (glucagon + GLP-1) — built to melt liver fat and reverse liver scarring, with strong but still early-stage human data.

Human / approved 22 citations
Class
Dual glucagon receptor (GCGR) / GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) agonist — a peptide "twincretin," administered subcutaneously once weekly
Primary target
Two G-protein-coupled receptors simultaneously: the GLP-1 receptor (gut/brain — appetite, insulin, satiety) and the glucagon receptor (liver — fat burning and energy expenditure)
Human status
INVESTIGATIONAL — not approved anywhere (as of mid-2026). Human evidence is genuinely strong for an experimental agent but still phase 2 for the liver indication: the pivotal data is a single 320-patient phase 2 biopsy-confirmed MASH RCT (Sanyal et al., NEJM 2024, PMID 38847460) plus a phase 2 obesity dose-finding trial (le Roux et al., Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2024, PMID 38330987). Confirmatory phase 3 MASH trials are RECRUITING/ongoing (LIVERAGE NCT06632444 and LIVERAGE-Cirrhosis NCT06632457), and a large phase 3 obesity program (SYNCHRONIZE-1/-2/-MASLD/-CVOT/-JP) is underway with the first phase 3 obesity and SYNCHRONIZE-MASLD readouts published 2026 (PMIDs 42253238, 42252333). Direct mechanistic claims about HOW it works in the liver and brain rest heavily on RODENT studies (PMIDs 41638399, 41388343) — the receptor pharmacology is established, but the tissue-level mechanism in humans is inferred, not directly demonstrated.

At a glance

  • MASH resolution without worsening fibrosis in up to ~83% of patients at higher doses (phase 2, biopsy-confirmed) vs ~18% placebo
  • Meaningful liver fibrosis improvement in a substantial subgroup — fibrosis regression, not just fat reduction
  • ~15% body-weight loss at top dose over 46 weeks in the phase 2 obesity trial
  • Improved glycemic control, insulin sensitivity and beta-cell function markers in type 2 diabetes / overweight
  • Dose-dependent GI adverse effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) requiring slow titration — the main tolerability limiter
  • Glucagon-arm cardiometabolic effects: small increases in heart rate; energy-expenditure and lipid effects under study

Mechanism, step by step

  1. One molecule, two receptors Established

    Survodutide is an engineered peptide that activates BOTH the GLP-1 receptor and the glucagon receptor. GLP-1 drives appetite suppression, slowed stomach emptying and better insulin response; glucagon adds direct liver and energy-expenditure effects. Combining them aims to beat single-target GLP-1 drugs on liver disease specifically. Zafer et al., Aliment Pharmacol Ther 2025, PMID 40364529 (mechanism review); Targher et al., Gut 2025, PMID 39592207

  2. GLP-1 arm: brain appetite circuits dim down Evidence-rodent

    The GLP-1 side acts on the brain to reduce hunger and food intake. Rodent imaging shows survodutide reaches appetite-regulating brain regions via the circumventricular organs (brain areas with a leaky blood-brain barrier) and switches on neurons tied to appetite control — driving the weight loss. Zimmermann et al., Mol Metab 2026, PMID 41638399

  3. Glucagon arm: the liver burns its own fat Evidence-rodent

    Activating the glucagon receptor on liver cells ramps up fat breakdown (lipolysis/fat oxidation) and energy expenditure inside the liver itself. In mice, REMOVING the liver glucagon receptor abolished the extra weight loss and metabolic benefit of a survodutide-like molecule — direct evidence the liver glucagon receptor is doing real work, not just riding along with appetite loss. Long et al., Diabetes Obes Metab 2026, PMID 41388343

  4. Less liver fat, less inflammation Evidence-human

    By cutting calorie intake (GLP-1) and pushing the liver to oxidize fat (glucagon), survodutide reduces the fat stored in liver cells (steatosis) that drives MASH. Falling liver fat lowers the lipotoxic stress and inflammation that damage liver tissue. (Phase-2 primary endpoint: MASH improvement WITHOUT worsening fibrosis.) Sanyal et al., NEJM 2024, PMID 38847460 (phase 2 MASH RCT)

  5. MASH resolves and fibrosis can regress Evidence-human

    In the pivotal 320-person phase 2 biopsy-confirmed MASH trial, survodutide resolved MASH (without worsening scarring) in up to ~83% of patients vs ~18% on placebo, and improved liver fibrosis in a meaningful share. This is the headline human result — measured on actual liver biopsies, not just blood markers. Sanyal et al., NEJM 2024, PMID 38847460; corroborated in network meta-analyses Souza et al., Hepatology 2025, PMID 39903735 and Andonie et al., Endocrinol Diabetes Metab 2026, PMID 41466530

  6. Substantial weight loss alongside Evidence-human

    In the phase 2 obesity dose-finding trial, survodutide produced roughly 15% body-weight loss at the top dose over 46 weeks. Much of the liver benefit travels with this weight loss — network meta-analyses suggest weight loss mediates a large part of the histological improvement, though the glucagon arm appears to add liver-specific effects beyond weight alone. le Roux et al., Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2024, PMID 38330987; Banerjee et al., Diabetes Obes Metab 2026, PMID 41063381 (weight-loss mediation NMA)

  7. The catch: dose-escalation and GI side effects Evidence-human

    The glucagon component and rapid up-titration drive nausea, vomiting and diarrhea — common enough that the trials used slow dose escalation. Glucagon can also nudge heart rate and glucose up, so tolerability and titration are central open questions, not footnotes. Sanyal et al., NEJM 2024, PMID 38847460; le Roux et al., Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2024, PMID 38330987

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Survodutide (BI 456906) is a synthetic, acylated 29-/30-amino-acid peptide derived from the gut hormone oxyntomodulin, engineered as a BALANCED dual agonist of two class-B G-protein-coupled receptors: the glucagon receptor (GCGR) and the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R). [Established; PMID 36356832] In the discovery paper (Zimmermann et al., Mol Metab 2022, PMID 36356832), BI 456906 is reported as a POTENT functional agonist at both human receptors in cell-based cAMP assays, with near-equipotent (balanced) activity at GCGR and GLP-1R — distinguishing it from GLP-1-dominant agents. NOTE: the published abstract characterizes it qualitatively as 'potent' and balanced; it does NOT list a single canonical Ki/EC50 pair in the abstract text retrieved this run, so exact molar EC50 values should be cited from the paper's results tables/figures rather than stated here as fact [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 36356832]. It is a full/strong agonist (not antagonist) at both targets. The C18 fatty-acid (palmitate-type) acylation drives albumin binding for half-life extension, NOT receptor binding. ChEMBL drug_search lookup failed this run (tool internal error), so no ChEMBL-curated affinity record was retrieved.

Signalling cascade. Agonist binding to GLP-1R and GCGR (both Gs-coupled GPCRs) activates adenylate cyclase, raising intracellular cAMP and activating PKA. (1) GLP-1R arm: cAMP/PKA signalling in pancreatic beta cells potentiates glucose-dependent insulin secretion and improves beta-cell function/insulin sensitivity [Evidence-human; PMID 42331726]; GLP-1R activation on hindbrain/hypothalamic neurons suppresses appetite. Mechanistically, survodutide acts CENTRALLY via the circumventricular organs (area postrema, arcuate nucleus) — a fluorophore-labelled survodutide accessed CVOs and adjacent nuclei WITHOUT uniformly crossing the blood-brain barrier, and induced c-Fos in satiety nuclei; this appetite/food-intake suppression is GLP-1R-dependent (a GCGR-only agonist did not activate satiety nuclei or cut food intake but still lowered weight) [Evidence-rodent; PMID 41638399]. (2) GCGR arm: hepatic GCGR cAMP signalling increases energy expenditure and drives hepatic lipid oxidation/reduced steatosis; in mice, hepatic GCGR is REQUIRED for the superior weight-loss and metabolic effects of a survodutide analogue [Evidence-rodent; PMID 41388343]. Target engagement biomarkers for the GCGR arm in vivo include raised FGF21, altered circulating amino acids, and hepatic NNMT mRNA [Evidence-rodent; PMID 36356832]. The net dual effect: reduced energy intake (GLP-1R) PLUS increased energy expenditure and direct hepatic fat burning (GCGR), giving weight loss that exceeded maximally-effective semaglutide in mice.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection, once weekly. [Established; PMID 36356832, 38330987]
Absorption / bioavailability
Absorbed from SC depot; designed for once-weekly dosing via fatty-acid acylation. Quantitative absolute bioavailability not stated in retrieved abstracts. Requires a multi-week DOSE-ESCALATION (titration) period to limit GI effects (e.g., 20-week escalation in the phase 2 obesity trial; ~24-week rapid escalation in the MASH trial) [Established; PMID 38330987, 38847460].
Half-life
Engineered for once-weekly dosing via a C18 fatty-acid acylation that promotes albumin binding to extend half-life [Established; PMID 36356832]. Exact terminal t1/2 in hours/days not given in retrieved abstracts.
Metabolism
As an acylated peptide, expected to undergo proteolytic/peptide catabolism to amino acids and small fragments rather than CYP450 metabolism (class-typical for acylated GLP-1-based peptides); the retrieved abstracts do not name specific enzymes — treat enzyme identity as not-established-from-this-run [Speculative/class-inference].
Excretion
Not specified in retrieved abstracts; peptide degradation products expected to be handled renally/via normal amino-acid turnover [Speculative/class-inference].
Notes
Acylated peptide with a C18 fatty acid as the half-life-extending principle (albumin binding), NOT an ester prodrug [Established; PMID 36356832]. Does NOT uniformly cross the blood-brain barrier; reaches appetite centres via circumventricular organs that lie outside the BBB [Evidence-rodent; PMID 41638399]. Dedicated hepatic-impairment PK trial: in people with Child-Pugh A/B/C cirrhosis, single-dose AUC and Cmax were SIMILAR to matched non-cirrhotic individuals (90% CIs of geometric mean ratios spanned 1), so NO PK-related dose adjustment is required for cirrhosis [Evidence-human; PMID 38857788].

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

HUMAN (well characterized for weight/glycemia/liver fat; doses are total weekly SC after titration). OBESITY without diabetes, phase 2 (46 wk, n=387): dose-dependent weight loss of -6.2% (0.6 mg), -12.5% (2.4 mg), -13.2% (3.6 mg), -14.9% (4.8 mg) vs -2.8% placebo [Evidence-human; PMID 38330987]. OBESITY phase 3 SYNCHRONIZE-1 (76 wk, n=725, no diabetes): -12.2% (3.6 mg) and -13.0% (6.0 mg) vs -5.4% placebo (treatment-regimen estimand); >=5% weight loss in ~72-73% vs 46% placebo [Evidence-human; PMID 42253238]. MASH phase 2 (48 wk, n=293): MASH improvement without worsening fibrosis in 47% (2.4 mg), 62% (4.8 mg), 43% (6.0 mg) vs 14% placebo — note a QUADRATIC (non-monotonic, inverted-U) dose-response where 4.8 mg outperformed 6.0 mg, plausibly because higher doses worsened tolerability/dropout [Evidence-human; PMID 38847460]. SYNCHRONIZE-MASLD phase 3 (48 wk, 6.0 mg, n=216): >=30% liver-fat reduction in 84.2% vs 24.3% placebo; weight -12.2% vs -1.0% [Evidence-human; PMID 42252333]. T2D dose-response (Diabetologia 2024): survodutide cut HbA1c and bodyweight dose-dependently vs placebo with open-label semaglutide comparator [Evidence-human; PMID 38095657]. Meta-analysis (6 RCTs, n=1272): weight -6.7 kg, HbA1c -0.66%, fasting glucagon -7 pmol/L vs placebo, with larger effects at >2.4 mg/week and >16 weeks [Evidence-human; PMID 40922121]. RODENT: survodutide produced GREATER weight loss than maximally-effective semaglutide in mice, via combined increased energy expenditure + reduced intake [Evidence-rodent; PMID 36356832]. GAP: there is no clean human dose-response separating the GCGR-driven energy-expenditure/liver-fat component from the GLP-1R appetite component at each dose; the inverted-U in MASH means 'more is better' does NOT hold for histology.

How it compares

  • vs Semaglutide (pure GLP-1R agonist)
    same: Both incretin-based, once-weekly SC, acylated peptides driving GLP-1R appetite suppression and weight loss; share GI AE profile and titration requirement
    different: Survodutide ADDS glucagon-receptor agonism (energy expenditure + direct hepatic fat burning), so it lost more weight than maximally-effective semaglutide in mice; the GCGR arm gives stronger liver-fat/MASH rationale but adds a counter-regulatory hyperglycemic component the GLP-1R arm must offset
  • vs Tirzepatide (GIP/GLP-1R dual agonist)
    same: Both are once-weekly SC dual-incretin-class agonists for obesity/metabolic disease with potent weight loss and GI tolerability concerns
    different: Tirzepatide's second target is GIP (adipose/insulin axis); survodutide's is GCGR (hepatic energy expenditure/fat oxidation). Survodutide's distinct hepatic mechanism underlies its MASH-fibrosis program, whereas the GIP arm is not a direct liver-fat-burning mechanism
  • vs Retatrutide (GIP/GLP-1/glucagon TRIPLE agonist)
    same: Both engage the glucagon receptor for energy expenditure plus GLP-1R for appetite; overlapping obesity/MASLD positioning and inverted-U/tolerability considerations at high dose
    different: Retatrutide adds a third (GIP) arm; survodutide is a cleaner two-receptor probe of the GCGR+GLP-1R combination specifically
  • vs Pemvidutide (GLP-1/glucagon dual agonist)
    same: Same GCGR+GLP-1R dual-agonist class targeting MASLD/MASH and weight, both reduce liver-fat content and ALT
    different: Different peptide/selectivity ratio and dosing; head-to-head data are absent — comparisons rest on separate trials (e.g., pemvidutide MASLD data, PMID 39002641) not direct comparison

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Amycretin shares target Established
    Survodutide is a GLP-1R+GCGR dual agonist and amycretin is a GLP-1R+amylin agonist, so they share the GLP-1 receptor arm: appetite suppression, slowed gastric emptying and on-target GI side effects are additive/redundant. Both are also reported to lower liver fat, but note this convergence is human/biopsy-confirmed for survodutide (via the glucagon arm) versus rodent-only for amycretin, so the shared GLP-1R/GI overlap is the load-bearing interaction. amycretin.json (GLP-1R agonist; reduced liver fat/MASLD in rodents only); survodutide.json (GLP-1R+GCGR; MASH/liver-fat reduction in humans) Watch: Watch for compounded nausea/vomiting and dehydration; track liver enzymes/steatosis markers and fasting glucose. No new receptor arm is added on the GLP-1 side.

History & legal status

"Origin: Discovered and developed by Boehringer Ingelheim (originally co-developed with Zealand Pharma's peptide platform); first disclosed pharmacologically as BI 456906, an oxyntomodulin-derived balanced GCGR/GLP-1R dual agonist (Zimmermann et al., Mol Metab 2022, PMID 36356832). INN: survodutide. Development program: phase 2 dose-finding in obesity (2024, PMID 38330987) and T2D (2024, PMID 38095657); landmark phase 2 MASH/fibrosis trial (NEJM 2024, PMID 38847460); now a broad phase 3 'SYNCHRONIZE' program — SYNCHRONIZE-1 obesity (NEJM 2026, PMID 42253238), SYNCHRONIZE-2 (obesity + T2D, PMID 41216778), SYNCHRONIZE-MASLD (Nat Med 2026, PMID 42252333), SYNCHRONIZE-JP (Japan, PMID 42219222), and SYNCHRONIZE-CVOT cardiovascular outcomes trial (PMID 39453356, 41329105). Regulatory status as of mid-2026: INVESTIGATIONAL — survodutide is NOT yet approved by FDA, EMA, or other major regulators for any indication; it remains an investigational drug across all retrieved sources. It received FDA Breakthrough Therapy designation for MASH with fibrosis (widely reported; not independently confirmed in this run's PubMed abstracts). Legal status: prescription-only investigational agent; not a controlled substance. Not approved means any non-trial use is off-label/unapproved."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Cardiovascular outcomes: the dedicated SYNCHRONIZE-CVOT event-driven trial is ongoing; net CV benefit/risk (including the GLP-1-class heart-rate increase and the glucagon arm's hemodynamic effects) is not yet established [PMID 39453356, 41329105].
  • Why the dose-response for MASH histology is non-monotonic (inverted-U, 4.8 mg > 6.0 mg): is it purely tolerability/dropout, or a genuine receptor-balance ceiling where excess GCGR signalling becomes counterproductive? [PMID 38847460].
  • Long-term durability and weight regain after stopping: all pivotal data are <=76 weeks; maintenance of weight, liver-fat and fibrosis benefit beyond ~1.5 years is unknown.
  • Exact human receptor selectivity/affinity ratio (GCGR:GLP-1R) and how it maps to the clinical efficacy/tolerability balance — the published balance is qualitative; precise human EC50/Ki values and the optimal ratio remain debated [PMID 38518602, 36356832].
  • Whether fibrosis REGRESSION (not just MASH resolution) is durable and clinically meaningful for hard liver outcomes (cirrhosis, decompensation) — phase 2 fibrosis improvement was modest (~34-36%) and biopsy endpoints need phase 3 confirmation.
  • Body-composition effects: degree of lean-mass loss vs fat loss with survodutide specifically, and whether the GCGR energy-expenditure arm changes this versus pure GLP-1R agonists, is not characterized.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT dosing guidance): (1) GI tolerability — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea are near-universal and concentrate during dose-escalation; watch for dehydration and inability to maintain intake, which is the main reason people discontinue [PMID 38847460, 42253238]. (2) Glycemia — because of the glucagon arm + GLP-1 arm interplay, monitor HbA1c/glucose; in people on insulin or sulfonylureas, hypoglycemia risk from co-therapy is the concern, while the GCGR arm is a theoretical hyperglycemic counterweight (net effect was glucose-lowering) [PMID 38095657, 42331726]. (3) Heart rate — track resting HR given the GLP-1 class chronotropic effect (formal CV safety pending) [PMID 39453356]. (4) Liver markers — ALT/AST and non-invasive fibrosis/imaging (MRI-PDFF, liver stiffness) are the efficacy AND safety signals being followed in MASH/MASLD use [PMID 42252333, 38857788]. (5) Hydration/renal function during periods of heavy vomiting or diarrhea (volume depletion can impair kidneys). (6) Nutritional adequacy and lean mass with rapid weight loss — monitor protein intake and resistance-training status. (7) Injection-site reactions. Note: cirrhosis does NOT require PK-based dose change, but advanced liver disease still warrants closer clinical follow-up [PMID 38857788]. This is monitoring information only and is not a recommendation to use survodutide, which remains investigational."

Caveats

FOR A FITNESS/PEPTIDE AUDIENCE — read this part carefully. (1) Survodutide is NOT approved and NOT available as a legitimate prescription product; anything sold as "survodutide" in the peptide/research-chemical gray market is unverified, unregulated, and of unknown identity, purity and dose — that is a real safety hazard, not a technicality. (2) The headline "83% MASH resolution" comes from ONE phase 2 trial of ~320 people; impressive, but phase 2 results often shrink in phase 3, and the confirmatory liver trials have not reported yet — do not present it as proven. (3) The biggest mechanism claims (brain appetite circuits, liver glucagon-receptor dependence) are from MICE — label them as rodent evidence on camera, not human fact. (4) GI side effects are common and dose-related; the slow titration in trials exists precisely because rapid dosing is poorly tolerated. (5) Glucagon agonism can raise heart rate and glucose — the cardiovascular outcomes trial is ongoing for a reason. (6) This is published-evidence synthesis, not medical advice.
