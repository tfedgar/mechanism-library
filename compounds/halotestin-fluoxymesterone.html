Library / Anabolic-androgenic steroids

Halotestin

Main target: Androgen receptor. Backed by human data.

Works in humans?Human evidence
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A non-aromatizing, 17-alpha-methylated oral androgen that cranks strength and aggression without adding water-weight or size, while punishing the liver and HDL cholesterol harder than almost any other steroid. It is an androgen-receptor agonist that binds the receptor only weakly, so its effects come from being a pure, dry, potent androgenic signal rather than a muscle-building bulker.

Strong human data 35 citations
Class
17-alpha-alkylated (C17alpha-methyl) oral anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS); a fluorinated, 11-beta-hydroxylated derivative of methyltestosterone
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR; NR3C4) - acts as a direct agonist (ChEMBL CHEMBL1445 -> target CHEMBL1871)
Human status
FDA-approved drug since 1956 (trade names Halotestin, Android-F, Androxy; ChEMBL max_phase 4, oral, not withdrawn). Legitimate human use is now largely historical/niche (hypogonadism, delayed puberty, and second-/third-line androgen therapy in ER-positive metastatic breast cancer, where it was studied in randomized trials). It has no approved athletic or physique indication; performance/strength use is off-label, non-medical, and at supraphysiologic doses far above anything ever studied clinically. Direct mechanistic data ON fluoxymesterone specifically is limited and old; much of the mechanism is inferred from the broader 17-alpha-alkylated AAS class plus a few compound-specific in-vitro and rodent studies.

At a glance

  • Strength and aggression/drive without size or water weight - the signature 'dry, hard, mean' oral androgen profile (Evidence-rodent for aggression mechanism; user-reported for strength) - PMID 22057031
  • No estrogen conversion (non-aromatizing): minimal water retention and low direct gyno risk - PMID 22421634
  • High hepatotoxicity: cholestatic jaundice, risk of peliosis hepatis and (rarely) liver tumors from the 17-alpha-methyl group - PMID 27372877, PMID 35068803
  • Severe lipid damage: large HDL drop and LDL rise, an oral-alkylated class effect via hepatic lipase upregulation - PMID 2915439
  • Cardiovascular risk: AAS use associated with cardiovascular disease; the dry, non-aromatizing, lipid-harming profile is considered relatively harsh - PMID 39945117
  • Off-target cortisol-handling effect via 11beta-HSD2 inhibition, a possible driver of blood-pressure/fluid effects (in vitro) - PMID 22273746
  • Suppresses the HPG axis / endogenous testosterone (impaired testicular function) like other androgens - PMID 137913
  • Weak direct AR binding affinity - effects are dose-driven, not receptor-affinity-driven - PMID 10077001

Mechanism, step by step

8 steps5 human-evidenced 3 preclinical

  1. The C17-alpha-methyl group makes it survive being swallowed Established

    Plain testosterone taken by mouth is destroyed almost immediately by the liver (first-pass metabolism). Fluoxymesterone carries a methyl group at the 17-alpha position - the same trick used by Dianabol, Anadrol, and Winstrol - which blocks the enzymes that would oxidize the 17-beta-hydroxyl, so the drug survives the gut and liver and reaches the bloodstream orally. This same alkyl group is exactly what makes the whole 17-alpha-alkylated class hard on the liver. Fluoxymesterone adds an 11-beta-hydroxyl and a 9-alpha-fluoro group on top of the methyltestosterone backbone. PMID 27372877 (Bond/Llewellyn/Van Mol 2016, Med Hypotheses 2016 - 17alpha-alkyl substitution retards hepatic metabolism, renders AAS orally bioavailable, and is the alkylation associated with hepatotoxicity); ChEMBL CHEMBL1445 (structure C20H29FO3, oral=true)

  2. It binds the androgen receptor - but only weakly Evidence-in vitro

    Like all AAS, fluoxymesterone works by entering cells and binding the androgen receptor (AR), then the activated receptor moves into the nucleus and switches on genes for muscle protein synthesis. The catch: in a direct receptor assay fluoxymesterone is a LOW-affinity AR ligand. Where high-affinity androgens (DHT, testosterone, R1881/mibolerone) trigger the receptor's internal 'N/C' folding interaction (a marker of strong activation) at sub-nanomolar levels, fluoxymesterone and oxandrolone need much higher concentrations to do the same. So its dramatic real-world effects come from being a potent, non-aromatizing androgenic signal at the doses people take, not from gripping the receptor tightly. PMID 10077001 (Kemppainen, Mol Endocrinol 1999 - fluoxymesterone and oxandrolone are lower-affinity AR ligands requiring higher concentrations to induce the AR N/C interaction); ChEMBL mechanism mec_id 1330 (AR AGONIST, direct_interaction=true)

  3. Activated AR drives the genomic muscle/strength program Established

    Once bound, the AR sheds its chaperone proteins, dimerizes, translocates to the nucleus, and docks onto androgen-response elements (AREs) in DNA to turn transcription up or down. In muscle and bone this increases protein synthesis and, over time, strength and lean tissue; AAS broadly act through this AR-dependent genomic route. Fluoxymesterone is specifically prized by users for strength and 'hardness' rather than mass - consistent with a strong androgenic signal that is not converted to estrogen (see step 5). PMID 33269425 (McCullough, Rev Endocr Metab Disord 2021 - AAS act via the androgen receptor to drive skeletal-muscle hypertrophy and metabolic effects); ChEMBL mechanism mec_id 1330

  4. AR signaling - not just 'roid rage myth' - underlies the aggression effect Evidence-rodent

    Fluoxymesterone has a reputation for boosting aggression and drive. Animal work supports a genuine AR-mediated component: in mice, AAS-induced aggressive behavior depends on a functional androgen receptor - testicular-feminized (Tfm) mice with a defective AR show blunted aggression responses to AAS. Androgen/serotonin crosstalk (5-HT1A/1B) also modulates this. This is rodent behavioral evidence; the leap to human 'aggression' is plausible but not cleanly quantified for fluoxymesterone specifically. PMID 22057031 (Morrison/Melloni, Horm Behav 2011 - AR dependence of AAS-induced aggression in C57BL/6J vs Tfm mice); PMID 10073487 (Cologer-Clifford, Physiol Behav 1999 - androgen modulation of 5-HT1A/1B effects on aggression)

  5. It does NOT aromatize - a 'dry' androgen Evidence-human

    Because of its modified structure, fluoxymesterone is essentially non-aromatizable: it is not converted to estrogen by aromatase. That means no estrogenic water retention, no 'puffy' look, and a low gyno risk from the drug itself - which is why it is used for a hard, dry, strength-focused effect rather than bulking. Non-aromatizable androgens behave differently from testosterone on body composition and metabolic markers, and the trade-off is a worse lipid/cardiovascular profile (next step). Note: the original 1963 rodent assay actually rated it as quite ANDROGENIC, not a cleanly 'anabolic' compound. PMID 22421634 (Pospisilova, J Steroid Biochem Mol Biol 2012 - aromatizable vs non-aromatizable androgens differ on body composition / metabolic syndrome risk); PMID 14086172 (Arnold, J Endocrinol 1963 - anabolic:androgenic ratio of fluoxymesterone and related methyl steroids)

  6. Hepatotoxicity: the 17-alpha-methyl group poisons the liver Established

    The same C17 methyl group that makes it oral makes it hepatotoxic. 17-alpha-alkylated AAS cause cholestatic jaundice and, with prolonged use, peliosis hepatis (blood-filled cavities) and rarely hepatic tumors; nonalkylated/injectable androgens largely do not. The exact molecular mechanism is still not fully nailed down, but it involves direct hepatocellular toxicity and disrupted bile flow. Fluoxymesterone is considered one of the more hepatotoxic orals. In vitro, alkylated AAS are directly toxic to primary hepatocytes. PMID 27372877 (Bond/Llewellyn/Van Mol 2016, Med Hypotheses 2016 - 17alpha-alkylated AAS are hepatotoxic: cholestasis, peliosis hepatis, hepatocellular tumors; mechanism incompletely understood); PMID 35068803 (Patil, J Clin Exp Hepatol 2022 - versatility of AAS-induced hepatotoxicity); PMID 8527826 (Welder, J Pharmacol Toxicol Methods 1995 - direct AAS toxicity in primary rat hepatocytes)

  7. It wrecks the lipid profile (HDL crash, LDL rise) Evidence-human

    Oral 17-alpha-alkylated androgens have an outsized, harmful effect on blood lipids - they sharply lower protective HDL ('good' cholesterol) and raise LDL, far more than injectable/aromatizing androgens like testosterone. The classic head-to-head showed the oral alkylated steroid stanozolol crashed HDL while injectable testosterone barely moved it; this is a class effect driven largely by upregulation of hepatic lipase in the liver. Combined with the non-aromatizing 'dry' profile, fluoxymesterone is regarded as one of the harder compounds on cardiovascular markers, and AAS use overall is linked to cardiovascular disease. PMID 2915439 (Thompson, JAMA 1989 - oral stanozolol sharply lowers HDL/HDL2 vs injectable testosterone, an oral-17alpha-alkyl class effect via hepatic lipase); PMID 39945117 (Windfeld-Mathiasen, Circulation 2025 - cardiovascular disease in AAS users)

  8. Off-target: it blocks cortisol breakdown (11beta-HSD2) Evidence-in vitro

    Beyond the androgen receptor, fluoxymesterone inhibits the enzyme 11-beta-HSD2, which normally inactivates the stress hormone cortisol. Blocking it lets cortisol over-stimulate the mineralocorticoid receptor (the same pathway aldosterone uses), which can push sodium/water retention and blood pressure up - a plausible contributor to its cardiovascular and blood-pressure effects, shown in a cell-based assay. This is a less-known, fluoxymesterone-specific finding and is in-vitro, so treat the in-body relevance as suggestive rather than proven. PMID 22273746 (Fuerstenberger, Toxicol Sci 2012 - fluoxymesterone inhibits 11beta-HSD2-dependent glucocorticoid inactivation)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. PRIMARY TARGET — Androgen receptor (AR / NR3C4), agonist (Established; ChEMBL CHEMBL1445 mechanism, target CHEMBL1871, action_type AGONIST, direct_interaction true). Despite its fearsome clinical reputation, fluoxymesterone is a comparatively WEAK direct AR binder. In the classic Saartok/Gustafsson competition study (rat/rabbit skeletal muscle + rat prostate, [3H]-methyltrienolone displacement), fluoxymesterone had a relative binding affinity (RBA) <0.05 vs methyltrienolone (R1881=reference), placing it in the same low-affinity bin as stanozolol and methandienone, far below nandrolone, methenolone or testosterone (Evidence-rodent; PMID 6539197). Functionally consistent: in a mammalian two-hybrid assay fluoxymesterone (with oxandrolone) required 10–100 nM to drive the AR N-terminal/C-terminal (N/C) interaction up to ~23-fold, whereas high-affinity agonists (DHT, mibolerone, testosterone, methyltrienolone) achieved >40-fold at 0.1–1 nM (Evidence-in vitro/human-AR construct; PMID 10077001). So fluoxymesterone is a low-potency, high-intrinsic-activity agonist — strong androgenic output per molecule that does bind, but poor occupancy efficiency, implying it relies on relatively high tissue concentrations. NO published human Ki/EC50 for fluoxymesterone–AR was located this run; affinity is characterized only relatively (RBA) and functionally (N/C EC range). OFF-TARGET (mechanistically important): potent competitive inhibitor of human 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 (11β-HSD2), IC50 60–100 nM (cell lysates), 160 nM (intact SW-620), 530 nM (MCF-7) — among the strongest 11β-HSD2 inhibitors of ~19 AAS screened, and it is itself oxidized by 11β-HSD2 to 11-oxo-fluoxymesterone (Evidence-in vitro, human enzyme; PMID 22273746). It is also a substrate/weak ligand at human serum albumin (spectroscopic/modeling; PMID 22217702). Unlike many AAS it is NOT a 5α-reductase substrate (already 9α-fluoro-11β-hydroxy-17α-methyl-testosterone, 4-ene A-ring intact but C9 fluorination/C11 hydroxyl block typical 5α-reduction to a more potent species) and is a poor aromatase substrate (17α-methyl group sterically hinders aromatization), so estrogenic effects are indirect (Contested/Speculative for exact pathway).

Signalling cascade. GENOMIC (classical) pathway (Established for AR class; PMID 10419007, 41898445): fluoxymesterone, being lipophilic (cLogP ~3.3, ChEMBL), diffuses across the plasma membrane and binds the cytosolic AR ligand-binding domain. Agonist binding repositions helix 12 (the AF-2 surface) and stabilizes the intramolecular N/C interaction (FxxLF motif of the NTD docking into AF-2) — the step that is inefficient for fluoxymesterone at low concentration (PMID 10077001). Ligand binding triggers dissociation of HSP90/HSP70/co-chaperones, receptor phosphorylation (PMID 8875135), homodimerization, and nuclear translocation. The AR dimer binds androgen-response elements (AREs) in promoter/enhancer regions, recruits p160/SRC coactivators, CBP/p300 and the Mediator complex, and drives transcription of anabolic genes (IGF-1 and local IGF-1 splice variants, myostatin suppression, contractile/structural protein genes) in skeletal muscle, plus androgenic programs in skin, larynx, CNS and bone (Established class mechanism; Evidence-human gap for fluoxymesterone-specific transcriptomics). NON-GENOMIC/rapid signaling (Evidence-rodent/in vitro for AAS class; PMID 41898445): membrane-associated AR and Src→Ras→ERK/MAPK and PI3K/Akt activation can occur within minutes, contributing to Ca2+ flux and possibly to mood/aggression effects — not specifically mapped for fluoxymesterone. CROSS-TALK that is fluoxymesterone-distinctive: by inhibiting 11β-HSD2, fluoxymesterone prevents local inactivation of cortisol→cortisone, allowing cortisol to illicitly occupy and activate the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) in kidney/colon ("apparent mineralocorticoid excess"-like), promoting Na+/water retention, hypokalemia and a pressor effect — a plausible molecular route to its reputation for hardening/water dynamics and to hypertension (Evidence-in vitro, human enzyme; mechanistically inferred; PMID 22273746). Because direct AR occupancy is modest but intrinsic activity high, the strong subjective "androgenic"/aggression and strength effects users report are partly attributed to this high efficacy plus non-AR routes (Contested).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral tablet, 17α-methylated for oral bioavailability (also historically given buccally). Brand/generic: Halotestin, Android-F, Androxy, Ora-Testryl (ChEMBL synonyms). Oral=true, parenteral=false in ChEMBL — there is no injectable ester; this is an unesterified oral 17α-methyl-androstane.
Absorption / bioavailability
Well absorbed orally. In a 6-subject Latin-square crossover (10 mg tablet, buccal vs swallowed), oral Cmax was ~80% and oral AUC ~76% of the buccal route, with Cmax 40–150 ng/mL and Tmax 1–2 h (Evidence-human, small n=6; PMID 4009439). The 17α-methyl group is the structural feature that blocks first-pass hepatic 17β-hydroxyl oxidation, conferring meaningful oral bioavailability (the defining trait of the oral 17-alkyl AAS class; Established).
Half-life
Short. Harmonic-mean terminal half-life ~2.0 h for parent fluoxymesterone in serum (Evidence-human; PMID 4009439). The commonly cited ~9 h "half-life" in lay/bodybuilding sources is NOT supported by this serum PK study and appears to conflate parent-drug clearance with prolonged metabolite/urinary detectability (see metabolism). Parent drug is undetectable in urine >1 day post-dose (PMID 2214783).
Metabolism
Extensive hepatic phase-I metabolism. In man the principal pathways are 6β-hydroxylation, 4-ene reduction (A-ring), 3-keto reduction, and 11β-hydroxy oxidation (to 11-oxo), yielding 4 firmly identified + 3 tentative urinary metabolites (Evidence-human; PMID 2214783). Modern GC-Orbitrap-HRMS and LC-TOF-MS/MS work confirms and extends this, identifying multiple long-term metabolites (incl. 6β-hydroxy and 18-nor-fluoxymesterone-type products) used for doping detection (Evidence-human; PMID 41721113, 22521423, 18035854, 8539789). 6β-hydroxylation implicates CYP3A4 (the canonical 6β-steroid hydroxylase; mechanistically inferred, Evidence-in vitro for the enzyme class — NOT directly enzyme-mapped for fluoxymesterone this run). Notably 11β-HSD2 oxidizes fluoxymesterone to 11-oxo-fluoxymesterone, so the drug is both inhibitor and substrate of that enzyme (Evidence-in vitro, human; PMID 22273746).
Excretion
Renal/urinary as hydroxylated and reduced conjugated metabolites; long-term metabolites detectable in urine for at least 5 days after a single 10 mg dose (PMID 2214783), and out to ~30 days with HRMS in recent doping-control work (PMID 41721113). Parent compound clears fast (urine <1 day).
Notes
NOT a prodrug and NOT esterified (ChEMBL prodrug=0). The 9α-fluoro and 11β-hydroxy substitutions and the 17α-methyl group make it metabolically stable enough orally and block efficient 5α-reduction and aromatization. Crosses cell membranes readily (lipophilic, MW 336.45, C20H29FO3, PSA 57.5, 0 Lipinski violations — ChEMBL); CNS penetration is expected from these properties but a specific BBB/brain-exposure measurement was not located. Molecular species: single oral small molecule, ATC G03BA01 (androgens, 3-oxoandrosten-(4) derivatives).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

HUMAN dose-response for performance/muscle endpoints is essentially UNCHARACTERIZED by controlled trials — no modern RCT of fluoxymesterone for strength/hypertrophy in athletes exists, so any dose→effect-size claim for that purpose is ungrounded (key human-data gap). What IS documented clinically: (1) Advanced breast cancer / hormonal therapy: the standard studied dose was 10 mg orally twice daily (20 mg/day). Added to tamoxifen in metastatic disease it raised objective response from 42% to 53% (one-sided P=.05) and lengthened median time-to-progression (350 vs 199 days) but gave NO survival advantage and more virilization (Evidence-human RCT, n=238; PMID 3284975); the adjuvant early-breast-cancer replication (10 mg BID for 1 yr added to tamoxifen, n=541) found NO relapse-free or overall survival benefit (PMID 16538529). (2) Anemia of chronic disease / myeloproliferative & renal anemia: studied at roughly 10–30 mg/day to stimulate erythropoiesis with mixed/modest benefit (Evidence-human, older trials; PMID 7103635, 608858). (3) Constitutional delay of growth/puberty and gonadal dysgenesis: LOW-dose fluoxymesterone (commonly ~2.5–5 mg/day ranges in the cited pediatric literature) accelerated growth velocity and bone maturation (Evidence-human, small studies; PMID 8464656, 4003064, 7158332) — illustrating that even sub-androgenic-feeling doses move hard biological endpoints. SPECIES note: rodent AR-binding data (RBA <0.05; PMID 6539197) predict that high tissue concentrations are needed for genomic effect, but rodents are a POOR model for the 11β-HSD2/MR off-target effect because mouse/rat 11β-HSD2 is much less inhibited than the human enzyme (PMID 22273746) — so animal dose-response understates the human cardiovascular/electrolyte liability. Bottom line: clinical doses cluster at ~10–20 mg/day; the muscle/strength dose-response humans actually use recreationally is unstudied and undocumented in the peer-reviewed record.

How it compares

  • vs Methyltestosterone (oral 17α-methyl-testosterone, the parent scaffold)
    same: Both are orally active 17α-methylated testosterone derivatives, AR agonists, hepatotoxic via the 17-alkyl group, both historically used for breast cancer/androgen replacement (methyltestosterone ATC also G03B androgens).
    different: Fluoxymesterone adds a 9α-fluoro and 11β-hydroxy group, which roughly increases potency per mg and blocks aromatization/5α-reduction more than methyltestosterone, and confers the strong 11β-HSD2 inhibition; fluoxymesterone is considered markedly more androgenic-feeling and "harder" with little estrogenic conversion, whereas methyltestosterone aromatizes more readily to methylestradiol (more gynecomastia/estrogenic).
  • vs Stanozolol (Winstrol, 17α-methyl heterocyclic DHT derivative)
    same: Both oral 17α-alkylated AAS, both LOW direct AR affinity (RBA <0.05 in the same Saartok study), both hepatotoxic and HDL-suppressing, both prized for 'hardening'/non-estrogenic look.
    different: Stanozolol is a DHT-derived pyrazole (no 4-ene 3-keto, cannot aromatize at all and is non-progestational), used clinically for hereditary angioedema; fluoxymesterone retains the 4-ene-3-one A-ring and uniquely carries the 9-fluoro/11-OH motif and potent 11β-HSD2 inhibition. Fluoxymesterone is reputed far more aggression/strength-oriented; stanozolol more joint-drying/connective-tissue-affecting.
  • vs Methandienone (methandrostenolone/Dianabol, 17α-methyl-1,4-androstadien-3-one)
    same: Oral 17α-methyl AAS, low AR RBA (<0.05, same study), hepatotoxic, oral t½ in the low hours range.
    different: Methandienone aromatizes appreciably (estrogenic — water retention, gynecomastia) and is strongly anabolic/mass-building; fluoxymesterone barely aromatizes (17α-methyl + 9-fluoro/11-OH) so it is non-bloating and skews strongly toward androgenic/strength and CNS effects rather than mass.
  • vs Testosterone (endogenous reference androgen)
    same: Shares the 4-androsten-3-one core and is an AR agonist driving the same genomic anabolic/androgenic program.
    different: Testosterone is a HIGH-affinity AR binder (RBA orders of magnitude above fluoxymesterone), is injectable/esterified (no oral 17-alkyl hepatotoxicity), aromatizes to estradiol and 5α-reduces to DHT, and does not meaningfully inhibit 11β-HSD2. Fluoxymesterone trades AR affinity and a clean liver/lipid profile for oral activity, non-aromatization, and a distinctive off-target glucocorticoid/MR liability.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Anadrol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Both are oral 17-alpha-alkylated AAS whose alkyl group resists first-pass hepatic clearance and concentrates the drug in hepatocytes, where it drives cholestasis (impaired canalicular bile-acid transport), transaminitis, and at the severe end peliosis hepatis and hepatic adenoma/carcinoma. Running two 17aa orals together loads the same hepatocellular/cholestatic insult onto one liver rather than spreading it across non-alkylated injectables, so the hepatotoxic burden is additive. Fluoxymesterone is regarded as one of the more hepatotoxic orals; oxymetholone is dose-dependently hepatotoxic (25-43% of subjects >5x baseline enzymes in RCTs). Halotestin monograph step 6 & side_effect_mechanisms (17aa cholestasis/peliosis; PMID 27372877, 36051334); anadrol monograph key_effects (marked dose-dependent hepatotoxicity; PMID 12646793, 27372877) Watch: Baseline and periodic ALT/AST, GGT, ALP, total/direct bilirubin; watch for jaundice, dark urine, right-upper-quadrant pain, pruritus. Rising transaminases or bilirubin is a signal to stop and seek medical evaluation.
  • Clenbuterol compounds risk ⚠ Contested
    Two independent routes to low potassium and cardiovascular strain converge. Clenbuterol is a beta-2 agonist whose cAMP/PKA activation of Na+/K+-ATPase drives potassium into cells (hypokalemia) and causes tachycardia, widened pulse pressure and cardiac stress. Fluoxymesterone inhibits 11-beta-HSD2, letting cortisol illicitly activate the mineralocorticoid receptor ('apparent mineralocorticoid excess'), promoting renal sodium/water retention, potassium wasting, and a pressor effect. Stacked, the hypokalemia and blood-pressure/cardiac burdens add up, raising arrhythmia risk. Note the clenbuterol hypokalemia arm is well established in humans, but the Halotestin 11b-HSD2/mineralocorticoid arm is shown only in vitro (human enzyme) and inferred in-body — so the convergence is real but its clinical magnitude from the Halo side is unproven. Halotestin monograph step 8 & side_effect_mechanisms (11b-HSD2 inhibition -> mineralocorticoid excess, hypokalemia, hypertension - in vitro/inferred; PMID 22273746); clenbuterol monograph side_effect_mechanisms (beta2/PKA hypokalemia; tachycardia; PMID 23844963, 18072161) Watch: Serum potassium and sodium, blood pressure, resting heart rate; watch for palpitations, cramps, or ECG (ST/T-wave, QT) changes. Low potassium plus tachycardia is an arrhythmia risk warranting medical attention.
  • Exemestane compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Same low-estrogen logic as with non-steroidal AIs: fluoxymesterone adds no estradiol, so pairing it with exemestane in a stack where the AI is already suppressing estrogen (~85-95%) risks tipping estradiol below its healthy window while Halotestin piles on an HDL-crashing, joint-drying dry androgen with no estrogenic buffer. The harms of low E2 (joint pain, low libido/mood, accelerated bone loss) are compounded rather than counteracted, because the added compound is not an estrogen source. Note exemestane is roughly lipid-neutral and yields a mildly androgenic AR-binding metabolite, so the lipid compounding here is driven mainly by Halo plus the low-E2 state rather than by the AI itself. Halotestin monograph steps 5 & 7; exemestane monograph key_effects (lowers estradiol ~85-95%; AIMSS joint pain, bone loss, low libido/mood; lipid-neutral) Watch: Sensitive estradiol, lipid panel, and joint/libido/mood symptoms; reconsider AI necessity when a non-aromatizing agent contributes most of the androgen load.
  • Letrozole compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Fluoxymesterone does not aromatize, so it contributes zero estradiol. When it is added on top of a base whose estrogen is being controlled with a potent aromatase inhibitor, the AI can drive estradiol toward the bottom of its U-shaped safety curve while Halotestin adds a dry, HDL-crashing androgenic load with no estrogen to buffer it. The result is compounded low-estrogen harm — worse lipids (HDL already low from the AI, further crashed by the oral), joint pain/stiffness, low libido, and bone/mood effects — because the added compound provides no estrogenic offset. This is compounding, not counteracting: an AI does not 'fix' Halotestin (Halo makes no estrogen); it can push an already-dry stack into estradiol deficiency. Halotestin monograph step 5 (non-aromatizing) & step 7 (HDL crash); letrozole monograph key_effects (>98% estrogen suppression; crashed-estrogen harms - AIMSS joint pain, bone loss, mood/libido) Watch: Sensitive-assay estradiol (avoid over-suppression), lipid panel (HDL/LDL), and symptom check for joint pain, low libido, low mood; reassess whether an AI is even warranted once a non-aromatizing agent contributes most of the androgen load.
  • RAD-140 compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both act through the androgen receptor, both suppress the HPG axis (low LH/FSH, suppressed endogenous testosterone), and both burden the liver and lipids. Fluoxymesterone drives AR-mediated negative feedback shutting down endogenous testosterone while crashing HDL via hepatic lipase and stressing the liver; RAD-140 is a strongly HPTA-suppressing SARM with human case reports of cholestatic drug-induced liver injury and hypogonadism/gynecomastia. Combined, they deepen gonadal-axis shutdown and add two hepatic/androgenic insults on top of Halotestin's marked hepatotoxicity and lipid harm. Halotestin monograph key_effects & side_effect_mechanisms (HPG suppression; hepatic-lipase HDL crash; hepatotoxicity); rad-140 monograph key_effects (HPG shutdown; case-report cholestatic DILI, hypogonadism, gynecomastia) Watch: LH/FSH, total/free testosterone (axis suppression), HDL/lipid panel, and liver enzymes/bilirubin; watch for testicular atrophy and low libido after cessation, and jaundice/pruritus (cholestatic DILI).
  • Stanozolol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Both are oral 17-alpha-alkylated, non-aromatizing 'dry' AAS and both are weak-affinity AR binders (RBA <0.05 in the same Saartok panel) that share the two hallmark oral-AAS harms: additive hepatotoxicity (17a-alkyl cholestasis/hepatocellular injury; stanozolol has documented rare fatal liver failure) and atherogenic dyslipidemia (both crash HDL via hepatic-lipase induction, with no estrogenic HDL support because neither aromatizes). Two dry orals together deepen both the liver and lipid hits. Halotestin monograph steps 6-7 & comparisons (vs stanozolol: both RBA <0.05, both hepatotoxic & HDL-suppressing; PMID 6539197, 2915439); stanozolol monograph key_effects (oral hepatotoxicity incl. rare fatal liver failure; adverse lipids; non-aromatizing) Watch: Liver panel (ALT/AST/GGT/bilirubin) and lipid panel (HDL/LDL/ApoB); watch for jaundice/RUQ pain and joint dryness. Rising liver markers or a crashed HDL are signals to stop.
  • Superdrol compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Two oral 17-alpha-methylated, non-aromatizing 'dry' AAS taken together compound both hepatotoxicity (additive cholestatic/hepatocellular injury from the shared 17a-alkyl group) and atherogenic dyslipidemia. Both induce hepatic lipase and crash HDL / raise LDL with no offsetting estrogenic HDL support because neither aromatizes, so the HDL fall is deeper than with an aromatizing injectable. Superdrol characteristically causes cholestatic jaundice with high bilirubin and severe HDL suppression, matching fluoxymesterone's profile. Halotestin monograph steps 6-7 & side_effect_mechanisms (hepatic-lipase HDL crash, 17aa hepatotoxicity; PMID 2915439, 36051334); superdrol monograph key_effects (cholestatic injury; sharp HDL drop; non-aromatizing) Watch: Full lipid panel (expect sharp HDL-C drop, higher LDL/ApoB) plus the full liver panel (ALT/AST/GGT/bilirubin); watch for jaundice and pruritus. Statin/ezetimibe blunt LDL but do not restore HDL; cessation is what recovers it.
  • Dutasteride redundant Established
    Dutasteride inhibits both 5-alpha-reductase isoforms for near-total (~90-95%) DHT suppression, used to curb DHT-driven androgenic effects of 5a-reducible androgens. Because fluoxymesterone is not a 5-AR substrate and signals directly at the AR, dutasteride cannot reduce Halotestin's androgenic action or its side-effect profile. Pairing it to 'tame' Halotestin is redundant — it only modifies 5a-reducible components of a stack (e.g. testosterone), not fluoxymesterone itself. Halotestin monograph expand.pharmacology (not a 5a-reductase substrate); dutasteride monograph (SRD5A1+2, ~90-95% DHT suppression; muscle anabolism from T unaffected by blocking DHT) Watch: Do not expect dutasteride to soften Halotestin's androgenic/scalp/skin effects; those remain direct-AR mediated. Watch hairline/skin and prostate symptoms as AR-driven.
  • Finasteride redundant Established
    Finasteride blocks 5-alpha-reductase (T->DHT), the strategy used to blunt DHT-mediated hair loss/prostate effects from androgens that depend on 5a-reduction. Fluoxymesterone is not a 5-alpha-reductase substrate — its 9a-fluoro / 11b-hydroxy / 17a-methyl structure blocks 5a-reduction, so it acts directly at the androgen receptor without needing conversion to a more potent DHT species. Finasteride therefore cannot lower or soften Halotestin's androgenic signal; adding it to 'tame' Halotestin is mechanistically redundant. Finasteride only affects the 5a-reducible components of a stack (e.g. testosterone). Halotestin monograph expand.pharmacology (NOT a 5a-reductase substrate; 9a-fluoro/11b-OH/17a-methyl blocks 5a-reduction); finasteride monograph (SRD5A2-selective, blocks T->DHT) Watch: No Halotestin-specific benefit to expect from 5-AR inhibition; if androgenic scalp/skin effects appear, recognize they are direct-AR (not DHT-conversion) driven. Track scalp/hairline and skin as AR-mediated signs.

History & legal status

"Synthesized and introduced in the mid-1950s by Upjohn; first approval 1956 (ChEMBL first_approval 1956). Early clinical reports appeared 1958–1961 for advanced breast cancer (Kennedy, N Engl J Med 1958, PMID 13590423; Hudson, Med J Aust 1959, PMID 14403738; Lowe/Gordan, Arch Intern Med 1961, PMID 13763814). Marketed in the US as Halotestin (Upjohn) and later as Android-F, Androxy, Ora-Testryl; ATC code G03BA01 (androgens). Approved indications historically included palliative treatment of androgen-responsive/metastatic breast cancer in women, male hypogonadism/androgen replacement, delayed puberty, and anemias of bone-marrow failure (it stimulates erythropoiesis). It is a US DEA Schedule III controlled substance under the Anabolic Steroids Control Act (1990, expanded 2004) — ChEMBL synonym 'Fluoxymesterone ciii' reflects CIII scheduling. It is prohibited at all times (in and out of competition) under the WADA Code as an exogenous anabolic androgenic steroid (S1), which is why so much modern fluoxymesterone literature is doping-control metabolite work (PMID 41721113, 22521423, 18035854, 2214783). Clinical use has dwindled to near-obsolete in oncology (superseded by aromatase inhibitors, SERMs, fulvestrant); branded products have been largely discontinued in many markets, though generic Androxy remained available. It survives mainly as a niche second/third-line endocrine option in heavily pretreated ER+ metastatic breast cancer (PMID 27663436) and as a controlled, illicitly-used performance/strength drug."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No quantitative human AR binding constant (Ki/EC50) exists for fluoxymesterone — affinity is known only relatively (RBA <0.05 vs methyltrienolone, rodent tissue) and functionally (10–100 nM for N/C interaction in vitro). Its true human-AR Kd is undefined.
  • Whether the dramatic subjective effects users attribute to it (aggression, strength, 'hardness') are driven by AR signaling at high occupancy, by 11β-HSD2/MR-mediated glucocorticoid effects, by non-genomic membrane signaling, or some combination — the relative contributions are unmapped in humans.
  • No controlled human dose-response data for muscle, strength, or performance endpoints at the doses people actually use; the entire performance pharmacology is anecdotal/uncharacterized in the peer-reviewed record.
  • The CYP isoform map for fluoxymesterone metabolism is inferred (6β-hydroxylation suggests CYP3A4) but not directly demonstrated; drug–drug interaction potential is therefore poorly defined.
  • Long-term cardiovascular outcomes specifically attributable to 11β-HSD2 inhibition (apparent-mineralocorticoid-excess-type hypertension) in human users have not been prospectively quantified — the mechanism is shown only in vitro and is essentially invisible in rodent models.
  • Brain/BBB exposure and the molecular basis of reported CNS toxicity (e.g., ataxia case PMID 7235823) are not established; no human brain pharmacokinetic or target-engagement data were located.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only — no dosing guidance. LIVER: baseline and periodic ALT/AST, ALP, GGT, total/direct bilirubin (cholestatic pattern is characteristic of 17α-alkylated orals); watch for jaundice, dark urine, right-upper-quadrant pain, pruritus; consider hepatic imaging if peliosis/adenoma suspected (PMID 36051334). CARDIOVASCULAR/METABOLIC: blood pressure (the 11β-HSD2/MR mechanism predicts hypertension and Na+/water retention — PMID 22273746); fasting lipid panel expecting sharp HDL-C drop and LDL-C rise; serum potassium and sodium (hypokalemia risk from mineralocorticoid effect). ENDOCRINE: total/free testosterone, LH, FSH (HPG suppression), and estradiol; in men watch for testicular atrophy, low libido after cessation, and breast changes (gynecomastia, which can be irreversible — PMID 25948845). HEMATOLOGIC: hematocrit/hemoglobin (erythrocytosis/polycythemia from erythropoietic stimulation — historically used for anemia, PMID 7103635). ANDROGENIC/VIRILIZING SIGNS (especially women and adolescents): voice deepening, hirsutism, clitoromegaly, acne, scalp hair loss — several are irreversible, so early detection matters; in adolescents monitor growth/bone age given premature epiphyseal closure risk. NEUROPSYCHIATRIC: screen for mood lability, irritability/aggression, and rare neurologic symptoms (the ataxia case). Any rising transaminases/bilirubin, uncontrolled blood pressure, or virilization are signals to seek medical evaluation and stop the drug — most biochemical changes normalize after discontinuation, but liver tumors, voice change, gynecomastia and androgenic alopecia may not."

Caveats

HARM-REDUCTION / TEACHING NOTE - no dosing is given anywhere here, by design. Key honesty flags for the teaching package: (1) Fluoxymesterone is an old drug (1956) with sparse modern, compound-specific mechanistic research; several steps are grounded in the broader 17-alpha-alkylated AAS class rather than fluoxymesterone itself - this is stated per step. (2) The aggression mechanism rests on rodent (Tfm-mouse) evidence; extrapolating a clean 'roid rage' claim to humans is not justified by the data - certainty is Evidence-rodent. (3) The 11beta-HSD2 inhibition finding is in-vitro; its in-body contribution to blood pressure is plausible but unproven (Evidence-in vitro). (4) The low AR binding affinity (Kemppainen 1999) is an important teaching point - fluoxymesterone is a weak binder whose strong real-world effect comes from dose and from being non-aromatizing, not from receptor grip. (5) Lipid harm is demonstrated for the oral-17alpha-alkylated class (stanozolol head-to-head); fluoxymesterone is reasonably grouped here but a direct fluoxymesterone HDL trial at performance doses does not exist. (6) The exact molecular cause of 17-alpha-alkyl hepatotoxicity remains officially 'incompletely understood' (Niedfeldt 2016) - do not overstate a single mechanism. (7) Many cited human trials are oncology (breast cancer) studies at therapeutic doses - not models of physique/strength misuse, which occurs at far higher doses with unknown safety margins. Backed by human data.
The C17-alpha-methyl group makes it survive being swallowed. Plain testosterone taken by mouth is destroyed almost immediately by the liver (first-pass metabolism). Fluoxymesterone carries a methyl group at the 17-alpha position - the same trick used by Dianabol, Anadrol, and Winstrol - which blocks the enzymes that would oxidize the 17-beta-hydroxyl, so the drug survives the gut and liver and reaches the bloodstream orally. This same alkyl group is exactly what makes the whole 17-alpha-alkylated class hard on the liver. Fluoxymesterone adds an 11-beta-hydroxyl and a 9-alpha-fluoro group on top of the methyltestosterone backbone.
