GlyNAC

Main target: Glutathione. Backed by human data.

Works in humans?Human evidence
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A two-amino-acid combo that refills the cell's master antioxidant (glutathione). It has real 16-24 week human RCT data in older adults showing better mitochondrial fuel oxidation, insulin sensitivity, and physical function, but the human trials are small, short, and all from a single lab.

Strong human data 7 citations
Class
Glutathione-precursor combination / redox-restoring nutraceutical (glycine + cysteine donor)
Primary target
Glutathione (GSH) biosynthesis: supplies the two rate-limiting substrates (glycine and cysteine, delivered as NAC) so cells can rebuild depleted intracellular glutathione, lowering oxidative stress and supporting mitochondrial fuel oxidation
Human status
Marketed and widely sold as an over-the-counter dietary supplement (glycine and NAC are both food/supplement ingredients; 'GlyNAC' is the Baylor-coined combination, not an FDA-approved drug). Human efficacy evidence is real but early-stage and concentrated in one group (Baylor College of Medicine, Sekhar lab): a 36-week open-label pilot with 24-week supplementation then 12-week withdrawal (n=8 older adults + 8 young; PMID 33783984), and a placebo-controlled randomized trial (24 older adults, 12 GlyNAC / 12 isonitrogenous alanine, 16 weeks, plus 12 young; NCT01870193; PMID 35975308, published J Gerontol A, 2023). Foundational stable-isotope studies established the glutathione-synthesis defect and its correction in aging (PMID 21795440) and in uncontrolled type 2 diabetes (PMID 20929994). No large multi-center RCT, no hard clinical endpoints, no long-term safety data, and no independent (non-Baylor) replication of the aging-hallmark reversal yet. Registered GlyNAC/precursor trials are ongoing in mild cognitive impairment (NCT03493178, Baylor, EARLY_PHASE1), Alzheimer's disease (NCT04740580, Baylor, recruiting), and a Vanderbilt Phase 4 RCT testing perioperative GlyNAC to reduce oxidative stress and chronic post-surgical pain after total knee arthroplasty (NCT06083480, recruiting). NAC alone additionally has a separate, established regulatory identity as an approved drug for acetaminophen overdose and as a mucolytic.

At a glance

  • Restores intracellular (RBC) glutathione synthesis rate and concentration toward youthful levels — the best-established effect, measured by isotope tracer (Established)
  • Lowers oxidative stress and oxidant damage (F2-isoprostanes, d-ROMs) in older adults, with lipid-peroxidation markers also falling in uncontrolled diabetes (Established)
  • Improves mitochondrial fatty-acid oxidation and markers of energy metabolism (human association; causal proof rodent)
  • Reduces insulin resistance (HOMA-IR) and inflammatory markers over 16-24 weeks in older adults (Evidence-human, small trials)
  • Improves muscle strength, gait speed, 6-minute walk distance, and body composition in older adults (Evidence-human, small trials)
  • Improves cognitive test scores and endothelial function; reduces genomic/DNA damage (Evidence-human, single-center)
  • Extends lifespan ~24% in mice (rodent-only; no human longevity data)

Mechanism, step by step

8 steps7 human-evidenced 1 preclinical

  1. Aging (and uncontrolled diabetes) depletes intracellular glutathione via low precursor supply and reduced synthesis Established

    Glutathione (GSH) is the cell's most abundant internal antioxidant. In older adults, red-blood-cell glycine and cysteine run low, and stable-isotope studies show GSH is not depleted from faster destruction but from a markedly reduced *synthesis rate* — the cell simply cannot build enough. The same reduced-synthesis pattern was measured in people with uncontrolled type 2 diabetes. This is the core rationale for supplying the missing building blocks. PMID 21795440 (Am J Clin Nutr 2011, DOI 10.3945/ajcn.110.003483); PMID 20929994 (Diabetes Care 2010, DOI 10.2337/dc10-1006)

  2. NAC and glycine supply the two rate-limiting substrates for GSH synthesis Established

    GSH is a tripeptide of glutamate, cysteine, and glycine. Glutamate is rarely limiting; cysteine and glycine are. NAC is a stable, well-absorbed cysteine donor (free cysteine is unstable and poorly tolerated in bulk), and glycine is given directly. Providing both together lets glutamate-cysteine ligase and GSH synthetase run at full rate. This is textbook biochemistry. PMID 34587244 (J Nutr 2021 review, DOI 10.1093/jn/nxab309); PMID 21795440 (DOI 10.3945/ajcn.110.003483)

  3. GSH synthesis rate and intracellular concentration are restored Established

    After ~2 weeks of cysteine+glycine, elderly subjects' RBC GSH concentration rose ~95% (94.6%), fractional synthesis rate ~79% (78.8%), and absolute synthesis rate ~231% (230.9%) — reaching levels not statistically different from young controls. A comparable restoration occurred in uncontrolled type 2 diabetes. This is measured directly with isotope tracers, not inferred — but in small groups (8 elderly; 12 diabetic). PMID 21795440 (DOI 10.3945/ajcn.110.003483); PMID 20929994 (DOI 10.2337/dc10-1006)

  4. Restored GSH lowers oxidative stress and oxidant damage Established

    With GSH refilled, plasma markers of oxidative stress fell: reactive oxygen metabolites (Carratelli/d-ROMs) and F2-isoprostanes (a well-validated lipid-peroxidation marker) dropped significantly in supplemented older adults, and lipid-peroxidation markers fell in diabetics. GSH also helps regenerate other antioxidants (vitamins C and E) through redox cycling. PMID 21795440 (DOI 10.3945/ajcn.110.003483); PMID 20929994 (DOI 10.2337/dc10-1006); PMID 33783984 (Clin Transl Med 2021, DOI 10.1002/ctm2.372)

  5. GSH supports mitochondrial fat oxidation — restoring it improves it (human association; causal proof is rodent) Evidence-human

    Mitochondria depend on GSH to buffer the reactive oxygen species they generate. In GSH-deficient elderly humans, low GSH tracked with impaired fasted mitochondrial fatty-acid oxidation, and restoring GSH reversed this — but this human evidence is an association plus a small reversal. The stronger *causal* evidence is rodent: in young mice, acutely depleting GSH alone was enough to lower mitochondrial fatty-acid oxidation, arguing GSH is a direct regulator rather than a bystander. Read the direct-regulator claim as rodent-anchored. PMID 23534396 (Aging Cell 2013, DOI 10.1111/acel.12073); PMID 35975308 (J Gerontol A 2023, DOI 10.1093/gerona/glac135)

  6. Improved mitochondrial redox tracks with better insulin sensitivity and lower inflammation Evidence-human

    In the human trials, GlyNAC lowered insulin resistance (HOMA-IR) and inflammatory markers alongside the mitochondrial improvements — consistent with the idea that oxidative stress and impaired fat oxidation contribute to insulin resistance. This is human co-occurrence, not proof of the causal chain; the direct 'restore GSH → reverse insulin resistance' step is demonstrated in mice. PMID 33783984 (DOI 10.1002/ctm2.372); PMID 35975308 (DOI 10.1093/gerona/glac135); PMID 23534396 (DOI 10.1111/acel.12073)

  7. Downstream: improvements in strength, gait, cognition, body composition, and 'aging hallmarks' — from small single-center trials Evidence-human

    Over 16-24 weeks in older adults, GlyNAC (and not isonitrogenous alanine placebo, in the RCT) improved muscle strength, gait speed, 6-minute walk distance, cognitive test scores, body composition (lower fat, smaller waist), systolic blood pressure, endothelial function, and reduced genomic (DNA) damage. Baylor frames these as improving multiple 'hallmarks of aging.' These are the clinically meaningful outputs — but they come from two small single-center trials (uncontrolled pilot n=8; 16-week RCT with 12 per arm) and are mostly surrogate/biomarker or short-term functional measures. PMID 35975308 (DOI 10.1093/gerona/glac135); PMID 33783984 (DOI 10.1002/ctm2.372)

  8. In mice, lifelong GlyNAC extended lifespan ~24% — NOT shown in humans Evidence-rodent

    C57BL/6J mice given GlyNAC lived about 24% longer than controls, with corrected GSH deficiency, oxidative stress, and improved mitophagy, nutrient-sensing, and genomic-damage markers. This is a rodent-only, single-strain finding. There is no human lifespan or hard-outcome data — treat lifespan-extension as hypothesis, not fact. PMID 35268089 (Nutrients 2022, DOI 10.3390/nu14051114)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No classic receptor. GlyNAC works by mass-action substrate supply to the glutathione biosynthetic enzymes. NAC is deacetylated to L-cysteine; cysteine and glutamate are joined by glutamate-cysteine ligase (GCL, the rate-limiting enzyme) to form γ-glutamylcysteine, which glutathione synthetase then joins with glycine to form the tripeptide GSH (γ-glutamyl-cysteinyl-glycine). Cysteine availability is the principal flux-limiting factor; glycine becomes co-limiting when GSH turnover is high. NAC also has some direct thiol-based free-radical scavenging and can reduce protein disulfides independent of GSH.

Signalling cascade. NAC → L-cysteine + glycine + glutamate → (GCL) γ-glutamylcysteine → (GSH synthetase) GSH → GSH acts as electron donor for glutathione peroxidases (neutralizing H2O2/lipid peroxides) and substrate for glutathione-S-transferases (detoxification), becoming oxidized GSSG, which glutathione reductase regenerates using NADPH. Restored GSH protects mitochondrial membranes/enzymes, supporting fatty-acid oxidation and lowering the oxidative-stress signals implicated in insulin resistance and inflammation.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (both glycine and NAC are orally administered; NAC also exists as IV/inhaled forms in its drug indications)
Absorption / bioavailability
Glycine is well absorbed. Oral NAC has low and variable systemic bioavailability as intact NAC because of extensive first-pass gut/liver metabolism, but it is efficient at delivering cysteine to tissues; plasma cysteine and downstream RBC GSH rise measurably after supplementation.
Half-life
NAC plasma half-life is short (hours). The functionally relevant readout is intracellular GSH, which builds over days to weeks of dosing and declines over weeks after stopping.
Metabolism
NAC is rapidly deacetylated to L-cysteine, which enters GSH synthesis or is further metabolized (e.g., to sulfate, taurine). Glycine enters one-carbon metabolism and multiple biosynthetic pools. GSH turns over intracellularly via the γ-glutamyl cycle.
Excretion
Renal excretion of sulfate/inorganic metabolites; glycine largely metabolized rather than excreted intact.
Notes
Because the target is a slowly-filling intracellular pool, effects are cumulative over ~2 weeks (biochemistry) to 16-24 weeks (functional endpoints) and reverse on withdrawal — kinetics of the pool, not of the plasma drug, govern the clinical picture.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Biochemical restoration of GSH synthesis is seen within ~2 weeks (Sekhar 2011 isotope study; PMID 21795440). Functional/clinical improvements accrued over 16-24 weeks in the trials. In the open-label pilot, stopping GlyNAC for 12 weeks let benefits decline back toward baseline, indicating an ongoing-exposure, roughly duration-dependent effect rather than a durable one-time reset (PMID 33783984). No formal human dose-ranging or ceiling study exists; the trials used a fixed glycine + NAC regimen scaled to body weight. This monograph intentionally gives no specific doses.

How it compares

  • vs NAC alone
    same: Both raise cysteine availability and can boost GSH; NAC alone also scavenges radicals directly
    different: Baylor's rationale is that both glycine AND cysteine are deficient in aging, so NAC alone leaves glycine co-limiting; GlyNAC supplies both. No head-to-head GlyNAC-vs-NAC-alone RCT exists, so the incremental benefit of adding glycine over NAC alone is inferred, not proven
  • vs Direct oral glutathione
    same: Goal is higher intracellular GSH
    different: Oral GSH is largely broken down in the gut and has poor/variable effect on intracellular pools; supplying precursors leverages the cell's own synthesis machinery. Liposomal/sublingual GSH is an alternative delivery approach with its own (limited) evidence
  • vs Glycine alone
    same: Provides one of the two limiting substrates and has independent metabolic/sleep effects
    different: Without a cysteine donor, glycine alone cannot maximally restore GSH when cysteine is the tighter constraint
  • vs NR/NMN (NAD+ boosters)
    same: Both are marketed as 'longevity/metabolic' interventions targeting mitochondrial function in aging
    different: Different node: NAD+ precursors target the NAD+/sirtuin/energy-cofactor axis; GlyNAC targets the glutathione/redox axis. Positioned as mechanistically complementary, not interchangeable — but combination benefit in humans is untested

History & legal status

The program originated at Baylor College of Medicine (Rajagopal Sekhar, using Farook Jahoor's stable-isotope methods). Milestones: 2010 Diabetes Care and 2011 Am J Clin Nutr isotope studies established that GSH deficiency in uncontrolled diabetes and in aging stems from reduced synthesis and is correctable by cysteine+glycine; 2013 Aging Cell linked GSH to mitochondrial fat oxidation and insulin resistance (causal evidence largely rodent); the acronym 'GlyNAC' and the aging-hallmark framing came with the 2021 open-label pilot (Clin Transl Med), the 2021 J Nutr review, the 2022 mouse lifespan study, and the placebo-controlled RCT (J Gerontol A, PMID 35975308, NCT01870193, print 2023). Regulatory status: glycine and NAC are sold as dietary supplements; GlyNAC is not an FDA-approved drug and carries no approved disease indication. Note the FDA's contested position on NAC's supplement status (post-2020 warning letters), which has created marketing uncertainty for NAC-containing products. NAC separately is an approved drug (acetaminophen antidote, mucolytic). Registered GlyNAC/precursor trials extend to mild cognitive impairment (NCT03493178, Baylor), Alzheimer's disease (NCT04740580, Baylor), and a Vanderbilt Phase 4 RCT of perioperative GlyNAC to reduce oxidative stress and chronic post-surgical pain after total knee arthroplasty (NCT06083480).

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether benefits replicate in large, multi-center, independent (non-Baylor) RCTs — nearly all human efficacy data come from one lab
  • Whether adding glycine to NAC gives real incremental benefit over NAC alone (no head-to-head human trial)
  • Whether the human data reflect the proposed causal chain, since the direct-regulator steps are proven mainly in mice
  • Any effect on hard clinical outcomes (falls, disability, cardiovascular events, mortality) — none measured in humans
  • Whether the mouse lifespan extension has any human longevity correlate — unknown and not directly testable soon
  • Optimal duration, whether tolerance develops, and long-term (>1 year) safety
  • Whether high-dose thiol antioxidants blunt exercise/training adaptations in athletic populations
  • Interactions with nitrates/antihypertensives (additive BP lowering) and safety in advanced renal/hepatic disease

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

What to watch rather than how to dose: (1) GI tolerance — nausea/loose stools are the most common issue; (2) blood pressure — NAC can add to BP-lowering, relevant for anyone on antihypertensives or nitrates; (3) in diabetes, glucose/insulin trends since insulin sensitivity may shift; (4) for athletes, be aware of the theoretical (unproven for GlyNAC) blunting of exercise-induced adaptations; (5) caution and clinician involvement in significant kidney or liver disease given the amino-acid/thiol load; (6) NAC-drug interactions (e.g., possible effect on nitroglycerin response). Objective response can be tracked with the oxidative-stress and metabolic biomarkers used in the trials (F2-isoprostanes/d-ROMs, fasting insulin/HOMA-IR, inflammatory markers) plus functional measures (grip strength, gait speed). This is monitoring guidance, not a protocol, and does not replace individualized medical advice.

Caveats

The human efficacy data are genuinely encouraging but thin: essentially two small trials (~20 older adults on active supplement across both) from a single lab, with the larger placebo-controlled but only 16 weeks and 12 per arm. Broad 'reverses aging hallmarks' framing outruns the evidence — many outcomes are surrogate biomarkers or short-term functional tests, effect sizes need independent replication, and there is heavy publication concentration in one group. Crucially, the causal/mechanistic steps (GSH as a direct regulator of mitochondrial fat oxidation and insulin resistance) are proven mainly in mice; the human trials show association and reversal, not proof of the chain. The dramatic ~24% lifespan extension is mouse-only (single strain) and must not be read as a human longevity claim. Benefits declined after supplementation was withdrawn (pilot), implying continuous use would be needed. NAC is a potent thiol that can lower blood pressure and, in some contexts, may blunt exercise-induced antioxidant adaptations; combined high-dose glycine plus NAC carries an amino-acid/nitrogen load relevant to renal/hepatic caution. This monograph describes mechanism and what to monitor — it is not medical advice and contains no dosing protocol.
