Monlunabant

A second-generation, would-be "peripheral-only" CB1 blocker that revives the rimonabant fat-loss mechanism and delivers real weight loss in Phase 2 - but new (rodent) data suggest it still reaches brain CB1, so the old psychiatric-safety promise is not yet proven in humans.

Limited human data 13 citations
Class
Peripherally-restricted cannabinoid receptor 1 (CB1R) inverse agonist; oral small-molecule; second-generation successor to rimonabant
Primary target
Cannabinoid receptor type 1 (CB1R, CNR1 gene) - a Gi/o-coupled GPCR
Human status
Clinical-stage / Phase 2 (pipeline). Developed by Inversago Pharma (Montreal), which Novo Nordisk acquired in August 2023 for up to ~US$1.075 billion, largely to obtain monlunabant (business fact; not a peer-reviewed citation). Human data to date: a Phase 1b metabolic-syndrome study (NCT05282446, n=37, INV-202 25 mg vs placebo, 28 days; Crater et al., Diabetes Obes Metab 2024, PMID 37941317) and a Phase 2a obesity/metabolic-syndrome dose-ranging trial (NCT05891834, n=243, 10/20/50 mg, 16 weeks; Knop et al., Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2025, PMID 41038215) both showed significant weight loss. A separate Phase 2 trial in diabetic kidney disease (NCT05514548, full analysis set n=254 of 265 enrolled; Cherney et al., Kidney Int 2026, PMID 41866124) FAILED its primary albuminuria (UACR) endpoint. A Phase 1 single-dose PK study in Japanese vs Caucasian men (NCT06542536) completed under Novo Nordisk in 2024. NOT approved for any indication anywhere; not available by prescription; no established human dosing regimen for weight management. Novo Nordisk publicly signaled the Phase 2a results were below expectations relative to incretin drugs, and the program's future scope (e.g., pursuing only low doses) is uncertain as of 2025-2026.

At a glance

  • Placebo-adjusted weight loss ~6.4-8.0 kg over 16 weeks (Phase 2a, all three doses); on-drug loss ~3.5 kg (3.3%) over 28 days at 25 mg in Phase 1b (placebo gained ~0.6 kg)
  • Reductions in waist circumference and BMI; signals of improved cardiometabolic/lipid markers (Phase 1b)
  • Mechanistically lowers hepatic fat synthesis (SREBP-1c/lipogenesis) and improves leptin/insulin sensitivity (preclinical, rodent)
  • Dose-dependent GI side effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) - the expected hallmark of CB1 blockade in the gut
  • Dose-dependent PSYCHIATRIC side effects (anxiety, irritability, sleep disorder) - the same class signal that sank rimonabant
  • High dose-dependent dropout: withdrawals due to adverse events up to 42% at 50 mg vs 0% on placebo (Phase 2a)
  • FAILED to meet its primary endpoint (albuminuria/UACR reduction) in the Phase 2 diabetic kidney disease trial

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Obesity raises endocannabinoid 'tone' at CB1 Established

    In obesity, levels of the body's own cannabinoids (anandamide and 2-AG) rise in fat, liver and other metabolic tissues, and CB1 receptors there are chronically over-activated. This overactive endocannabinoid system pushes the body toward storing fat, making more fat in the liver, and becoming resistant to insulin and leptin. Monlunabant is built to switch this signal off in the periphery. PMID 33595385; PMID 22780328

  2. Monlunabant binds CB1 and acts as an inverse agonist Established

    Monlunabant sits in the same pocket of the CB1 receptor that THC and the body's own cannabinoids use. As an 'inverse agonist' it does more than just block the receptor - it pushes the receptor's baseline activity below normal, silencing even the constitutive (agonist-free) signaling. Cryo-EM structures of this peripherally-restricted class (MRI-1867/MRI-1891, close chemical cousins - not monlunabant itself) show how polar groups let such molecules keep tight CB1 binding while limiting brain entry. PMID 39695122; PMID 23902803

  3. Turning off hepatic CB1 cuts new fat synthesis in the liver Evidence-rodent

    CB1 receptors on liver cells normally drive 'de novo lipogenesis' - the liver making new fat from sugar - partly by turning up the master fat-making switch SREBP-1c and its downstream enzymes (FAS, ACC). Blocking hepatic CB1 lowers this program, reducing liver fat and improving how the liver handles glucose and lipids. This is a core rationale for using peripheral CB1 blockers in fatty liver and metabolic disease. Demonstrated mechanistically in mice. PMID 15864349

  4. Turning off adipose CB1 reverses leptin resistance Evidence-rodent

    In diet-induced obese mice, a peripherally-restricted CB1 inverse agonist (JD5037, a research prototype - not monlunabant) lowered fat mass and appetite as well as a brain-penetrant version did - by re-sensitizing the body to its own leptin. It did this by reducing fat-cell leptin production/secretion and speeding leptin clearance through the kidney, so the satiety signal works again. This reframed part of CB1-driven weight loss as a peripheral, leptin-mediated effect rather than a purely brain effect. PMID 22841573

  5. In the kidney, CB1 blockade dampens fibrosis and albuminuria (preclinical only) Evidence-rodent

    In streptozotocin-induced type-1 diabetic mice, monlunabant/INV-202 reduced albumin leakage into urine, protected podocytes (the kidney's filter cells), lowered local angiotensin-II and AT1-receptor (Agtr1a) signaling, and reduced oxidative-stress (Nox2/Nox4/p47phox) and fibrosis - without changing body weight. This suggested a possible organ-protective use in diabetic kidney disease, separate from weight loss. IMPORTANT: this benefit did NOT translate in the human Phase 2 kidney trial (see caveats). PMID 37675364

  6. Net result in humans: real weight loss over 16 weeks Evidence-human

    In adults with obesity and metabolic syndrome, once-daily oral monlunabant produced clear, placebo-adjusted weight loss of about 6-8 kg over 16 weeks across the 10, 20 and 50 mg doses - confirming the CB1 fat-loss mechanism works in humans. Notably, higher doses gave only slightly more weight loss but a lot more side effects, hinting the useful window may be at the low end. PMID 41038215; PMID 37941317

  7. The 'peripheral-only' claim is contested - rodent data suggest it still hits brain CB1 Contested

    A 2025 mouse study directly challenged the marketing story: monlunabant suppressed appetite and blocked a CB1-agonist effect (HU-210-induced hypothermia) at the SAME doses, a pattern that only makes sense if it is reaching brain CB1 receptors. Lower doses that would still saturate peripheral receptors did NOT suppress appetite. The authors conclude appetite suppression is mainly central and warn monlunabant 'may carry a similar risk of adverse psychiatric effects' as rimonabant. This is a single rodent behavioural study, but it aligns with the dose-dependent anxiety, irritability and sleep problems seen in the human Phase 2a trial. PMID 39969070; PMID 41038215

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Orthosteric CB1R inverse agonist. Binds the same hydrophobic ligand pocket used by endocannabinoids (anandamide, 2-AG) and THC. As an inverse agonist it suppresses CB1R's constitutive (agonist-independent) Gi/o signaling below baseline, not merely competitively blocking agonists. Its peripherally-restricted chemotype is designed to limit blood-brain-barrier penetration while preserving high CB1R affinity - the same design principle characterized structurally for the sibling compounds MRI-1867/MRI-1891 by cryo-EM (PMID 39695122). Note: the published cryo-EM structures are of the sibling peripheral inverse agonists, not of monlunabant itself.

Signalling cascade. CB1R is Gi/o-coupled: agonism normally inhibits adenylyl cyclase (lowers cAMP), modulates MAPK, and can recruit beta-arrestin. Inverse agonism reverses tonic Gi/o signaling. Downstream in metabolic tissue this de-represses catabolic pathways and, in rodents, down-regulates the hepatic lipogenic transcription factor SREBP-1c (and targets FAS/ACC), lowering de novo lipogenesis; in adipose it reduces leptin secretion and restores leptin signaling; peripherally it improves insulin sensitivity. Some newer peripheral CB1 ligands are also biased against beta-arrestin, which may matter for on-target tolerability (PMID 39695122).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral, once daily (tablet).
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally bioavailable; administered as a once-daily oral tablet across Phase 1b and Phase 2 trials (10-50 mg once daily studied). Quantitative human bioavailability not publicly detailed.
Half-life
Consistent with once-daily dosing; a dedicated single-dose PK study measured terminal half-life (NCT06542536) but the exact human t1/2 is not published in the retrieved peer-reviewed literature.
Metabolism
Not fully disclosed in the public peer-reviewed literature; a small-molecule CB1 ligand. Trial exclusion criteria barred strong CYP3A4/2D6/2C19 inducers/inhibitors (NCT05891834, NCT05514548), implying clinically relevant CYP-mediated metabolism, but detailed CYP/metabolite data are not published.
Excretion
Not publicly detailed for humans.
Notes
A dedicated Phase 1 single-dose PK study compared PK in Japanese vs Caucasian men (NCT06542536, Novo Nordisk, completed 2024), implying attention to ethnic PK differences for global development. The intended pharmacologic distinction from rimonabant is tissue distribution (peripheral restriction), but rodent data (PMID 39969070) suggest meaningful CNS exposure at efficacious doses, so any 'restriction' appears quantitative rather than absolute.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

In Phase 2a (PMID 41038215), placebo-adjusted weight loss was -6.4 kg (10 mg; 95% CI -8.0 to -4.9), -6.9 kg (20 mg; -8.5 to -5.3), and -8.0 kg (50 mg; -9.7 to -6.4) at 16 weeks - a strikingly FLAT efficacy curve where quadrupling the dose added only ~1.6 kg. In contrast, harm was steeply dose-dependent: any-adverse-event rates rose from 69% (10 mg) to 78% (20 mg) to 92% (50 mg) (placebo was also 69%), and withdrawals due to adverse events climbed from 13% (10 mg) to 27% (20 mg) to 42% (50 mg), versus 0% on placebo. This dissociation (flat benefit, steep harm) is the central dose-response story and is why the authors call for testing LOWER doses. Phase 1b at 25 mg over 28 days gave an on-drug loss of 3.5 kg / 3.3% vs placebo gaining 0.6 kg (PMID 37941317). The rodent appetite data (PMID 39969070) add nuance: only higher doses (that spill into the CNS) suppressed appetite, while peripheral-saturating low doses did not - implying the weight-loss dose range and the CNS-risk dose range may substantially overlap.

How it compares

  • vs Rimonabant (first-generation, brain-penetrant, withdrawn 2008)
    same: Same molecular target (CB1 inverse agonism) and same core fat-loss mechanism; both produce GI and psychiatric adverse events
    different: Monlunabant was designed for peripheral restriction to avoid CNS harm - but rodent data (PMID 39969070) suggest it still engages brain CB1, so the intended safety advantage is unproven in humans; monlunabant's human weight loss (~6-8 kg/16 wk) is real but its dose-dependent psychiatric-AE signal is concerning
  • vs GLP-1 / GIP incretins (semaglutide, tirzepatide)
    same: Anti-obesity agents targeting metabolic disease; both reduce weight and improve cardiometabolic markers
    different: Incretins achieve substantially greater and more dose-scalable weight loss with a better-characterized safety profile; monlunabant's efficacy is modest and flat, and Novo Nordisk (which owns both) publicly framed monlunabant's Phase 2a as underwhelming versus its incretin franchise
  • vs JD5037 / MRI-1867 / MRI-1891 (research peripheral CB1 tools)
    same: Same peripherally-restricted CB1 inverse-agonist strategy; provide the preclinical and structural rationale
    different: These are experimental/tool compounds (some multi-target, e.g., MRI-1867 also inhibits iNOS); monlunabant is the most clinically advanced peripheral CB1 agent, with actual Phase 2 human weight-loss data
  • vs Nimacimab (anti-CB1 monoclonal antibody, peripheral)
    same: Both aim to block peripheral CB1 for metabolic disease while sparing the CNS
    different: Nimacimab is a large antibody largely excluded from the brain (a physically 'cleaner' peripheral-restriction approach), whereas monlunabant is a small molecule whose brain exclusion appears incomplete. (Nimacimab positioning is a pipeline/business fact, not a peer-reviewed head-to-head.)

History &amp; legal status

The CB1-antagonist obesity story began with rimonabant (Acomplia), approved in Europe (2006) but never in the US; it was withdrawn in 2008 after depression/anxiety/suicidality signals, and taranabant and other candidates were halted for the same reason (PMID 21951371; PMID 24846238). This CNS-toxicity lesson drove a second generation of peripherally-restricted CB1 inverse agonists (JD5037, TM38837, and the NIH/NIAAA MRI-1867/1891 series). Monlunabant (INV-202) was developed by Inversago Pharma (Montreal), a company built around peripheral CB1 blockade. On the strength of early metabolic and kidney data, Novo Nordisk acquired Inversago in August 2023 for up to ~US$1.075 billion, principally to obtain monlunabant (business fact; not a peer-reviewed citation). The Phase 2a obesity readout (2024-2025) showed weight loss but a modest, flat dose-response with psychiatric AEs, and the Phase 2 diabetic-kidney-disease trial failed its primary endpoint - tempering enthusiasm. No regulatory approval exists; the program's future (including whether only low doses are pursued) is undecided as of 2025-2026.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether monlunabant is truly 'peripheral-only' in humans at efficacious doses, or whether it engages enough brain CB1 to carry rimonabant-like psychiatric risk (rodent data suggest the latter, but direct human CNS-occupancy data are lacking)
  • Long-term neuropsychiatric safety - all human trials are <=16 weeks and excluded people with recent depression/suicidality, so depression/suicidality risk cannot be characterized from these studies
  • Whether a low-dose 'sweet spot' exists that keeps weight loss while minimizing CNS/GI harm (the authors' proposed next step)
  • Durability and magnitude of weight loss beyond 16 weeks, and how it compares head-to-head with incretins
  • Cardiovascular and hepatic (MASLD/MASH) outcomes - mechanistically plausible but not demonstrated clinically
  • Detailed human pharmacokinetics/metabolism (CYP pathways, metabolites, exact half-life) are not fully public
  • Whether the failed DKD endpoint reflects true lack of renal benefit or trial-design issues (large placebo response, high variability, as the authors note)
  • Novo Nordisk's development intentions - whether monlunabant advances to Phase 3 for obesity at all

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For clinicians/researchers evaluating this class (NOT a usage protocol and NOT dosing guidance): the two dominant monitorable domains are (1) NEUROPSYCHIATRIC - baseline and ongoing screening for depression, anxiety, irritability, sleep disturbance and any suicidal ideation, given the class history and monlunabant's dose-dependent psychiatric adverse events; this is the single most important safety signal and would warrant stopping. (2) GASTROINTESTINAL tolerability - nausea, vomiting, diarrhea drive early dropout and dehydration risk. It is also reasonable to track body weight/waist trajectory (efficacy), and, because trials measured them, metabolic labs (lipids, glucose/HbA1c) and, in any renal context, urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio and eGFR. Individuals with a personal/family history of depression, anxiety, or other psychiatric illness are the group in whom CB1 blockade is most concerning (and were excluded from the trials). Because this is an unapproved investigational drug, the only appropriate 'monitoring' setting is a supervised clinical trial - there is no validated at-home use, and no dosing guidance should be inferred from this entry.

Caveats

This is a PIPELINE compound - not approved, not proven safe long-term, and not available legally as a supplement or medicine. Treat any online 'research chemical' sold under this name with extreme suspicion (identity/purity unverified). Four honest caveats dominate: (1) The central 'peripheral-only = psychiatrically safe' selling point is CONTESTED. A 2025 rodent behavioural study (PMID 39969070) indicates monlunabant reaches and blocks brain CB1 at the doses that suppress appetite, and the Phase 2a human trial (PMID 41038215) reported dose-dependent psychiatric adverse events (anxiety, irritability, sleep disorder) plus withdrawals up to 42% at the top dose - echoing why rimonabant was withdrawn. The 'we fixed rimonabant' narrative is not yet supported by human safety data (and the direct CNS-engagement evidence is so far rodent-only). (2) Efficacy, while real (~6-8 kg placebo-adjusted over 16 weeks), is modest and roughly flat across doses, and is generally considered underwhelming versus GLP-1/GIP incretins (semaglutide, tirzepatide) that achieve larger loss - which is why Novo Nordisk downplayed the readout. (3) Organ-protection hopes did not translate: the mouse kidney benefit failed in the human DKD Phase 2 (large placebo response and high variability complicate interpretation). (4) All human trials are short (<=16 weeks) and modest in size; nothing is known about long-term neuropsychiatric safety, cardiovascular outcomes, or durability. This entry describes mechanism and evidence only - it is not medical advice and contains no dosing protocol.
