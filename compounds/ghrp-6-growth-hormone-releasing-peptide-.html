GHRP-6

A synthetic hexapeptide that tricks the body's hunger-and-growth-hormone switch (the ghrelin receptor) into firing — driving a pulse of growth hormone and a strong spike in appetite. Almost all of its 'tissue-repair' hype is rodent and dish data, not human.

Limited human data 36 citations
Class
Growth hormone secretagogue (GHS); ghrelin-receptor (GHS-R1a) agonist — a peptide, not a hormone the body makes
Primary target
Growth hormone secretagogue receptor type 1a (GHS-R1a) — the same G-protein-coupled receptor that the natural hunger hormone ghrelin activates; expressed in the hypothalamus and pituitary
Human status
Not an approved drug for body composition, anti-aging, or healing. In humans GHRP-6 has been studied chiefly as a diagnostic agent to provoke a GH pulse and test for GH deficiency (PMID 11030292, 11980622), plus a single-dose pharmacokinetic study in 9 healthy men (PMID 23099431). There are NO published human efficacy trials supporting the muscle-building, fat-loss, joint-repair or anti-aging claims common in fitness/peptide communities — those rest on rodent and cell studies. It is sold only as a 'research chemical,' is not pharmaceutical-grade for consumer use, and is a WADA-prohibited substance (detectable in urine; PMID 25869809).

At a glance

  • Acute pulse of growth hormone release (human-confirmed; basis of its use as a GH-deficiency diagnostic test, not a therapy)
  • Strong increase in appetite/food intake (ghrelin-like; well shown in animals)
  • Synergy with GHRH — much larger GH pulse when combined; weak/absent when the hypothalamus is bypassed
  • Preclinical tissue protection and repair: cardioprotection, anti-organ-failure, wound healing, neuroprotection — rodent/in-vitro only
  • Possible transient effects on cortisol/prolactin and water balance reported with GHS class (watch in any human use)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a) Established

    GHRP-6 was designed to mimic the body's own hunger hormone, ghrelin. It docks onto the ghrelin receptor (GHS-R1a) on cells in the hypothalamus and pituitary — the same lock ghrelin uses. This is the master switch for everything downstream. PMID 34417468 (cryo-EM of ghrelin receptor activation); PMID 1552831 (GHRP binding to pituitary/hypothalamic membranes); PMID 11322507 (GHS-R family and ligands)

  2. Fires the receptor's internal calcium/PLC signal Evidence-in vitro

    Once bound, the receptor activates the phospholipase-C pathway inside the cell (IP3/DAG), releasing calcium. That calcium surge is the trigger that makes a pituitary cell dump growth hormone. This is the molecular 'go' signal. PMID 8903947 (GHRP-6 activates IP3/DAG pathway in rat pituitary cells); PMID 11518797 (GHRP-6 regulates Pit-1 via GHS-R)

  3. Works mainly at the hypothalamus, and needs your own GHRH Evidence-human

    GHRP-6 is not a standalone 'GH injection.' Human studies show its main action is up in the hypothalamus, and it needs the body's own growth-hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH) present to produce a big GH pulse — that's why GHRP-6 + GHRH together hit far harder than either alone. In people whose hypothalamus is disconnected from the pituitary, GHRP-6 barely works. PMID 7883854 (no effect with hypothalamo-pituitary disconnection); PMID 9543138 (requires endogenous GHRH for maximal GH)

  4. Triggers a pulse of growth hormone (and indirectly IGF-1) Evidence-human

    The net result in humans is a sharp, short-lived burst of growth hormone release. Sustained GH would in turn raise IGF-1 from the liver — but GHRP-6's GH effect is a pulse, which is exactly why it was studied as a diagnostic test for GH deficiency rather than as a long-term GH therapy. PMID 7920995 (GHRP-6 regulation of GH secretion); PMID 11030292 (GHRH + GHRP-6 as GH-deficiency diagnostic test, Lancet); PMID 11980622 (GHRP-6 +/- GHRH vs insulin tolerance test)

  5. Drives appetite — the signature ghrelin-like effect Evidence-rodent

    Because it hits the ghrelin receptor, GHRP-6 also reproduces ghrelin's hunger signal: it strongly increases food intake. This is well documented in animal models and is the effect users most reliably notice. It is a feature of the receptor, not a side benefit unique to the compound. PMID 22349352 (GHRP-6 mimics ghrelin-induced food-intake stimulation); PMID 15814848 (review: GHS and appetite)

  6. Activates cell-survival pathways in injured tissue (preclinical) Evidence-rodent

    In animal and lab models, GHRP-6 switches on 'pro-survival' programs that protect cells from dying after injury — studied in heart damage, organ failure, wound healing and stroke. This is the basis of the 'healing/cytoprotective' claims online. Important: this is almost entirely rodent and in-vitro work, not proven in humans. PMID 16417467 (prevention of multiple organ failure); PMID 38873418 (protects against doxorubicin heart damage); PMID 27200188 (improves wound healing); PMID 27665924 (neuroprotection in experimental stroke)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. GHRP-6 is a synthetic hexapeptide, His-D-Trp-Ala-Trp-D-Phe-Lys-NH2 (development code SK&F 110679), and is the prototype of the growth-hormone-releasing peptide / growth hormone secretagogue (GHS) class. PRIMARY TARGET: the growth hormone secretagogue receptor type 1a (GHS-R1a / GHSR; UniProt Q92847, gene GHSR), a class-A (rhodopsin-like) 7-transmembrane Gq/11-coupled GPCR of 366 amino acids expressed on pituitary somatotrophs, hypothalamic arcuate neurons (NPY/AgRP), and peripheral tissues (stomach, heart, vasculature). [Established] Affinity: GHRP-6 binds GHS-R1a in the low-nanomolar range (literature Kd/Ki on the order of ~6 nM; the same receptor is the endogenous ghrelin receptor). [Evidence-human/in vitro] CRITICAL NUANCE — GHRP-6 is a BIASED, partial/allosteric agonist, not a clean full agonist: in a head-to-head signaling study its measured potency varied ~600-fold across readouts (EC50 ~0.1–61 nM depending on the assay — calcium mobilization vs IP turnover vs CRE/SRE transcription vs arrestin), and it behaved as a NEGATIVE allosteric modulator of ghrelin's own signaling, i.e. it can blunt the response to co-present ghrelin while still activating the receptor itself (citation PMID 15905359). This contrasts with MK-677/ibutamoren, which acts as a simple, balanced full agonist (affinity 6.5 nM; uniform 0.2–1.4 nM potency across all readouts). [Evidence-in vitro] SECONDARY / NON-GHSR target: like its analog hexarelin, GHRP-6 also interacts with the scavenger receptor CD36 (relevant to cardiovascular/anti-ischemic effects), a distinct, GHS-R1a-independent binding site. [Evidence-rodent] SELECTIVITY: not 'specific' for GH — it also drives ACTH, cortisol and prolactin release (see side effects), reflecting receptor expression beyond somatotrophs. The widely used antagonist tool [D-Lys3]-GHRP-6 blocks GHS-R1a in vitro/in rodents but notably FAILED to antagonize ghrelin/hexarelin responses in humans (PMID 17112585), a caution about extrapolating rodent receptor pharmacology to people.

Signalling cascade. Mechanistically, GHRP-6 binding to GHS-R1a couples predominantly to Gq/11 → activation of phospholipase C-beta (PLCβ) → hydrolysis of PIP2 into inositol-1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP3) and diacylglycerol (DAG). IP3 releases Ca2+ from endoplasmic-reticulum stores (raising intracellular [Ca2+]i) and DAG activates protein kinase C (PKC). In pituitary somatotrophs this Ca2+/PKC rise, together with attenuation of K+ conductances and opening of voltage-gated Ca2+ channels, triggers exocytotic release of stored growth hormone. GHS-R1a is also notable for very high CONSTITUTIVE (ligand-independent) activity (~50% of maximal), so agonists like GHRP-6 act on a receptor that is already partly signaling. Beyond Gq, GHS-R1a recruits β-arrestin (driving desensitization/internalization and arrestin-biased transcriptional readouts such as SRE) and can engage Gi/o and 13 pathways context-dependently. A second, complementary mechanism operates centrally: GHRP-6 acts on the hypothalamus to amplify GHRH release and to functionally oppose somatostatin (the inhibitory tone), which is why GHRP-6 + GHRH produce strongly SYNERGISTIC GH output — more than the sum of each alone. In cardiac and other peripheral tissue the same PLC/PKC cascade modulates ion currents (e.g. inhibition of the transient outward K+ current Ito, prolonging action-potential duration) independently of cAMP/PKA (PMID 20056829).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Parenteral peptide — used investigationally by IV bolus, subcutaneous and intranasal routes; modestly orally active at high doses (an unusual feature for a peptide). No approved human dosage form exists. [Established]
Absorption / bioavailability
Subcutaneous bioavailability is high and near-complete: ~89–103% in rat and dog for GHRP-6 (SK&F 110679), with the apparent ~1 h terminal half-life after SC reflecting slow absorption from the injection depot rather than slow elimination (PMID 8149896). Intranasal absorption occurs but is comparatively LOW for GHRP-6 relative to other GHRPs (GHRP-2 ~50%, ipamorelin ~20%; GHRP-6 'somewhat lower') (PMID 9879640). Oral GHRP-6 retains GH-releasing activity in humans even after short-term oral pretreatment, though oral potency is far below parenteral (PMID 7952160). [Evidence-human/animal]
Half-life
Short. After IV bolus, plasma levels decline biexponentially with an initial (distribution) half-life of only ~2–4 minutes (rat/dog; PMID 8149896). The biological GH-releasing effect outlasts plasma exposure but is still acute (GH peak within ~15–30 min, resolving over a couple of hours). No validated human terminal-half-life figure is established. [Evidence-rodent; human gap]
Metabolism
Proteolytic cleavage by peptidases rather than CYP metabolism; a substantial fraction is excreted as INTACT peptide. Characterized urinary metabolites (used for anti-doping detection) include shorter fragments — e.g. His-D-Trp-Ala-Trp-D-Phe and related cleavage products — identified by mass spectrometry after nasal dosing (PMID 25869809). [Evidence-human]
Excretion
Predominantly BILIARY (hepatic) excretion for GHRP-6, contrasting with the more urinary route of some analogs (e.g. ipamorelin). In bile-duct-exteriorized rats ~60–70% of the dose appeared in bile, largely as intact peptide, after both IV and SC dosing (PMID 8149896; PMID 9879640). Systemic plasma clearance ~27 mL/min/kg (rat) and ~17 mL/min/kg (dog). [Evidence-rodent]
Notes
GHRP-6 is itself the active drug (not an ester or prodrug); D-amino acids (D-Trp, D-Phe) confer protease resistance and the unusual oral activity. It crosses into the CNS sufficiently to engage hypothalamic ghrelin signaling — intranasal GHS-R ligands demonstrably activate the brain ghrelin system in mice (PMID 39813130), consistent with central appetite/GH effects. Because it stimulates endogenous pulsatile GH (rather than supplying exogenous GH), effects are gated by the somatotroph reserve and physiologic feedback. [Established/Evidence-rodent]

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

GH RELEASE (humans): In the GHRP class, IV doses of ~1 µg/kg already produce a strong GH pulse, and ~2 µg/kg gives a larger response than 1 µg/kg (clear dose-dependence in the 1–2 µg/kg IV range for the closely related GHRP-2/hexarelin; GHRP-6 behaves similarly but is somewhat less potent) (PMID 9285939). GHRP-6's GH-releasing activity is preserved in normal aging and after oral pretreatment, indicating no rapid loss of somatotroph responsiveness at the doses studied (PMID 7952160). SYNERGY: combining a GHRP with GHRH yields GH peaks that EXCEED the arithmetic sum of each alone — e.g. in GH-deficient children, GHRP-1+GHRH gave a peak ~34 µg/L vs ~7.5 (GHRP alone) and ~11 (GHRH alone) µg/L, with response rate rising from ~60% to ~86% (PMID 7745018). APPETITE/FOOD INTAKE (humans): demonstrated for the analog GHRP-2 — SC infusion ~1 µg/kg/h increased ad libitum energy intake by ~36% vs saline in lean men, with every subject eating more (PMID 15699539); GHRP-6 is a well-established orexigen in animals via the same GHS-R1a/NPY-AgRP pathway, but a clean human dose–response curve for GHRP-6 appetite specifically is NOT established. CORTICOTROPIC AXIS (humans): GHRP-6/hexarelin reproducibly raise ACTH and cortisol; in Cushing's disease GHRP-6 elicited marked ACTH/cortisol release (PMID 12809173). HUMAN DOSE-RESPONSE GAP: there are essentially NO modern, controlled human dose-titration studies of GHRP-6 for body-composition or performance endpoints; nearly all human data are acute single/few-dose neuroendocrine challenge studies from the 1990s–2000s, and almost all chronic/efficacy data are rodent. Quantitative human dose–response for muscle, fat, IGF-1 or recovery outcomes is unknown. [Evidence-human for acute GH/ACTH; Evidence-rodent for chronic; large human efficacy gap]

How it compares

  • vs GHRP-2 (pralmorelin)
    same: Same class (GHS hexapeptide, GHS-R1a agonist); both drive pulsatile GH plus ACTH/cortisol/prolactin and appetite; both have short half-lives and are WADA-banned.
    different: GHRP-2 is more potent/efficacious for GH release per microgram and has better intranasal bioavailability (~50% vs GHRP-6's lower value); GHRP-6 is the more notorious 'hunger' peptide subjectively, while GHRP-2 has the strongest direct human food-intake data (PMID 15699539; PMID 9879640).
  • vs Hexarelin
    same: Direct GHRP-6 analog (His-D-2-methyl-Trp substitution); GHS-R1a agonist; also binds CD36; raises GH, ACTH, cortisol, prolactin.
    different: More potent GH secretagogue than GHRP-6 and a stronger HPA/sleep-architecture disruptor (decreases slow-wave sleep); shows more prominent cardiac/CD36-mediated effects and is more prone to desensitization with continuous dosing (PMID 15177700; PMID 28321024).
  • vs MK-677 (ibutamoren)
    same: Same receptor (GHS-R1a), same downstream GH/IGF-1 elevation and appetite/water-retention/insulin-resistance liabilities.
    different: MK-677 is a non-peptide, ORALLY active full agonist with a LONG half-life (~24 h) giving sustained once-daily GH/IGF-1 elevation and balanced (non-biased) signaling; GHRP-6 is a peptide, biased/partial agonist with minutes-long half-life giving sharp pulses. MK-677's sustained exposure makes desensitization and metabolic effects more clinically relevant (PMID 15905359).
  • vs Ghrelin (endogenous ligand)
    same: Same receptor and core Gq/PLC/PKC/Ca2+ cascade; both stimulate GH and appetite.
    different: Ghrelin is a 28-aa acyl-modified hormone (requires octanoylation for activity) and is the full physiologic agonist; GHRP-6 is a small synthetic hexapeptide that binds a different receptor subsite and can act as a NEGATIVE allosteric modulator of ghrelin's own signaling, plus has practical oral/parenteral usability ghrelin lacks (PMID 15905359).

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • T3 compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Both stress glucose handling: GHRP-6's GH elevation is diabetogenic (counter-regulatory, lipolytic, anti-insulin), and T3 independently increases hepatic glucose output and can worsen insulin resistance. Stacked, they compound the drift toward hyperglycemia and impaired glucose tolerance. GH-axis manipulation can also shift thyroid hormone economy, so the two additionally interact on the thyroid/metabolic axis. Monograph side-effect table + monitoring (GH insulin resistance; GH-axis shifts thyroid economy); GH counter-regulatory PMID 9893708 Watch: Fasting glucose/HbA1c for compounded insulin resistance; full thyroid panel (TSH, free T4, free T3) since GH stimulation can shift thyroid hormone economy; resting heart rate.
  • CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin potentiates ↑ Evidence-human
    GHRP-6 acts mainly at the hypothalamus, needs endogenous GHRH to fire a large GH pulse, and functionally opposes somatostatin tone. A GHRH-receptor agonist (CJC-1295) supplies that permissive GHRH signal through a separate Gs/cAMP receptor, while GHRP-6 and ipamorelin both drive the Gq/PLC/Ca2+ GHS-R1a pathway. Co-activating the two distinct receptor families on the same somatotroph produces a GH pulse larger than the sum of either alone (the canonical GHRH+GHRP synergy). Note the ipamorelin half of this product is itself a GHS-R1a agonist, so it is partly redundant with GHRP-6 at that receptor. Monograph steps 3-4 + cascade (dual hypothalamic+pituitary GHRH synergy, PMID 9893708); GHRH+GHRP supra-additive GH PMID 7745018; GHRP-6 requires endogenous GHRH PMID 7883854, 9543138 Watch: Serum IGF-1 as the integrated readout of stacked GH stimulation (watch for drift toward supraphysiologic/acromegaly-like levels), fasting glucose/HbA1c, and signs of GH excess (edema, joint pain, carpal-tunnel paresthesias).
  • Sermorelin potentiates ↑ Evidence-human
    Sermorelin is GHRH(1-29), a pure GHRH-receptor agonist. GHRP-6's GH output is gated by available GHRH and is weak/absent when the hypothalamo-pituitary link is bypassed; adding exogenous GHRH restores and amplifies the pulse. The GHRH-R (Gs/cAMP) and GHS-R1a (Gq/Ca2+) pathways converge at the somatotroph, giving supra-additive GH release — the canonical GHRH+GHRP synergy. Monograph step 3 + cascade (PMID 7883854, 9543138, 9893708); GHRH+GHRP synergy PMID 7745018 Watch: IGF-1 trend for the amplified GH signal; fasting glucose/HbA1c for GH-driven insulin resistance; symptoms of fluid retention or joint discomfort from GH/IGF-1 excess.
  • Tesamorelin potentiates ↑ Evidence-human
    Tesamorelin is a stabilized GHRH analog acting on the GHRH receptor. Because GHRP-6 requires endogenous GHRH tone for a maximal pulse, a GHRH-R agonist provides the permissive co-signal, and the two separate receptor pathways (Gs vs Gq) summate at the pituitary to raise GH beyond either alone. Monograph step 3 + cascade (PMID 7883854, 9543138, 9893708); GHRH+GHRP synergy PMID 7745018 Watch: IGF-1 and fasting glucose/HbA1c (both tesamorelin and GH-axis stimulation can worsen glucose handling); watch for edema, arthralgia, paresthesias.
  • Cabergoline counteracts ↓ Evidence-human
    GHRP-6 is a GHS-R1a agonist whose receptor engagement in humans produces prolactin release (alongside ACTH/cortisol) in addition to GH, reflecting receptor expression beyond somatotrophs. Cabergoline lowers prolactin through pituitary D2 agonism, counteracting that GHRP-6-induced prolactin elevation — the same lactotroph endpoint pushed the opposite way. cabergoline.json mechanism_steps#7; ghrp-6 monograph side_effect 'Prolactin elevation' (PMID 9285939; PMID 15177700) Watch: Serum prolactin to verify the elevation and prevent over-suppression. GHRP-6 also raises cortisol/ACTH; cabergoline does not fix that. Monitor for cabergoline's dopaminergic AEs (dizziness on standing, impulse-control behaviors).
  • Insulin opposes Established
    GH released by GHRP-6 is a counter-regulatory, anti-insulin hormone: sustained GH/IGF-1 elevation reduces peripheral insulin sensitivity and raises glucose, directly working against exogenous insulin's glucose-lowering action. The GHS-R1a is also annotated for negative regulation of insulin secretion. The result is a moving insulin-sensitivity target — glucose control becomes harder to predict when GH stimulation and injected insulin are combined. Monograph side-effect table (GH counter-regulatory/anti-insulin; GHSR GO annotation negative regulation of insulin secretion, UniProt Q92847; PMID 9893708) Watch: Fasting glucose, HbA1c, and glucose-response patterns; watch for both rising glucose (GH-driven resistance) and, conversely, hypoglycemia when insulin is present — meaning tighter glucose surveillance is warranted.
  • Semaglutide opposes Evidence-rodent
    GHRP-6's signature effect is strong hunger, driven by GHS-R1a agonism on hypothalamic arcuate NPY/AgRP (orexigenic) neurons — the same circuit ghrelin uses. GLP-1 receptor agonists such as semaglutide act on the opposite side of that circuit: they quiet AgRP neurons and activate POMC neurons to suppress food intake, and slow gastric emptying. So GHRP-6 pushes appetite up while semaglutide pulls it down at the same arcuate node — directly opposing effects on food intake. GHRP-6 appetite via arcuate NPY/AgRP (monograph step 5, side-effect table, PMID 39813130); GLP-1 AgRP-down/POMC-up appetite suppression is rodent-mapped, human net effect observed (semaglutide monograph) Watch: Appetite/food-intake and weight trend as the behavioral readout of the opposing signals; glucose/HbA1c (GHRP-6 worsens, GLP-1 improves insulin sensitivity — net effect is unpredictable and worth tracking).
  • Tirzepatide opposes Evidence-rodent
    Tirzepatide (GIP+GLP-1 receptor agonist) suppresses appetite in part by quieting the same hypothalamic arcuate AgRP/NPY neurons that GHRP-6 activates via GHS-R1a to drive hunger, plus it delays gastric emptying. The two therefore push the arcuate feeding circuit in opposite directions, opposing GHRP-6's characteristic appetite spike. GHRP-6 orexigenic via arcuate NPY/AgRP (monograph step 5, PMID 39813130); tirzepatide arcuate appetite suppression rodent-mapped, human net effect observed (tirzepatide monograph) Watch: Appetite and weight trend (opposing drives); glucose/HbA1c, since GHRP-6's GH-induced insulin resistance works against tirzepatide's glucose-lowering effect.
  • GHRP-2 redundant Established (shared GHS-R1a target); desensitization Evidence-in vitro
    GHRP-6 is the parent hexapeptide that GHRP-2 was derived from and hits the identical target, GHS-R1a. Stacking them does NOT recruit a second pathway - they compete for and saturate the same receptor, so the GH benefit is largely redundant, while the off-target liabilities STACK: both drive appetite (arcuate NPY/AgRP), both raise cortisol/ACTH and prolactin, and repeated same-receptor activation promotes homologous desensitization (a second GHS pulse within ~1 h is blunted). Net effect is compounded side effects with little extra GH. GHRP-2 monograph (GHS-R1a agonist; steps 6-7; desensitization PMID 8169551); GHRP-6 monograph (same GHS-R1a target, appetite/cortisol/prolactin) Watch: Appetite/weight/body-fat trend (additive hunger); morning cortisol and ACTH; prolactin if libido drops or galactorrhea appears; watch for a diminishing GH response (tachyphylaxis) with frequent same-receptor dosing.
  • Hexarelin redundant Established
    Hexarelin is a direct GHRP-6 analog (single methyl-Trp substitution) acting on the identical GHS-R1a receptor and also binding CD36. Stacking it with GHRP-6 adds no new pathway and drives shared beta-arrestin desensitization at the receptor, so the GH response does not simply add. Their off-target ACTH/cortisol/prolactin elevations and appetite effects compound on the same mechanism, and hexarelin is a notably strong HPA/sleep-architecture disruptor. Monograph comparisons (hexarelin analog, GHS-R1a + CD36, stronger HPA/desensitization; PMID 15177700, 28321024); receptor desensitization PMID 15905359 Watch: IGF-1 (shared ceiling/desensitization); morning cortisol/ACTH and prolactin for compounded off-target stimulation; sleep quality and appetite/weight.
  • MK-677 redundant Established
    MK-677 is a non-peptide GHS-R1a full agonist acting on the exact same ghrelin receptor as GHRP-6. Running both does not add a new pathway — they co-occupy one receptor, and continuous GHS-R1a occupancy drives beta-arrestin desensitization/internalization, so the combined GH response is blunted rather than doubled. Their GH-axis, appetite, water-retention and insulin-resistance liabilities also stack on the same mechanism. Monograph comparisons (same receptor GHS-R1a; MK-677 balanced full agonist, long half-life); biased agonism + beta-arrestin desensitization PMID 15905359 Watch: IGF-1 (combined ceiling and desensitization), fasting glucose/insulin/HbA1c (both raise insulin resistance via GH), appetite/weight; note MK-677's long half-life sustains exposure and favors desensitization.

History & legal status

"GHRP-6 (His-D-Trp-Ala-Trp-D-Phe-Lys-NH2; SmithKline code SK&F 110679) is the prototype synthetic growth-hormone-releasing peptide, descended from Cyril Bowers' met-enkephalin-derived GH secretagogue work of the late 1970s–1980s; it was the key pharmacological tool that, via the search for its receptor, led Merck to clone GHS-R1a in 1996 and to the 1999 discovery of the natural ligand ghrelin (PMID 9893708). It was investigated for GH-deficiency diagnosis/therapy and later for tissue-protective indications (e.g. an experimental cytoprotective/anti-ischemic agent — the Cuban CIGB program studied GHRP-6 for multiple-organ-failure protection; PMID 16417467). GHRP-6 has NEVER received marketing approval as a drug in the US, EU, or elsewhere — it is not FDA/EMA-approved for any indication and is sold only as a 'research chemical.' Regulatory status: the entire GHRP/GHS class (GHRP-6, GHRP-2, GHRP-1, hexarelin, ipamorelin) is on the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) Prohibited List under S2 'Peptide Hormones, Growth Factors' (banned at all times, in and out of competition); validated urinary detection methods exist (PMID 25869809). In the US it is not a controlled substance but is not approved for human use and its sale/marketing for human consumption is unlawful; in many jurisdictions it occupies a gray 'not for human use' market. [Established]"

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No controlled human dose–response data for body-composition, performance, recovery or IGF-1 endpoints — virtually all human evidence is acute single-dose neuroendocrine challenge from the 1990s–2000s; chronic efficacy and safety in healthy users is essentially uncharacterized.
  • Long-term consequences of repeated HPA-axis activation (chronic cortisol/ACTH stimulation) with ongoing GHRP-6 use are unknown — acute pulses are documented, but cumulative effects on adrenal regulation, mood, and body composition are not studied.
  • Real-world tachyphylaxis kinetics in humans: how quickly GH responses desensitize with repeated dosing schedules, and the optimal pulsatile interval to avoid β-arrestin-mediated down-regulation, are not defined for humans (only in-vitro/rodent inference).
  • Clinical significance of GHRP-6's biased agonism / negative allosteric modulation of endogenous ghrelin — whether it meaningfully suppresses physiologic ghrelin signaling (appetite, gastric, cardiac) in chronic users is unknown.
  • The role and magnitude of the CD36 (non-GHS-R1a) pathway in humans — its cardiovascular/anti-ischemic relevance is shown in rodents but unquantified in people, and whether it carries any cardiac risk vs benefit chronically is unknown.
  • No human cancer-risk data: because the GH/IGF-1 axis is mitogenic and GHS-R1a is expressed on some tumors, the theoretical proliferative risk of chronic GH/IGF-1 elevation from sustained GHRP-6 use has not been studied in humans.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a how-to; describes what a clinician would watch). METABOLIC/GLUCOSE: fasting glucose, fasting insulin, HbA1c, and ideally HOMA-IR — GH-axis stimulation is counter-regulatory and can erode insulin sensitivity; watch for new hyperglycemia or rising HbA1c. GH/IGF-1 AXIS: serum IGF-1 (the integrated readout of GH stimulation) to gauge whether levels are drifting into supraphysiologic/acromegaly-like range; symptoms of GH/IGF-1 excess to watch for include peripheral edema/water retention, joint pain, paresthesias/carpal-tunnel symptoms, and acral or soft-tissue changes. HPA AXIS: morning cortisol and ACTH if there are signs of cortisol excess (central weight gain, mood disturbance, hypertension, poor recovery) given GHRP-6's documented ACTH/cortisol stimulation. PROLACTIN: serum prolactin if galactorrhea, libido change, or menstrual disturbance appears. GENERAL: blood pressure and resting heart rate (vasoactive GHS effects), thyroid panel (TSH/free T4, since GH-axis manipulation can shift thyroid economy), and appetite/weight tracking (marked hunger is expected and can drive unwanted weight/fat gain). RED FLAGS warranting medical review: persistent hyperglycemia, rapidly rising IGF-1, signs of acromegaly, new visual changes or severe headache (pituitary), and any features of cortisol excess. Because GHRP-6 is unapproved and unregulated, product identity/purity is itself a hazard — third-party assay/contaminant concerns apply. No bloodwork substitutes for not using an unapproved agent; monitoring is risk-mitigation, not endorsement."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE: nearly every exciting GHRP-6 claim (heart protection, organ rescue, wound/scar healing, neuroprotection, muscle differentiation) comes from rodents, fish, or cell culture — see PMIDs 38873418, 16417467, 27200188, 29464859, 27665924, 25644636. Do not present these as human benefits. The only solid human data are short GH-stimulation/diagnostic studies and one small PK study. The GH it produces is a brief pulse, not sustained replacement — and it requires the body's own GHRH to work well (PMID 7883854, 9543138). The same receptor that drives 'healing' hype also drives hunger, so appetite spikes are expected. Newest cardioprotection/lung/kidney papers (2024-2026, e.g. PMID 41901314, 41534456, 41327290) are still preclinical and several share one research group (Berlanga-Acosta et al.) — independent replication is limited. Purity/dosing of grey-market 'research' GHRP-6 is unverified, and it is banned in sport. This is mechanism education, not a usage recommendation.
