Mibolerone

A veterinary "estrus pill" for dogs that some lifters repurpose as an ultra-potent, short-acting oral androgen for a pre-competition aggression jolt — a real, well-characterized canine drug, but with essentially no human-use data and a hepatotoxic 17-alkyl backbone.

Limited human data 19 citations
Class
19-nortestosterone (nandrolone) derivative; 7α,17α-dimethylated oral anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS)
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR, gene NR3C4) — an extremely high-affinity, androgen-specific agonist (Kd in the low-nanomolar range; used experimentally as a radiolabeled AR probe)
Human status
Not approved for humans anywhere; there is no human therapeutic indication and essentially no human clinical-use data. Mibolerone's documented human role was as a laboratory research reagent — a radiolabeled ligand for measuring androgen and progesterone receptors in human prostate, breast and other tissues (PMIDs 2410244, 2419120, 18515094). Its only real-world clinical approval was VETERINARY: in the United States it was marketed as Cheque Drops (Upjohn) for estrus prevention in adult female dogs and for canine pseudopregnancy/false pregnancy, and it was used off-label in cats, wolves and captive big cats (PMIDs 921032, 6376444, 16626799, 4077632). The veterinary product was later discontinued/withdrawn from the U.S. market, making pharmaceutical-grade material scarce; material circulating in the fitness/PED world is therefore effectively research-chemical or gray-market. As an anabolic-androgenic steroid it is a U.S. Schedule III controlled substance and is prohibited in sport under the WADA Prohibited List (class S1). Bottom line: human use is entirely non-medical and off-label, and the 'ultra-potent pre-competition aggression oral' reputation rests on anecdote plus extrapolation from testosterone/AAS pharmacology, NOT on controlled human data.

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds the androgen receptor with very high affinity Established

    Mibolerone (7α,17α-dimethyl-19-nortestosterone, 'DMNT') slots into the androgen receptor and holds on tightly. In primate seminiferous-tubule tissue its dissociation constant (Kd) was ~0.6–5.4 nM, and binding was androgen-specific (it does not bind the glucocorticoid receptor). It binds AR so cleanly that laboratories historically used radiolabeled mibolerone as a standard tool to quantify androgen receptors in prostate and other tissues. PMID 2419120; PMID 2410244; PMID 3487680 (Kd 0.6–5.4 nM, primate tubule); PMID 6333679 (mibolerone as AR-assay ligand)

  2. Barely binds SHBG, so more drug stays 'free' Established

    Unlike dihydrotestosterone, mibolerone shows no appreciable binding to human sex-hormone-binding globulin (testosterone-estradiol binding globulin). That means little of it is mopped up by carrier protein, so a larger fraction is free to reach receptors — part of why milligram-for-milligram it is described as extremely potent. PMID 2419120; PMID 2410244; PMID 6333679 (no appreciable SHBG/TeBG binding)

  3. Activates androgen-responsive genes (the classic AR transcription program) Established

    Once bound, the AR–mibolerone complex moves to the nucleus and switches androgen-responsive genes on and off. Mibolerone is a full high-affinity AR agonist and drives the AR N-terminal/C-terminal 'agonist' interaction >40-fold (like DHT, testosterone and R1881), the molecular signature of a true agonist rather than a blocker. Downstream this is the mechanism behind muscle-protein anabolism, virilization and libido/aggression effects shared by potent androgens. PMID 10077001 (mibolerone induces AR N/C agonist interaction like DHT/T/R1881)

  4. Also binds and activates the progesterone receptor (progestogenic 'second personality') Established

    Mibolerone is not a 'pure' androgen: it binds the progesterone receptor (Kd ~2.35 nM in calf uterine cytosol) and behaves as a progestin. In breast cancer cells its antiproliferative actions were mediated by BOTH AR and PR, and its effects resembled the progestin medroxyprogesterone acetate. Progestogenic activity contributes to reproductive (gonadotropin) axis suppression — part of the basis of its canine estrus-blocking use — and, in a human context, would be expected to add progestin-type side effects (e.g., libido/mood changes) on top of pure androgen effects. The receptor-level progestin activity is established in vitro; the clinical human consequences are extrapolated. PMID 3564084 (PR Kd ~2.35 nM, calf uterus); PMID 18515094 (AR- and PR-mediated actions in human breast cancer cells; progestin-like); PMID 2419120 (binds AR and PR)

  5. Suppresses the reproductive axis and blocks estrus (its actual approved job) Established

    Given daily to female dogs, mibolerone reliably prevents estrus (heat) and treats false pregnancy (pseudopregnancy) — improving both the behavioral and physical signs. This reflects combined androgenic + progestogenic negative feedback on the brain's reproductive signaling. The same feedback in humans would be expected to suppress natural testosterone (HPG-axis shutdown), the well-known consequence of exogenous androgens — but this specific effect has been demonstrated in dogs, not measured in humans. PMID 921032 (estrus inhibition in Beagles); PMID 6376444 (dose-titration for canine pseudopregnancy)

  6. 17α-methyl group makes it orally active — and shifts toxicity to the liver Established

    The 17α-methyl substitution lets mibolerone survive first-pass liver metabolism and work as a pill, but this is the same modification that defines the hepatotoxic oral-AAS class: slower hepatic clearance of 17α-alkylated steroids makes them more liver-toxic (cholestasis, and in severe abuse, hepatic necrosis/failure). Mibolerone is metabolized in the liver to hydroxylated/glucuronide products and excreted in urine; the 17α-methyl group itself is not removed, sustaining hepatic exposure. Note: mibolerone-specific hepatotoxicity is inferred from the 17α-alkylated class, not from mibolerone human liver studies. PMID 29521706 (17α-alkylation → oral activity + hepatotoxicity); PMID 8527826 (direct hepatocyte toxicity of 17α-alkylated AAS in rat cultures); PMID 11110098 (hepatic glucuronide metabolites in dogs); PMID 28234904 (hydroxylated metabolites; fungal biotransformation model)

  7. Androgen-driven aggression/behavioral effect (the 'why lifters use it' step) Speculative

    The pre-competition reputation is an acute androgen-driven arousal/aggression effect. Direct human data for mibolerone specifically do not exist, so this is extrapolated: exogenous testosterone can rapidly increase aggressive behavior in men (especially dominant/impulsive individuals), and heavy AAS use is associated with altered brain connectivity. Because mibolerone is a very high-affinity AR agonist that also masculinized female dogs and enlarged the clitoris (potent virilization), a strong acute androgenic behavioral effect is biologically plausible — but the 'aggression on demand' framing is anecdotal / testosterone-extrapolated, not measured for this compound. PMID 27524498 (testosterone rapidly ↑ aggression in dominant/impulsive men); PMID 27942448 (AAS brain-connectivity changes); PMID 921032 & PMID 727584 (potent virilization in dogs, supporting high androgenic potency)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. High-affinity, androgen-specific full agonist of the androgen receptor (AR/NR3C4). Reported Kd ~0.6–5.4 nM in primate seminiferous-tubule tissue and low-nanomolar, single-site high-affinity binding in human hypertrophic prostate cytosol and nuclear extracts — comparable to the reference synthetic androgen R1881 (methyltrienolone), which is why radiolabeled mibolerone was itself used as an AR assay ligand. It does NOT bind the glucocorticoid receptor. Separately, it binds the progesterone receptor (Kd ~2.35 nM in calf uterine cytosol, slightly weaker than the progestins R5020/ORG2058, and dissociating faster) and acts as a functional progestin. It has negligible binding to human SHBG (testosterone-estradiol binding globulin), increasing the free (active) fraction.

Signalling cascade. Agonist-bound AR undergoes the N-terminal/C-terminal (N/C) interaction characteristic of true androgen agonists (>40-fold induction, like DHT/testosterone/R1881), translocates to the nucleus, and drives androgen-responsive gene transcription → protein anabolism, virilization, and CNS androgenic effects. In parallel, PR activation contributes progestin signaling and reproductive-axis (gonadotropin) suppression, part of the basis of canine estrus blockade. As a 19-nor (nandrolone-family) compound its androgenicity does not depend on 5α-reduction to DHT, and the 7α-methyl blocks certain metabolic inactivation, contributing to potency.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (17α-methylation confers oral bioavailability); the canine product was an oral liquid/drops.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally active due to 17α-methyl protection against hepatic first-pass inactivation. Quantitative human bioavailability is not established (no human PK studies).
Half-life
Reputationally short-acting in the PED community (basis of pre-event timing), but no rigorous human elimination half-life has been published; treat any specific number as unverified.
Metabolism
Hepatic. In greyhound dogs, oral mibolerone is biotransformed mainly to glucuronide-conjugated (and some free) hydroxylated metabolites; sulfation is minor (PMID 11110098). Fungal/microbial biotransformation models produce hydroxylation at 1α, 6β, 10β and 11β positions plus CH2/CH3 hydroxylation, indicating classic CYP-type Phase I oxidation followed by Phase II glucuronidation (PMID 28234904). An avian ([14C], chicken) study also profiled metabolic disposition (PMID 546943). The 17α-methyl group is not removed, sustaining hepatic exposure. There is no rodent hydroxylation-site or human PK dataset.
Excretion
Renal excretion of glucuronide-conjugated metabolites (urinary metabolite profiling underpins doping-control detection in racing greyhounds; PMID 11110098).
Notes
Human PK is essentially absent; the metabolic picture is built from canine (PMID 11110098), fungal/microbial (PMID 28234904) and avian/chicken (PMID 546943) data. Detection of parent/metabolites in urine is feasible and relevant to anti-doping.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Quantitative dose-response exists only for the VETERINARY indication and only illustrates potency, not human dosing. In Beagle bitches, ~60 µg/day (immature) or ~200 µg/day (mature) inhibited estrus, and ~20 µg/day was ~70% effective, with a dose-related increase in clitoral size — demonstrating activity in the microgram range and a clear dose-dependent virilization signal (PMID 921032). For canine pseudopregnancy, a controlled titration found ~0.016 mg/kg/day for 5 days optimal (PMID 6376444). The key translational point: mibolerone is active at microgram-to-low-microgram/kg canine doses, i.e., it is extraordinarily potent by mass, so in humans the gap between a trivial-looking amount and a large androgen/progestin overdose is small. No validated human dose-response curve exists, and none should be inferred. NO human dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs Methyltrienolone (metribolone, R1881)
    same: Both are extremely high-affinity synthetic AR agonists used historically as radioligands; both are 17α-methylated and non-aromatizable.
    different: R1881 is often the AR reference standard; mibolerone is notably MORE selective (it does not bind the glucocorticoid receptor, whereas R1881 does), and mibolerone reached real veterinary market use. Both have progestin-receptor cross-reactivity.
  • vs Nandrolone (19-nortestosterone parent)
    same: Both are 19-nor compounds with progestogenic tendency and androgenic action that does not require DHT conversion.
    different: Nandrolone is injectable, non-17-alkylated, and comparatively mild/anabolic-leaning; mibolerone adds 7α- and 17α-methyls making it orally active, far more potent by weight, and in the hepatotoxic 17α-alkylated class.
  • vs Methyltestosterone / typical oral 17α-alkylated bulking orals (e.g., Dianabol, Anadrol)
    same: Shared 17α-alkyl oral-activation and the associated hepatotoxicity + HDL-crash class effects.
    different: Those aromatize or are more anabolic-for-bulk; mibolerone is used in far smaller (microgram-range in dogs) amounts, is non-aromatizing (19-nor), and is reputationally taken acutely for a behavioral/aggression effect rather than for mass.
  • vs Exogenous testosterone
    same: Both are AR agonists that suppress the natural HPG axis and can raise aggression.
    different: Testosterone binds SHBG, aromatizes to estradiol and converts to DHT; mibolerone barely binds SHBG, does not aromatize, and layers on progestin activity — a different side-effect balance and much higher per-milligram potency.

History & legal status

Developed by Upjohn in the 1960s. Its most enduring scientific footprint is as a laboratory reagent: through the 1980s–1990s [3H]mibolerone was a standard high-affinity radioligand for quantifying androgen (and progesterone) receptors in human prostate, breast and animal tissues (PMIDs 2410244, 2419120, 3487680, 6333679). Clinically, its only approved use was veterinary — marketed in the U.S. as Cheque Drops (Upjohn) for suppression of estrus in adult female dogs and for pseudopregnancy, with off-label use documented in cats, wolves and captive Panthera species (PMIDs 921032, 6376444, 16626799, 4077632). The veterinary product was later discontinued/withdrawn from the U.S. market (contributing to gray-market scarcity). As an anabolic-androgenic steroid it is a U.S. Schedule III controlled substance and is prohibited in sport under the WADA Prohibited List (S1); anti-doping/veterinary racing programs developed urinary detection methods for it (PMID 11110098). No human therapeutic approval has ever existed.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No human pharmacokinetics: absorption, half-life, and clearance in people are unpublished; the 'short-acting' claim is unverified in humans.
  • No controlled human safety data: hepatotoxicity, lipid effects, and cardiovascular risk are inferred from the 17α-alkylated oral-AAS class, not measured for mibolerone in humans.
  • The signature 'acute aggression' effect has never been quantified for mibolerone in humans — it is extrapolated from testosterone/AAS studies.
  • Human dose-response and any therapeutic index are unknown; only canine microgram-range dosing exists, so human-relevant potency and overdose thresholds are uncertain.
  • Human HPG suppression is expected but has not been directly measured for mibolerone; the axis-suppression evidence is canine plus in-vitro receptor data.
  • Long-term human carcinogenicity is unknown; the only tumor signal is benign ovarian fibromas in chronically dosed dogs (PMID 4070927).
  • Product identity/purity in the PED market is uncertain given veterinary discontinuation — gray-market material may be mislabeled or misdosed.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For harm-reduction awareness only (not an endorsement of use, and NOT a dosing guide): because mibolerone is a 17α-alkylated oral AAS with dual androgen/progestin activity, the parameters a clinician would watch include — liver function tests (ALT, AST, ALP, bilirubin/GGT) for cholestatic or hepatocellular injury; a full lipid panel (expect a sharp HDL-C drop and rising LDL/ApoB, an independent cardiovascular-risk driver); blood pressure and cardiovascular risk markers; hematocrit/hemoglobin (androgen-driven erythrocytosis); reproductive-axis labs (total/free testosterone, LH, FSH — expect suppression) and fertility effects; signs of virilization in women (voice change, clitoral enlargement, hirsutism — largely irreversible, so early recognition matters); mood/aggression and sleep changes; and, given the animal ovarian-tumor signal, awareness that chronic hormonal stimulation is not benign. Any of these moving abnormally — especially rising liver enzymes, jaundice, a crashed HDL, or early virilization — is a signal to seek medical evaluation and stop. There is no safe self-administration protocol, and none is provided here.
