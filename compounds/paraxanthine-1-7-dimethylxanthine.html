Paraxanthine

The main caffeine metabolite, sold as 'caffeine 2.0' — real human RCTs and a genuinely distinct rodent pharmacology, but a thin, largely industry-funded human evidence base.

Limited human data 14 citations
Class
Methylxanthine / primary human caffeine metabolite (dietary-supplement stimulant; ChEMBL CHEMBL1158, natural product, max clinical phase 0
Primary target
Non-selective adenosine A1/A2A receptor antagonist (like caffeine), plus a second, caffeine-distinct mechanism seen in rodents: NO/cGMP signaling with inhibition of a cGMP-preferring phosphodiesterase (PDE9), driving striatal dopamine release
Human status
Emerging / early human data — NOT an approved drug. Paraxanthine is a natural caffeine metabolite marketed as a dietary-supplement ingredient (branded enfinity/ENFINITY by Ingenious Ingredients) and appearing in pre-workouts and nootropics across 2024-2026. In the US it is positioned as a supplement ingredient (self-affirmed GRAS / New Dietary Ingredient framing); it is NOT FDA-approved as a drug (ChEMBL max phase 0.5). Human evidence consists of a handful of small (n~12-45), acute or short-term (<=7 day) double-blind crossover RCTs plus one registered Phase 1/2 supplement study (NCT06628596). Most efficacy trials involve the ingredient maker (Ingenious Ingredients / Increnovo) as funder or co-author, and several were run by two academic labs (Texas A&M's Kreider lab; Lindenwood University's Kerksick lab); other registered trials are sponsored by a supplement company (Iovate Health Sciences) or independent academic centers. At least one independent (non-industry) human RCT now exists (Bingol Diedhiou 2026, a Turkish rowing/sleep study). There are still no independent long-term safety trials, no large head-to-head trials in caffeine-naive vs habituated users, and no data in adolescents, pregnancy, or cardiovascular/psychiatric populations. The mechanistic 'why it's different from caffeine' story (NO/cGMP/PDE9, dopamine) is currently rodent-only.

At a glance

  • Increased alertness/wakefulness and reduced sleepiness (adenosine antagonism, shared with caffeine)
  • Improved attention, reaction time, working memory and 'focus' in small human RCTs at ~50-200 mg (most consistent 100-200 mg), including preservation of some cognitive measures after fatiguing exercise
  • Reportedly 'cleaner' subjective feel — marketed as such and, in rodents, shown as less anxiety-provoking with a wider safety margin than caffeine (not quantified in humans)
  • Possibly less sleep disruption than caffeine — one human crossover study reported more favorable subjective sleep with paraxanthine; plausibly related to a shorter half-life (~3.1 h vs ~4.1 h)
  • Rodent-only: stronger locomotor activation, striatal dopamine release, and gains in muscle mass/strength/endurance
  • Possible small increase in resting energy expenditure (acute human data); fat-breakdown markers were dose-dependent and inconsistent
  • Ergogenic (physical performance) evidence in humans is currently weak/inconsistent — a clear benefit was shown mainly when paraxanthine was combined with caffeine, not for paraxanthine alone

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Enters the body pre-formed instead of being made from caffeine Established

    Normally your liver (mainly the CYP1A2 enzyme) strips one methyl group off caffeine to make paraxanthine — it is the dominant caffeine metabolite in humans, roughly 70-80% of caffeine's breakdown. Supplements skip that step and give paraxanthine directly, so the effect does not depend on how fast (or slow) your personal CYP1A2 converts caffeine. Lelo 1986, PMID 3756065; Perera 2012 (CYP1A2/caffeine probe review), PMID 22554278

  2. Blocks adenosine receptors — the shared 'anti-sleepiness' switch Established

    Like caffeine, paraxanthine is an antagonist at adenosine A1 and A2A receptors: it plugs the receptors that adenosine (a build-up-during-wakefulness 'tiredness' signal) uses, so the brakes on arousal and neuronal firing are released. In radioligand binding assays the affinity rank is theophylline > paraxanthine >= caffeine >> theobromine, and functional assays show all act as pure antagonists. Chou & Vickroy 2003, PMID 12602592; Snyder 1981, PMID 6265942; Muller 1993, PMID 8230124

  3. Adds a second, caffeine-distinct trick (in rodents): nitric oxide / cGMP / PDE inhibition Evidence-rodent

    This is the proposed reason paraxanthine differs from caffeine rather than being just 'cleaner caffeine.' In rats, paraxanthine (but not caffeine) raises cGMP in the striatum; its locomotor effects are blunted by the nitric-oxide inhibitor L-NAME, and it behaves like a cGMP-preferring phosphodiesterase (PDE9) inhibitor. So in rodents paraxanthine engages a NO->cGMP->PDE pathway that caffeine largely does not. This has not been shown in humans. Orru 2013, PMID 23261866

  4. Raises dopamine in the striatum (rodent) Evidence-rodent

    Through that NO/cGMP mechanism, paraxanthine (but not caffeine at matched doses) increased extracellular dopamine in the dorsolateral striatum of rats in microdialysis experiments, and this rise was blocked by L-NAME. Extra striatal dopamine is a plausible substrate for a 'focus/drive' and motor-activating feel — but this is rodent neurochemistry, not measured in human brains. Orru 2013, PMID 23261866

  5. Produces stronger locomotor activation than caffeine in animals Evidence-rodent

    In rats, paraxanthine caused a larger increase in movement than equal doses of caffeine, theophylline, or theobromine. Notably, in drug-discrimination tests paraxanthine did NOT fully 'feel like' caffeine to the animals, implying its behavioral profile is driven by more than adenosine blockade alone (consistent with the NO/cGMP/dopamine arm). Orru 2013, PMID 23261866

  6. Promotes wakefulness — potent and longer-lasting than caffeine in mice, with a wider safety margin Evidence-rodent

    In narcoleptic (orexin/ataxin-3) and wild-type mice, oral paraxanthine promoted wakefulness more strongly and for longer than equimolar caffeine (~100 mg/kg paraxanthine roughly corresponded to ~200 mg/kg modafinil for light-period wake promotion). Higher caffeine and modafinil doses caused hypothermia and REDUCED movement (toxicity signs); paraxanthine did not, and produced less anxiety-like behavior — the empirical basis for the 'lower toxicity, less anxiogenic' claim. All rodent. Okuro 2010, PMID 20614853

  7. Translates (modestly and selectively) to human cognition and reaction time Evidence-human

    In small double-blind crossover RCTs, single doses of ~50-200 mg improved some measures of attention, working memory, reasoning and reaction time versus placebo (most consistent at 100-200 mg), and after a fatiguing 10-km run paraxanthine preserved some cognitive measures better than caffeine. Effects are real but selective — many test measures were unchanged, samples were tiny (n=12), and both trials were funded by / co-authored with the ingredient maker and came from one lab. Xing 2021, PMID 34960030; Yoo 2024, PMID 38725238

  8. May modestly raise metabolic rate; separately, as a caffeine biomarker it tracks with cognition in older adults Evidence-human

    A dose-response human trial reported increased resting energy expenditure at 200 mg (with free fatty acids rising only at 300 mg and glycerol unchanged), so 'lipolysis' evidence is mixed. Separately, in NHANES survey data, higher urinary paraxanthine correlated with better cognitive scores in older adults — but that is cross-sectional caffeine-drinker data (paraxanthine as a biomarker of caffeine intake), not proof paraxanthine itself caused the benefit. Gross 2024, PMID 38745415; Liu 2023 (NHANES), PMID 36543015; Fu 2023 (NHANES), PMID 35715983

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Non-selective competitive antagonist at adenosine A1 and A2A receptors (Gi/Gs-coupled GPCRs). Radioligand binding places the affinity order theophylline > paraxanthine >= caffeine >> theobromine, with paraxanthine roughly comparable to caffeine at both A1 and A2A; functional [35S]GTPgammaS assays confirm pure antagonism (no partial agonism). Structure-activity work on 3-unsubstituted xanthines shows the 1-position substituent is critical for adenosine-receptor affinity, and that 1,7-disubstituted xanthines (paraxanthine class) generally have somewhat lower affinity than 1,3-disubstituted analogs. Beyond adenosine, in rodents paraxanthine engages a nitric oxide -> cGMP pathway and behaves as a cGMP-preferring phosphodiesterase (PDE9) inhibitor, which caffeine does not — the proposed basis for its claimed distinct profile (not demonstrated in humans).

Signalling cascade. Adenosine antagonism -> disinhibition of dopaminergic/cholinergic arousal circuits and blockade of adenosine-mediated neuronal/vascular effects. In parallel (rodent only): NO -> soluble guanylyl cyclase -> cGMP rise -> (via PDE9-preferring inhibition) sustained cGMP -> increased striatal dopamine release. The classic non-selective PDE inhibition and calcium-release actions attributed to methylxanthines occur only at supraphysiologic concentrations and are unlikely to dominate at supplemental doses.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (capsule/powder; supplement doses studied in trials ~50-300 mg)
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapidly absorbed after oral dosing; xanthines are generally well absorbed. Formal absolute bioavailability of exogenous paraxanthine in humans is not well characterized in the public literature.
Half-life
~3.1 h in healthy adults (shorter than caffeine ~4.1 h; theophylline ~6.2 h; theobromine ~7.2 h) — Lelo 1986
Metabolism
Hepatic, primarily CYP1A2-mediated demethylation and 8-hydroxylation to 1-methylxanthine, 1-methyluric acid, and 5-acetylamino-6-formylamino-3-methyluracil (AFMU) via NAT2. Endogenously, paraxanthine is itself the 3-demethylation product of caffeine (CYP1A2). Because CYP1A2 activity varies widely (genetics, smoking, oral contraceptives, pregnancy), exogenous paraxanthine partly sidesteps the inter-individual variability that governs how people respond to caffeine.
Excretion
Renal excretion of demethylated and oxidized metabolites; little unchanged drug in urine. Total plasma clearance ~2.20 mL/min/kg (similar to caffeine 2.07); volume of distribution at steady state ~0.63 L/kg.
Notes
The shorter half-life vs caffeine is the most defensible pharmacokinetic argument for 'less sleep disruption if taken later in the day.' Values from a 6-subject oral crossover study in healthy men (Lelo 1986, PMID 3756065).

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Human cognition RCTs tested 50, 100, and 200 mg acutely: benefits appeared across doses but were more consistent at 100-200 mg (Xing 2021). A dose-response metabolic trial used 100/200/300 mg, with energy expenditure rising at 200 mg and free fatty acids at 300 mg (Gross 2024). Registered energy/focus trials used 200-300 mg (NCT06117280, NCT06628596). No established maximum tolerated dose from formal escalation; manufacturer safety framing centers on doses <=300 mg. Rodent efficacy used ~10-100 mg/kg. There is no validated dose-response for a human performance (ergogenic) endpoint, and no long-term dose-titration data. NOTE: this is descriptive of what studies used — not a dosing recommendation.

How it compares

  • vs Caffeine
    same: Both antagonize adenosine A1/A2A receptors; both are CNS stimulants that increase alertness; similar total plasma clearance
    different: Paraxanthine has a shorter half-life (~3.1 vs ~4.1 h), adds a caffeine-distinct NO/cGMP/PDE9->dopamine mechanism in rodents, does not depend on the drinker's CYP1A2 conversion rate, and in animals shows stronger locomotor activation with less anxiety and a wider safety margin. In humans these advantages are only partly demonstrated (some cognition/reaction-time endpoints, subjective sleep) and are NOT clearly demonstrated for physical performance.
  • vs Theophylline
    same: Direct caffeine metabolite; adenosine antagonist (theophylline has the highest adenosine affinity of the group)
    different: Theophylline is a licensed drug (asthma/COPD) with a narrow therapeutic index, longer half-life (~6.2 h), and significant cardiac/CNS toxicity and drug interactions; paraxanthine is positioned as a lower-toxicity supplement, not a bronchodilator drug.
  • vs Theobromine
    same: Caffeine metabolite; methylxanthine; mild adenosine antagonist
    different: Theobromine is the weakest adenosine antagonist and mildest stimulant of the group (dominant in chocolate), with the longest half-life (~7.2 h); paraxanthine is substantially more psychoactive.
  • vs Modafinil
    same: Wake-promoting; used experimentally for hypersomnia in the same mouse models
    different: Modafinil is a prescription eugeroic with a different (dopamine-transporter-linked) mechanism; in mice ~100 mg/kg paraxanthine matched ~200 mg/kg modafinil for light-period wakefulness, but no human head-to-head or approved wakefulness indication exists for paraxanthine.

History &amp; legal status

Paraxanthine was characterized decades ago as the major human caffeine metabolite (Lelo 1986 PK, PMID 3756065; Snyder 1981 and Muller 1993 receptor pharmacology). The pivotal 'it's different from caffeine' mechanism paper is Orru 2013 (NIDA, PMID 23261866). Interest as a standalone ingredient is recent: Ingenious Ingredients markets it as enfinity/ENFINITY (with Increnovo) and co-authors much of the human research, which has been run largely by Texas A&M's Kreider lab and Lindenwood University's Kerksick lab, generating a cluster of 2021-2026 RCTs. Registered trials span several sponsors, not only the ingredient maker: energy/focus trials NCT06117280 and NCT06628596 (Phase 1/2) are sponsored by Iovate Health Sciences; a metabolic-rate trial NCT05811923 by Lindenwood University; and an L-theanine+paraxanthine pilot in adults with ADHD/ASD (NCT07189442) by Texas Tech University Health Sciences Center. Regulatory status: marketed in the US as a dietary-supplement ingredient (self-affirmed GRAS / New Dietary Ingredient positioning); it is NOT an FDA-approved drug (ChEMBL CHEMBL1158, max phase 0.5) and has no approved therapeutic indication. It is appearing in pre-workouts and nootropic 'caffeine-alternative' products across 2024-2026.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether the rodent NO/cGMP/PDE9 and dopamine mechanism actually operates in humans at supplement doses (no human neurochemistry data).
  • Long-term safety, tolerance, dependence and withdrawal profile in humans (all trials are acute or <=7 days).
  • Cardiovascular effects (BP, heart rate, arrhythmia risk) at repeated real-world doses and in at-risk populations.
  • Whether paraxanthine has any standalone ergogenic (physical performance) benefit in humans — current data suggest benefit mainly in combination with caffeine.
  • How much of the reported efficacy survives independent, non-industry replication with adequately powered samples (only one independent human RCT so far).
  • Interactions with caffeine co-ingestion (nearly all users also consume caffeine), other stimulants, and CYP1A2-affecting drugs/smoking.
  • Effects in caffeine-naive vs highly habituated users, and in adolescents, pregnancy, and psychiatric/anxiety populations (untested).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a dosing protocol): Treat paraxanthine as a stimulant with a real cardiovascular and sleep footprint. Watch for and track: (1) heart rate and blood pressure, and any palpitations, chest discomfort, or lightheadedness — stop and seek care if these occur, especially in anyone with hypertension, arrhythmia, or cardiac disease; (2) subjective anxiety/jitteriness and any panic symptoms; (3) sleep quality and latency, particularly with later-in-day use — despite the shorter half-life it is still a stimulant; (4) total daily stimulant load, since most users also drink caffeine and stack pre-workouts — the doses add up; (5) GI upset, headache, or tremor. Use extra caution with other stimulants, MAO inhibitors, theophylline/bronchodilators, and drugs affecting cardiac conduction; avoid in pregnancy/breastfeeding and in adolescents given the absence of data. Because supplement products are not tightly regulated, prefer third-party-tested products and verify the actual paraxanthine content. Anyone on prescription medication or with a heart, anxiety, or sleep disorder should consult a clinician before use.

Caveats

Take the marketing 'caffeine 2.0 / cleaner energy' framing with caution. What is genuinely well-established: paraxanthine is the dominant human caffeine metabolite, it antagonizes adenosine receptors, and it has a shorter half-life (~3.1 h) than caffeine (~4.1 h). What is rodent-only: the distinctive NO/cGMP/PDE9 and dopamine mechanism, the 'stronger stimulant but lower toxicity and less anxiety' profile, and the muscle/endurance gains — these come from mouse/rat studies and cannot be assumed to hold in humans. What is human but weak: cognitive/reaction-time benefits are real in small RCTs but selective (many endpoints null), the samples are tiny, and most efficacy studies are industry-funded and clustered in a few labs, which is a substantial conflict-of-interest and publication-bias concern (one independent RCT now exists). Ergogenic (performance) benefit for paraxanthine ALONE is not convincingly demonstrated — the clearest performance signal came when it was combined with caffeine. The metabolic story is modest and mixed: energy expenditure rose acutely, but lipolysis markers were inconsistent. It is still a CNS stimulant: it can raise heart rate/blood pressure, cause jitteriness, and disrupt sleep, and it can interact with other stimulants and with drugs affecting adenosine or cardiac conduction. 'Metabolite of a familiar substance' does not equal 'proven safe at supplemental doses over the long term.'
