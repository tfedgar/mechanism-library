SkQ1

A "Skulachev ion" — plastoquinone bolted to a lipophilic cation so it concentrates hundreds-fold inside mitochondria, where it soaks up membrane oxidants and protects cardiolipin. Reported to roughly double median lifespan in some rodent colonies and is an approved eye drop in Russia, but human systemic benefit is essentially unproven.

Rodent / preclinical 9 citations
Class
Mitochondria-targeted antioxidant (triphenylphosphonium–plastoquinone conjugate; "SkQ"-class rechargeable lipophilic-cation antioxidant)
Primary target
The mitochondrial inner membrane
Human status
Human evidence is limited to one indication and one route: topical ocular dry-eye disease. SkQ1 is the active ingredient in Visomitin eye drops, developed by Mitotech and registered/approved in Russia (marketed there as the first mitochondria-targeted antioxidant drug). A multicenter, randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled Russian/Ukrainian trial (240 subjects, PMID 26660938; industry-funded by Mitotech) reported significant improvement in dry-eye signs and symptoms versus placebo over a 6-week treatment course. Mitotech SA ran a US clinical program for dry eye: a completed Phase 2 (NCT02121301, n=91, Controlled Adverse Environment model) and two completed Phase 3 trials (VISTA-1 NCT03764735, n=452; VISTA-2 NCT04206020, n=610). As of the knowledge cutoff, SkQ1 is NOT FDA-approved, and there is no published human evidence of systemic anti-aging, longevity, cardiovascular, metabolic, or neuroprotective benefit — all of those claims rest on rodent and in-vitro data. Any oral/systemic 'longevity' use is off-label, unapproved, and unstudied in humans.

At a glance

  • Concentrates in mitochondria and blocks membrane lipid peroxidation, specifically protecting cardiolipin (in vitro / isolated mitochondria)
  • Extends median lifespan in fungi, crustaceans, Drosophila and several mouse/rodent colonies — reported roughly doubled median lifespan in non-sterile (higher-pathogen) mouse vivaria, with a much smaller or absent effect in clean SPF/low-pathogen conditions and in some inbred strains (rodent)
  • Delays age-related phenotypes in rodents: cataract, retinopathy, glaucoma, hair loss and greying, thymic involution, loss of estrous cycles; reverses aging biomarkers even when started late in life (rodent)
  • Improves healthspan and lifespan in mtDNA-mutator (premature-aging) mice and preserves mitochondrial ultrastructure/function (rodent)
  • As Visomitin eye drops: modestly improves signs and symptoms of dry eye disease in humans (tear-film stability, corneal staining, symptom scores) in an industry-funded RCT — the only human efficacy signal to date, and it is topical/ocular, not systemic
  • Preclinical protection in models of ischemia-reperfusion (heart, kidney, brain/stroke) and anti-tumor effects in p53-deficient mice and xenografts — all animal-only
  • No demonstrated systemic anti-aging, longevity, cardiovascular, or metabolic benefit in humans

Mechanism, step by step

  1. The cation drags the molecule into mitochondria Established

    SkQ1 is a plastoquinone antioxidant chemically tethered by a 10-carbon linker to triphenylphosphonium, a lipophilic 'penetrating' cation. Because the mitochondrial inner membrane is negatively charged inside (its membrane potential), positively charged SkQ1 is electrophoretically pulled in and concentrated hundreds- to thousands-fold at the inner membrane surface, far above the surrounding cell. This is the 'Skulachev ion' delivery principle shared with MitoQ. PMID 19159610; PMID 20307489

  2. It embeds where cardiolipin and the ROS are made Evidence-in vitro

    Once inside, the antioxidant head sits in the inner-membrane lipid bilayer right next to the electron transport chain — the main source of mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (ROS) — and next to cardiolipin, a signature inner-membrane phospholipid rich in oxidation-prone unsaturated fatty acids that is critical for respiratory-chain function. PMID 19159610; PMID 28209927

  3. The plastoquinol head quenches lipid peroxyl radicals Evidence-in vitro

    In its reduced (quinol) form, SkQ1 donates a hydrogen atom to lipid peroxyl radicals, interrupting the self-propagating chain reaction of membrane lipid peroxidation. It is an especially efficient chain-breaking antioxidant at nanomolar concentrations — far more potent per molecule than water-soluble antioxidants like N-acetylcysteine. PMID 20307489; PMID 19159610

  4. It specifically protects cardiolipin from peroxidation Evidence-in vitro

    Nanomolar SkQ1 selectively prevents oxidation of cardiolipin. Molecular-dynamics modelling places the plastoquinol group next to the peroxyl radicals forming on cardiolipin's linoleate chains. Protecting cardiolipin preserves respiratory-chain organization and blocks release of toxic aldehydes (malondialdehyde, 4-hydroxynonenal) that otherwise form protein adducts and impair mitochondria. PMID 19159610; PMID 20307489; PMID 28209927

  5. It is 'recharged' by the respiratory chain — a renewable antioxidant Evidence-in vitro

    After neutralizing a radical, SkQ1 is oxidized to a radical of its own (SkQ•), but complex III of the respiratory chain (via heme bH, in an antimycin-sensitive way) reduces it back to the active quinol. This continuous re-reduction lets a tiny amount of SkQ1 keep working catalytically instead of being consumed, which is a key reason it is effective at sub-micromolar doses. PMID 20307489; PMID 19159610

  6. A second, cation-driven mechanism: mild uncoupling Evidence-in vitro

    Separately from radical scavenging, the SkQ1 cation shuttles fatty-acid anions across the membrane ('fatty-acid cycling'), giving a weak protonophoric (uncoupling) effect. This modestly lowers the inner-membrane potential, and because ROS production by reverse electron transport rises steeply with membrane potential, a small drop in potential cuts ROS generation at the source. So SkQ1 both scavenges existing ROS and dampens their production. PMID 20307489

  7. Dose is a knife-edge: antioxidant low, pro-oxidant high Evidence-in vitro

    The therapeutic window is narrow and bell-shaped. In isolated mitochondria and cells, sub-micromolar (nanomolar) SkQ1 is an antioxidant, while higher (micromolar) concentrations of the same molecule become pro-oxidant and can damage mitochondria. This is intrinsic to redox-cycling quinones and is why effective doses are extraordinarily low (nanomolar in cells; nmol/kg in animals). PMID 19159610

  8. Downstream: less oxidative damage, delayed aging phenotypes Evidence-rodent

    By lowering mitochondrial oxidative damage, chronic low-dose SkQ1 in rodents delays or reverses many age-associated changes — cataract, retinopathy, hair loss/greying, immune (thymic) involution, loss of estrous cycling, and abnormal aging biomarkers — and in several colonies extends lifespan. The framing that it 'interrupts an aging program' is the Skulachev group's hypothesis, not a proven mechanism. PMID 19120018; PMID 22166671; PMID 23104863; PMID 28209927

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No classical receptor. SkQ1 partitions into the mitochondrial inner-membrane lipid bilayer, driven by the triphenylphosphonium cation and the inner-membrane potential (Nernstian accumulation, ~100–1000-fold over cytosol). The plastoquinol moiety acts as a chain-breaking lipid-phase antioxidant, donating H atoms to lipid peroxyl radicals; it shows selective protection of cardiolipin. The cationic moiety independently mediates fatty-acid cycling that produces mild protonophoric uncoupling.

Signalling cascade. Two parallel antioxidant mechanisms: (1) direct radical scavenging with catalytic regeneration — oxidized SkQ• is re-reduced to the active quinol by respiratory complex III (heme bH, antimycin-sensitive), making it a 'rechargeable' antioxidant effective at sub-micromolar levels; (2) fatty-acid-cycling mild uncoupling that lowers membrane potential and thereby suppresses ROS generation from reverse electron transport. Net downstream effect: reduced cardiolipin/lipid peroxidation, fewer reactive aldehyde–protein adducts (MDA, 4-HNE), and preserved mitochondrial structure and bioenergetics.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Topical ophthalmic (Visomitin eye drops) in humans; oral (drinking water/diet) and injectable in rodent studies. Effective animal doses are extraordinarily low (roughly 0.5–50 nmol/kg/day for geroprotective effects).
Absorption / bioavailability
Highly lipophilic cation; readily crosses membranes and accumulates down the mitochondrial potential gradient. Quantitative human systemic bioavailability, plasma levels, and tissue distribution are not well characterized in the public literature; the marketed product is a low-concentration topical (ocular-surface) formulation, minimizing systemic exposure.
Half-life
Not reliably established in humans. Mitochondrial-targeted lipophilic cations tend to be retained in tissues via membrane-potential trapping, so tissue residence can exceed plasma half-life; specific SkQ1 human PK is unpublished.
Metabolism
The plastoquinol/quinone head undergoes redox cycling and is enzymatically re-reduced by the respiratory chain (complex III). Detailed hepatic metabolic pathways are not well defined publicly.
Excretion
Not well characterized in humans.
Notes
SkQ1 (and related cationic antioxidants) are substrates of the P-glycoprotein (MDR1) efflux pump, which reduces intracellular accumulation in P-gp-high tissues/tumors and is a source of PK variability (PMID 19120016). The dose–response is non-monotonic, so 'exposure' must be interpreted against the narrow antioxidant window.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Strongly biphasic (hormetic/bell-shaped). In isolated mitochondria and cells, sub-micromolar (nanomolar) SkQ1 is antioxidant; micromolar concentrations flip to pro-oxidant and damage mitochondria (PMID 19159610). In rodents, geroprotective and lifespan effects appear at very low doses (~0.5–50 nmol/kg/day), with higher doses offering no added benefit (PMID 21137217; PMID 22166671). This means the effective therapeutic index is defined by staying LOW; extrapolating to higher 'supplement' doses is mechanistically unjustified. No validated human systemic dose–response exists.

How it compares

  • vs MitoQ (mitoquinone)
    same: Both are lipophilic-cation (TPP+) mitochondria-targeted antioxidants delivered by membrane-potential accumulation; MitoQ is the more widely studied sibling with actual human trials (e.g., vascular/aging endpoints).
    different: MitoQ uses a ubiquinone head; SkQ1 uses plastoquinone, reported by its developers as a more effective and less pro-oxidant antioxidant with a wider antioxidant window and specific cardiolipin protection (SkQ1 > MitoQ potency in the founders' assays, PMID 19159610). MitoQ has the better independent human evidence base.
  • vs Elamipretide (SS-31 / MTP-131)
    same: Both are mitochondria-targeted, cardiolipin-centric protectants that reduce lipid peroxidation and preserve bioenergetics; both have advanced to human trials.
    different: Elamipretide is a cell-permeable tetrapeptide that binds cardiolipin directly (not a redox-cycling quinone and not potential-dependent for accumulation); SkQ1 is a small-molecule quinone that scavenges radicals, is recharged by the respiratory chain, and also mildly uncouples.
  • vs N-acetylcysteine (NAC) / conventional antioxidants
    same: All aim to reduce oxidative damage.
    different: SkQ1 works at vastly lower doses because it is concentrated inside mitochondria at the ROS source, whereas NAC is untargeted and diffuse; in head-to-head rodent work SkQ1 outperformed NAC at ~10^4–10^6-fold lower doses (PMID 23104863; PMID 19120016).
  • vs MitoTEMPO
    same: Mitochondria-targeted (TPP+) antioxidant used in preclinical oxidative-stress models.
    different: MitoTEMPO is a targeted SOD-mimetic (superoxide dismutase mimic) rather than a lipid-phase chain-breaking, rechargeable quinone; different chemistry and mechanism of ROS handling.

History & legal status

SkQ1 emerged from Vladimir Skulachev's long-running 'Skulachev ions' program at Moscow State University (Belozersky Institute / Institute of Mitoengineering) and the associated 'Ion Project,' commercialized through Mitotech. The compound was selected (2008-era work) from a series of plastoquinone–cation conjugates as having the best membrane permeability and antioxidant profile. Its first — and to date only — regulatory success is as Visomitin ophthalmic drops for dry eye, registered/approved in Russia (marketed as the first mitochondria-targeted antioxidant drug there). Mitotech SA pursued Western approval for dry eye via a US clinical program (Phase 2 NCT02121301; Phase 3 VISTA-1 NCT03764735 and VISTA-2 NCT04206020, all completed). As of the knowledge cutoff, SkQ1 is not FDA- or EMA-approved. Systemic 'anti-aging' indications remain preclinical and have not entered a successful human program.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether any systemic (oral) human benefit exists for aging, cardiovascular, metabolic, or neurodegenerative endpoints — untested in controlled human trials
  • Human systemic pharmacokinetics: bioavailability, plasma/tissue levels, half-life, and whether the narrow antioxidant window can even be hit safely in vivo in people
  • Whether the rodent lifespan effect reflects true anti-aging biology or mainly reduced infection/immune-related mortality (housing-dependent effect)
  • Independent, non-founder-group replication of the large systemic lifespan claims
  • Long-term systemic safety of chronic dosing in humans (only short-term topical ocular safety is documented)
  • Outcome and regulatory fate of the completed US Phase 3 dry-eye trials (efficacy magnitude, approvability)
  • Optimal human dose given the biphasic antioxidant/pro-oxidant behavior
  • Purity, identity, and dosing accuracy of any non-pharmaceutical 'SkQ1' sold to consumers

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

There is no validated systemic human use, so there is no established monitoring protocol — the honest position is that chronic oral/systemic self-dosing is unstudied and cannot be made safe by monitoring alone. For the approved topical eye drop, watch for local ocular tolerability (stinging, irritation, redness) and stop and seek care if vision changes or persistent irritation occur. For anyone using research-grade or supplement 'SkQ1' off-label: recognize the biphasic dose curve means higher amounts may be net harmful (pro-oxidant); be aware of unknown drug interactions via P-glycoprotein; there is no biomarker proven to confirm benefit or safe exposure in humans; and any unexpected symptoms warrant discontinuation and medical evaluation. This is harm-reduction information, not an endorsement or dosing guide.

Caveats

Species gap is the central caveat: the dramatic 'roughly doubles lifespan' figure comes from mice in non-sterile vivaria, where SkQ1's benefit largely coincided with reduced infection-related mortality — in cleaner (low-pathogen/SPF) housing and in several inbred strains the effect was small or absent, and simply improving housing hygiene reproduced much of the lifespan gain (PMID 22166671). Much of the foundational rodent and mechanistic work comes from a single group (Skulachev and collaborators, Moscow State University / Mitotech), and independent replication of the systemic lifespan claims is limited; the 'aging program' framing is a hypothesis, not established biology. SkQ1 has a genuinely narrow, bell-shaped dose window — antioxidant at nanomolar/nmol-per-kg doses, pro-oxidant and mitochondriotoxic at higher concentrations (shown in isolated mitochondria and cells) — so 'more is better' is false and potentially harmful. It is a P-glycoprotein substrate, which affects tissue accumulation (PMID 19120016). Human safety data exist only for the low-dose topical eye drop; there is essentially no controlled human data on chronic oral/systemic dosing. Treat any supplement or research-chemical 'SkQ1' for longevity as unapproved and unproven, with unknown purity, dose, and long-term safety.
