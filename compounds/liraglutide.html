Liraglutide

The original once-daily GLP-1: the foundational acylated GLP-1 analogue that every newer weight-loss and diabetes drug is benchmarked against.

Limited human dataFDA-approved 11 citations

Human status
Established, FDA/EMA-approved. Marketed as Victoza (up to 1.8 mg/day) for type 2 diabetes since 2010 (EU 2009) and as Saxenda (3.0 mg/day) for chronic weight management since 2014-2015; an adolescent (12-17) obesity indication followed, and a younger-pediatric (down to age 6) obesity indication has since been reported. At a glance

  • Lowers blood glucose and HbA1c in type 2 diabetes with low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk (insulin secretion is glucose-dependent)
  • Produces clinically meaningful weight loss (~6-8% at 3.0 mg over ~56 weeks in people without diabetes; less in those with diabetes)
  • Reduces appetite and food intake, increases satiety, and slows gastric emptying
  • Reduces major adverse cardiovascular events in high-risk type 2 diabetes (LEADER)
  • Lowers systolic blood pressure modestly and improves several cardiometabolic markers
  • Common trade-off: dose-dependent nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation; increased gallbladder/biliary events

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds and activates the GLP-1 receptor Established

    Liraglutide is a modified copy of the human gut hormone GLP-1 (differing from GLP-1(7-37) by a single Arg34Lys substitution, ~97% sequence identity) that acts as a full agonist at the GLP-1 receptor, a class B G-protein-coupled receptor. A C16 palmitic-acid chain attached via a glutamic-acid spacer lets it bind reversibly to blood albumin, which slows its breakdown and gives it a much longer action than natural GLP-1 (which lasts only minutes). ChEMBL CHEMBL414357 mechanism (target CHEMBL1784, AGONIST, direct); FDA label; PMID 33068776

  2. Triggers cAMP signaling in the cell Established

    When liraglutide activates GLP-1R, the receptor couples to the Gs protein and raises intracellular cyclic AMP, activating downstream protein kinase A and Epac2 signaling. In pancreatic beta cells this primes the machinery that releases insulin. PMID 33068776

  3. Boosts insulin only when glucose is high, and suppresses glucagon Established

    The insulin-release effect is glucose-dependent: it amplifies insulin secretion when blood sugar is elevated but tapers off as glucose normalizes, which is why GLP-1 agonists rarely cause hypoglycemia on their own. It also suppresses glucagon (the sugar-raising hormone) at high/normal glucose without blocking the protective glucagon response to true hypoglycemia. PMID 33068776

  4. Slows gastric emptying Established

    Liraglutide slows how fast the stomach empties into the intestine, blunting the post-meal glucose spike and prolonging fullness. For long-acting agents this gastric-emptying effect partly wanes over time (tachyphylaxis), unlike short-acting GLP-1 agonists. Excessive slowing underlies much of the nausea and the surgical/endoscopy aspiration concern. PMID 33068776; PMID 39096914

  5. Acts on brain appetite centers to reduce food intake Evidence-human

    GLP-1 receptors in the hypothalamus and hindbrain (including the area postrema and other circumventricular/appetite regions that liraglutide can reach) are engaged to increase satiety and reduce hunger and energy intake, driving weight loss largely by making people eat less rather than by boosting metabolism. Human trials confirm reduced appetite and food intake; the precise neuronal circuitry is mapped mostly in rodents. PMID 33977495; PMID 37188932

  6. Downstream cardiometabolic effects Evidence-human

    Sustained GLP-1R activation, together with the resulting weight loss, better glucose control and modest blood-pressure lowering, translates into fewer major cardiovascular events in high-risk type 2 diabetes: the reduction in cardiovascular events is an established human outcome (LEADER RCT). The precise mediators (weight/glycemia versus possible direct vascular or anti-inflammatory actions) are not fully established. PMID 27295427

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Full agonist at the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R), a class B secretin-family G-protein-coupled receptor. Liraglutide shares ~97% amino-acid identity with native human GLP-1(7-37); the changes are an Arg34Lys substitution and attachment of a C16 palmitoyl fatty-acid chain to Lys26 via a glutamic-acid (gamma-Glu) spacer. This acylation enables reversible binding to serum albumin and promotes self-association (heptamer formation) at the injection site, both of which protect it from DPP-4 degradation and renal clearance and extend its duration to permit once-daily dosing.

Signalling cascade. GLP-1R activation couples predominantly to Gs, raising intracellular cAMP and activating PKA and Epac2. In beta cells this potentiates glucose-stimulated insulin secretion and supports beta-cell function; in alpha cells it suppresses glucagon at hyper/euglycemia. Additional GLP-1R populations in the gut/vagal afferents and CNS (hypothalamus, hindbrain including area postrema) mediate delayed gastric emptying and reduced appetite.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection, once daily (abdomen, thigh, or upper arm)
Absorption / bioavailability
Absolute bioavailability after SC injection ~55%; slow absorption from the SC depot (peak plasma concentration around 8-12 hours) due to fatty-acid-mediated self-association and albumin binding.
Half-life
Terminal half-life approximately 13 hours, supporting once-daily dosing (contrast with native GLP-1 half-life of ~1-2 minutes and once-weekly agents such as semaglutide, whose half-life is ~1 week).
Metabolism
Metabolized endogenously like a large peptide/fatty-acid, chiefly via dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4) and neutral endopeptidase; no specific organ (liver/kidney) is the main route of elimination and no intact drug is recovered in urine or feces.
Excretion
Cleared by widespread proteolytic degradation; metabolites excreted; no clinically relevant accumulation in renal or hepatic impairment requiring specific dose adjustment per label, though caution is advised.
Notes
Slow dose escalation (weekly up-titration) is used clinically to mitigate GI adverse effects; this reflects tolerability, not a change in mechanism.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Clear dose-response for both glycemia and weight. For diabetes, Victoza is titrated to 1.2-1.8 mg/day. For weight management, Saxenda is titrated to 3.0 mg/day. In SCALE Diabetes (NCT01272232), weight loss was greater at 3.0 mg (6.0%) than 1.8 mg (4.7%) vs placebo (2.0%) (PMID 26284720). Gastrointestinal adverse events also rise with dose. Because the incretin/insulinotropic effect is glucose-dependent, escalating dose increases efficacy without a proportional rise in hypoglycemia when used as monotherapy. This monograph gives approved-dose context for interpreting trials only and is not a protocol.

How it compares

  • vs Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy, once weekly)
    same: Both acylated GLP-1R mono-agonists (albumin-binding fatty-acid chain), both slow gastric emptying and reduce appetite, similar GI side-effect profile and class warnings
    different: Semaglutide is once weekly and substantially more potent for weight loss (~15-16% vs ~6-8%); STEP 8 head-to-head showed roughly double the weight loss (-15.8% vs -6.4%)
  • vs Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound)
    same: Shares GLP-1R agonism and incretin mechanism, similar GI tolerability pattern
    different: Tirzepatide is a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist, once weekly, with the largest weight loss to date (~20%); liraglutide is GLP-1-only and daily
  • vs Exenatide (Byetta/Bydureon)
    same: Both injectable GLP-1R agonists that improve glycemia with low hypoglycemia risk
    different: Exenatide is exendin-4-based (from Gila monster venom, ~53% identity to human GLP-1) and renally cleared; liraglutide is a near-human analogue (~97% identity), acylated, and not primarily renally cleared, giving smoother once-daily kinetics
  • vs Oral semaglutide (Rybelsus)
    same: Same GLP-1R mechanism and effects
    different: Oral semaglutide is a tablet (with SNAC absorption enhancer) vs liraglutide's subcutaneous injection

History & legal status

Developed by Novo Nordisk as a long-acting human GLP-1 analogue to overcome native GLP-1's ~1-2 minute half-life. FDA-approved for type 2 diabetes as Victoza in January 2010 (EU 2009). The weight-management formulation Saxenda (3.0 mg) was approved by the FDA in December 2014 and EMA in 2015 based on the SCALE program. The LEADER cardiovascular outcome trial (2016, PMID 27295427) established a MACE benefit, leading to a CV-risk-reduction indication. Pediatric use followed: an adolescent (12-17) obesity indication, and a younger-pediatric (down to age 6) obesity indication has since been reported. Carries a boxed warning for thyroid C-cell tumors and is contraindicated with personal/family history of MTC or MEN2. Now largely superseded on efficacy by once-weekly semaglutide and tirzepatide but remains the reference daily GLP-1 and a widely studied comparator.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether chronic human GLP-1R activation carries any real thyroid C-cell/MTC risk (rodent-derived warning; reassuring but not definitive human data)
  • Long-term (multi-decade) safety and durability of weight loss, and the magnitude of weight regain after discontinuation
  • Extent and mechanisms of non-glycemic/direct vascular, renal, hepatic (MASLD), and possible neurodegenerative/anti-inflammatory effects independent of weight loss
  • Precise human neuronal circuitry mediating appetite suppression (mapped largely in rodents; e.g., hypothalamic and hindbrain circuits)
  • Clinical significance of tachyphylaxis of the gastric-emptying effect over time
  • Real-world aspiration risk during surgery/endoscopy and optimal peri-procedural management

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring (not a dosing protocol): watch for and report persistent severe abdominal pain (possible pancreatitis or gallbladder disease) - clinicians may check pancreatic enzymes and imaging if suspected. Screen for personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2 before use (contraindication); report any neck mass, difficulty swallowing, or hoarseness. Monitor for dehydration from vomiting/diarrhea and consequent acute kidney injury, especially in those on diuretics or ACE inhibitors/ARBs. In people also using insulin or sulfonylureas, watch for hypoglycemia. Be aware of delayed gastric emptying before elective surgery or endoscopy (aspiration risk) and inform the anesthesia/procedure team. Monitor heart rate. In adolescents and children, growth and psychiatric/mood changes warrant attention. Any GLP-1 therapy should be initiated and monitored by a qualified clinician.

Caveats

Human vs rodent honesty: The thyroid C-cell tumor warning (a boxed warning and contraindication in people with a personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2) comes from rodents. Rat/mouse thyroid C-cells express abundant GLP-1R and respond to liraglutide with calcitonin release and C-cell proliferation/tumors (PMID 20203154), but human and monkey C-cells express little GLP-1R and did not respond, and calcitonin monitoring in over 5,000 liraglutide-treated humans over 2 years showed no signal (PMID 21209033). The human relevance therefore remains unproven but not fully resolved. Hype vs data: liraglutide's weight loss (~6-8% at 3.0 mg) is real but modest compared with once-weekly semaglutide 2.4 mg (~15-16%) and tirzepatide (~20%); the STEP 8 head-to-head trial (NCT04074161) showed semaglutide roughly doubled weight loss vs liraglutide (-15.8% vs -6.4%; PMID 35015037). GI side effects are very common and drive discontinuation. Not a metabolism booster: weight loss is driven mainly by reduced food intake, not increased energy expenditure. Pancreatitis is a labeled precaution though the LEADER trial found a numerically lower (non-significant) rate on liraglutide, so a causal signal is weak; gallbladder/biliary disease risk is modestly increased, and higher at weight-loss doses (PMID 35344001). This monograph describes mechanism and what to monitor only; it is not a dosing or how-to-use guide, and decisions about GLP-1 therapy require a clinician.
