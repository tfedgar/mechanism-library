Pentadeca Arginate

A marketing-driven "next-gen BPC-157" arginate salt with essentially zero direct published data — everything you read about it is borrowed from BPC-157 preclinical (mostly rodent) studies.

Rodent / preclinical 8 citations
Class
Synthetic gastric pentadecapeptide (BPC-157 analog / same 15-mer core sequence GEPPPGKPADDAGLV), formulated as an arginate (arginine) salt
Primary target
Presumed identical to BPC-157: the VEGFR2–Akt–eNOS angiogenic/cytoprotective axis, plus growth-factor-receptor upregulation (growth hormone receptor) and nitric-oxide-system modulation
Human status
No human data exist for Pentadeca Arginate itself — zero clinical trials (ClinicalTrials.gov returns none for PDA/pentadeca arginate) and zero PubMed articles. It is sold as a compounded/research peptide, not an FDA-approved drug, and reaches users through wellness clinics and grey-market vendors marketing it as a 'more stable next-gen BPC-157.' All human evidence is for the PARENT compound BPC-157, and even that is thin: fewer than ~30 subjects across three small, mostly uncontrolled pilots — an intravesical injection pilot in 12 women with interstitial cystitis (PMID 39325560), plus small intra-articular knee-pain and IV safety/PK pilots — none of them rigorous RCTs. That picture is beginning to change for BPC-157 specifically: a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled Phase 2 trial in acute grade II hamstring strain (NCT07437547, sponsor Hudson Biotech, n=120, subcutaneous BPC-157 vs placebo) is registered and recruiting as of 2026, and an earlier Phase 1 PK study exists (NCT02637284) — but none of this involves PDA. BPC-157 is on the FDA's 503A/503B Category 2 bulk-substances list (bulk drug substances that may present significant safety risks for compounding) and has been flagged as a prohibited/unapproved 'research chemical' by DoD/OPSS. Bottom line: PDA is an emerging, essentially untested product riding on extrapolated preclinical BPC-157 data.

At a glance

  • Claimed (extrapolated from BPC-157): accelerated healing of tendon, ligament, muscle, and gut tissue
  • Claimed: improved local blood supply / angiogenesis in poorly-vascularized tissue via VEGFR2–eNOS–NO
  • Claimed: anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects at injury sites
  • Claimed by vendors: greater stability, shelf-life, and bioavailability than acetate BPC-157 (unproven)
  • Actually demonstrated for PDA specifically: nothing — no efficacy or safety data exist
  • Human evidence for the parent BPC-157 remains limited (a few small uncontrolled pilots), though the first randomized placebo-controlled Phase 2 trial of BPC-157 is now recruiting (NCT07437547)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. PDA's mechanism is assumed, not measured Speculative

    Pentadeca Arginate is the BPC-157 15-amino-acid peptide paired with arginine as a salt (an 'arginate'). There is no published pharmacology, animal study, or human trial on PDA itself as of mid-2026 — a PubMed and ClinicalTrials.gov search returns zero PDA-specific records. Every mechanism below is extrapolated from BPC-157 and assumes the arginate salt behaves like the parent peptide once in solution. That assumption is untested. No PDA-specific records found in PubMed or ClinicalTrials.gov (searches performed 2026); parent-compound scoping review PMID 40789979

  2. Upregulates and internalizes VEGFR2 → Akt–eNOS Evidence-rodent

    For BPC-157, the best-defined mechanism is that it raises VEGFR2 (the master receptor for new blood-vessel growth) expression and drives its internalization, switching on the downstream VEGFR2–Akt–eNOS pathway. This was shown in cultured human endothelial cells and in a rat hind-limb ischemia model, where blocking endocytosis (dynasore) abolished the effect. Notably it did this without raising VEGF-A itself. PDA is assumed to do the same, but this has never been tested on PDA. Hsieh 2017, J Mol Med, PMID 27847966 (human endothelial cells in vitro + rat hind-limb ischemia)

  3. Modulates the nitric oxide (eNOS/NO) system bidirectionally Evidence-rodent

    Activated eNOS produces nitric oxide (NO), which relaxes vessels, improves blood flow to injured tissue, and supports vascular integrity. BPC-157 interacts with the NO system in a balanced way — in rat models it can counteract both an NO-blocker (L-NAME) and an NO/arginine excess (L-arginine). This is invoked for PDA because the salt is arginate (an arginine form): arginine is itself the substrate eNOS uses to make NO, so vendors imply a synergy, but no study has measured whether the arginate counterion adds any NO effect. Evidence is from rodent pharmacology and review, not from PDA. Sikiric 2014 review, Curr Pharm Des, PMID 23755725

  4. Drives fibroblast migration/survival via FAK–paxillin Evidence-in vitro

    In tendon-cell experiments, BPC-157 increased the outgrowth, spreading, and migration of tendon fibroblasts and helped them survive oxidative (H2O2) stress. Mechanistically it phosphorylated FAK and paxillin — the 'focal adhesion' proteins cells use to grip and crawl through tissue — without simply making cells divide faster. This is the proposed basis for faster tendon/ligament repair. Shown for BPC-157 only, in cultured rat tendon fibroblasts (in vitro/ex vivo). Chang 2011, J Appl Physiol, PMID 21030672 (rat tendon fibroblasts, in vitro/ex vivo)

  5. Upregulates growth hormone receptor → JAK2 Evidence-in vitro

    BPC-157 was one of the strongest upregulators of the growth hormone receptor gene in a microarray of tendon fibroblasts; adding growth hormone to BPC-157-treated cells then boosted proliferation and activated JAK2 (the receptor's signaling kinase). This is a plausible route by which the peptide could sensitize healing tissue to the body's own growth hormone. Again, BPC-157 in cultured rat cells only — never studied for PDA. Chang 2014, Molecules, PMID 25415472 (rat tendon fibroblasts, in vitro)

  6. Broad cytoprotection and anti-inflammatory / ERK1/2 effects Evidence-rodent

    Across many rat models, BPC-157 accelerates healing of gut, skin, muscle, nerve, and bone and blunts inflammation, engaging additional pathways such as ERK1/2. A 2025 scoping review summarizes this as 'robust preclinical support' but stresses the effects are almost entirely from animal work (much of it from one research group) and remain investigational. PDA inherits these claims by association only. McGuire 2025 scoping review, Curr Rev Musculoskelet Med, PMID 40789979; Seiwerth 2021 review, Front Pharmacol, PMID 34267654

  7. The arginate salt's only rationale is stability — and it's unproven Speculative

    The marketing pitch is that pairing the peptide with arginine yields a more stable, longer-shelf-life, 'more bioavailable' product than acetate-salt BPC-157. There is no published pharmacokinetic or stability comparison supporting this for PDA. A 2026 biopharmaceutical review of BPC-157 concludes even the parent peptide has no pharmaceutical-grade validated formulation and only fragmentary PK (a formal 2-species preclinical ADME study plus a preliminary 2-subject human half-life) — so claims that the arginate 'fixes' these problems are unsubstantiated. BPC-157 biopharmaceutical review 2026, Pharmaceutics, PMID 42198317

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No PDA-specific target or binding data exist. For BPC-157, no single high-affinity receptor has been cleanly identified; the best-characterized action is functional upregulation and endocytic internalization of VEGFR2 (rather than acting as a direct VEGFR2 ligand), which then activates the VEGFR2–Akt–eNOS cascade. Additional documented actions of BPC-157 include phosphorylation of FAK and paxillin (focal-adhesion proteins governing cell migration) and transcriptional upregulation of the growth hormone receptor with downstream JAK2 activation. It also modulates the nitric-oxide system bidirectionally in rodent models, counteracting both NO-synthase inhibition (L-NAME) and NO/arginine excess (L-arginine).

Signalling cascade. Proposed (BPC-157-derived) flow: peptide → ↑VEGFR2 expression + internalization → Akt phosphorylation → eNOS activation → NO → vasodilation, endothelial tube formation, angiogenesis; in parallel FAK–paxillin → fibroblast migration/spreading/survival, and GH-receptor↑ → JAK2 → proliferation. Net effect framed as 'cytoprotection' plus pro-healing angiogenesis. The arginate salt supplies arginine (eNOS substrate) as a counterion, which vendors claim augments the NO arm — mechanistically plausible but entirely unmeasured for PDA.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Marketed for subcutaneous injection (most common), with oral and topical also promoted; PDA-specific route data do not exist. BPC-157 has been studied by intraperitoneal, oral (drinking water), topical, IV, and IM routes in animals, by local injection in human pilots, and subcutaneously in the now-recruiting Phase 2 hamstring trial (NCT07437547).
Absorption / bioavailability
Unknown for PDA. No published bioavailability figure exists for PDA. For BPC-157, a 2026 biopharmaceutical review cites a formal 2-species preclinical ADME study reporting intramuscular bioavailability of roughly 14–51% depending on species; there is still no validated human bioavailability figure, and no comparative PK study supports the vendor claim that the arginate salt improves absorption versus the acetate salt.
Half-life
Not established for PDA. For BPC-157, a 2026 review reports a plasma half-life of under 30 minutes — confirmed in a formal 2-species preclinical ADME study and in a preliminary two-subject human pilot — which contrasts with biological effects lasting hours to days (a PK–PD disconnect). No robust, multi-subject human half-life has been published for BPC-157, and none for PDA.
Metabolism
Presumed rapid proteolytic degradation typical of small linear peptides. BPC-157 is described as unusually 'stable in human gastric juice,' which is the origin of the parent name; whether the arginate form changes metabolic stability is unstudied.
Excretion
Not characterized. Presumed peptidic breakdown to constituent amino acids with renal handling of fragments; no PDA data.
Notes
The 2026 Pharmaceutics review concludes BPC-157 has no pharmaceutical-grade validated formulation, no validated dosing regimen, and no completed Phase II trial, with only fragmentary PK — underscoring that PDA's 'improved stability/PK' selling point is unsubstantiated (PMID 42198317).

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

No dose-response data exist for PDA in any species. This monograph intentionally provides no dosing protocol. For context only: BPC-157 rodent studies used an extraordinarily wide effective range (roughly 10 ng/kg to 10 µg/kg) with a characteristically flat/bell-shaped response and 'no reported toxicity (LD1 not achieved),' but these figures are from rats and do not translate to human PDA dosing. The human interstitial-cystitis pilot used a single fixed 10 mg local (intravesical) injection, not a systemic regimen. Because product concentration and purity of compounded/grey-market PDA are unverified, any assumed dose-response is unreliable.

How it compares

  • vs BPC-157 (acetate salt, parent compound)
    same: Identical 15-amino-acid peptide sequence and presumed identical mechanism (VEGFR2/eNOS, FAK–paxillin, GH-receptor).
    different: PDA is an arginate rather than acetate salt and is marketed as more stable/bioavailable, but — unlike BPC-157, which now has a recruiting Phase 2 RCT — it has literally no direct published studies of its own; every claim is inherited.
  • vs TB-500 (thymosin β4 fragment)
    same: Both are grey-market 'healing' peptides, both pro-angiogenic, both carry the theoretical tumor-feeding caveat and anti-doping/FDA Category 2 flags.
    different: Different molecule and mechanism (actin-binding/cell migration vs VEGFR2 pathway); often stacked with BPC-157 in anecdote, not on evidence.
  • vs Tesamorelin (an actually-approved peptide)
    same: Both are injectable peptides discussed in longevity/fitness circles.
    different: Tesamorelin is FDA-approved with RCT evidence and a formal label (including cancer-risk and IGF-1 warnings); PDA has no approval, no trials, and no label — a stark contrast in evidence maturity.

History &amp; legal status

BPC-157 ('Body Protection Compound-157') is a synthetic 15-mer originally described as a fragment isolatable from human gastric juice, developed in the 1990s–2000s (Pliva/Croatia, codes PL-10 / PLD-116 / PL-14736) and taken into early inflammatory-bowel-disease/ulcerative-colitis trials, but never brought to approval. It surged in popularity in fitness/biohacking circles in the 2020s, and the first randomized placebo-controlled Phase 2 trial (NCT07437547, hamstring strain) began recruiting in 2026. Regulatory status is restrictive: BPC-157 is not FDA-approved; it appears on the FDA's Category 2 list of bulk drug substances nominated for compounding under sections 503A/503B that may present significant safety risks, meaning it cannot be freely compounded; DoD/OPSS flags it as a prohibited, unapproved 'research chemical' ingredient. WADA temporarily addressed the peptide class — BPC-157 was noted as banned in 2022 in some summaries, though it is not currently listed as banned by WADA and its listing status has shifted. Pentadeca Arginate emerged around 2024–2026 as a rebranded 'next-generation, more-stable' arginate-salt version, positioned by clinics and vendors partly to sidestep BPC-157's regulatory baggage and stability criticisms — despite the absence of any PDA-specific data.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does PDA actually behave like BPC-157 in vivo, or does the arginate salt change absorption, distribution, or activity? (Untested.)
  • Is the arginate salt genuinely more stable/bioavailable than acetate BPC-157, as marketed? (No comparative data.)
  • What is PDA's human pharmacokinetics — bioavailability, half-life, clearance? (Completely unknown; even BPC-157 human PK is limited to a preliminary 2-subject half-life.)
  • Does the pro-angiogenic mechanism carry a real (not just theoretical) tumor-promotion risk in humans?
  • Are the dramatic rodent healing effects reproducible in rigorous human RCTs — for either BPC-157 (the first, NCT07437547, is ongoing) or PDA?
  • What are the long-term and systemic safety effects with repeated dosing? (No toxicology exists.)
  • How variable are real-world compounded/grey-market PDA products in identity, purity, sterility, and dose?

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction context, not an endorsement or a protocol, and it contains no dosing guidance. Because PDA is untested and unregulated, the most protective steps are upstream: recognize there is no established safe dose and no quality guarantee, and involve a qualified clinician rather than self-experimenting. Given the pro-angiogenic mechanism, a personal or family history of cancer is a strong reason for caution and for a candid medical discussion before any use. If someone uses it despite the unknowns, sensible things to WATCH for include: injection-site reactions or signs of infection (spreading redness, warmth, pus, fever) that warrant prompt medical care; any new or unusual lumps, growths, or unexplained symptoms (given the theoretical tumor concern); and general changes in blood pressure or how they feel systemically. Product-quality vigilance matters — third-party purity/sterility testing, verified source, and awareness that 'research chemical' labeling means no consumer safety oversight. Finally, competitive athletes should note anti-doping prohibitions on this peptide class. None of this substitutes for individualized medical advice.

Caveats

The single most important caveat: there is NO published evidence on Pentadeca Arginate specifically — not one animal study, human trial, PK measurement, or stability comparison. Every benefit and every mechanism is extrapolated from BPC-157, and the entire premise (that the arginate salt is 'more stable/bioavailable') is an unverified marketing claim. Even the parent BPC-157 evidence base is weak and lopsided: much of the preclinical work comes from a single research group, effects are almost entirely in rodents/cell culture, and human data amount to a few tiny uncontrolled pilots with obvious placebo/expectation confounds (the first randomized placebo-controlled BPC-157 trial, NCT07437547, is only now recruiting and has no results). A theoretical safety concern runs through the whole class — the core mechanism is pro-angiogenic (VEGFR2/eNOS), so it could in principle nourish an undetected tumor; this is a reason for genuine caution in anyone with cancer history, even though no such harm has been documented. Because PDA is compounded or sold as a 'research chemical,' product identity, purity, sterility, dose accuracy, and endotoxin content are unverified and vary by source. It is not FDA-approved, BPC-157 is on the FDA Category 2 bulk-substances list, and the peptide class carries anti-doping/regulatory prohibitions. This monograph explains mechanism and what to watch for; it is not an endorsement and contains no dosing guidance.
