A soil bacterium's growth-blocking drug that flips cells from "build and store" to "clean house and recycle" — proven to extend lifespan in mice, still unproven for human longevity.

Class
mTOR inhibitor (macrolide; rapalog)
Primary target
mTOR kinase — specifically mTOR Complex 1 (mTORC1), reached indirectly by first binding the intracellular protein FKBP12
Human status
HONEST SEPARATION. Mechanism (FKBP12 -> mTORC1 inhibition -> less protein synthesis + more autophagy): ESTABLISHED biochemistry. LIFESPAN extension: proven in mice and other lab organisms (Harrison 2009, PMID 19587680), NOT demonstrated in humans — there is no human trial showing rapamycin makes people live longer, and there cannot be one yet given the timescale. Real HUMAN RCT evidence exists but for narrow endpoints: low-dose/intermittent rapalogs improved vaccine/immune response and reduced infections in older adults (Mannick 2014 PMID 25540326; Mannick 2018 PMID 29997249, both Sci Transl Med, using the rapalogs everolimus/RTB101, not rapamycin itself). The PEARL trial (Moel 2025, Aging, PMID 40188830) is the first RCT of rapamycin in healthy adults for healthspan — it found weekly low-dose rapamycin reasonably SAFE over a year but showed only modest, mostly non-significant healthspan signals; it is small, sponsor-linked (Aging journal/longevity-clinic ecosystem), and not proof of longevity benefit. The Kaeberlein 333-adult report (PMID 37191826) is an off-label survey, not a controlled trial. Bottom line for the audience: the cellular mechanism is real and the mouse longevity data are genuinely impressive, but human longevity benefit is SPECULATIVE/CONTESTED, dosing is unsettled, and current off-label use runs ahead of the evidence.

At a glance

  • Extends lifespan in genetically diverse mice, even when started in old age — robust, replicated rodent finding (Harrison 2009, PMID 19587680)
  • Improves immune/vaccine response in older adults at low intermittent doses — actual human RCT evidence (Mannick 2014, PMID 25540326; Mannick 2018, PMID 29997249)
  • Activates autophagy and dampens 'inflammaging' / senescent-cell signaling (senomorphic) — mostly rodent and cell data, some human immune-cell support
  • Approved human uses are NOT longevity: organ-transplant rejection prevention, coronary stents, and the rare lung disease lymphangioleiomyomatosis (LAM)
  • Off-target mTORC2 inhibition with chronic dosing can raise blood sugar, worsen insulin sensitivity and alter lipids — the main metabolic downside
  • Other known risks at immunosuppressive doses: mouth ulcers (canker sores), impaired wound healing, infections, and lab changes

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps2 human 3 established 2 rodent / preclinical

  1. Rapamycin grabs FKBP12, then jams mTORC1 Established

    Rapamycin doesn't block mTOR directly. It first latches onto a small cell protein called FKBP12, and that drug-protein pair wedges into mTOR Complex 1 (mTORC1), the cell's master 'grow now' switch. This physically blocks mTORC1 from acting on many of its targets. Arriola Apelo & Lamming 2016, J Gerontol A (PMID 27208895); Battaglioni et al. 2022, Cell (PMID 35580586)

  2. mTORC1 is the nutrient/growth sensor Established

    Normally mTORC1 senses that food (amino acids), insulin and energy are plentiful and tells the cell to build proteins, make fat, divide and store — the anabolic 'good times' program. Shutting it off mimics the cellular state of fasting or calorie restriction even when you've eaten. Simcox & Lamming 2022, Dev Cell (PMID 35316619); Yang et al. 2021, Sci Signal (PMID 34546793)

  3. Protein-building slows down Established

    With mTORC1 silenced, the cell dials down its protein factories (via targets S6K1 and 4E-BP1) and stops cranking out new protein. Less wasteful building means less cellular stress and fewer mistakes piling up — one leading theory for why it slows aging. Battaglioni et al. 2022, Cell (PMID 35580586); Simcox & Lamming 2022, Dev Cell (PMID 35316619)

  4. Autophagy (cellular recycling) switches on Evidence-rodent

    mTORC1 normally suppresses autophagy — the cell's self-cleaning system that digests damaged proteins and worn-out mitochondria. Release that brake and autophagy ramps up, clearing cellular junk. This recycling is one of the most cited reasons rapamycin may protect against age-related decline. Hofer et al. 2024, Autophagy (PMID 39212197); Arriola Apelo & Lamming 2016 (PMID 27208895)

  5. Lower inflammation and 'senomorphic' effects Evidence-human

    Blocking mTORC1 calms chronic low-grade inflammation ('inflammaging') and quiets the harmful signals that worn-out 'senescent' cells pump out. It doesn't kill those cells, it muffles them — that's why it's called senomorphic rather than senolytic. Selvarani, Mohammed & Richardson 2021, GeroScience (PMID 33037985); Aging Cell 2025 human immune study (PMID 41524558)

  6. Chronic dosing also hits mTORC2 — the source of side effects Evidence-human

    The catch: with ongoing daily dosing, rapamycin also partially blocks a second complex, mTORC2, which isn't its intended target. mTORC2 disruption is linked to the glucose-intolerance, insulin-resistance and lipid changes seen with continuous use — a key reason researchers test intermittent (weekly) dosing to keep the benefits while sparing mTORC2. Arriola Apelo & Lamming 2016 (PMID 27208895); Mukhopadhyay et al. 2016, Mol Cancer Ther (PMID 26916116)

  7. In mice, this extends lifespan — even started late Evidence-rodent

    When fed to genetically diverse mice starting at 20 months (old age in mouse terms), rapamycin extended remaining lifespan in both sexes — the landmark result that launched the longevity interest. This is robust and replicated in rodents, but is a mouse result, not a human one. Harrison et al. 2009, Nature (PMID 19587680)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Rapamycin is NOT a direct mTOR active-site (ATP-competitive) inhibitor — it is an ALLOSTERIC inhibitor that works as a two-component molecular glue. Step 1: rapamycin binds with very high affinity to the small (~12 kDa) cytosolic immunophilin FKBP12 (gene FKBP1A; the FK506-binding protein). Measured affinity for FKBP12 is sub-nanomolar: Kd ≈ 0.2 nM by NMR (ChEMBL CHEMBL413, J Med Chem 2014; pChEMBL 9.7) — i.e. it binds FKBP12 ~essentially stoichiometrically at therapeutic concentrations [Established, ChEMBL]. Step 2: the FKBP12–rapamycin binary complex then docks onto the FRB (FKBP12–Rapamycin Binding) domain of mTOR, a serine/threonine kinase. This composite surface sterically wedges into mTOR. ChEMBL/DailyMed list the action_type as INHIBITOR, direct_interaction = true, disease_efficacy = true (mechanism_id 383). Selectivity: against a ~300-kinase kinobead panel (Science 2017), rapamycin showed NO appreciable direct binding to ABL1, ABL2, AAK1, ACVR1, etc. (all apparent Kd > 30,000 nM = inactive) — its potency is overwhelmingly funneled through FKBP12, not promiscuous kinase inhibition [Established, ChEMBL]. Note the listed 'eIF4E Kd 0.5 nM' in ChEMBL is from one assay and is not the canonical primary target; FKBP12 is the bona fide direct target. Because the FKBP12–rapamycin complex contacts mTOR only when mTOR is in the mTORC1 (raptor-containing) conformation, mTORC1 is the acutely inhibited complex; mTORC2 (rictor-containing) lacks an accessible FRB docking geometry and is acutely 'spared' [Established].

Signalling cascade. mTOR sits in two physically distinct complexes. mTORC1 = mTOR + RAPTOR + mLST8 + PRAS40 + DEPTOR; it is the rapamycin-sensitive, nutrient/growth-factor-sensing, anabolic master switch on the lysosomal surface (activated by amino acids via the Rag GTPases/Ragulator and by growth factors via PI3K–Akt–TSC1/2–Rheb) [Established, corpus textbook]. mTORC2 = mTOR + RICTOR + mLST8 + mSIN1 + PROTOR + DEPTOR; relatively rapamycin-insensitive acutely; it phosphorylates Akt at Ser473 (the 'hydrophobic motif') plus SGK1 and PKC, governing insulin/PI3K signalling, cytoskeleton and survival [Established, corpus textbook]. When FKBP12–rapamycin inhibits mTORC1 it BLOCKS phosphorylation of the two canonical substrates: (1) p70 S6 kinase 1 (S6K1) at Thr389 → less rpS6 phosphorylation → reduced ribosome biogenesis and cap-dependent translation; and (2) 4E-BP1 → when 4E-BP1 stays hypophosphorylated it stays bound to eIF4E, sequestering it and shutting down cap-dependent translation initiation. (Notably, rapamycin inhibits the S6K1 arm far more completely than the 4E-BP1 arm — 4E-BP1 is relatively 'rapamycin-resistant', one reason rapalogs are partial mTORC1 inhibitors.) Net downstream effects: (a) protein synthesis and cell growth fall; (b) ULK1 is de-repressed (mTORC1 normally phosphorylates/inhibits ULK1), so AUTOPHAGY is induced; TFEB is dephosphorylated and enters the nucleus to drive lysosomal/autophagy gene programs; (c) lipogenesis (SREBP) and nucleotide synthesis fall. The autophagy induction is widely held to be central to rapamycin's pro-longevity action [Established, corpus + Hofer 2024 PMID 39212197]. The crucial wrinkle for side effects: although mTORC2 is acutely spared, CHRONIC rapamycin exposure sequesters newly synthesized mTOR into FKBP12-bound complexes and over time DISRUPTS mTORC2 assembly in vivo, lowering Akt-Ser473 phosphorylation — this is the molecular root of rapamycin's paradoxical glucose-handling effects (Lamming, Science 2012, PMID 22461615) [Established-rodent, mechanistic].

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (tablet and oral solution; brand Rapamune). Also formulated as an intravascular drug-eluting coating on coronary stents and as nab-sirolimus (albumin-bound, IV) for cancer. Longevity/biohacker use is oral, typically intermittent weekly dosing of compounded capsules — monitoring only, NO protocol given here.
Absorption / bioavailability
Rapidly but INCOMPLETELY and VARIABLY absorbed. Oral bioavailability is LOW — on the order of ~14% for the oral solution in transplant pharmacology literature — driven down by extensive first-pass intestinal/hepatic CYP3A metabolism and active efflux by intestinal P-glycoprotein (ABCB1) [Established, Gallant-Haidner 2000 PMID 10688254]. A high-fat meal alters exposure (raises AUC, lowers peak), so it is dosed consistently relative to food (PMID 10579146) [Evidence-human]. There is large inter-patient variability, which is why transplant use relies on whole-blood trough therapeutic drug monitoring.
Half-life
Long terminal half-life — roughly ~60 hours (commonly cited range ~57–63 h) in adults, supporting once-daily (transplant) or once-weekly (longevity) dosing; the long half-life is why steady state takes several days and why weekly pulse dosing still produces meaningful trough exposure [Established, Gallant-Haidner 2000 PMID 10688254].
Metabolism
Metabolized chiefly by CYP3A4 (and CYP3A5) in the gut wall and liver, producing multiple demethylated/hydroxylated metabolites; it is also a P-glycoprotein (ABCB1) substrate. The CYP3A5 genotype materially changes clearance: CYP3A5 expressers clear sirolimus faster (PMID 16815317, PMID 17192769) [Evidence-human]. This makes rapamycin highly prone to drug–drug and drug–food interactions: strong CYP3A inhibitors (e.g. azole antifungals, grapefruit juice, certain calcium-channel blockers) raise levels; inducers (rifampin, St John's wort) lower them.
Excretion
Predominantly biliary/fecal elimination of metabolites; renal excretion of parent drug is minor [Established, Gallant-Haidner 2000 PMID 10688254].
Notes
Rapamycin is the parent macrolide; the rapalogs everolimus and temsirolimus are semi-synthetic ESTERS/derivatives engineered for better solubility/PK (temsirolimus is an IV prodrug rapidly hydrolyzed to sirolimus). Rapamycin is large and lipophilic; CNS/BBB penetration is limited and it is a P-gp substrate at the blood–brain barrier, so brain exposure is modest relative to periphery [Evidence-rodent]. Metabolite clinical significance remains unproven (PMID 10688254).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Dose-response is far better defined in rodents than in humans, and the magnitude of human longevity/healthspan effect is essentially UNKNOWN. RODENT: In the NIA Interventions Testing Program, encapsulated rapamycin at ~14 ppm in chow (≈2.24 mg/kg/day food intake) extended median/maximal lifespan even when started at 600 days of age — +14% (females) / +9% (males) at the age of 90% mortality (Harrison/Strong, Nature 2009, PMID 19587680) [Evidence-rodent, strong]. Higher dietary doses (e.g. 42 ppm) and earlier-onset dosing produce larger lifespan gains in subsequent ITP cohorts, indicating a roughly dose-dependent longevity effect in mice. Mechanistically, in muscle/in-vivo rodent work, an injected ~2.5 mg/kg/day pellet was sufficient to suppress mTORC1-dependent growth (corpus PMID 29897818). The critical pharmacodynamic concept (Lamming 2012, PMID 22461615): LOW/intermittent exposure preferentially inhibits mTORC1 (desired, longevity), whereas HIGH/continuous exposure additionally disrupts mTORC2 (undesired, insulin resistance) — so effect QUALITY, not just magnitude, is dose- and schedule-dependent. HUMAN: There is no validated dose that demonstrably extends human lifespan. Transplant immunosuppression uses daily dosing titrated to whole-blood troughs (~5–15 ng/mL), a range chosen for immunosuppression, not longevity. Geroscience trials deliberately use much lower, intermittent dosing: Mannick used the rapalog RAD001/everolimus 0.5 mg daily, 5 mg weekly, or 20 mg weekly to improve vaccine response (PMID 25540326); the PEARL trial used 5 mg or 10 mg compounded rapamycin WEEKLY for 48 weeks (PMID 40188830). Human dose-RESPONSE for healthspan endpoints is not established — PEARL found weekly low-dose safe but largely failed its primary efficacy endpoint (no change in visceral fat). [Human dose-response: Contested/unknown]

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Everolimus (RAD001) — rapalog
    same: Same two-step mechanism: binds FKBP12, complex inhibits mTORC1 via the FRB domain; same downstream S6K1/4E-BP1/autophagy biology and same class side-effect profile (stomatitis, hyperlipidemia, immunosuppression).
    different: A 40-O-(2-hydroxyethyl) ester of rapamycin engineered for shorter half-life and more predictable oral PK; this is the agent used in the pivotal HUMAN geroscience immune trials (Mannick 2014/2018) and is FDA-approved in oncology (renal cell, breast, NET) and transplant. Shorter half-life makes daily titration easier and intermittent dosing schedules cleaner.
  • vs Temsirolimus (CCI-779) — rapalog prodrug
    same: Same FKBP12→mTORC1 allosteric mechanism and class effects.
    different: An IV-administered dihydroxymethyl propionic acid ESTER prodrug that is rapidly hydrolyzed in vivo TO sirolimus (sirolimus is effectively its active metabolite). FDA-approved for advanced renal cell carcinoma. Used clinically as an anticancer infusion, not for longevity.
  • vs Tacrolimus (FK506) — calcineurin inhibitor
    same: Also binds the SAME immunophilin FKBP12 with high affinity (the shared FKBP12 'docking' is why they compete for the same protein).
    different: Mechanistically OPPOSITE downstream: the FKBP12–tacrolimus complex inhibits CALCINEURIN (blocking NFAT/IL-2 transcription), NOT mTOR — so it does NOT inhibit mTORC1 and has no autophagy/longevity rationale. Because both compete for FKBP12, co-administration is pharmacologically antagonistic at the immunophilin. Different toxicity: tacrolimus is notably nephrotoxic/neurotoxic, whereas sirolimus is comparatively renal-sparing but causes the lipid/glucose/stomatitis profile.
  • vs Second-generation ATP-competitive mTOR kinase inhibitors (e.g. Torin, sapanisertib/MLN0128)
    same: Target the same mTOR kinase and suppress the anabolic program.
    different: These bind the ATP catalytic site DIRECTLY (no FKBP12 needed) and inhibit BOTH mTORC1 and mTORC2 fully, including the rapamycin-resistant 4E-BP1 arm. More complete pathway shutdown but predictably worse metabolic toxicity (full mTORC2 loss) — illustrating why rapamycin's PARTIAL, allosteric, mTORC1-biased inhibition is considered advantageous for longevity.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

"Rapamycin (sirolimus) was isolated in the early 1970s from Streptomyces hygroscopicus, a soil bacterium found in a soil sample from Rapa Nui (Easter Island) — hence the name 'rapamycin'. Originally pursued as an antifungal, it was found to be a potent immunosuppressant and antiproliferative. The mechanistic target it inhibits was later named after it: TOR ('Target Of Rapamycin', discovered in yeast genetic screens for rapamycin resistance), mTOR in mammals. FDA approval came on 15 September 1999 (brand Rapamune, Wyeth/Pfizer) for prophylaxis of organ rejection in kidney transplantation (Gallant-Haidner 2000, PMID 10688254). It is also used as a drug-eluting coronary stent coating (restenosis prevention) and was approved for the rare lung disease lymphangioleiomyomatosis (LAM). The rapalog derivatives everolimus and temsirolimus are FDA-approved in oncology and (everolimus) transplant. There is NO regulatory approval for rapamycin as an anti-aging/longevity drug — all longevity use is OFF-LABEL, frequently via compounded prescriptions (as in the AgelessRx-run PEARL trial). It is a prescription drug, not a controlled substance and not banned, but it is not an over-the-counter supplement; the WADA status is not as a classic PED. Current status (2025): widely prescribed for its labeled indications; intense but still-investigational interest in geroscience, where human efficacy for healthspan remains unproven (PEARL 2025, PMID 40188830)."

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Does rapamycin extend HUMAN lifespan or healthspan at all? Rodent lifespan extension is robust and replicated, but no human study has shown a longevity benefit — PEARL (2025) was safe but null on its primary endpoint (visceral fat). The entire human efficacy case rests on surrogate immune endpoints (Mannick) and animal data.
  • What is the optimal DOSE and SCHEDULE to inhibit mTORC1 (benefit) while sparing mTORC2 (avoiding insulin resistance)? Intermittent weekly dosing is the leading hypothesis from Lamming 2012, but the dose/interval that maximizes autophagy/longevity signaling without metabolic harm in humans is unknown.
  • Why does rapamycin extend lifespan even when started in OLD age (600-day mice)? Whether this reflects genuine slowing of aging vs. suppression of specific lethal pathologies (especially cancer) is genuinely contested — Neff 2013 (PMID 23863708) argues many effects are aging-independent.
  • How much of the longevity effect is autophagy-dependent vs. translation-suppression vs. metabolic? The spermidine/autophagy dependence (Hofer 2024, PMID 39212197) suggests autophagy is necessary, but the relative contributions are not resolved in mammals.
  • What are the real-world risks of long-term (years/decades) low-dose rapamycin in otherwise healthy people — infection risk, wound healing, cancer surveillance, gonadal/reproductive effects, bone? Trials are weeks to ~1 year; multi-decade safety in healthy adults is entirely unstudied.
  • Does rapamycin's limited blood-brain-barrier penetration (P-gp substrate) blunt any potential neuroprotective/cognitive longevity benefit, and would a brain-penetrant rapalog behave differently?

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (NOT a protocol, NOT dosing advice — these are signs/labs a clinician would track). METABOLIC: fasting glucose and HbA1c (chronic/higher exposure can cause insulin resistance via mTORC2 disruption — Lamming 2012); a fasting LIPID PANEL (triglycerides and LDL commonly rise). HEMATOLOGY: complete blood count — watch for thrombocytopenia, anemia, leukopenia (dose-dependent myelosuppression). RENAL: urinalysis for PROTEINURIA and serum creatinine (podocyte/glomerular effects). MUCOSA/SKIN: self-monitor for aphthous MOUTH ULCERS (mTOR-inhibitor stomatitis) and for slow-healing wounds or skin infections — and flag any planned SURGERY to a clinician, since impaired wound healing is a known on-target effect. INFECTION: because it is immunosuppressive, be alert to unusual, recurrent, or opportunistic infections; vaccination response/timing is worth discussing. DRUG INTERACTIONS: rapamycin is a CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein substrate, so grapefruit, azole antifungals, certain antibiotics/antivirals, and many other CYP3A drugs can sharply raise or lower blood levels — a medication/supplement review is essential. In supervised settings, whole-blood sirolimus TROUGH levels are the standard pharmacokinetic check. None of this is a substitute for individualized medical care."

Caveats

SEARCHER NOTES on this evidence set (8 sources returned this run, all retraction-checked clean via live Crossref/OpenAlex/PubMed): The broad mechanism query returned ~58,662 hits (above the 10k ESearch cap), so I did NOT accept any silent top-N as 'the evidence' — I narrowed to specific named landmark papers (Harrison ITP, Mannick RCTs, PEARL) plus high-quality mechanism reviews, and report that truncation here. CERTAINTY-INFLATION WARNINGS for the writer: (1) Do not let mouse lifespan data imply human lifespan benefit — keep that wall up. (2) Mannick's human RCTs used rapalogs (everolimus, RTB101), not rapamycin/sirolimus itself, and measured immune endpoints, not lifespan — attribute precisely. (3) The PEARL trial is published in 'Aging (Albany NY)' and tied to the longevity-clinic/supplement ecosystem — treat as suggestive safety data, flag the commercial-interest/conflict context, not as efficacy proof. (4) A high-credibility-but-low-weight Twitter source surfaced in the corpus (commercial_interest=True) — anecdote only, excluded from claims. (5) Autophagy-as-longevity-mechanism is well-supported in rodents/cells but the human causal chain to healthy aging is inferred, not measured — label Evidence-rodent. EXCLUDED as off-topic: numerous mTORC1 cancer/LAM/transplant mechanism papers (e.g. PMIDs 24631838, 37410387, 38195686) — relevant to mTOR biology but not to the longevity teaching package.
