6-OXO

The first mass-market OTC "estrogen blocker" — a genuine mechanism-based aromatase inhibitor whose single human trial raised free testosterone, DHT and the T:E ratio, but only partially, and via a hormonal/metabolite profile that overlaps with banned anabolic steroids.

Human / approved 7 citations
Class
Steroidal (type-I, mechanism-based / "suicide-substrate") aromatase inhibitor
Primary target
Aromatase (CYP19A1) — irreversible active-site inactivation that lowers conversion of androgens to estrogens, raising free testosterone, DHT and the testosterone:estradiol ratio
Human status
Marketed OTC in the US in the early-to-mid 2000s (by Ergopharm, associated with chemist Patrick Arnold — a figure also publicly linked to the BALCO designer-steroid affair) as an early mainstream 'aromatase inhibitor' supplement riding the post-androstenedione prohormone wave; the exact launch year and corporate history are reported in trade/journalistic sources rather than the peer-reviewed literature. Human evidence rests on essentially ONE small controlled study: Rohle/Willoughby 2007 (J Int Soc Sports Nutr; PMID 17949492), N=16 resistance-trained eugonadal men, 8 weeks at 300 or 600 mg/day, double-blind but with NO placebo arm (dose-comparison only, with a 3-week washout). It found real, statistically significant rises in free testosterone, DHT and T:E ratio over each subject's own baseline, but incomplete aromatase inhibition (estrone rose; authors stated aromatase was not completely inhibited) and no change in body composition. The enzymology (irreversible/suicide inhibition) is firmly established in human placental microsomes (Covey 1981; Numazawa series). 6-OXO is NOT an approved drug and was never FDA-approved for any indication. It is prohibited by WADA. In the US, androstenedione-class steroids were brought under Schedule III control primarily by the Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2004 (effective January 2005), with the Designer Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2014 later adding further designer steroids; products marketed today under this name are unapproved and, where they contain the steroid, subject to controlled-substance/adulteration concerns.

At a glance

  • Raised free testosterone (~84-90% over baseline) and free-T:estradiol ratio (~53-67%) in the single 8-week human trial
  • Large rise in DHT (~192-265%) — a potent androgen, relevant to hair-loss, acne and prostate concerns
  • Only PARTIAL aromatase inhibition: estrone rose and the authors concluded aromatase was not completely inhibited — not a full estrogen block
  • No measured change in body composition despite the hormone shifts, in the one trial
  • No adverse changes in clinical safety chemistry (liver, lipids, blood counts) over 8 weeks in that small trial
  • Produces anabolic-steroid-classed urinary metabolites and is a WADA-banned doping agent, detectable ~24-37 h after a dose
  • Rebound risk: plausible post-discontinuation estrogen overshoot, but NOT directly measured in the trial

Mechanism, step by step

  1. 6-OXO binds the aromatase active site as a substrate mimic Established

    6-OXO (androst-4-ene-3,6,17-trione) is an androstenedione analogue with an extra ketone at C-6. It occupies the substrate pocket of aromatase (CYP19A1), the enzyme that normally converts testosterone and androstenedione into estrogens. In human placental microsomes it binds with an apparent Ki of 0.43 microM and causes a time-dependent (not purely competitive) loss of activity. PMID 7472286 (Covey & Hood, Endocrinology 1981) — apparent Ki 0.43 microM in human placental microsomes

  2. The enzyme oxidises 6-OXO at its own C-19 methyl group Established

    Because 6-OXO sits in the pocket like a real substrate, aromatase performs its normal chemistry on it — the C-19 methyl group is oxygenated (an NADPH/O2-dependent process, as for testosterone), generating a 19-oxo intermediate. That the inactivation requires NADPH, is prevented by adding the natural substrate androstenedione, and is not blocked by the nucleophile cysteine are the classic hallmarks of active-site-directed, mechanism-based chemistry. PMID 8937433 (Numazawa, Biochem Pharmacol 1996); NADPH-dependent, substrate-protectable, cysteine-resistant kinetics also shown for AT/AT-analogues in PMID 8430444 (Numazawa, Steroids 1993)

  3. A reactive electrophile forms and covalently binds the enzyme — irreversible 'suicide' inactivation Established

    The 19-oxo intermediate is converted (via a 19-hydroxy-19-hydroperoxide) into a 4beta,5beta-epoxy-19-oxo species — a reactive electrophile that latches covalently onto the aromatase active site and permanently inactivates that enzyme molecule. This is 'mechanism-based' (suicide-substrate) inhibition: unlike a reversible blocker, the enzyme has to be re-synthesised to recover. The synthesised epoxide reproduces the irreversible, active-site-directed inactivation, confirming its identity as the reactive intermediate. PMID 8937433 (Numazawa, Biochem Pharmacol 1996) — 4beta,5beta-epoxy-19-oxo derivative identified as the reactive electrophile

  4. Less estrogen is produced from circulating androgens Evidence-human

    With a fraction of aromatase molecules permanently disabled, less testosterone and androstenedione get converted to estradiol and estrone. This is the intended effect — lowering the estrogen a man makes from his own androgens rather than lowering testosterone. In the one human trial the T:estradiol ratio rose, consistent with reduced aromatization, but the blockade was clearly incomplete (see step 6). PMID 17949492 (Rohle/Willoughby, J Int Soc Sports Nutr 2007) — T:E ratio rose; incomplete aromatase inhibition

  5. Free testosterone, DHT and the T:E ratio rise Evidence-human

    In the one controlled human study (16 resistance-trained, eugonadal men, 8 weeks, 300 or 600 mg/day), free testosterone rose ~84-90%, DHT ~192-265%, and the free-T:estradiol ratio ~53-67% versus each man's own baseline. Falling estrogen relative to androgen can also relieve estrogen's negative feedback on the hypothalamic-pituitary axis, the classic rationale for using aromatase inhibitors to raise a man's own testosterone; note this feedback step is inferred from AI pharmacology rather than directly demonstrated for 6-OXO. PMID 17949492 (Rohle/Willoughby 2007)

  6. Blockade is only partial and incomplete at supplement doses Evidence-human

    Crucially, the authors concluded that 300-600 mg/day 'did not completely inhibit aromatase' — estrone actually ROSE (22% at 300 mg, 52% at 600 mg) and there were no significant differences between the two doses. So 6-OXO behaves as a weak, partial AI in vivo, not a full estrogen shutdown like prescription letrozole/anastrozole/exemestane. Effects were not clearly dose-proportional between 300 and 600 mg. PMID 17949492 (Rohle/Willoughby 2007)

  7. 6-OXO is itself metabolised to androgenic metabolites and is detectable as doping Established

    6-OXO is not inert once absorbed: it is metabolised to androst-4-ene-6alpha-ol-3,17-dione (major) and androst-4-ene-6alpha,17beta-diol-3-one (minor), is itself classed as an anabolic steroid, and yields a urinary steroid profile that doping labs can detect — which is why WADA prohibits it and GC-MS/LC-MS methods were developed to catch it. This dual identity (aromatase inhibitor plus steroid metabolites) is why calling it a clean 'estrogen blocker' is misleading. PMID 15828056 (Van Thuyne 2005); PMID 16213800 (Deventer 2005)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Androst-4-ene-3,6,17-trione is an androstenedione analogue bearing an additional 6-keto group. In human placental microsomes it binds aromatase (CYP19A1) with an apparent Ki of 0.43 microM and produces a time-dependent decline in enzyme activity (Covey & Hood 1981). It is a type-I steroidal inhibitor (a substrate-mimic acted on by the enzyme), distinct from azole (type-II) inhibitors that coordinate the heme iron. Certain ring-substituted AT analogues are far more potent (Ki 25-42 nM), but those are different compounds, not 6-OXO itself.

Signalling cascade. Because it is accepted as a pseudo-substrate, aromatase carries out oxygenation of the C-19 methyl group (NADPH- and O2-dependent), generating a 19-oxo intermediate; this is converted to a 4beta,5beta-epoxy-19-oxo reactive electrophile (via a 19-hydroxy-19-hydroperoxide) that covalently alkylates the active site, permanently inactivating that enzyme copy. Inactivation is time-dependent, requires NADPH, is prevented by the natural substrate androstenedione, and is NOT rescued by the nucleophile L-cysteine — hallmarks of active-site-directed mechanism-based (suicide) inhibition. Net physiological consequence: reduced androgen->estrogen conversion, relief of estrogenic negative feedback on the HPG axis, and a rise in free T, DHT and the T:E ratio.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (marketed as capsules/tablets).
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally absorbed to a pharmacologically meaningful extent (measurable hormonal effects and urinary excretion of parent plus metabolites in humans), but no rigorous human bioavailability/AUC data have been published; oral bioavailability of steroidal AIs is generally modest and first-pass-limited (general class expectation, not a 6-OXO-specific measurement).
Half-life
Not formally characterised in humans; the doping-control detection window is short — parent and the 6alpha,17beta-diol metabolite detectable ~24 h, the 6alpha-ol metabolite ~37 h after a manufacturer-recommended dose, implying relatively rapid clearance.
Metabolism
Hepatic/peripheral hydroxylation: major urinary metabolite androst-4-ene-6alpha-ol-3,17-dione; minor androst-4-ene-6alpha,17beta-diol-3-one. Parent is predominantly excreted in conjugated form; both metabolites are excreted solely as conjugates.
Excretion
Renal, predominantly as conjugated (glucuronide/sulfate) parent and phase-I metabolites.
Notes
The short detection window is consistent with a short-lived compound; irreversible enzyme inactivation, however, means functional aromatase suppression can outlast plasma levels until new enzyme is synthesised.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Human data exist at only two doses. In Rohle/Willoughby 2007, both 300 mg/day and 600 mg/day for 8 weeks significantly raised free testosterone (~90% vs ~84%), DHT (~192% vs ~265%) and T:E ratio (~53% vs ~67%) over baseline, with estrone rising more at 600 mg (22% vs 52%). There were NO statistically significant differences between the 300 and 600 mg groups, so a clear monotonic dose-response was not demonstrated across this narrow range, and the authors concluded aromatase was not completely inhibited at either dose — indicating a ceiling of only partial inhibition. In vitro the parent has moderate potency (Ki ~0.43 microM), far weaker than prescription steroidal AIs. No dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs Anastrozole / letrozole (non-steroidal, reversible AIs)
    same: All lower androgen->estrogen conversion and are used off-label in men to raise the T:E ratio
    different: Anastrozole/letrozole are reversible, potent, FDA-approved prescription AIs that strongly suppress estrogen; 6-OXO is an OTC steroidal irreversible inhibitor that only PARTIALLY inhibited aromatase in humans (aromatase not completely inhibited; estrone rose)
  • vs Exemestane (steroidal suicide-substrate AI, prescription)
    same: Same mechanistic family — irreversible, mechanism-based inactivation of aromatase by a steroidal substrate mimic
    different: Exemestane is a validated, FDA-approved breast-cancer drug with robust human efficacy/safety data; 6-OXO is a far weaker, unapproved supplement-grade analogue with a single small trial and doping-agent metabolites
  • vs ATD (androsta-1,4,6-triene-3,17-dione)
    same: Both are irreversible steroidal AI 'supplements' (ATD was the active ingredient in products such as Novedex Xtreme), banned in sport, and share the suicide-substrate mechanism
    different: In the same assay ATD has a somewhat lower Ki (0.18 microM vs 0.43 microM for 6-OXO) and a distinct doping fingerprint that includes boldenone-type metabolites; 6-OXO was the earlier, higher-profile first-to-market product
  • vs Androstenedione ('andro', the prohormone)
    same: Structurally the parent scaffold of 6-OXO; both were marketed to raise testosterone
    different: Androstenedione is an aromatase SUBSTRATE that can raise estradiol (the opposite estrogen effect); 6-OXO is the 6-keto analogue that instead INHIBITS aromatase

History & legal status

6-OXO was one of the early mainstream OTC products explicitly marketed as an aromatase-inhibiting 'estrogen blocker' for men and bodybuilders, sold by Ergopharm (associated with chemist Patrick Arnold, also publicly linked to the BALCO designer-steroid affair) during the post-androstenedione prohormone era of the 2000s; specifics of launch date and corporate history come from trade/journalistic reporting rather than peer-reviewed sources. Doping laboratories characterised its metabolism in 2005 (Van Thuyne; Deventer), and WADA prohibits it as an anabolic agent/aromatase inhibitor. In the US, androstenedione-class steroids were placed under Schedule III control chiefly by the Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2004 (effective January 2005), with the Designer Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2014 later adding further designer steroids; products still sold under the 6-OXO name are unapproved and, where they contain the steroid, raise controlled-substance/adulteration concerns. It was never an FDA-approved drug for any indication.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No placebo-controlled human trial exists — the single study compared only two doses, so the true effect size vs placebo is unknown
  • No functional/clinical outcomes (strength, muscle, libido, symptom relief) were demonstrated; body composition did not change
  • Long-term safety (>8 weeks), effects in older or hypogonadal men, and bone/lipid consequences of chronic partial aromatase inhibition are uncharacterised
  • Magnitude and clinical significance of any estrogen rebound after discontinuation were not directly measured
  • How much of the 'testosterone' effect is direct androgen action of its metabolites versus pure aromatase inhibition is not disentangled
  • Real-world product identity/purity is unknown for anything sold today given its banned/adulterated status

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is a harm-reduction/awareness section, not an endorsement or protocol. Anyone who has used a product like this and is concerned should involve a clinician; no dosing guidance is given. Relevant things a clinician might MONITOR: a full sex-hormone panel (total and free testosterone, estradiol by a sensitive/LC-MS assay, DHT, LH, FSH, SHBG) to detect over- or under-suppression or rebound; a fasting lipid panel (potent AIs can worsen the lipid profile as a class effect); markers of estrogen deficiency in men (bone density with prolonged use, joint symptoms, libido/mood); and liver enzymes and general chemistry (reassuringly unchanged over 8 weeks in the trial, but anabolic-steroid-class agents generally warrant checks). Anyone subject to drug testing should be aware that 6-OXO and its metabolites are WADA-banned and detectable for at least ~24-37 h. Features that would warrant stopping and seeking care include breast tenderness or gynecomastia (which could reflect rebound or incomplete estrogen control), rapidly worsening acne or hair loss, mood disturbance, or joint pain.

Caveats

HYPE-vs-DATA: the entire human case for 6-OXO is one 16-person, 8-week, placebo-free trial with no functional outcomes (strength, muscle, symptoms) — and body composition did not change. The headline percentage increases (e.g. +90% free T) are within-subject vs baseline in a tiny sample, not vs placebo, so regression-to-the-mean and assay variability cannot be excluded. It is a WEAK, PARTIAL aromatase inhibitor: the authors explicitly concluded aromatase was not completely inhibited, and estrone actually rose — unlike prescription letrozole/anastrozole/exemestane, which strongly suppress estrogen. It is NOT a clean 'estrogen blocker' — it is itself classed as an anabolic steroid, yields steroid metabolites, is banned in sport, and is detectable in doping tests. The large DHT rise is a real androgenic-side-effect signal (hair loss, prostate, acne), not a benefit. Because it works partly through the HPG feedback loop and is short-acting/incomplete, stopping it could allow estrogen to rebound, though this was not directly measured. Safety data extend only to 8 weeks of normal clinical chemistry in healthy young men — nothing on long-term use, older or hypogonadal men, or interaction with other anabolic steroids. No dosing guidance is given here by design.
