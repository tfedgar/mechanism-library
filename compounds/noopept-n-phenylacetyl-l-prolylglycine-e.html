Noopept

Main target: Proposed primary molecular target. Human evidence is still limited.

EvidenceLimited human data
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A synthetic dipeptide "smart drug" that is really a prodrug — your body converts it into a brain peptide (cycloprolylglycine) that flips on a low-oxygen survival switch (HIF-1) and nudges up the brain's own growth factors. Strong rodent and test-tube story; the human evidence is thin and mostly Russian.

Limited human data 25 citations
Class
Proline-containing dipeptide nootropic / neuroprotective agent; designed as a peptide analog of piracetam (a "racetam-derived" dipeptide)
Primary target
Proposed primary molecular target: HIF prolyl-hydroxylase (PHD2) inhibition → stabilization of HIF-1alpha → increased HIF-1 transcriptional activity
Human status
Not approved in the US/EU; no FDA approval and not a dietary supplement — sold as a research chemical / unregulated nootropic online. Marketed and studied as a prescription nootropic ('Noopept'/omberacetam) primarily in Russia and nearby countries. Human evidence is limited to small, mostly open-label or EEG studies in cognitively impaired patients (stroke, post-traumatic/vascular MCI), not large blinded RCTs, and there is essentially no controlled human evidence for enhancement in healthy people. The entire molecular mechanism story (HIF-1, CPG prodrug, NGF/BDNF, alpha7) is built from rodent and in-vitro data — be explicit about that gap on camera.

At a glance

  • Stabilizes HIF-1alpha (inhibits prolyl hydroxylase PHD2) — proposed master mechanism, drives a hypoxia/adaptive-survival gene program (in-vitro human cells)
  • Raises NGF and BDNF mRNA in hippocampus with no tolerance on chronic dosing (rodent) — basis for the 'neurotrophic' reputation
  • Acts as a prodrug for cycloprolylglycine (CPG), an endogenous brain dipeptide (rodent PK)
  • Engages cholinergic memory circuitry via alpha7 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (rodent)
  • Neuroprotective: antioxidant, lowers calcium overload, reduces tau hyperphosphorylation and amyloid/alpha-synuclein toxicity, anti-apoptotic (in-vitro / rodent disease models)
  • Reported pro-cognitive + mild anxiolytic effect and favorable EEG changes in small human MCI/stroke studies (weak, open-label, Russian)
  • Does NOT stimulate cell proliferation (i.e., the HIF-1 effect is not the cancer-promoting kind) per the developers' own in-vitro work (PMID 30788746)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps2 human / established 5 rodent / preclinical

  1. Noopept is a prodrug — it barely survives as itself Evidence-rodent

    After dosing, Noopept is rapidly broken down and is essentially undetectable in rat brain within an hour. One of its breakdown products, cyclo-L-prolylglycine (CPG), rises ~2.5-fold — and CPG is identical to a peptide the brain already makes on its own. So the working idea is that Noopept is a delivery vehicle and CPG is the actual active molecule. (Human pharmacokinetics confirm Noopept itself is short-lived and CPG appears as the active metabolite, but the 'CPG does the work' claim is built on rodent data.) PMID 9358206; PMID 30378564

  2. The likely master switch: it stabilizes HIF-1, the low-oxygen survival program Evidence-in vitro

    In human cell lines (HEK293 and SH-SY5Y neuroblastoma), Noopept selectively boosted the activity of one transcription factor — HIF-1 — out of nine tested, while leaving CREB, NF-kB, p53 and others untouched. The proposed mechanism is that Noopept (and its metabolite) bind and inhibit prolyl hydroxylase (PHD2), the enzyme that normally tags HIF-1alpha for destruction. Block that enzyme and HIF-1alpha is stabilized, switching on a suite of adaptive 'survive-low-oxygen' genes. The authors propose this HIF-positive effect as Noopept's PRIMARY mechanism. Note: this is in-vitro human-cell and molecular-docking work, not whole-human data. PMID 27099787; PMID 33119829

  3. It nudges up the brain's own growth factors (NGF and BDNF) Evidence-rodent

    In rat hippocampus, both single and 28-day dosing raised messenger-RNA for NGF and BDNF — the brain's key 'fertilizer' growth factors for neurons and synapses. Importantly there was NO tolerance: chronic dosing actually potentiated the effect rather than wearing off. This is the real basis for the 'Noopept raises BDNF/NGF' claim you'll see repeated online. CAUTION: a separate 2021 Heliyon paper often cited for Noopept-BDNF-microglia effects has been RETRACTED — do not rely on it. The clean support is this rat-hippocampus mRNA study. PMID 19240853

  4. It works through the cholinergic system — via alpha7 nicotinic receptors Evidence-rodent

    Behaviorally, Noopept reverses memory deficits caused by blocking BOTH muscarinic and nicotinic acetylcholine receptors, linking it to the cholinergic 'memory' system. Mechanistically, in rat hippocampal slices Noopept increased firing of inhibitory interneurons and boosted inhibitory currents onto CA1 pyramidal cells — and this was almost completely abolished by alpha7-nicotinic-receptor blockers (alpha-bungarotoxin, methyllycaconitine). So alpha7 nicotinic acetylcholine receptors appear to be an important site of action. Rodent electrophysiology and behavior only. PMID 36195298; PMID 19145351

  5. Neuroprotection: antioxidant, anti-amyloid, anti-tau, anti-apoptotic Evidence-in vitro

    Across disease-model systems Noopept blunts several damage pathways: in a PC12 cell Alzheimer's model it cut reactive oxygen species, calcium overload, mitochondrial-driven apoptosis and tau hyperphosphorylation; in mice it restored spatial memory and changed antibody responses to amyloid; and in test tubes it sequestered toxic alpha-synuclein and amyloid oligomers into less-harmful aggregates, rescuing cells. This is the 'neuroprotective' half of its profile — consistent and repeated, but entirely in rodent and in-vitro models. PMID 25096780; PMID 17092975; PMID 21986202

  6. Designed on purpose as a piracetam 'beta-turn' mimic Established

    Noopept was not found by accident — Russian chemists (Zakusov Institute) built it by taking the active 3D shape (a peptide beta-turn) of the classic nootropic piracetam and converting it into a small, stable, orally-available dipeptide. Being a dipeptide lets it cross the gut and blood-brain barrier using natural peptide transporters, and it is active at far lower doses than piracetam. This is design/medicinal-chemistry background, not a clinical claim. PMID 30295186; PMID 39298839

  7. What the human data actually shows (and doesn't) Evidence-human

    Human evidence is limited, small, and almost entirely Russian. An open-label study in 60 stroke patients reported improved cognitive scores (MMSE) after 2 months at 20 mg/day with good tolerability; an EEG study in mild cognitive impairment (post-traumatic and vascular) showed nootropic-type EEG shifts (more alpha/beta, less delta) plus a mild anxiolytic pattern. There are NO large, blinded, placebo-controlled Western trials, and there is essentially no human evidence for cognitive enhancement in healthy young people. Treat 'it boosts memory' as unproven in humans. PMID 22500312; PMID 19008801

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Noopept (GVS-111; ethyl ester of N-phenylacetyl-L-prolylglycine) has NO well-characterized high-affinity receptor of its own — there is no published Ki/EC50 at a classical neurotransmitter receptor, and it is NOT a piracetam-style AMPA-receptor ligand in any binding sense. The best-supported molecular target is the enzyme HIF-prolyl hydroxylase 2 (PHD2/EGLN1): molecular docking shows the active L-isomer of Noopept and its metabolite (L-N-phenylacetylprolyl) bind the PHD2 active site, while the pharmacologically inactive D-isomer does not, and at 10 microM Noopept selectively raised HIF-1 DNA-binding activity in a reporter screen of 9 transcription factors (CREB, NFAT, NF-kB, p53, STAT1, GAS, VDR, HSF1, HIF-1) — ONLY HIF-1 responded [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 27099787, 33119829]. Functionally it behaves as a partial PHD2 INHIBITOR / HIF-1 stabilizer. Electrophysiology in mollusc/rodent neurons shows weak, non-selective modulation of voltage-gated currents: at 1-2 microM GVS-111 reduced high-threshold Ca2+ current ~39%, IK(V) ~49% (1 microM), and Ca2+-dependent K+ current ~69% (0.1 microM) — but only in a subset of cells, i.e. low-potency, non-specific channel effects rather than a defined receptor [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 9195198]. EEG/glutamatergic-blocker work suggests its acute cortical/hippocampal effects depend on NMDA-receptor activation and its tolerance on AMPA/quisqualate receptors, implying indirect glutamatergic involvement, not direct receptor agonism [Evidence-rodent; PMID 21414388]. Claimed muscarinic-cholinergic 'sensitization' and benzodiazepine-site effects are described in the originator literature but lack independent affinity data [Contested].

Signalling cascade. Proposed primary cascade (originator-group data): Noopept is a PRODRUG. After absorption it is rapidly cleaved to the cyclic dipeptide cyclo-L-prolylglycine (cycloprolylglycine, CPG), which is also an endogenous brain neuropeptide; CPG is regarded as the active species [Evidence-rodent; PMID 9358206, 30378564]. Branch 1 (HIF pathway): Noopept/metabolite inhibit PHD2, preventing prolyl-hydroxylation and proteasomal degradation of the HIF-1alpha subunit; HIF-1alpha is stabilized, dimerizes with HIF-1beta, and drives hypoxia-adaptive gene transcription (erythropoietic, glycolytic, angiogenic, pro-survival genes) — confirmed in SH-SY5Y and HEK293 cells with an ODD-Luc reporter and HIF-1alpha protein increase [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 27099787, 33119829]. Branch 2 (neurotrophin pathway): chronic and acute dosing increases NGF and BDNF mRNA in rat hippocampus (acute raises both; 28-day dosing potentiates rather than producing tolerance) — these neurotrophins then act through TrkA/TrkB->PI3K/Akt and MAPK to promote neuronal survival, synaptic plasticity and memory consolidation [Evidence-rodent; PMID 19240853]. Branch 3 (neuroprotection/anti-apoptosis): in Abeta(25-35)-injured PC12 cells Noopept lowered ROS and intracellular Ca2+, preserved mitochondrial membrane potential, reduced early/late apoptosis and tau hyperphosphorylation at Ser396, and restored neurite outgrowth [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 25096780]; it also rescued H2O2- and Down-syndrome cortical-neuron apoptosis (IC50 ~1.21 microM) [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 12711349] and sequestered toxic alpha-synuclein oligomers into harmless fibrils via hydrophobic (non-stereospecific) interaction [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 21986202]. Notably Noopept does NOT stimulate cell proliferation (no change in Ki-67 or cell-cycle distribution), arguing the neurotrophic effect is plasticity/survival, not mitogenesis [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 30788746].

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (tablet/sublingual in Russian clinical use) and intraperitoneal in rodent studies; intranasal explored experimentally. Orally active even though it is a peptide, because the dipeptide is small enough to use ATP-dependent peptide transporters in enterocytes and at the blood-brain barrier [Evidence-rodent/Established for the formulation; PMID 11782792, 30295186].
Absorption / bioavailability
Designed for oral activity and BBB penetration; the parent dipeptide is enzymatically labile. In rats the PARENT Noopept is essentially undetectable in brain 1 h after 5 mg/kg i.p. (below HPLC limit of detection) — it is consumed by first-pass/plasma/brain esterase and peptidase cleavage [Evidence-rodent; PMID 9358206]. Human absolute bioavailability is not robustly published; quantitative %F numbers should be treated as unknown [Evidence-human: gap].
Half-life
Short. The parent compound's half-life is on the order of minutes (rapidly metabolized; undetectable in brain within ~1 h in rat). The active metabolite cycloprolylglycine (CPG) persists longer and has a distinct, longer pharmacokinetic profile than the parent, which is why effects outlast measurable parent drug [Evidence-rodent; PMID 9358206, 30378564]. Precise human t1/2 values are not well established [gap].
Metabolism
Hydrolytic/peptidase metabolism. Plasma and brain esterases/peptidases cleave the ethyl ester and peptide bonds, yielding phenylacetic acid, prolylglycine and cyclo-L-prolylglycine (CPG); only CPG rose (~2.5-fold) in rat brain 1 h after dosing, and CPG formation from Noopept was reproduced in vitro with plasma/brain enzymes — establishing Noopept as a prodrug for the endogenous cyclopeptide CPG [Evidence-rodent; PMID 9358206, 30378564].
Excretion
Not well characterized in the public literature; metabolites (phenylacetic acid, glycine/proline fragments) enter normal amino-acid/organic-acid catabolic and renal elimination routes. No quantitative human renal/biliary excretion data identified [gap].
Notes
Prodrug -> active metabolite cycloprolylglycine (an endogenous brain dipeptide). Stereospecific: the L-isomer is active (binds PHD2 in silico) and the D-isomer is inactive [PMID 27099787]. ~1000x more potent by weight than piracetam in rodent nootropic assays, allowing low absolute doses [Evidence-rodent; PMID 30295186, 12596521]. BBB-permeant by design. Human PK (bioavailability, half-life, excretion) is the single largest data gap.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Rodent (well documented): nootropic/neuroprotective effects appear at very low oral or i.p. doses, typically 0.1-1 mg/kg, far below piracetam's 200-400 mg/kg — e.g. cognitive/anti-amyloid restoration in olfactory-bulbectomized mice at 0.01 mg/kg x ~26 days [Evidence-rodent; PMID 17092975]; EEG alpha/beta activation in conscious rats at 0.2 mg/kg s.c. [Evidence-rodent; PMID 21414388]; NGF/BDNF mRNA increase in hippocampus after single and 28-day dosing with a non-tolerance, potentiating profile [Evidence-rodent; PMID 19240853]. In vitro neuroprotection is seen from ~10 nM to 100 microM (IC50 ~1.2 microM against H2O2 apoptosis) [Evidence-in vitro; PMID 12711349], with transcription-factor and PC12 effects studied at ~10 microM [PMID 27099787, 25096780]. Human dose-response: a single comparative clinical study used a fixed Noopept regimen (low milligram-range daily, roughly an order of magnitude lower in total dose than piracetam) versus piracetam in mild cognitive impairment — it reported comparable or modestly better symptom improvement, but it was NOT a placebo-controlled dose-ranging study, so a true human dose-response curve is UNKNOWN [Evidence-human, low certainty; PMID 19234797]. No validated human threshold, ceiling, or toxic-dose data exist; the rodent low-dose potency does not translate to a known human mg target. This profile is descriptive only and is not a dosing recommendation.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Piracetam
    same: Both are 'racetam-family' nootropics targeting cognition/memory; Noopept was explicitly designed as a dipeptide topological analog of piracetam's pyrrolidinone, and both share NMDA-dependent EEG activation
    different: Noopept is ~1000x more potent by weight (active at sub-mg/kg vs hundreds of mg/kg), is a peptide prodrug (->cycloprolylglycine) rather than a stable small molecule, and adds distinct neurotrophic (NGF/BDNF) and HIF-1 mechanisms piracetam lacks (piracetam did not activate HIF-1 or any screened TF) [PMID 30295186, 27099787, 21414388]
  • vs Cycloprolylglycine (CPG)
    same: CPG is Noopept's active metabolite and an endogenous brain dipeptide; it reproduces Noopept's nootropic spectrum and is proposed to mediate the BDNF effect
    different: CPG is the downstream endogenous species (not a designed drug); Noopept is the orally stable, BBB-permeant prodrug delivering it. CPG has its own longer, distinct PK [PMID 9358206, 30378564]
  • vs Semax (ACTH4-10 analog peptide)
    same: Both are Russian-developed peptide nootropics that upregulate hippocampal/cortical NGF and BDNF and are used clinically in Russia for cognitive/cerebrovascular indications
    different: Semax is a heptapeptide (intranasal, melanocortin-related) acting largely through neurotrophin transcription and BDNF/TrkB, with no HIF-1/PHD2 mechanism; Noopept is a smaller orally active dipeptide whose primary molecular target appears to be PHD2/HIF-1 [PMID 11457573, 19662538 vs 27099787]
  • vs Selank (anxiolytic peptide)
    same: Both are Zakusov-Institute Russian peptide drugs; co-studied for hippocampal synaptic modulation — at low microM both increased frequency of spike-dependent inhibitory postsynaptic currents in CA1, suggesting interneuron activation
    different: Selank is primarily an anxiolytic (tuftsin analog, immuno/GABA-modulatory); Noopept is primarily a nootropic/neuroprotectant with the HIF/neurotrophin mechanism [PMID 22390072]

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

"Developed in the 1990s at the V.V. Zakusov State Research Institute of Pharmacology (Russian Academy of Medical Sciences), Moscow, by Gudasheva, Ostrovskaya, Seredenin and colleagues as a designed dipeptide analog of piracetam (development codes GVS-111 / DVD-111). The strategy was 'peptide drug-based design' — converting a known nonpeptide nootropic into a dipeptide beta-turn mimic [PMID 30295186, 12596521]. It is a registered/approved prescription medicine in Russia and some neighboring countries (marketed as Noopept) for mild cognitive impairment, asthenic and emotional-labile disorders of vascular/traumatic origin. It has NEVER been approved by the FDA or EMA and is not an approved drug in the US, UK or EU. Regulatory status elsewhere is patchwork: sold as an unapproved 'research chemical'/nootropic supplement in many markets; it is a SCHEDULED/controlled or prescription-only substance in some jurisdictions (e.g. restricted in countries that regulate unapproved nootropics) — buyers should verify local law. Most efficacy and mechanism data originate from the single originator institute, which is an important provenance caveat."

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Human pharmacokinetics are essentially unpublished in robust form: absolute oral bioavailability, human half-life, the actual brain exposure of cycloprolylglycine in people, and excretion routes are all unknown.
  • Whether the rodent BDNF/NGF upregulation and HIF-1 activation actually occur in the human brain at the doses used — no human biomarker or imaging study confirms target engagement.
  • Independent replication: nearly all efficacy/mechanism data come from one originator institute (Zakusov); large, blinded, placebo-controlled human trials by independent groups are absent, so effect size and even basic efficacy in healthy or impaired humans remain uncertain.
  • Long-term safety of chronic HIF-1 stabilization (haematologic, vascular, and theoretical oncologic consequences) has not been studied in humans, despite the in-vitro finding that Noopept itself is non-proliferative.
  • Dose-response in humans: no validated minimum-effective, ceiling, or toxic dose; the extreme rodent potency (sub-mg/kg) does not map to a known human target dose.
  • The precise primary mechanism is still debated — PHD2/HIF-1 vs neurotrophin induction vs the endogenous-CPG hypothesis — and whether cycloprolylglycine's reported BDNF-raising effect is real and reproducible (a key supporting paper, PMID 27599510, should be re-checked for retraction/expression-of-concern at publication time; a live check on 2026-06-30 found it NOT currently flagged).

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only (not a recommendation to use, and NOT dosing guidance). Because the strongest molecular effect is HIF-1 stabilization, watch markers a HIF agonist could plausibly move: a periodic CBC (haematocrit/haemoglobin/RBC — HIF drives erythropoiesis) and resting blood pressure / heart rate. Track CNS over-stimulation signs that dominate user reports: new or worsening irritability, anxiety, restlessness, insomnia, or headache — these are the practical 'too much' signals and are reversible on stopping (short parent half-life). Because data are dominated by one lab and human safety is thin, baseline-and-periodic liver and kidney panels are a reasonable precaution for anyone taking it chronically, and anyone with hypertension, a seizure history, pregnancy/breastfeeding, or a personal/strong family cancer history should treat the HIF mechanism as an unquantified theoretical risk and seek clinician input. Watch for additive over-stimulation when combined with other stimulants/nootropics (community stacks frequently pair it with modafinil/phenylpiracetam). Stop and seek care for allergic reaction, chest symptoms, or marked blood-pressure rise."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE: the slick mechanism (HIF-1 master switch, BDNF/NGF up, CPG prodrug, alpha7 cholinergic) is almost all rodent + test-tube, much of it from a single research lineage (Zakusov Institute / Gudasheva-Ostrovskaya-Seredenin), which is a conflict-of-narrative risk — independent replication is sparse. RETRACTION FLAG: the 2021 Heliyon paper (PMID 33644478) tying Noopept to microglial BDNF/pro-BDNF is RETRACTED (confirmed live across PubMed, Crossref/Retraction Watch, OpenAlex) — do not cite it; the BDNF/NGF claim should rest on the rat-hippocampus mRNA study (PMID 19240853) only. The 'raises BDNF in humans' line you see in forums is NOT established. HIF-1 stabilization sounds great but is a double-edged pathway in principle (it's involved in tumor angiogenesis); the developers report Noopept does not stimulate proliferation in vitro (PMID 30788746), but long-term human safety data are absent. Human trials are small, short, open-label, and from one region — high bias risk. No legitimate dosing guidance here. Forum hype massively overstates the strength of evidence; the honest framing is 'promising mechanism, largely unproven in humans.'
