Library / GLP-1 / incretin / metabolic

Maridebart cafraglutide

A once-monthly injectable that pairs GLP-1 "go" with GIP "block" on a single antibody scaffold — betting that antagonizing GIP (not activating it, like tirzepatide) plus a long antibody half-life delivers deep, durable weight loss with monthly dosing. Phase 2 delivered ~12-16% loss over a year; Phase 3 is the real test.

Limited human data 9 citations
Class
Bispecific peptide-antibody conjugate (antibody-drug-conjugate format): a fully human anti-GIPR monoclonal antibody (GIP receptor ANTAGONIST) covalently conjugated to two GLP-1 receptor agonist peptides via amino-acid linkers
Primary target
Dual: (1) GIP receptor (GIPR) — antagonized by the antibody backbone; (2) GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) — agonized by the two conjugated GLP-1 analogue peptides
Human status
PIPELINE / Phase 3 (investigational; NOT approved anywhere as of mid-2026). Human data to date: a completed Phase 1 SAD/MAD in obesity (NCT04478708, n=110, published Nat Metab 2024, PMID 38316982) and a completed Phase 2 dose-ranging trial (NCT05669599, n=592, published NEJM 2025, PMID 40549887). Phase 2 showed a mean ~12.3-16.2% weight loss at 52 weeks in obesity without diabetes and ~8.4-12.3% with type 2 diabetes (treatment-policy estimand). A broad Phase 3 MARITIME program is underway: obesity without T2DM (MARITIME-1, NCT06858839, n=3,853, active/not recruiting, 72-week primary), T2DM (MARITIME-2, NCT06858878, n=1,105, active/not recruiting), a ~12,800-participant cardiovascular outcomes trial (MARITIME-CV, NCT07037433, recruiting, primary completion ~2028), an HFpEF/HFmrEF morbidity-mortality trial (MARITIME-HF, NCT07037459, n=5,056, recruiting), an obstructive sleep apnea trial (MARITIME-OSA-2, NCT07226765, n=250, recruiting), and a GLP-1-to-MariTide switch trial (MARITIME-SWITCH, NCT07575399, n=300, recruiting). Sponsor: Amgen. Efficacy and safety at population/long-term scale remain to be confirmed. (All NCT records and enrollment/status figures verified on ClinicalTrials.gov.)

At a glance

  • Substantial weight loss: mean ~12.3% to 16.2% at 52 weeks in obesity without diabetes; ~8.4% to 12.3% in obesity with type 2 diabetes (Phase 2, treatment-policy estimand) vs ~2.5% / ~1.7% with placebo
  • HbA1c reduction of 1.2 to 1.6 percentage points in the diabetes cohort (vs +0.1 with placebo)
  • Once-monthly (every-4-week) subcutaneous dosing; every-8-week dosing also tested — a major convenience differentiator
  • Durable effect: Phase 1 weight loss maintained up to ~150 days after the last dose, reflecting antibody-length half-life
  • Gastrointestinal adverse events (nausea/vomiting) common — the main tolerability issue; less frequent with a lower starting dose plus dose escalation
  • No unexpected safety signals in Phase 2; a broad Phase 3 program now tests CV outcomes, HFpEF/HFmrEF, OSA, T2DM, and GLP-1 switch

Mechanism, step by step

  1. The molecule: antibody blocks GIPR, tethered peptides fire GLP-1R Established

    MariTide is one engineered molecule with two jobs. Its backbone is a fully human antibody that binds the GIP receptor and BLOCKS it (antagonist). Conjugated onto that antibody are two copies of a GLP-1 analogue peptide that TURN ON the GLP-1 receptor (agonist). So a single injection simultaneously silences GIP signaling and activates GLP-1 signaling. Both activities were confirmed in cell-based receptor assays. PMID 38316982 (Veniant 2024, Nat Metab); PMID 41941715 (Wu 2026, J Med Chem discovery paper)

  2. GLP-1R agonism suppresses appetite and slows the gut Established

    The GLP-1 arm does what GLP-1 drugs do (semaglutide; the GLP-1 half of tirzepatide): it acts on GLP-1 receptors in the brain's appetite centers to reduce hunger and increase fullness, and on the gut to slow stomach emptying. This is the dominant, best-established driver of the weight loss and also the main source of nausea. PMID 36509857 (Hammoud & Drucker 2022, Nat Rev Endocrinol)

  3. The paradox: blocking GIP ADDS weight loss on top of GLP-1 Contested

    Here is the counterintuitive part. Tirzepatide ACTIVATES GIPR and loses weight; MariTide BLOCKS GIPR and also loses weight — both on top of GLP-1 agonism. In obese mice and monkeys, adding GIPR antagonism to a GLP-1 agonist produced greater weight loss than the GLP-1 agonist alone. How antagonism and agonism can both help remains genuinely unresolved ('the paradox'); the field has not settled the mechanism. PMID 40507574 (Douros 2025, J Clin Med, 'Premise of the Paradox'); PMID 38316982

  4. Leading hypothesis: chronic GIPR agonism desensitizes the receptor, so blocking it mimics that Evidence-human

    One prominent idea reconciling the paradox: sustained strong GIPR agonism (as with tirzepatide) may DESENSITIZE/downregulate the receptor, functionally resembling loss of GIP signaling — so agonism and antagonism could converge on reduced net GIPR tone. Human GIPR genetics support that reduced GIPR signaling can be metabolically favorable: variants impaired in BOTH Gs (cAMP) and beta-arrestin recruitment are associated with lower adiposity. This is a mechanistic hypothesis, not proven for MariTide. PMID 38871982 (Kizilkaya 2024, Nat Metab, GIPR variants + beta-arrestin); PMID 40507574

  5. GIP antagonism may reduce food intake and might blunt nausea (unproven) Speculative

    A second (not mutually exclusive) hypothesis: GIPR is present in brain regions relevant to appetite and nausea, so blocking central GIPR might independently reduce food intake and possibly DAMPEN the nausea that GLP-1 agonism causes, improving tolerability. In obese mice, a GIPR antagonist did reduce food intake (with more sustained appetite suppression than a GIPR agonist). But the nausea-dampening idea specifically has NOT been demonstrated in rodents or humans for MariTide and remains speculative. PMID 41287212 (Davies 2026, Diabetes Obes Metab — GIPR antagonist reduced food intake in obese mice); PMID 36509857 (review framing of GIP/appetite biology)

  6. Antibody format = ultra-long half-life = once-monthly dosing Evidence-human

    Because the active peptides ride on an IgG-type antibody, the whole conjugate clears very slowly (antibodies persist for weeks). In the Phase 1 multiple-ascending-dose cohorts, weight loss was maintained for up to ~150 days after the LAST dose. This long exposure is what enables every-4-week (and tested every-8-week) dosing — the key differentiator from weekly GLP-1/GIP drugs. PMID 38316982 (Phase 1 MAD, NCT04478708)

  7. Net effect: sustained energy deficit and fat loss Evidence-rodent

    Combined appetite suppression and reduced intake create a sustained calorie deficit, producing weight loss driven mainly by fat mass. In the rodent comparison study, GIPR-antagonist weight loss was matched by pair-fed controls with energy expenditure unchanged — meaning the loss came from eating less, not from a measurable rise in energy expenditure. As with all potent anorectic agents, some lean-mass loss is expected to accompany fat loss (not yet quantified in humans for MariTide). PMID 41287212 (energy expenditure unchanged; weight loss matched pair-fed controls)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Bispecific antibody-drug-conjugate architecture. Backbone: a fully human IgG-type monoclonal antibody that binds and ANTAGONIZES the human GIP receptor (GIPR). Payload: two GLP-1 analogue peptides conjugated to the antibody via amino-acid linkers, each acting as a GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) AGONIST. In cell-based systems the molecule shows confirmed GIPR antagonist activity and GLP-1R agonist activity. Net receptor pharmacology: GLP-1R ON + GIPR OFF — the opposite GIPR direction from tirzepatide (a GIPR/GLP-1R dual agonist).

Signalling cascade. GLP-1R agonism -> Gs/cAMP signaling in CNS appetite circuits (reduced hunger, increased satiety) and gut (delayed gastric emptying) -> reduced energy intake. GIPR antagonism -> blockade of GIP-driven signaling; mechanistically debated, hypothesized to reduce net GIPR tone (mimicking desensitization seen with chronic agonism) and possibly to act on central GIPR relevant to appetite (and, speculatively, nausea). Human GIPR genetics implicate BOTH Gs (cAMP) and beta-arrestin recruitment in GIPR function, with loss-of-function of both pathways associated with lower adiposity.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection.
Absorption / bioavailability
SC administration. Quantitative human absolute bioavailability is not detailed in the published Phase 1/2 reports; formal bioavailability/PK work is part of the broader development program but specific values are not established in the peer-reviewed record reviewed here.
Half-life
Ultra-long, on the order of an IgG antibody (weeks). Functional durability: in Phase 1 MAD, weight loss was maintained up to ~150 days after the last dose — the basis for once-monthly (and tested every-8-week) dosing.
Metabolism
As an antibody-peptide conjugate, expected to follow monoclonal-antibody-type catabolism (proteolytic degradation to peptides/amino acids) rather than CYP-mediated metabolism.
Excretion
Antibody-type clearance (proteolytic/reticuloendothelial catabolism); not renally cleared as intact drug.
Notes
The long half-life is the defining PK feature and the main clinical differentiator (monthly dosing) — but it also means the drug cannot be rapidly cleared if an adverse reaction occurs.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Clear dose-dependence. Phase 1: dose-dependent weight loss. Phase 2 (NCT05669599, 52 weeks, treatment-policy/ITT estimand): OBESITY cohort (n=465) received 140/280/420 mg every 4 weeks, plus a 420 mg every-8-week arm and dose-escalation arms; mean weight change ranged from -12.3% to -16.2% vs -2.5% with placebo. OBESITY+T2DM cohort (n=127, 140/280/420 mg every 4 weeks): -8.4% to -12.3% vs -1.7% placebo, with HbA1c -1.2 to -1.6 percentage points (vs +0.1 placebo). Weight loss was attenuated in the diabetes cohort (a pattern common to incretin obesity drugs). GI adverse events were dose-related and less frequent with a lower starting dose plus dose escalation. No dosing protocol is provided here — these figures describe trial arms, not clinical recommendations.

How it compares

  • vs Tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound; GIPR + GLP-1R DUAL AGONIST)
    same: Both combine GLP-1R agonism with GIPR targeting; both are potent injectable incretin-based anti-obesity agents with GI tolerability issues.
    different: Opposite GIPR direction — tirzepatide ACTIVATES GIPR, MariTide BLOCKS it, yet both aid weight loss ('the paradox'). Tirzepatide is weekly; MariTide is monthly (antibody half-life). Tirzepatide has completed Phase 3 + approval with ~20-22.5% weight loss in SURMOUNT; MariTide has only Phase 2 (~12-16%; placebo-adjusted ~14%) — do NOT infer superiority from cross-trial numbers.
  • vs Semaglutide (Wegovy/Ozempic; GLP-1R mono-agonist)
    same: Shared GLP-1R agonist activity and the same class GI side-effect profile.
    different: Semaglutide is single-target (GLP-1R only), weekly, approved with CV outcomes data (SELECT). MariTide adds GIPR antagonism and is monthly but investigational. Semaglutide gives ~15% weight loss in STEP; MariTide Phase 2 is broadly in that range but unproven at scale.
  • vs Retatrutide (GLP-1R/GIPR/GCGR TRIPLE agonist)
    same: Both are next-generation multi-target incretin agents pursuing deeper weight loss than mono-agonists.
    different: Retatrutide AGONIZES GIPR (and adds glucagon-receptor agonism thought to raise energy expenditure); MariTide ANTAGONIZES GIPR and has no glucagon arm. Retatrutide is weekly and investigational.
  • vs AT-7687 + GLP-1 (investigational GIPR-antagonist PEPTIDE + GLP-1 agonist)
    same: Both pursue the GIPR-antagonism + GLP-1-agonism combination; AT-7687 monkey data corroborate that this combination enhances weight loss (PMID 39128651).
    different: AT-7687 is a small PEPTIDE GIPR antagonist (short half-life, more frequent dosing) co-administered with a GLP-1 agonist; MariTide fuses antagonist + agonist into ONE long-acting antibody conjugate enabling monthly dosing.

History & legal status

Developed by Amgen as AMG 133; international nonproprietary name maridebart cafraglutide, brand name MariTide. Preclinical characterization and the Phase 1 study were published in Nature Metabolism (Feb 2024, PMID 38316982); the medicinal-chemistry discovery story was published later in J Med Chem (PMID 41941715). The Phase 2 dose-ranging trial (NCT05669599) read out and was published in NEJM (Sep 2025, PMID 40549887), accompanied by a published letter/reply exchange (correspondence PMID 41337722 verified; the paired letter and author reply were indexed in PubMed but were not independently cross-confirmable at the time of this review and are therefore not cited individually). As of mid-2026 the drug is INVESTIGATIONAL and NOT approved in any jurisdiction. A broad Phase 3 MARITIME program is running: obesity without T2DM (MARITIME-1, NCT06858839), T2DM (MARITIME-2, NCT06858878), a ~12,800-patient CV outcomes trial (MARITIME-CV, NCT07037433), HFpEF/HFmrEF outcomes (MARITIME-HF, NCT07037459), obstructive sleep apnea (MARITIME-OSA-2, NCT07226765), and a GLP-1-switch study (MARITIME-SWITCH, NCT07575399).

Key unknowns / open questions

  • The central mechanistic paradox: WHY does GIPR antagonism (like GIPR agonism) add weight loss on top of GLP-1? Competing hypotheses (receptor desensitization; central appetite effects) remain unresolved, and the nausea-dampening idea is speculative.
  • Head-to-head efficacy vs tirzepatide and semaglutide — no direct comparative trials; cross-trial numbers are not reliable.
  • Long-term (multi-year) efficacy, weight maintenance, and weight regain after discontinuation.
  • Body composition: magnitude of lean-mass and bone loss in humans (rodent data suggest deficit-driven loss; human DEXA quantification pending).
  • Cardiovascular, heart-failure (HFpEF/HFmrEF), and renal outcomes — being tested in large Phase 3 outcome trials, results not yet available (primary completions ~2027-2028).
  • Safety of the ultra-long half-life if a serious adverse reaction occurs (non-reversibility for weeks).
  • Efficacy/safety in older, frail, or specific populations, and in adolescents.
  • Immunogenicity over long-term dosing of an antibody conjugate.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

General harm-reduction monitoring themes drawn from the GLP-1/incretin class and MariTide trial data — NOT a treatment protocol, and there is no approved product: (1) GI tolerability — watch for nausea, vomiting, and dehydration, especially early and after dose changes; the class also carries pancreatitis and gallbladder-disease considerations. (2) Glycemia — HbA1c/glucose, and hypoglycemia risk if combined with insulin or sulfonylureas. (3) Body composition and function — attention to muscle/lean mass, protein intake, and resistance exercise given the drug's anorectic potency. (4) Nutritional adequacy during rapid weight loss. (5) Given the weeks-long half-life, awareness that adverse effects cannot be rapidly reversed by stopping the drug. (6) Standard incretin-class cautions (e.g., personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2, and pregnancy) apply pending any future label. Any real-world use should occur within a clinical trial or, if/when approved, under a qualified clinician — this synthesis is not medical advice and provides no dosing guidance.

Caveats

Read MariTide as PROMISE, not proof. (1) The longest human efficacy data are 52-week Phase 2 in ~592 people; head-to-head superiority vs semaglutide/tirzepatide has NOT been demonstrated, and the ~16% top figure (placebo-adjusted ~14%) does not by itself beat tirzepatide's ~20% in its own trials — cross-trial comparisons are unreliable. (2) The core GIP-antagonism mechanism is genuinely UNRESOLVED (the 'paradox' — agonism and antagonism both work); the reconciling ideas (receptor desensitization; central appetite/nausea effects) are hypothesis- or rodent-level, and the specific claim that GIP antagonism blunts nausea is speculative. (3) GI adverse events are common; the Phase 2 design used a lower starting dose plus dose escalation to manage them. (4) Once-monthly antibody kinetics cut both ways: if a serious adverse reaction occurs, the drug cannot be quickly withdrawn (weeks-long half-life). (5) Lean-mass loss, bone effects, long-term CV/renal outcomes, weight regain on discontinuation, and effects in older/frail people are not yet characterized in humans. (6) This is a monograph on published trial evidence, not medical advice; there is no approved product and no established human dosing.
