Methylstenbolone

Main target: Androgen receptor. Human evidence is still limited.

EvidenceLimited human data
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A grey-market 17a-methylated oral designer steroid sold in prohormone products for hard, "dry" mass gains — no aromatization, no water bloat, and a documented capacity to poison the liver.

Limited human data 11 citations
Class
17alpha-methylated designer oral anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS); a 2alpha,17alpha-dimethyl, 5alpha-reduced (DHT-type) androstane
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR, gene NR3C4) — a direct, non-aromatizing AR agonist
Human status
Never approved as a drug and never studied in a clinical efficacy trial in humans (0 registered trials on ClinicalTrials.gov). Human data on methylstenbolone consist entirely of (1) controlled oral-dose metabolism/excretion studies in a handful of volunteers done for anti-doping purposes (e.g., a single 10 mg dose of deuterium-labelled drug in one study), and (2) adverse-event case reports and analytical/toxicological investigations of contaminated 'nutritional supplements.' It emerged around 2012-2013 as a grey-market prohormone ingredient (e.g., in 'Super DMZ Rx 2.0') and is a documented cause of cholestatic drug-induced liver injury in otherwise healthy young men. There are zero randomized data on its anabolic efficacy, potency ranking, or long-term safety. It is prohibited in sport by WADA (anabolic agent, class S1); in the U.S. the Designer Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2014 makes sale of such unlisted designer AAS as 'dietary supplements' illegal.

At a glance

  • 'Dry,' hard muscle mass and strength gains without estrogenic water bloat — the marketed appeal; consistently claimed by users but NEVER quantified in any human efficacy trial
  • Non-aromatizing (predicted from structure): little/no estrogen conversion, so classic estrogen side effects (gyno, water retention) are expected to be minimal — but this does NOT make it safe
  • Notably hepatotoxic: cholestatic jaundice, dark urine, itching and elevated liver enzymes are documented in humans taking methylstenbolone-containing products
  • Sharp HDL-cholesterol suppression / dyslipidemia — an under-appreciated cardiovascular risk marker of AAS/designer-steroid use
  • Testosterone (HPG axis) suppression with post-use low-testosterone symptoms; androgenic effects include acne, hair loss, and virilization (documented in an accidentally-exposed child)
  • Frequently mislabeled or co-formulated: products sold as 'methylstenbolone' have been found to also contain methasterone (Superdrol) and dimethazine, compounding toxicity and making the true exposure unknown
  • No legitimate medical use; detectable in anti-doping urine testing for up to ~4 weeks via long-term metabolites

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps5 human / established 1 rodent / preclinical 2 contested / speculative

  1. 17alpha-methyl group survives first-pass liver metabolism Established

    A methyl group on carbon-17 slows the liver enzymes that would normally destroy an oral androgen on first pass. This is what makes the pill orally active instead of requiring injection — but the same feature is tied to liver toxicity of 17aa orals. Human excretion studies confirm intact methylstenbolone and its glucuronide appear in urine after an oral dose, proving oral bioavailability. PMID 23200734; PMID 31733090; PMID 27372877

  2. Binds and activates the androgen receptor Evidence-in vitro

    In circulation, methylstenbolone is expected to act like a synthetic DHT-type androgen: bind the androgen receptor in muscle and other tissues and switch it on. Its structure (a 5alpha-reduced, 2,17-dimethyl androstane) is the classic shape of a direct AR agonist. Important caveat: methylstenbolone itself has NOT been assayed in a published AR-binding study; its close analog methasterone was confirmed strongly androgenic in an AR-reporter (luciferase) assay by the lab that also detected methylstenbolone in the same supplements. PMID 36796473; PMID 23200734

  3. No aromatization to estrogen, no further 5alpha-reduction Speculative

    Because carbon-2 is methylated and the steroid is already 5alpha-reduced, aromatase is not expected to convert it to estrogen and 5alpha-reductase cannot act on it further. Mechanistically this predicts the 'dry' look users seek — little estrogen-driven water retention — and predicts that classic anti-estrogen 'on-cycle support' would do nothing for it. This is inferred from its structure/class, NOT from a direct aromatization assay of methylstenbolone. PMID 31733090; PMID 25684733

  4. Activated AR drives muscle protein synthesis and nitrogen retention Established

    AR-driven transcription of muscle protein synthesis and nitrogen retention is the established anabolic mechanism shared by all AAS as a class. For methylstenbolone SPECIFICALLY there are NO human muscle-biopsy, body-composition, or strength studies — its anabolic effect in people is extrapolated from the drug class and from user reports, not measured in any trial. The 'Established' tag here refers to the general class mechanism, not to methylstenbolone-specific human efficacy. PMID 25684733; PMID 27372877

  5. Metabolically 'stealthy' — slow, hydroxylation-based clearance Established

    Methylstenbolone is hard to break down: human-excretion and liver-microsome work show it is cleared mainly by slow ring hydroxylation and then glucuronidation, with unchanged parent drug detectable only briefly (~45 h) but hydroxylated/diol metabolites plus the glucuronide detectable for up to ~4 weeks (to day 29 in a labelled-drug study). This 'advertised metabolic stability' means the liver is exposed to the intact 17aa steroid for a prolonged time. PMID 24496964; PMID 23200734; PMID 31733090

  6. Prolonged 17aa exposure impairs bile flow (cholestasis) Established

    17alpha-alkylated oral steroids interfere with the liver's bile-export machinery — the bile-salt export pump BSEP/ABCB11 and the conjugate transporter MRP2 — so bile backs up inside the liver. Human liver biopsies from men taking a 2alpha,17alpha-dimethyl AAS supplement showed 'bland' intralobular cholestasis with diminished canalicular BSEP/ABCB11 marking. This is the classic 17aa injury pattern and matches the cholestatic labs seen in the methylstenbolone jaundice case. PMID 23750872; PMID 26425606; PMID 27372877; MoA:hepatotoxicity_17aa_orals

  7. Oxidative stress / reactive metabolites injure hepatocytes (hypothesis) Contested

    The leading (still hypothesis-level) explanation for why 17aa steroids damage the liver is that their metabolism generates reactive oxygen species and reactive intermediates that stress hepatocytes. In rat primary-hepatocyte culture, 17aa steroids (methyltestosterone, stanozolol, oxymetholone) were directly cytotoxic and depleted glutathione whereas non-alkylated steroids were not — supporting mechanism, but this is rodent in-vitro data and the exact pathway remains unproven, and was not tested with methylstenbolone itself. PMID 27372877; PMID 8527826

  8. Systemic androgen excess suppresses the HPG axis and worsens lipids Evidence-human

    Sustained AR activation signals the brain to shut down endogenous testosterone production (hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal suppression), and oral non-aromatizing androgens strongly lower HDL ('good') cholesterol. As a class these drive post-use low testosterone; human case reports of designer-steroid users (including methylstenbolone-containing products) document marked dyslipidemia, and severe virilization was reported in a child exposed to a methylstenbolone/methasterone-containing product. PMID 25684733; PMID 27612377; PMID 36796473

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Presumed direct agonist at the androgen receptor (AR / NR3C4). Structurally a 2alpha,17alpha-dimethyl-17beta-hydroxy-5alpha-androst-1-en-3-one — i.e., a 5alpha-reduced (DHT-type) androstane bearing a 17alpha-methyl group (oral protection) and a 2alpha-methyl group with a 1,2 double bond in the A-ring. No published direct AR-affinity (Ki/EC50) value exists for methylstenbolone itself; androgenicity is inferred from class and from the closely related methasterone, which was strongly androgenic in an AR-luciferase reporter assay, with activity persisting for days after exposure.

Signalling cascade. Ligand binds cytoplasmic AR -> receptor sheds heat-shock proteins, dimerizes, translocates to nucleus -> binds androgen-response elements -> transcription of genes driving myofibrillar protein synthesis, nitrogen retention, and satellite-cell activity; centrally, the same AR signaling suppresses GnRH/LH/FSH (HPG axis) and endogenous testosterone. The 2-methyl/1-ene A-ring and 5alpha configuration are predicted to block aromatization and further 5alpha-reduction, removing the estrogenic arm — inferred from structure, not directly assayed for this compound.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (tablet/capsule in 'prohormone' supplements). Orally active because of the 17alpha-methyl group.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally bioavailable (intact parent drug recovered in urine after oral dosing); no quantitative human bioavailability figure published. Highly lipophilic androstane.
Half-life
No formally determined plasma half-life in humans. Behavior is 'slow-clearing' relative to many orals; the practical readout is a multi-week urinary detection window driven by long-term metabolites, NOT a measured t1/2.
Metabolism
Hepatic. Phase I is dominated by slow ring hydroxylation (10 mono-hydroxylated metabolites identified with human liver microsomes; di-hydroxylated metabolites in the humanized-liver chimeric-mouse model), followed by extensive phase II glucuronidation. 'Metabolically stable' — this stealth is a marketed feature and the reason it evaded early doping tests. Reported human metabolites include 16-hydroxylated species and 2alpha,17alpha-dimethyl-5alpha-androst-1-ene-3,17-diols.
Excretion
Renal, mainly as glucuronic-acid conjugates. Unchanged methylstenbolone detectable only briefly (~45 h) after a dose, but hydroxylated and diol long-term metabolites plus the glucuronide remain detectable to ~day 29, giving a long anti-doping detection window.
Notes
Metabolism characterized across three complementary anti-doping models: human excretion (volunteers), human liver microsomes + uPA/SCID chimeric (humanized-liver) mouse, and deuterium-labeled/IRMS in-vivo work. All were done for doping-control detection, NOT clinical pharmacokinetics.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

No human dose-response or efficacy data exist. Anti-doping metabolism studies used single low oral doses (e.g., 10 mg of labelled drug) purely to map metabolites, not to assess anabolic effect. Grey-market products and forum use involve higher, undefined daily amounts, but there is NO validated relationship between dose and muscle gain, and NO threshold below which hepatotoxicity is known to be safe. Reported liver injury has occurred with label-directed use of a methylstenbolone-containing product (Super DMZ Rx 2.0) for ~30 days, indicating that clinically significant harm can occur within a single short course at 'as-directed' intake. This monograph intentionally provides no dosing guidance.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Methasterone (Superdrol)
    same: Both are 2alpha,17alpha-dimethyl-5alpha oral 17aa designer steroids, both non-aromatizing, both give 'dry' gains and both are hepatotoxic; frequently co-present in the same products.
    different: Different molecules: methylstenbolone has a 1,2 double bond in the A-ring (androst-1-en-3-one) whereas methasterone has a saturated A-ring (androstan-3-one). They are easily confused/mislabeled; 'methylstenbolone' products have tested positive for methasterone. Methasterone has longer notoriety, an AR-reporter androgenicity confirmation, and more case reports; do not treat them as interchangeable.
  • vs Stanozolol (Winstrol)
    same: Both oral 17aa, non-aromatizing, DHT-derived, marketed for a hard/dry look, and both hepatotoxic (cholestasis; stanozolol has fatal-DILI reports).
    different: Stanozolol is a pyrazole-fused molecule with far more clinical/PK history and (historically) therapeutic use; methylstenbolone has essentially no legitimate history and near-zero human efficacy data.
  • vs Trenbolone
    same: Both are potent non-aromatizing androgens sought for lean mass without estrogenic bloat.
    different: Trenbolone is an injectable 19-nor (not 17aa), so it lacks the hepatotoxic 17aa liability but adds strong central/neuroendocrine and progestogenic effects; trenbolone has documented tissue-selective anabolic action in rodents, whereas methylstenbolone's tissue selectivity is entirely unstudied.
  • vs Non-steroidal SARMs (e.g., ostarine-type)
    same: Both target the AR and are pursued for muscle gain with a 'cleaner profile' marketing angle.
    different: SARMs are non-steroidal, designed for tissue selectivity and reduced androgenic/hepatic burden, and some have entered formal clinical trials; methylstenbolone is a full steroidal 17aa androgen with documented human liver injury and no trials.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Methylstenbolone is a 'designer steroid' — a molecule created/marketed to sidestep steroid law while producing steroid effects. It appeared on the grey market around 2012-2013 (e.g., branded products such as 'Super DMZ Rx 2.0'), and was rapidly characterized by anti-doping labs (first human metabolism study 2013). It is prohibited in sport by WADA (anabolic agent, class S1). In the U.S., the Designer Anabolic Steroid Control Act of 2014 (DASCA) amended the Controlled Substances Act to sweep in unlisted designer AAS and empower scheduling of such compounds, making sale as a 'dietary supplement' illegal; the FDA has warned about AAS-containing 'supplements.' Despite this, analytical surveys (through 2023) keep finding it — often mislabeled or mixed with methasterone/dimethazine — in products still reaching consumers, underscoring persistent enforcement gaps.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Actual anabolic potency and efficacy in humans — no body-composition, strength, or dose-response trial has ever been done.
  • Human pharmacokinetics proper (plasma half-life, Cmax, volume of distribution) — only urinary metabolite/detection kinetics are known.
  • Its own androgen-receptor binding affinity and tissue selectivity — inferred from class/analog (methasterone), never directly measured for methylstenbolone.
  • Whether it truly does not aromatize — predicted from structure but not confirmed by a direct aromatase assay of methylstenbolone.
  • The precise molecular mechanism of its hepatotoxicity (the oxidative-stress hypothesis is unproven for this specific compound) and whether any 'liver support' (TUDCA/NAC) meaningfully changes risk (evidence limited).
  • Long-term and cardiovascular outcomes, and dose/duration thresholds for liver and lipid harm.
  • How much reported harm is due to methylstenbolone itself versus co-contaminant methasterone/dimethazine in mislabeled products.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Educational/harm-reduction only (this is NOT medical advice and NOT a protocol to use the drug). Because documented harms are hepatic, lipid, and endocrine: (1) Liver — jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, itching, right-upper-quadrant pain, nausea, and unusual fatigue are warning signs that warrant stopping and urgent medical review; clinicians track ALT/AST, GGT, alkaline phosphatase and total/direct bilirubin, and the typical injury is cholestatic (bilirubin/ALP-predominant). (2) Lipids — a fall in HDL and rising LDL is an early, sometimes asymptomatic signal (fasting lipid panel). (3) Endocrine — total/free testosterone and LH/FSH to detect HPG suppression, plus symptoms of low testosterone (libido, mood, energy) after use. (4) Product risk — assume mislabeling: a product sold as methylstenbolone may contain methasterone/dimethazine, compounding hepatotoxicity. The single most effective mitigation is avoidance; if injury markers appear, discontinuation is the intervention shown to reverse the cholestasis and dyslipidemia in reported cases. Anyone with jaundice or marked liver-enzyme elevation needs prompt clinical evaluation, not self-management. This monograph gives no dosing, cycling, stacking, or 'PCT' instructions.

Caveats

CRITICAL EVIDENCE GAP: methylstenbolone's muscle-building 'benefits' have never been measured in any human trial — no body-composition, strength, or dose-response study exists. Everything about efficacy is extrapolated from its drug class and from bodybuilder anecdote (forum/vendor reports), which are marketing-adjacent and unreliable. What IS documented in humans is harm: real cholestatic liver injury from products containing it. Two further hazards inflate real-world risk: (1) Mislabeling/co-contamination — chemical analyses repeatedly find that 'methylstenbolone' products also contain methasterone (Superdrol) and dimethazine, so a user cannot know what they are actually taking, and reported injuries may reflect the mixture rather than methylstenbolone alone. (2) 'Non-aromatizing / dry gains' is a plausible mechanistic property (inferred from structure, not directly assayed) but is often mis-sold as meaning 'safer' — it is not; removing estrogenic bloat does nothing about hepatotoxicity, HDL crash, or testosterone suppression, and may worsen lipids. Also note that several mechanistic claims (AR binding, no aromatization, ROS-driven liver injury) are extrapolated from the drug class or the close analog methasterone, NOT directly measured for methylstenbolone. This entry is educational and harm-reduction oriented: it deliberately gives NO dosing, cycling, stacking, or 'PCT' protocol. Distinct-from-Superdrol note: methylstenbolone (2a,17a-dimethyl, 1-ene A-ring) and methasterone/Superdrol (2a,17a-dimethyl, saturated A-ring) are separate molecules that are easily confused and are sometimes present together in the same product.
