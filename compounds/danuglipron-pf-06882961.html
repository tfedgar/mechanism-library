Danuglipron

A potent oral non-peptide GLP-1 receptor agonist that Pfizer discontinued for chronic weight management on 14 April 2025 after a single potential drug-induced liver-injury case — despite that case resolving on withdrawal and aggregate liver-enzyme rates being in-line with the class. A case study in how a compound can hit its efficacy marks yet still be abandoned on a narrow tolerability window plus a safety signal and regulatory feedback.

Human / approved 13 citations
Class
Oral small-molecule (non-peptide) GLP-1 receptor agonist — investigational, discontinued
Primary target
Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP-1R), a class B G-protein-coupled receptor
Human status
Investigational; DISCONTINUED for chronic weight management. Danuglipron was never approved or marketed anywhere. Pfizer developed an original twice-daily immediate-release formulation and later pivoted to a once-daily modified-release version to reduce pill burden and smooth exposure. Separately, in June 2023 Pfizer discontinued its sister oral GLP-1 candidate lotiglipron (PF-07081532) specifically for transaminase elevations — at that time transaminase elevations had NOT been observed in the danuglipron program. On 14 April 2025 Pfizer discontinued danuglipron for chronic weight management after a single asymptomatic participant in a dose-optimization study had a potential drug-induced liver injury (DILI) that resolved on withdrawal; Pfizer stated overall liver-enzyme-elevation frequency across the >1,400-participant database was in-line with approved class agents, and cited that case plus regulatory feedback. Human data therefore consist of completed phase 1 and phase 2 trials only; no phase 3, no cardiovascular outcomes, no long-term safety. Any material sold to consumers as 'danuglipron' would be a research-chemical / gray-market product with no approval, no manufacturing oversight, and an explicitly liver-flagged safety history.

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Non-peptide agonist binds the GLP-1R transmembrane pocket Established

    Unlike semaglutide or tirzepatide (large injectable peptides), danuglipron is a small drug-like molecule you can swallow. It slots into a pocket inside the receptor's membrane-spanning region rather than mimicking the whole GLP-1 peptide. A sensitized high-throughput screen found a 5-fluoropyrimidine hit that was optimized (adding a carboxylic-acid group) into danuglipron, giving nanomolar potency at the human receptor. Griffith DA et al., J Med Chem 2022, PMID 35647711, DOI 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.1c01856

  2. Activity is primate-specific — it needs a tryptophan-33 residue humans have and rodents lack Established

    A cryo-EM structure plus receptor mutagenesis showed danuglipron's binding pocket depends on a tryptophan at position 33 present in primate (and human) GLP-1R but not in the ordinary rodent receptor. That is why danuglipron raised insulin in primates but not in ordinary mice — a critical quirk that limited how much rodent safety modeling could predict human effects, and forced reliance on humanized-receptor models. Griffith DA et al., J Med Chem 2022, PMID 35647711, DOI 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.1c01856

  3. Receptor activation raises intracellular cAMP (Gs pathway) Established

    Once bound, danuglipron switches the receptor into its active shape, coupling to the Gs protein and boosting cyclic AMP inside the cell. This is the same downstream second-messenger step injectable GLP-1 drugs use; danuglipron was optimized to promote endogenous GLP-1R (G-protein/cAMP) signaling. Griffith DA et al., J Med Chem 2022, PMID 35647711, DOI 10.1021/acs.jmedchem.1c01856

  4. On pancreatic beta cells: glucose-dependent insulin release Evidence-human

    In the beta cell, the cAMP rise amplifies insulin secretion — but only when blood glucose is already elevated (glucose-dependent). That glucose-dependence is why GLP-1R agonism lowers high sugar with low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk. In humans with type 2 diabetes, danuglipron cut HbA1c and fasting glucose across phase 1/2 trials. Saxena AR et al., Nat Med 2021, PMID 34127852, DOI 10.1038/s41591-021-01391-w; Saxena AR et al., JAMA Netw Open 2023, PMID 37213102, DOI 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2023.14493

  5. In the brain and gut: reduced appetite and slowed gastric emptying → weight loss Evidence-human

    GLP-1R signaling in appetite-regulating brain circuits and the gut reduces hunger and slows how fast the stomach empties, so people eat less and feel full sooner. In the danuglipron obesity phase 2b, all doses produced statistically significant, dose-related weight loss (placebo-adjusted least-squares mean reductions reaching roughly the low-teens percent at the higher doses). Saxena AR et al. (danuglipron phase 2b obesity), Diabetes Obes Metab 2025, PMID 40539310, DOI 10.1111/dom.16534; Saxena AR et al., JAMA Netw Open 2023, PMID 37213102, DOI 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2023.14493

  6. The same gut/brain signaling drives dose-limiting nausea and vomiting Evidence-human

    The mechanism cuts both ways: slowed gastric emptying and central GLP-1R activation cause nausea, vomiting and diarrhea. With a twice-daily oral molecule these on-target GI effects were prominent and drove very high dropout — in the obesity phase 2b, only ~39% completed treatment and ~38% stopped for adverse events. Some medicinal-chemistry work argues the carboxylic-acid group also shaped the PK/dosing that fed these GI events. Saxena AR et al., Diabetes Obes Metab 2025, PMID 40539310, DOI 10.1111/dom.16534; Wang X et al., Bioorg Med Chem 2024, PMID 38795571, DOI 10.1016/j.bmc.2024.117761

  7. A separate, mechanism-unclear liver-injury case ended the weight-management program Contested

    The decisive stopping event was NOT the class-typical GI effect but a hepatic-safety signal. On 14 April 2025 Pfizer discontinued danuglipron for chronic weight management after a single asymptomatic participant in a dose-optimization study had a potential drug-induced liver injury (DILI) that resolved after the drug was stopped. Pfizer stated that across the >1,400-participant safety database the overall frequency of liver-enzyme elevations was in-line with approved drugs in the class; the decision reflected that single case combined with regulatory feedback (and the drug's already-narrow tolerability window). The liver signal is company-reported, its molecular cause was never established in the peer-reviewed literature, and it is not a recognized on-target GLP-1R effect — so it is best read as a possible compound/chemotype liability rather than a proven danuglipron hepatotoxicity mechanism. (Note: Pfizer's own sister compound lotiglipron was dropped in 2023 specifically for transaminase elevations, but at that time transaminase elevations had NOT been observed in the danuglipron program.) Company-reported (Pfizer press release, 2025-04-14). Metabolite/PK context: Jaiswal A et al., J Pharm Biomed Anal 2026, PMID 40865303, DOI 10.1016/j.jpba.2025.117128

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Non-peptide (small-molecule) agonist of GLP-1R, a class B GPCR. Nanomolar potency at the human receptor. Binds a transmembrane-domain pocket whose activity requires a primate-specific tryptophan-33 (Trp33) residue; confirmed by receptor mutagenesis and a cryo-EM structure. A carboxylic-acid moiety was engineered in during lead optimization to boost potency, improve off-target pharmacology and reduce metabolic clearance. Separately flagged in an in-silico docking study as having higher predicted binding affinity to cannabinoid receptors CB1/CB2 than the endocannabinoid ligands tested (docking only, no functional/human data).

Signalling cascade. GLP-1R activation → Gs coupling → adenylate cyclase → increased intracellular cAMP → in beta cells, glucose-dependent potentiation of insulin secretion; in CNS/gut, appetite suppression and slowed gastric emptying. Danuglipron was optimized to promote endogenous GLP-1R (G-protein/cAMP) signaling.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral. Originally immediate-release, twice-daily (BID), taken with food; later reformulated as once-daily modified-release (MR) to reduce pill burden and smooth exposure.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally bioavailable with dose-proportional systemic exposure in first-in-human studies (NCT03309241). Unlike oral semaglutide, it did not require strict fasting/water administration rules.
Half-life
Consistent with BID (then reformulated once-daily MR) dosing; exact terminal t½ not emphasized in the primary human reports reviewed here.
Metabolism
Hepatic biotransformation including oxetane-ring and oxidative pathways. A metabolite-profiling study characterized seven phase I/II metabolites (hydroxylation, O-dealkylation, oxetane-ring hydrolysis followed by acetylation/methylation) across human liver microsomes, rat liver microsomes and human S9 fractions and in-vivo rat matrices; in-vitro half-life was longer in human than rat microsomes. Exact intrinsic-clearance figures are as reported in that study.
Excretion
Renal clearance of unchanged drug is minimal; elimination is predominantly non-renal (metabolic/hepatobiliary), consistent with the renal-impairment PK study showing no clinically meaningful effect of renal function on danuglipron exposure.
Notes
Renal impairment (mild→severe) had no clinically meaningful effect on danuglipron PK — no renal dose adjustment anticipated (PMID 37840155). Note the eventual hepatic signal emerged only in a 2025 dose-optimization study, after these PK characterizations.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Clear dose-dependence for both efficacy and toxicity. TYPE 2 DIABETES (phase 2b, 16 wk, JAMA Netw Open 2023, PMID 37213102): across 2.5–120 mg BID, HbA1c fell progressively (up to roughly -1.2% placebo-adjusted at 120 mg BID) and FPG fell (up to roughly -33 mg/dL); weight loss reached statistical significance at the higher doses. PHASE 2a (12 wk, Diabetes Obes Metab 2023, PMID 37311722): dose-escalation-scheme study in T2D (n=123) and obesity (n=28); discontinuation ranged 27.3%–72.7% across danuglipron arms (vs ~17–19% placebo), mostly for GI adverse events (nausea 20–48%, vomiting 18–41%). OBESITY (phase 2b, 26/32 wk, Diabetes Obes Metab 2025, PMID 40539310): 40–200 mg BID; all danuglipron groups showed statistically significant placebo-adjusted weight reduction (least-squares mean decreases from -5.0% upward), with GI adverse events rising at higher doses. The efficacy-tolerability window was narrow: doses that drove meaningful weight loss also drove the nausea/vomiting that produced ~38% AE-related discontinuation (only ~39% completed). This poor tolerability ceiling, plus the later hepatic-safety case and regulatory feedback, is what led to the program's cancellation.

How it compares

  • vs Orforglipron (Lilly)
    same: Both oral non-peptide small-molecule GLP-1R agonists for T2D/obesity; both cause dose-related GI effects
    different: Orforglipron advanced through phase 3 obesity trials (ATTAIN program; ATTAIN-1 ~11% mean weight loss over 72 weeks, published in NEJM) and did NOT trigger danuglipron's program-ending liver decision — the key contrast suggesting the hepatic concern was danuglipron/chemotype-specific rather than an oral-small-molecule-class problem.
  • vs Lotiglipron (PF-07081532, Pfizer's own once-daily candidate)
    same: Same sponsor, same oral small-molecule GLP-1R class, developed in parallel
    different: Lotiglipron was discontinued in June 2023 specifically for elevated liver transaminases (>3× ULN in ~19–31% of phase 1 participants, linked to reduced hepatic drug transport/metabolism); at that time transaminase elevations had NOT been observed in the danuglipron program, so lotiglipron's liver problem was a distinct, better-characterized signal (PMID 42053447).
  • vs Oral semaglutide (Rybelsus)
    same: Both oral GLP-1R agonists lowering glucose and weight
    different: Oral semaglutide is a peptide requiring an absorption enhancer (SNAC) and strict fasting/water dosing rules with low bioavailability; danuglipron is a true small molecule with dose-proportional exposure and no strict fasting requirement — but it was discontinued while semaglutide is approved.
  • vs Injectable semaglutide / tirzepatide
    same: Shared GLP-1R glucose-lowering and weight-loss mechanism
    different: Injectables are peptides (weekly SC), approved, with cardiovascular-outcome data; danuglipron promised oral convenience but reached only phase 2, with a narrow tolerable-dose window, and was discontinued.

History & legal status

Discovered at Pfizer (originally PF-06882961) via a sensitized high-throughput screen and structure-guided optimization; described in J Med Chem 2022 (PMID 35647711). Advanced through phase 1 (from 2017) and multiple phase 2 studies in T2D and obesity. Pfizer developed a twice-daily immediate-release formulation and later a once-daily modified-release version to reduce pill burden and smooth exposure. In June 2023 Pfizer discontinued its separate oral GLP-1 candidate lotiglipron (PF-07081532) specifically for transaminase elevations, while continuing danuglipron — Pfizer stated transaminase elevations had NOT been observed in the danuglipron program at that time. On 14 April 2025 Pfizer discontinued danuglipron for chronic weight management after a single asymptomatic participant in a dose-optimization study had a potential drug-induced liver injury that resolved on withdrawal; Pfizer cited that case plus regulatory feedback, noting overall liver-enzyme-elevation frequency across the >1,400-participant database was in-line with approved class agents. Never submitted for approval; never marketed. Regulatory status: investigational, discontinued.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • The molecular mechanism of the danuglipron liver-injury case is unknown — no peer-reviewed hepatology characterization; reactive-metabolite vs idiosyncratic-immune vs other mechanisms are all speculative.
  • Whether any hepatic risk was dose-, exposure-, or formulation-dependent (BID IR vs once-daily MR) is not publicly resolved.
  • No phase 3, no long-term (>32 week), no cardiovascular-outcome, and no mortality data exist.
  • The precise nature of the regulatory feedback that contributed to the discontinuation was not publicly detailed.
  • Whether the narrow efficacy-tolerability window could ever have supported approvable dosing is untested at scale.
  • Long-term consequences of the docking-predicted CB1/CB2 cross-reactivity are entirely unknown (in-silico only, PMID 40838124).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

HARM-REDUCTION / MONITORING ONLY (this is a discontinued drug with no legitimate supply — the safest action is not to use gray-market 'danuglipron'). If exposure occurs, the flagged organ is the LIVER: watch for signs of hepatotoxicity — right-upper-quadrant/abdominal pain, unusual fatigue, dark urine, pale stools, jaundice (yellow skin/eyes), nausea/vomiting that is worsening rather than settling — and seek medical evaluation with liver-function tests (ALT, AST, bilirubin) if any appear, because early liver injury can masquerade as ordinary GI upset. GI tolerability (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration) is the other major issue and can itself be dangerous if it causes volume depletion. General GLP-1-class monitoring points that a clinician would track: heart rate, symptoms of pancreatitis (severe persistent abdominal pain radiating to the back), gallbladder symptoms, and — if combined with insulin or sulfonylureas — hypoglycemia. This entry provides NO dosing, titration, or 'how to run it' guidance by design.

Caveats

Danuglipron is a discontinued investigational drug — this monograph is a mechanism/safety case study, not a usage guide, and there is no legitimate supply. Efficacy on glucose and weight was real and mechanism-consistent, but the compound had a narrow efficacy-tolerability window (very high GI-driven dropout, ~38% AE discontinuation and only ~39% completion in the obesity phase 2b) and its weight-management program was ended after a hepatic-safety signal. IMPORTANT ACCURACY POINTS: (1) The liver signal that ended the program was a single asymptomatic potential-DILI case that resolved on drug withdrawal; Pfizer reported that overall liver-enzyme-elevation frequency across the >1,400-participant safety database was in-line with approved class agents, and the discontinuation reflected that case combined with regulatory feedback. Do NOT overstate this as a proven, dose-defined hepatotoxicity. (2) The exact molecular mechanism of the potential DILI is NOT established in any peer-reviewed paper — do not present a specific liver-toxicity mechanism as fact. (3) The 2023 events concerned the sister compound lotiglipron (dropped for transaminase elevations); at that time danuglipron itself had NO observed transaminase signal, so the danuglipron twice-daily-to-once-daily pivot was a strategic/tolerability/dosing-burden decision, not a liver decision. The primate-specific Trp33 requirement means rodent data poorly predict human effects, a genuine translational limitation. Efficacy figures come from short (12–32 week) phase 2 studies; there are no long-term, cardiovascular-outcome, or head-to-head phase 3 data. The liver signal appears to be a compound/chemotype question rather than an inherent property of oral small-molecule GLP-1 agonism as a class — the competitor orforglipron advanced through phase 3 obesity trials (ATTAIN program). This entry describes what to monitor for harm-reduction; it contains NO dosing protocol.
