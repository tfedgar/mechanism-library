HU6

The engineered "safe DNP" — a liver-biased, controlled-release dinitrophenol prodrug meant to raise resting metabolism and burn fat and liver triglyceride, now in Phase 2 for MASH and obesity-related HFpEF. Efficacy signals are early and sponsor-run; the "safe" claim is a hypothesis under test, not settled fact.

Limited human data 10 citations
Class
Oral controlled-release mitochondrial uncoupler (2,4-DNP prodrug); developer term "controlled metabolic accelerator"
Primary target
Hepatocyte inner-mitochondrial-membrane proton gradient — intended mild, sustained protonophore uncoupling that raises resting metabolic rate (liver-biasing demonstrated for the CRMP precursor in animals; true degree of human liver-selectivity not yet confirmed)
Human status
PIPELINE / Phase 2. HU6 is an investigational compound developed by Rivus Pharmaceuticals — not approved anywhere for any indication and not legally available. Human data so far: completed Phase 1 studies including formulation-bridging PK (tablet vs capsule, NCT06486558 [n=43, completed] and NCT07170189 [n=59, completed]) and a radiolabeled [14C]-HU6 ADME/mass-balance study (NCT06325930 [n=8, healthy males, completed]); a completed 61-day Phase 2a in NAFLD/high-BMI (NCT04874233, n=80, completed) showing ~27-36% relative liver-fat reduction vs placebo with no serious adverse events; and the completed HuMAIN-HFpEF Phase 2a (NCT05284617, completed; 66 randomized per the published RCT, registry enrollment 67) showing ~2.9 kg fat-specific weight loss over 19 weeks but NO improvement in peak VO2 or functional endpoints. A 6-month Phase 2 in NASH/MASH (NCT05979779, n=219) is active/not-recruiting, and a 4-arm Phase 2a in MASH (NCT07491458, acronym AMPLIFY, n=180) began recruiting in Feb 2026. All trials are sponsored by Rivus Pharmaceuticals. Long-term (>6 month) safety, cardiovascular outcomes, liver histology (MASH resolution/fibrosis), and durability of effect remain unestablished. This is genuinely emerging — treat efficacy signals as promising but early, and the 'safe' claim as a hypothesis under test, not a settled fact.

At a glance

  • Reduces liver fat: relative reductions of -26.8% (150 mg), -35.6% (300 mg), and -33.0% (450 mg) over 61 days vs +5.4% on placebo (human Phase 2a NAFLD, PMID 37806314) — a clear effect above placebo
  • Fat-specific weight loss: between-group difference -2.86 kg vs placebo over 19 weeks, with loss of total and visceral fat but no significant muscle loss (human HFpEF trial, PMID 40072462)
  • Lowers plasma triglycerides and LDL-cholesterol (~20-30% reductions shown in nonhuman primates; human lipid data limited/supportive)
  • Improves markers of insulin resistance and normalizes liver transaminases — demonstrated in rodent/primate models, not established as a human efficacy endpoint
  • Raises resting metabolic rate / substrate oxidation — the intended pharmacodynamic effect; directly quantified mainly in animal work
  • Did NOT improve peak VO2, 6-minute walk distance, KCCQ quality-of-life score, hs-CRP, NT-proBNP, or diastolic function in the HFpEF trial despite weight loss — an important negative (PMID 40072462)
  • Dose-limiting tolerability effects in humans: flushing (~32%), diarrhea (~25%), and palpitations (~12%) — a thermogenic/vasomotor signature (PMID 37806314)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Oral prodrug, slow release of DNP with intended liver bias Evidence-rodent

    HU6 is swallowed and metabolized to 2,4-dinitrophenol (DNP), the actual uncoupler. It is designed as a 'controlled metabolic accelerator' so that active DNP appears slowly and is intended to stay biased toward the liver, avoiding the sharp systemic spikes that make raw DNP dangerous. The controlled-release concept was first proven with the precursor molecule CRMP in animals. Important honesty: the prodrug-to-DNP conversion is real, but the actual DEGREE of liver-selectivity in humans has not been directly measured and remains an open question (see key unknowns). PMID 25721504 (Perry 2015, Science — CRMP, rat); PMID 31578240 (Goedeke 2019 — functionally liver-targeted in primates); PMID 37806314 (Noureddin 2023 — human HU6 metabolized in liver to DNP)

  2. DNP acts as a protonophore, short-circuiting the mitochondrial proton gradient Established

    DNP is a lipophilic weak acid. It picks up a proton on one face of the inner mitochondrial membrane, diffuses across as the neutral molecule, and drops the proton in the matrix — carrying H+ back in without going through ATP synthase. This dissipates the electrochemical gradient the cell normally uses to make ATP. This is textbook, decades-old uncoupler biochemistry. PMID 35441048 (Fifty years of protonophore research, Acta Naturae 2022); PMID 33545391 (Goedeke/Shulman review 2021, Mol Metab)

  3. Oxidation is uncoupled from ATP production; energy leaves as heat Established

    Because protons re-enter without driving ATP synthase, the cell must keep running the electron transport chain (burning fuel) to try to rebuild the gradient. Nutrient oxidation is 'uncoupled' from ATP output, and the surplus energy is released as heat. Net effect: more substrate is burned for the same ATP work. PMID 33545391 (Shulman-lab review 2021, Mol Metab); PMID 41487961 (DNP poisoning respirometry 2025/2026, Toxicol Rep)

  4. Hepatic fat and carbon substrates are preferentially oxidized (animal mechanism; human outcome) Evidence-rodent

    In dysmetabolic monkeys, controlled-release DNP (CRMP) raised hepatic mitochondrial fat-oxidation rates by ~40% after a single dose and lowered liver and blood triglyceride. In humans, HU6 lowered MRI-measured liver fat — but the human trial measured the OUTCOME (liver-fat reduction), not the fat-oxidation MECHANISM. So: the increased-hepatic-fat-oxidation step is shown in primates/rodents; the resulting liver-fat drop is confirmed in humans. PMID 31578240 (Goedeke 2019, Sci Transl Med — nonhuman primate, +40% hepatic fat oxidation); PMID 37806314 (Noureddin 2023 — human liver-fat reduction, outcome not mechanism)

  5. Lower hepatic acetyl-CoA / DAG reduces gluconeogenesis and improves insulin sensitivity Evidence-rodent

    Increased fat oxidation lowers intracellular lipid intermediates (diacylglycerol) and hepatic acetyl-CoA. In mice this reduced DAG-driven PKC activity and restored insulin signaling; in primates it cut endogenous glucose production ~18% via reduced pyruvate carboxylase flux. This is the proposed route from uncoupling to better glucose control — shown in animals, not yet demonstrated as the operative human mechanism. PMID 28330852 (Abulizi 2017, FASEB J — mice, DAG/PKC); PMID 31578240 (Goedeke 2019 — primate glucose flux)

  6. Raised resting metabolic rate is intended to produce fat-specific weight loss Evidence-human

    By making the body burn more fuel, chronic dosing drove weight loss that in the human HFpEF trial was fat-specific — total and visceral fat fell while muscle mass was preserved. This muscle-sparing, expenditure-driven profile is the headline contrast versus appetite-suppressing weight-loss drugs. Note: the trial measured body-composition change; it did not directly quantify a sustained rise in resting metabolic rate as its endpoint. PMID 40072462 (Pandey 2025, JAMA Cardiol — HuMAIN-HFpEF; fat-specific weight loss, muscle preserved)

  7. Mild vs runaway uncoupling is the whole safety thesis — and the open risk Contested

    The entire 'safe DNP' argument rests on keeping uncoupling MILD and slow. Raw DNP causes runaway uncoupling: a self-amplifying feedback loop of heat, respiratory acidosis, hyperthermia and death, documented in poisoning cases where oxygen consumption and body temperature spiral out of control. HU6's controlled-release design aims to stay in the mild window — but whether that window holds across large populations, overdose, long duration, and drug interactions is exactly what remains unproven. PMID 41487961 (Runaway uncoupling in DNP poisoning 2025/2026, Toxicol Rep); PMID 37806314 (Noureddin 2023 — no serious AEs at studied HU6 doses)

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No discrete protein receptor. The active moiety, 2,4-dinitrophenol, is a small lipophilic weak acid (phenolic pKa ~4) that partitions into the inner mitochondrial membrane and functions as a protonophore/uncoupler — it binds and carries H+ across the lipid bilayer rather than engaging a defined binding pocket. HU6 itself is a prodrug engineered for controlled, intended-liver-biased release of DNP; the pharmacophore is the classic nitrophenol uncoupler, the same chemical class as the banned 1930s weight-loss drug and as the Yale CRMP precursor.

Signalling cascade. DNP protonates on one side of the inner membrane, diffuses across as the neutral species, deprotonates in the matrix, and returns as the anion — a catalytic proton shuttle. This collapses part of the proton-motive force, so ATP synthase makes less ATP per O2 consumed. Cells respond by increasing electron-transport-chain flux and substrate oxidation (fat, glucose-derived carbon) to defend ATP and membrane potential; the surplus energy exits as heat. Downstream in liver (shown mechanistically in rodents/primates): raised fat oxidation -> lower intrahepatic triglyceride, diacylglycerol and acetyl-CoA -> reduced gluconeogenic drive and restored insulin signaling. The insulin-signaling and glucose-flux steps are animal-demonstrated, not yet confirmed as the human mechanism.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral, once-daily (tablet and capsule formulations under development; one Phase 1 study also explored a twice-daily arm).
Absorption / bioavailability
Prodrug absorbed orally; designed for controlled/sustained conversion so active DNP does not spike. Phase 2a NAFLD dosing was under fasting conditions; a Phase 1 study assessed food effect on the tablet. Dedicated formulation-bridging PK studies (tablet vs capsule; NCT06486558, NCT07170189) and a radiolabeled [14C]-HU6 ADME study (NCT06325930) are completed, but full human PK parameters are not yet in the peer-reviewed literature.
Half-life
Not firmly established in the public literature for HU6; once-daily dosing implies a PK/PD profile supporting ~24-h coverage, and the AMPLIFY Phase 2a (NCT07491458) lists elimination half-life among its PK endpoints. DNP itself has a relatively long human elimination half-life (on the order of ~a day or more; a poisoning-case series estimated a functional respirometric half-life of ~4.9 days for the uncoupling effect), which historically contributed to accumulation/toxicity with repeated raw-DNP dosing — a key reason controlled release matters.
Metabolism
HU6 is metabolized to 2,4-dinitrophenol, the pharmacologically active uncoupler. Liver conversion is central to the intended targeting strategy. DNP is then subject to hepatic nitroreduction and conjugation. Note: the degree of true liver-selectivity in humans has not been directly quantified in published data.
Excretion
DNP and metabolites (amino-nitrophenol reduction products, conjugates) are excreted renally and in feces; the [14C]-HU6 mass-balance study (NCT06325930) was run specifically to characterize absorption, metabolism, and excretion in humans (target >=85% recovery of the 14C label).
Notes
Doses studied clinically: 150, 300, and 450 mg once daily (the Phase 2a NAFLD trial's lowest arm is reported as 150 mg in the published paper). The HFpEF trial titrated 150 -> 300 -> 450 mg within-subject based on tolerability. Exact human Cmax/AUC/half-life values await full publication of the Phase 1 ADME/formulation studies. NO dosing protocol or self-administration guidance is implied — HU6 is investigational.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

In the Phase 2a NAFLD trial (PMID 37806314), relative liver-fat reduction was -26.8% at 150 mg, -35.6% at 300 mg, and -33.0% at 450 mg over 61 days (vs +5.4% placebo) — a clear effect above placebo with an apparent plateau between 300 and 450 mg rather than a steep dose-linear increase. Tolerability effects (flushing, diarrhea, palpitations) were the practical dose-limiting factor. The HFpEF trial titrated from 150 mg up to 450 mg based on individual tolerability, reflecting a therapeutic-window (rather than maximize-dose) strategy consistent with the mild-uncoupling thesis. No dosing protocol or self-administration guidance is provided here — HU6 is investigational and there is no safe way to self-dose it or DNP.

How it compares

  • vs Raw 2,4-DNP (illicit fat-burner)
    same: Identical active pharmacophore and uncoupling mechanism; both raise metabolic rate and burn fat.
    different: HU6 is a controlled-release prodrug engineered to keep uncoupling MILD and (intendedly) liver-biased; raw DNP delivers uncontrolled systemic spikes with a lethally narrow margin and is a leading cause of fatal 'fat-burner' poisoning. The point of HU6 is to make the same mechanism survivable — a claim still under clinical test, not proven.
  • vs GLP-1 / GIP agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide)
    same: Both target obesity/metabolic disease and produce weight loss and liver-fat reduction.
    different: Incretins work via appetite/central and incretin signaling (reduced intake) and are associated with significant lean-mass loss alongside fat; HU6 works by raising energy expenditure and appears fat-specific with muscle preservation in early data. Incretins are approved with large cardiovascular/outcome trials; HU6 is Phase 2 with no outcome data and no head-to-head comparison.
  • vs Other mitochondrial uncouplers (BAM15 and imidazopyridine derivatives, OPC-163493)
    same: Same therapeutic thesis — mild uncoupling for metabolic disease/MASH.
    different: Most competitors are structurally novel uncouplers designed to avoid DNP's baggage entirely (e.g., OPC-163493, BAM15-derived imidazo[4,5-b]pyridines); HU6 instead re-uses DNP but controls its delivery. HU6 is among the most clinically advanced (Phase 2 human efficacy signals) of the class, though these comparators remain largely preclinical.

History & legal status

2,4-DNP was sold as a weight-loss drug in the early 1930s, then effectively withdrawn/banned in the US in 1938 after deaths, cataracts and severe toxicity; it has since re-emerged as an illicit 'fat-burner' with recurrent fatalities and is described by regulators and toxicologists as 'unfit for human consumption.' The rehabilitation of the mechanism came from Gerald Shulman's lab at Yale, which built a controlled-release oral DNP formulation (CRMP) and showed in rats (2015), lipodystrophic mice (2017) and dysmetabolic primates (2019) that mild, liver-targeted uncoupling could reverse steatosis, dyslipidemia and diabetes without the systemic toxicity of raw DNP. Rivus Pharmaceuticals developed HU6 as the clinical 'controlled metabolic accelerator' embodiment of this idea, advancing it through Phase 1 (SAD/MAD, [14C]-ADME, formulation-bridging PK) and into Phase 2a in NAFLD (2023) and obesity-related HFpEF (HuMAIN, 2024-2025), with ongoing/planned Phase 2 programs in MASH (2023-2026). As of 2026 HU6 is investigational, not approved in any jurisdiction, and has no marketing authorization.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does the liver-fat/weight benefit translate into MASH resolution and fibrosis improvement on liver histology? (No histology endpoint data reported yet.)
  • Long-term (>6 month) and real-world safety — including whether the mild-uncoupling window holds across overdose, drug interactions, CYP/metabolic variability, and comorbid/older populations.
  • Why weight loss did NOT improve exercise capacity in HFpEF — is chronic mild uncoupling energetically costly to heart/skeletal muscle, or was the trial simply too small/short?
  • Full human PK: definitive DNP half-life, accumulation risk with once-daily chronic dosing, and the TRUE degree of liver-selectivity in humans (a central assumption not yet directly measured).
  • Cardiovascular outcome and mortality data — none exist; the HFpEF signal (weight loss without functional benefit) tempers enthusiasm.
  • Durability: does the metabolic effect persist or adapt (compensatory efficiency/appetite) over time?
  • How HU6 compares head-to-head with incretins on fat-vs-lean composition, and whether combination has additive benefit — untested.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Framed for harm-reduction UNDERSTANDING only — HU6 is investigational and this is NOT a use guide, and there is no safe way to self-administer HU6 or DNP. In trials, the on-mechanism signals a clinician would watch are: body temperature and any heat-intolerance/sweating (the sentinel for excessive uncoupling); heart rate and palpitations/arrhythmia awareness (increased sympathetic/cardiac work); and hydration/GI status given diarrhea and flushing. Because the active moiety is a hepatic prodrug used in liver-disease populations, liver enzymes (ALT/AST) and liver-fat imaging are tracked for both efficacy and safety. Metabolic labs (lipids, glucose/HbA1c) capture the intended benefit, and body composition (fat vs lean mass) confirms whether loss is fat-specific. The overriding real-world message: any product sold to consumers as 'HU6' or DNP is unverified and potentially lethal — the danger signs of DNP toxicity (rising body temperature, rapid heart rate, profuse sweating, agitation) are a medical emergency, and there is no safe self-administration of this mechanism outside a controlled trial.

Caveats

HU6 is NOT the same as taking DNP, and this monograph is not a green light for either. Key honest caveats: (1) The safety premise is that controlled release keeps uncoupling MILD and liver-biased; that thesis has held at studied doses over weeks-to-months but is unproven at scale, over long durations, in overdose, or with drug interactions/metabolic variability — and the actual DEGREE of human liver-selectivity has not been directly measured. Raw DNP kills via a self-amplifying 'runaway uncoupling' loop and hyperthermia; the therapeutic margin is the whole story. (2) Efficacy is early-phase: liver-fat and weight-loss signals are real but short (<=6 months) and sponsor-run; there are no liver-histology (MASH resolution/fibrosis) or cardiovascular-outcome data yet. (3) The HFpEF trial is a cautionary data point: HU6 caused weight loss but did NOT improve exercise capacity (peak VO2), walk distance, quality of life, or diastolic function — weight loss did not translate to function, in a small, short study. (4) Several mechanistic steps (increased hepatic fat oxidation, lower acetyl-CoA/DAG, improved insulin signaling) are demonstrated in rodents/primates, not confirmed in humans; the human trials measured outcomes (liver fat, body composition), not these mechanisms. (5) Tolerability effects (flushing, palpitations, diarrhea, potential heart-rate/temperature elevation) are on-mechanism and could matter more in real-world, comorbid, or older populations. (6) There is NO legitimate way to obtain pharmaceutical HU6; any 'HU6' sold to consumers is unverified and unsafe. Do not confuse the controlled prodrug with illicit DNP fat-burners, which are a leading cause of dietary-supplement fatalities.
