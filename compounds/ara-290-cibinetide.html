ARA-290

A tiny 11-amino-acid scrap of erythropoietin (EPO) that keeps EPO's "heal and calm the tissue" job while throwing away its "make more red blood cells" job — a non-erythropoietic repair signal that mostly lives in rodent studies and small Phase 2 trials.

Limited human data 20 citations
Class
Non-erythropoietic erythropoietin-derived peptide (innate repair receptor / IRR agonist); also called pyroglutamate helix B surface peptide (pHBSP)
Primary target
The Innate Repair Receptor (IRR): a heteromeric receptor complex built from the erythropoietin receptor (EPOR) plus the β-common receptor (βcR / CD131)
Human status
INVESTIGATIONAL — never approved by any regulator; available only as a research peptide. Human evidence is limited to a handful of SMALL, SHORT (28-day) Phase 2 trials, mostly sponsored by the developer Araim Pharmaceuticals: sarcoidosis small-fiber neuropathy (NCT02039687, n=64, Phase 2b — positive on corneal nerve-fiber area and symptoms; PMID 28475703, 24136731, 23168581) and type 2 diabetes with neuropathy (n=~24, exploratory metabolic + neuropathy signal; PMID 25387363, NCT01933529). A diabetic macular edema Phase 2 trial was TERMINATED with only 9 enrolled (NCT06626971). There are NO Phase 3 trials, NO long-term safety/efficacy data, and NO mortality or hard-outcome data in humans. Short-term safety in these small studies looked clean, but that is not the same as established safety. Bottom line for an audience: real, plausible mechanism with some genuine human nerve-repair signal, but it sits firmly in 'early/experimental' — the vast majority of the impressive effects (wound healing, organ protection, stroke, bone, transplant) are rodent or test-tube only.

At a glance

  • Reduces neuropathic pain / allodynia without EPO's red-blood-cell (hematocrit-raising, clot-risk) effects — shown in rodents and small human trials
  • Increases corneal and intraepidermal nerve-fiber density (a marker of small-fiber nerve regeneration) in Phase 2 trials
  • Broad anti-inflammatory / 'tissue-protective' action by dampening innate immune cells (macrophages, microglia, astrocytes)
  • Anti-apoptotic / pro-survival signaling in stressed or injured tissue
  • Signals of improved metabolic control (HbA1c, lipids) in a small type 2 diabetes trial — exploratory only
  • Wide range of PRECLINICAL (rodent/in-vitro) protective effects: wound healing, islet transplant survival, colitis, kidney injury, stroke, bone density — none confirmed in humans

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Built from EPO's helix B, stripped of the blood-making part Established

    EPO (erythropoietin) is the hormone that tells your bone marrow to make red blood cells, but it ALSO protects and repairs injured tissue — two separate jobs handled by two different receptors. ARA-290 is an 11-amino-acid peptide copied from one surface (helix B) of the EPO molecule. That region carries the tissue-repair signal but not the red-blood-cell signal, so ARA-290 keeps the healing effect and drops the hematopoietic one. This is why it is called 'non-erythropoietic' — it doesn't raise your hematocrit the way EPO does. PMID 25728128 (Collino 2015, review); PMID 25387363

  2. It binds the Innate Repair Receptor (EPOR + βcR), not the red-cell receptor Established

    The EPO receptor exists in two forms. Two copies stuck together (the EPOR homodimer) drive red-blood-cell maturation. But EPOR can also pair with a different partner, the β-common receptor (CD131), to form a heteromeric complex called the Innate Repair Receptor (IRR). ARA-290 binds SELECTIVELY to this IRR heterocomplex and leaves the homodimer alone — that selectivity is the whole point of the molecule. PMID 29026145 (Nairz 2017); PMID 35008482; PMID 25728128

  3. The receptor only shows up where there's injury or inflammation Evidence-rodent

    Healthy resting tissue barely expresses the IRR. Injury, low oxygen, or inflammation rapidly switches it on. That means ARA-290 acts like a targeted signal — it mostly 'lights up' at sites that are already damaged, rather than acting everywhere in the body at once. This is part of why a peptide that's cleared from blood in roughly 2 minutes can still produce effects that last much longer. PMID 25728128 (Collino 2015, review)

  4. Activating the IRR calms inflammation and switches cells toward repair Evidence-rodent

    Once the IRR is engaged, it dials down the activity of innate immune cells (like macrophages and, in the nervous system, microglia and astrocytes), reduces pro-inflammatory signaling, and tilts cells away from death (apoptosis) and toward survival and repair. In a colitis model, cibinetide dampened innate immune cell function and improved the disease course. Think of it as flipping a switch from 'attack/inflame' to 'protect/heal'. PMID 29026145 (Nairz 2017); PMID 24529189

  5. In nerve-pain models it quiets spinal microglia — and the β-common receptor is required Evidence-rodent

    In rats with nerve injury, ARA-290 produced long-lasting relief of allodynia (pain from normally non-painful touch) that tracked with suppression of spinal cord microglia activation. Crucially, in mice genetically lacking the β-common receptor (CD131), ARA-290 lost its pain-relieving effect — direct evidence that the IRR is the route through which it works, not an off-target painkiller mechanism. PMID 24529189 (Swartjes 2014); PMID 23936499 (βcR knockout)

  6. A possible direct brake on the TRPV1 pain channel (secondary, less certain) Evidence-in vitro

    Beyond calming immune cells, one in-vitro study reported that ARA-290 can directly inhibit TRPV1 — the capsaicin ('chili-heat') ion channel on peripheral pain-sensing neurons — and blunted capsaicin-induced hypersensitivity. This hints at a second, more direct analgesic route on top of its anti-inflammatory action, but it rests on a single cell/behavior study and should be treated as a working hypothesis, not settled biology. PMID 26774587 (Zhang 2016)

  7. Net effect: nerve-fiber regrowth and symptom relief in small human trials Evidence-human

    In small Phase 2 trials in sarcoidosis-related small-fiber neuropathy and in type 2 diabetes, 28 days of ARA-290 improved neuropathic symptoms and, strikingly, increased corneal and skin nerve-fiber density — a surrogate marker suggesting actual nerve repair, not just pain masking. In the diabetes trial it also nudged HbA1c and lipids. These are encouraging but small, short, mostly company-sponsored studies — promising signal, not proof. PMID 24136731; PMID 28475703; PMID 25387363; PMID 23168581

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. ARA-290 (cibinetide; pyroglutamate helix-B surface peptide, pHBSP) is an 11-amino-acid linear peptide (sequence pyroGlu-E-Q-L-E-R-A-L-N-S-S; ChEMBL CHEMBL3545305; molecular formula C51H84N16O21; MW ~1257.3 Da) modelled on the aqueous-face B-helix of erythropoietin's tertiary structure. ChEMBL curates a single mechanism: 'Innate repair receptor agonist', AGONIST, direct_interaction=true (ChEMBL mechanism mec_id 9382; ref PMID 28475703). Reported target = the Innate Repair Receptor (IRR), a proposed heteromeric complex of the EPO receptor (EPOR) plus the beta-common receptor (CD131/CSF2RB, the shared subunit of the GM-CSF/IL-3/IL-5 receptor family). KEY CERTAINTY CAVEAT: it agonizes the tissue-protective IRR but does NOT bind/activate the (EPOR)2 homodimer that drives erythropoiesis — hence 'non-erythropoietic' (Established for the functional selectivity; the physical EPOR-CD131 heteromer itself is CONTESTED, see PMID 30127368 which found no direct extracellular association in vitro). No published equilibrium Kd/Ki affinity value for ARA-290 at EPOR or CD131 was returned by any tool this run — quantitative binding affinity appears not to be in the indexed literature (state as a gap, do not invent a number).

Signalling cascade. Across rodent/in-vitro models the proposed cascade: ARA-290 engages the EPOR-CD131 heteromer on injured/inflamed cells (the IRR is reported to be induced by injury/metabolic stress, not constitutive on normal tissue) -> activation of JAK2 -> branch 1: PI3K/Akt -> eNOS phosphorylation, raising NO/VEGF and promoting angiogenesis, anti-apoptosis and cytoprotection (wound-healing model, PMID 29223734, Evidence-rodent); branch 2: suppression of NF-kB (p65/RelA) signalling, shown CD131- and JAK2-dependent in LPS-activated macrophages, reducing pro-inflammatory cytokines/chemokines and iNOS/NOS2 (colitis, PMID 29026145, Evidence-rodent/in vitro); branch 3: inhibition of NLRP3 inflammasome activation in Schwann cells via reduced NF-kB phosphorylation and lower ROS (sciatic nerve injury, PMID 40216181, Evidence-rodent). In the CNS it suppresses spinal microglial activation and astrocyte responses (PMID 24529189, Evidence-rodent) and is neuroprotective via the beta-common receptor in stroke (siRNA-knockdown abolishes effect; PMID 38488446, Evidence-rodent). A related EPOR-CD131 agonist (pegmolesatide) signals through JAK2/STAT3 (PMID 41092772), consistent with a JAK2->STAT3 arm for the heteromer. Net pharmacodynamic 'molecular switch': a very short-lived ligand triggers durable reprogramming from a pro-inflammatory/tissue-damaging state to anti-inflammatory tissue repair (review PMID 25728128).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Parenteral only — subcutaneous self-injection in all human trials (e.g. 4 mg/day s.c. for 28 days, PMID 25387363, 24136731, 28475703, 32674280). It is a peptide and is not given orally; preclinical work also used i.p. and i.v. dosing (PMID 21873879).
Absorption / bioavailability
No quantitative human bioavailability/Cmax/Tmax value was returned by tools this run. Given subcutaneous peptide delivery, systemic absorption is assumed but absolute bioavailability is not characterised in the indexed literature (gap).
Half-life
Plasma half-life ~2 minutes (very short), explicitly stated in the Araim/Brines pharmacology review (PMID 25728128, Evidence-human/PK summary). This ultra-short exposure contrasted with long-lasting (days-to-weeks) pharmacodynamic effects is the defining PK/PD feature ('flipping the molecular switch').
Metabolism
As a small linear peptide it is expected to be cleared by ubiquitous peptidases/proteolysis rather than CYP450 metabolism; no specific metabolizing enzymes or metabolite identification were returned by tools this run (gap — describe as general peptidase-mediated proteolysis, certainty: inferred not measured).
Excretion
No specific renal/biliary excretion data for ARA-290 were returned this run (gap). Peptide fragments/amino acids would be expected to follow normal catabolic/renal handling — inferred, not measured.
Notes
Not a prodrug and not esterified. The N-terminal pyroglutamate (cyclized glutamate) is a deliberate structural feature that resists aminopeptidase degradation. No published evidence of CNS penetration as intact peptide was returned; CNS effects (microglia/astrocyte suppression, antidepressant-model signals) may be mediated peripherally or via induced IRR — mechanism of CNS action is unresolved. Immunogenicity: a phase-2 DME trial detected NO anti-cibinetide antibodies after a 12-week course (PMID 32674280, Evidence-human).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

HUMAN dose-response is only partially characterised, and only over a narrow 1-8 mg/day subcutaneous range in small phase-2 trials. (1) Type 2 diabetes + neuropathy: 4 mg/day s.c. x 28 days improved HbA1c and lipid profile and PainDetect neuropathic symptom scores vs placebo, with corneal nerve fiber density rising in those with the greatest baseline deficit (PMID 25387363, n in the tens, Evidence-human). (2) Sarcoidosis small-fiber neuropathy: 28-day daily s.c. dosing improved neuropathic/autonomic symptoms and increased corneal nerve fiber density and 6-minute walk distance (PMID 24136731, Evidence-human). The phase-2b dose-ranging trial (1, 4, 8 mg/day, n=64) showed a clear INVERTED-U / non-monotonic signal: the 4 mg arm gave the largest, statistically significant increase in corneal nerve fiber area (placebo-corrected +697 um2, 95% CI 159-1236, P=0.012) and the only significant rise in regenerating GAP-43+ epidermal fibers, while 1 mg and 8 mg were not significant — i.e. 4 mg/day appears near-optimal and higher is not better (PMID 28475703, Evidence-human). (3) Diabetic macular edema: 4 mg/day s.c. x 12 weeks (n=9) was safe but did NOT improve BCVA or central retinal thickness on primary endpoints, with only scattered exploratory improvements (PMID 32674280, Evidence-human). (4) Antidepressant human model: a single 2 mg dose modulated some emotional-processing measures but did not produce a clear antidepressant profile (PMID 26431906, Evidence-human). ANIMAL: rodents typically dosed ~30 ug/kg (e.g. five 30 ug/kg injections relieved tactile/cold allodynia for up to 15 weeks; effect abolished in beta-common-receptor knockouts, PMID 21873879; wound healing 30 ug/kg/day s.c., PMID 29223734). KEY GAP: no formal human exposure-response (PK/PD) modelling, no characterised maximum tolerated dose, and no data above 8 mg/day were returned this run.

How it compares

  • vs Erythropoietin (EPO / recombinant human epoetin)
    same: ARA-290 is engineered from EPO's helix-B region and reproduces EPO's tissue-protective, anti-inflammatory, anti-apoptotic, pro-repair effects via the same proposed EPOR-CD131 tissue-protective signalling
    different: ARA-290 does NOT bind the erythropoietic (EPOR)2 homodimer, so it does not raise hematocrit/platelets/blood pressure or carry EPO's thrombotic/cardiovascular risk; it is an 11-aa peptide (~1.3 kDa) with a ~2-min half-life vs EPO's ~30 kDa glycoprotein with hours-long half-life (PMID 25728128; PMID 23964738)
  • vs Pegmolesatide (and other EPOR-CD131-targeting tissue-protective agents)
    same: Targets the same EPOR-CD131 tissue-protective axis and signals via JAK2 (and STAT3) to confer cytoprotection without the goal of erythropoiesis-only stimulation
    different: Pegmolesatide is a pegylated EPO-mimetic peptide engineered for long action/anemia indications and explicitly drives EPOR-CD131-dependent JAK2/STAT3 (PMID 41092772); ARA-290 is deliberately non-erythropoietic and ultra-short-acting
  • vs Ketamine (as a comparator for neuropathic pain via the same receptor)
    same: In rodent neuropathic pain, both ketamine and ARA-290 require an intact beta-common-receptor/IRR to relieve allodynia — convergent on the IRR pathway (PMID 23936499)
    different: Ketamine is an NMDA-receptor antagonist producing profound antinociception but with psychomotor side effects; ARA-290 lacks NMDA activity and psychomotor effects and acts via anti-inflammatory tissue-protective signalling, not direct analgesia (PMID 23936499; PMID 21873879)

History & legal status

"Origin: developed by Anthony Cerami and Michael Brines (Araim Pharmaceuticals, Tarrytown/Ossining NY) as a rationally engineered, non-erythropoietic EPO derivative — the pyroglutamate helix-B surface peptide (pHBSP), designed to retain EPO's tissue-protective signalling while eliminating erythropoietic/thrombotic liabilities (reviews PMID 25728128, PMID 29392190). USAN 'cibinetide' assigned 2015 (-tide stem; ChEMBL CHEMBL3545305). Regulatory status: investigational only — ChEMBL lists max_phase 2 with NO first_approval and no black-box warning; it has never been approved by FDA or EMA for any indication. Clinical development reached phase-2/2b in sarcoidosis small-fiber neuropathy, type-2-diabetic neuropathy, and diabetic macular edema (e.g. trial NCT02039687 'ARA 290 on corneal nerve fiber density in sarcoidosis'; DME trial EudraCT 2015-001940-12 / ISRCTN16962255). It is not a scheduled/controlled substance; legally it is an unapproved investigational peptide. As such it is not lawful to market as a medicine, and it circulates in the gray 'research peptide' market for fitness/PED use without any approved-product quality control — a key harm-reduction point."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does the EPOR-CD131 'Innate Repair Receptor' heteromer physically exist? Biophysical work (PMID 30127368) found no direct extracellular association even with ARA-290 present, so the very identity of the molecular target is unresolved — the alternative is that ARA-290 acts through a CD131-dependent but structurally different mechanism.
  • No published binding affinity (Kd/Ki/EC50) for ARA-290 at EPOR or CD131 was found this run — the quantitative pharmacology of target engagement is essentially uncharacterised in the indexed literature.
  • How does a peptide with a ~2-minute plasma half-life produce pharmacodynamic effects lasting days to weeks? The 'molecular switch' downstream signalling that sustains the effect is described phenomenologically but not mechanistically pinned down (PMID 25728128).
  • Human efficacy is inconsistent across indications — positive on neuropathic/nerve-regrowth endpoints (sarcoidosis, T2D) but negative on diabetic macular edema (PMID 32674280); which patient populations and endpoints truly respond is unknown, and trials are small and largely industry-sponsored.
  • Long-term safety is unknown: all human data are <=12 weeks in tens of subjects. Chronic effects on CD131-shared myeloid/eosinophil (GM-CSF/IL-3/IL-5) signalling, bone metabolism, and immune surveillance are uncharacterised in humans.
  • CNS mechanism of action is unresolved — antidepressant/emotional-processing and microglial effects are reported, but whether intact ARA-290 crosses the blood-brain barrier or acts peripherally/via induced IRR is not established.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (NOT a dosing protocol). Because ARA-290 is an unapproved gray-market peptide, product identity/purity/sterility cannot be assumed — endotoxin and bacterial contamination from non-sterile reconstitution are realistic risks; watch for fever, injection-site infection, cellulitis, or abscess. Mechanistically it should NOT raise red cells, but anyone using it should still get a baseline and periodic CBC (hematocrit/hemoglobin, platelets) — a rise would signal either an off-target effect or that the product is mislabeled/adulterated (e.g. contains EPO). Monitor blood pressure given the EPO-class thrombotic/hypertensive concern that ARA-290 is supposed to avoid. Track glucose/HbA1c and lipids in diabetic users (trials reported metabolic changes). Watch injection sites for persistent reactions. Be alert to allergic/immune reactions (rash, wheeze) given CD131's role in eosinophil/myeloid signalling and theoretical immunogenicity. Any unexplained bruising, chest pain, shortness of breath, leg swelling, or neurological symptoms warrant stopping and seeking medical care. None of this substitutes for clinician supervision — there is no approved formulation, so quality and dose accuracy are unverifiable."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE: most claims circulating about ARA-290 come from rodent or in-vitro studies (colitis, islet transplant, stroke, kidney injury, bone density, wound healing, lupus, EAE/MS models) and have NEVER been tested in humans — do not present these as human benefits. The human trials that DO exist are small (n=22–64), short (28 days), and largely funded by the developer (Araim), so positive results carry sponsor and small-sample bias and need independent replication. Surrogate endpoints (corneal/skin nerve-fiber density) suggest repair but are not the same as proven long-term clinical benefit. The TRPV1-channel mechanism (step 6) rests on a single in-vitro paper. 'No hematocrit rise' is a designed advantage but long-term human safety is simply unknown — absence of signal in tiny short studies is not evidence of safety. Forum/peptide-community claims of broad anti-aging, fat-loss, or general 'tissue regeneration' benefits are NOT supported by human evidence. This is a teaching summary of mechanism and evidence quality, NOT medical advice or an endorsement of use; ARA-290 is unapproved and its long-term risks in humans have not been characterized. All certainty labels and PMIDs were grounded against PubMed and a curated mechanism source this session; retraction checks on the four human RCTs (PMID 23168581, 24136731, 25387363, 28475703) returned clean.
