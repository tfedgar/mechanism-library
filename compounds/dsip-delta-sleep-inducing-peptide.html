DSIP

Main target: Unknown / unconfirmed. Evidence is animal- and lab-only — no human trials yet.

EvidenceRodent / preclinical
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A 9-amino-acid brain peptide named for "delta sleep" — but 45 years on, nobody has found its gene or its receptor, and its own sleep effect is shaky. It behaves less like a sleeping pill and more like a stress/homeostasis buffer.

Rodent / preclinical 33 citations
Class
Endogenous neuropeptide (nonapeptide, sequence Trp-Ala-Gly-Gly-Asp-Ala-Ser-Gly-Glu); a putative neuromodulator / "homeostatic" peptide
Primary target
Unknown / unconfirmed
Human status
Minimal and old. The only human efficacy data are small double-blind IV-infusion studies from the early-to-mid 1980s by the original Basel group (n=6 healthy volunteers; n=6 and n=14 chronic insomniacs) reporting improved sleep efficiency and daytime alertness with no overt sedation (Evidence-human, but tiny, single-group, decades-old, never replicated by independent labs). A 2009 anaesthesia RCT (n=24) found DSIP paradoxically *reduced* EEG delta and lightened anaesthetic depth, contradicting the "delta-promoting" name. There are NO modern, adequately powered human sleep RCTs, no pharma development, and no regulatory approval. Subcutaneous self-injection by biohackers is entirely unstudied — human data used a controlled IV route. Treat all "it fixed my sleep/anxiety" forum reports as anecdote, not evidence.

At a glance

  • Subjective "sleep pressure" and modestly improved sleep efficiency / fewer awakenings in small 1980s human trials — without classic sedation (Evidence-human, weak/unreplicated)
  • Anti-stress / HPA-axis dampening: blunts CRF-driven corticosterone release at the pituitary and reduces stress-induced c-fos in hypothalamus/septum (Evidence-rodent)
  • Shifts neurotransmitter balance toward inhibition — raises GABA, lowers glutamate/aspartate — giving anticonvulsant effects under hyperbaric oxygen / chemoconvulsant seizure models (Evidence-rodent)
  • Antioxidant / mitochondrial protection: boosts endogenous antioxidant enzymes and preserves oxidative phosphorylation under hypoxia (Evidence-rodent / in vitro)
  • Possible neuroprotection: phosphorylated DSIP improved sleep architecture and spatial memory at simulated high altitude; intranasal DSIP aided motor recovery after experimental stroke (Evidence-rodent)
  • Paradoxical EEG effect: in anaesthetised humans it cut delta power and raised heart rate (reduced parasympathetic tone), opposite to a pure hypnotic (Evidence-human, single small RCT)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

6 steps1 rodent / preclinical 5 contested / speculative

  1. A tiny peptide that gets into the brain — but with no known "lock" Contested

    DSIP is a 9-amino-acid peptide first pulled from the blood of sleep-deprived rabbits in 1977 and named for boosting EEG "delta" (deep slow-wave) sleep. It crosses the blood-brain barrier. The honest headline: despite decades of work, no one has isolated a DSIP gene, a precursor protein, or a dedicated DSIP receptor — so we literally don't know the "lock" this "key" fits. That makes every downstream mechanism below a correlation/effect, not a confirmed receptor pathway. IMPORTANT: better-controlled trials found the sleep effect statistically weak and clinically insignificant — the positive reports are mostly older/uncontrolled. PMID 16539679 (Kovalzon & Strekalova, J Neurochem 2006, review — abstract only)

  2. Calms the stress (HPA) axis at the pituitary Contested

    One of DSIP's most reproducible actions isn't sleep at all — it's anti-stress. In rats, DSIP blunts the corticosterone (cortisol-equivalent) surge driven by CRF, the brain's master stress hormone, and it does so at the level of the pituitary (it blocks CRF's effect but not ACTH's). It also damps stress-triggered c-fos gene activation in stress-related brain regions. This is the leading candidate for why people report it feels "calming.". IMPORTANT: better-controlled trials found the sleep effect statistically weak and clinically insignificant — the positive reports are mostly older/uncontrolled. PMID 2995861 (Graf et al., Neuroendocrinology 1985); PMID 11766904 (c-fos, Neurosci Behav Physiol)

  3. Tips the brain's chemistry toward "brake" over "gas" Contested

    DSIP shifts the balance of neurotransmitters toward inhibition: it raises GABA (the brain's main calming/"brake" signal) and lowers the excitatory amino acids glutamate and aspartate. In rodent seizure models (e.g. under high-pressure oxygen, or chemoconvulsants) this inhibitory shift produces a measurable anticonvulsant, seizure-delaying effect. A quieter, more inhibited cortex is plausibly how it could ease the transition into sleep — but this is shown in rodents, not human brains. IMPORTANT: better-controlled trials found the sleep effect statistically weak and clinically insignificant — the positive reports are mostly older/uncontrolled. PMID 8829679 (Fiziol Zh Sechenova); PMID 14751449 (Stanojlović, Pharmacol Biochem Behav 2004)

  4. Part of the effect may run through opioid signalling Contested

    In a rat high-altitude study, phosphorylated DSIP improved sleep and rescued memory — and the mu-opioid receptor blocker naloxone reversed those benefits. That points to the body's own opioid system as one downstream mediator of DSIP's sleep/memory effects. It's a clue, not a full pathway, and it's from one rodent study, so treat it as a lead rather than settled. IMPORTANT: better-controlled trials found the sleep effect statistically weak and clinically insignificant — the positive reports are mostly older/uncontrolled. PMID 30107169 (Roy et al., Life Sci 2018 — abstract only)

  5. Protects cells from stress: antioxidant and mitochondrial support Evidence-rodent

    Beyond neurotransmitters, DSIP behaves like a cellular stress-buffer. In rat tissue it ramps up the body's own antioxidant enzymes (superoxide dismutase, catalase) and curbs lipid peroxidation, and in isolated brain mitochondria it preserves efficient ATP production under low-oxygen stress. This "protect the cell's power plants" theme is why Russian groups frame DSIP as a homeostatic/geroprotective agent rather than a hypnotic. PMID 21809625 (Adv Gerontol); PMID 12668217 (Khvatova et al., Peptides 2003)

  6. Net result in humans: gentle sleep normalisation — or maybe not even that Contested

    In small 1980s double-blind IV studies, DSIP made healthy volunteers feel "sleep pressure," raised total sleep time, and in chronic insomniacs improved sleep efficiency and next-day alertness — all without the grogginess of a classic sedative, suggesting it "normalises" rather than forces sleep. But the catch is huge: these are tiny, old, single-lab studies. Independent replication never followed, DSIP itself often fails to change sleep in animals (only certain synthetic analogues work), and a 2009 human RCT found it cut EEG delta — the opposite of its name. So the human sleep claim is real but fragile. IMPORTANT: better-controlled trials found the sleep effect statistically weak and clinically insignificant — the positive reports are mostly older/uncontrolled. PMID 6895513 & 7028502 (Schneider-Helmert 1981); PMID 3622582 (1987); contra: PMID 19142086 (Pomfrett, Eur J Anaesthesiol 2009, RCT)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. NO molecular target has ever been identified for DSIP. After ~48 years there is no cloned/sequenced DSIP receptor, no DSIP gene, no precursor protein, and no GPCR or ion-channel binding site with a measured Ki/EC50 for the native nonapeptide (Established that the target is unknown — Kovalzon & Strekalova 2006, PMID 16539679; Graf & Kastin reviews PMID 6145137, 3550726). DSIP sequence is Trp-Ala-Gly-Gly-Asp-Ala-Ser-Gly-Glu (MW ~849 Da), structurally unrelated to any known peptide family. Because no receptor exists to assay, ALL reported affinities/selectivities are absent — there are no published Ki, IC50, or EC50 values for a DSIP receptor. What the literature instead reports are downstream MODULATORY effects at micromolar-to-nanomolar tissue concentrations: (1) In vitro, P-DSIP (phosphorylated analog) at 2 nM and DSIP at 20-300 nM potentiated alpha-1-adrenergic enhancement of norepinephrine-stimulated pineal N-acetyltransferase, blocked by the alpha-1 antagonist prazosin (Evidence-in vitro/rodent, Graf & Schoenenberger 1987, PMID 3029331) — this is allosteric/modulatory, NOT direct receptor agonism. (2) An old clinical hypothesis framed DSIP as having 'potential agonistic activity on opiate receptors' to explain its anti-withdrawal effects (Dick 1983, PMID 6328354), but this was never confirmed by radioligand binding and is best labeled Contested/Speculative. There is no agonist/antagonist designation that survives because there is no defined receptor.

Signalling cascade. All proposed cascades are downstream/correlative, not receptor-anchored. (1) Excitatory/inhibitory amino-acid balance: DSIP shifts brain neurochemistry toward inhibition — raising GABA and homocarnosine while lowering glutamate and aspartate in rat cortex, proposed to underlie its anticonvulsant effect under hyperbaric oxygen (Evidence-rodent, PMID 9406227, 8829679). (2) NMDA-receptor coupling: microiontophoretic DSIP activates cortical/hippocampal/thalamic neurons and blunts glutamate-evoked firing; the non-competitive NMDA antagonist MK-801 (dizocilpine) reduces the number of DSIP-responsive neurons and prevents DSIP's suppression of stress-induced c-fos in the hypothalamic paraventricular nucleus — implicating NMDA-dependent signaling and new protein synthesis (cycloheximide-sensitive) as part of the anti-stress cascade (Evidence-rodent, PMID 15364118, 22094385, 12669586, 10955299). (3) Monoaminergic/adrenergic modulation: proposed modulation of adrenergic transmission and a dopaminergic route for DSIP-induced GH release (pimozide-blockable) (Evidence-rodent, PMID 3575154, 3550726). (4) Antioxidant/mitochondrial: DSIP induces endogenous antioxidant enzymes (superoxide dismutase, catalase, ceruloplasmin) and improves coupled mitochondrial respiration/oxidative phosphorylation under hypoxia (Evidence-rodent/in vitro, PMID 21809625, 12668217). The unifying problem: none of these connect to an identified DSIP receptor, so the proximal molecular trigger is unknown.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Research/clinical use has been parenteral — intravenous and subcutaneous in humans (typical investigational dose 25 nmol/kg IV), intracerebroventricular and intraperitoneal in animals; an intranasal route was tested in humans for a P300 EEG effect (Evidence-human, PMID 11763019). No oral form is viable (see absorption). It is a synthetic nonapeptide, not an ester or prodrug; a phosphoserine-7 analog (DSIP-P / P-DSIP) and amidated/substituted analogs exist as more stable/active derivatives (PMID 6548966, 3029331).
Absorption / bioavailability
Oral bioavailability is effectively negligible. In cultured human intestinal (Caco-2) monolayers DSIP applied apically was ~92% degraded within 2 h (only 8.2% remaining), with NO intact DSIP detectable on the basolateral side even when peptidases were inhibited — i.e. it does not cross the gut epithelium intact (Evidence-in vitro/human cells, Augustijns & Borchardt 1995, PMID 8689946). Conversely, DSIP does cross the blood-brain barrier: BBB penetration was demonstrated for the native peptide, a notable property for a small hydrophilic peptide and attributed to its solution conformation (Evidence-rodent/in vitro, PMID 6548966, 8312250; reviewed PMID 25805003).
Half-life
Very short. Proteolytic split-off of the N-terminal tryptophan by rat brain slices/homogenate gives a half-life of ~15 minutes in vitro (Evidence-in vitro, Schoenenberger 1984, PMID 6548966). Free plasma/CNS DSIP is degraded rapidly; a precise validated human plasma half-life is not established (Evidence-human gap).
Metabolism
Degraded primarily by aminopeptidases that cleave the N-terminal Trp. In rat brain membranes/synaptosomes the inactivating enzyme is a puromycin-sensitive aminopeptidase (bestatin- and puromycin-inhibitable), liberating tryptophan as the major product (Evidence-rodent, Nakamura 1993, PMID 8474631). In human intestinal cells, aminopeptidases (bestatin/amastatin-sensitive) and dipeptidyl-peptidase IV (diprotin-A-sensitive) plus peptidyl-dipeptidase A / ACE (captopril-sensitive) sequentially trim the peptide; the major metabolites are free Trp and the dipeptide Trp-Ala (Evidence-in vitro/human, PMID 8689946). Known fragment metabolites include residues 1-8, 2-9, 2-8, 1-4 and 5-9 (PMID 265572).
Excretion
Immunoreactive DSIP-like material is detectable in human urine, CSF and milk, implying renal elimination of peptide/fragments and free amino acids re-entering metabolic pools; quantitative renal clearance data are not established (Evidence-human descriptive, PMID 6548966).
Notes
Endogenous DSIP-like immunoreactivity in plasma/CSF/urine is largely bound to a larger carrier protein, which protects it from proteolysis and likely explains why endogenous material persists far longer than free synthetic peptide (Evidence-human/rodent, PMID 6548966). A phosphorylated form (DSIP-P, phospho-Ser7) co-occurs with DSIP in human CSF and is more potent in some assays. Because the molecule is rapidly cleaved and BBB-permeable, much modern work uses stabilized fusion constructs (e.g. Pichia-secreted DSIP fused to a BBB-crossing peptide) to improve delivery (Evidence-rodent, PMID 39444618).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Dose-response is unusual and poorly defined, and human dose-response is essentially unestablished. ANIMAL: DSIP shows a non-monotonic, U-shaped (parabolic) dose-AND-time response — both the dose and the timing of infusion have an optimum, so more is not more (Established for the animal data, PMID 6145137, 6548966). Specific anchors: anticonvulsant protection under hyperbaric oxygen in rats was optimal at 12 µg/100 g (~120 µg/kg) i.p., delaying seizure onset 2-2.5x (Evidence-rodent, PMID 9406227); stress/mitochondrial protection under hypoxia used ~120 µg/kg i.p. (PMID 12668217); geroprotective/antioxidant dosing was 10 µg/100 g (~100 µg/kg) s.c. (PMID 21809625); DSIP-induced GH release in rats was dose-related from a minimal effective 0.1 µg ICV up to 10 µg, and in pituitary cells peaked at ~10^-10 M then DECLINED at 10^-7-10^-5 M (a clear bell-shaped curve) (Evidence-rodent/in vitro, PMID 3575154). Notably, native DSIP itself often did NOT increase slow-wave sleep in rabbits, whereas certain analogs ([NMeAla2]DSIP, [Pro2]DSIP) raised slow-wave sleep by 10-15% — i.e. the parent peptide is a weak/unreliable hypnotic (Evidence-rodent, PMID 11525128, 16539679). HUMAN: the only consistently reported clinical dose is 25 nmol/kg IV (used in insomnia and withdrawal studies); at this dose the largest controlled insomnia trial found only weak effects (see evidence_base). There is NO validated human dose-response curve for any endpoint, and no dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Melatonin
    same: Both are endogenous, BBB-permeable sleep/circadian-associated molecules promoted as natural sleep aids; both implicated in circadian timing (DSIP modulates pineal N-acetyltransferase, the rate-limiting melatonin-synthesis enzyme).
    different: Melatonin has a fully defined mechanism — high-affinity GPCRs MT1/MT2 (Ki in the picomolar-nanomolar range), a known biosynthetic pathway, oral bioavailability, and large RCT evidence for circadian/sleep-onset effects. DSIP has NO identified receptor, negligible oral bioavailability, and weak/contested human efficacy. Melatonin is a hormone; DSIP is an orphan neuromodulator.
  • vs Orexin/hypocretin-targeting hypnotics (e.g. DORAs like suvorexant) and the orexin system
    same: Both concern endogenous neuropeptide control of sleep-wake state and slow-wave/delta EEG.
    different: Orexin peptides have cloned receptors (OX1R/OX2R), a known precursor gene (HCRT), defined pharmacology, and FDA-approved receptor antagonists with robust RCTs. DSIP has none of these — no gene, no precursor, no receptor, no approved drug. Orexin is wake-promoting (antagonism induces sleep); DSIP was claimed sleep-promoting but the native peptide is an unreliable hypnotic.
  • vs Endogenous opioid peptides (e.g. enkephalins/endorphins)
    same: DSIP's anti-withdrawal use rests on a hypothesized opiate-receptor agonism, paralleling endogenous opioids; both are small endogenous peptides studied for withdrawal/analgesia.
    different: Enkephalins/endorphins have well-characterized mu/delta/kappa opioid receptor binding with measured affinities and a known precursor (POMC/proenkephalin). DSIP's opioid action was never confirmed by binding studies, and DSIP is structurally unrelated to opioid peptides — the 'opioid agonist' label is Contested.
  • vs DSIP analogs (DSIP-P / phospho-Ser7, [NMeAla2]DSIP, [Pro2]DSIP, Deltaran, fusion constructs)
    same: Same core sequence/scaffold and same proposed activities (sleep, stress/antioxidant protection).
    different: Several analogs are MORE active or more stable than the native peptide — e.g. [NMeAla2]DSIP and [Pro2]DSIP raised slow-wave sleep where native DSIP did not, and BBB-crossing fusion peptides improve delivery — highlighting that the parent molecule is a weak, rapidly degraded agent and that most 'DSIP' efficacy signals may actually require engineered derivatives.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Discovered/isolated in 1977 by Guido Schoenenberger and Marcel Monnier (University of Basel) from the cerebral venous blood of rabbits induced to sleep by electrical thalamic stimulation — hence 'delta sleep-inducing peptide' (PMID 265572). Intensively studied through the 1980s (Graf & Kastin reviews, PMID 6145137, 3550726) for insomnia, pain, stress and drug-withdrawal indications, but enthusiasm faded as the link to sleep proved weak and no DSIP gene/receptor/precursor was ever found — by 2006 it was termed a 'still unresolved riddle' (PMID 16539679). REGULATORY STATUS: DSIP has NEVER been approved as a drug by the FDA, EMA, or any major regulator for any indication; there is no approved or licensed DSIP pharmaceutical product anywhere. It is not a scheduled/controlled substance. It is sold only as a 'research chemical'/'not for human use' peptide; in the US it is not an approved drug or a lawful dietary-supplement ingredient, and in sport it is widely treated as a prohibited/non-approved substance under WADA's S0 ('non-approved substances') catch-all. A DSIP-based product 'Deltaran' was developed in Russia. No pharmacopoeial monograph governs the consumer product, so identity and purity are unverified."}

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Does a DSIP receptor exist at all? After ~48 years no receptor, gene, or precursor protein has been identified — it is unknown whether DSIP acts via a specific receptor, a carrier/transport mechanism, or only as a degradation-product/modulator. This is the single biggest unknown.
  • Is the endogenous active species native DSIP, the phosphorylated DSIP-P, a carrier-bound complex, or a structurally related 'DSIP-like' peptide? Kovalzon & Strekalova hypothesized the real bioactive entity may be a distinct DSIP-like peptide, not DSIP itself (PMID 16539679).
  • Does DSIP genuinely promote sleep in humans? The native peptide is an unreliable hypnotic in animals and the best controlled human trial (Bes 1992) showed only weak effects — whether any clinically meaningful sleep benefit exists is unresolved.
  • What is the true human pharmacokinetic profile (validated plasma half-life, clearance, the protective role of the plasma carrier protein, fraction reaching CNS) at any given dose? Current PK is largely in vitro/animal.
  • Are the anti-withdrawal, anti-stress, anticonvulsant and antioxidant effects reproducible under controlled conditions, and through what molecular pathway? Nearly all positive human reports are uncontrolled open trials.
  • What are the long-term safety, immunogenicity, and neuroendocrine consequences (e.g. GH/LH/circadian effects) of repeated exogenous administration in humans? No long-term human safety data exist.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring only — no dosing guidance. Because there is no approved product and no validated human safety dataset, the priority is watching for product- and effect-related signals. SIGNS TO WATCH: at injection sites, redness, swelling, warmth, pain, or pus (contamination/infection from unregulated peptide); systemic fever, chills, or malaise after injection (possible endotoxin reaction/sepsis — urgent); rash, itching, swelling, wheeze or lightheadedness (allergic/anaphylactic reaction to peptide or impurities — urgent). Watch for excess daytime sedation, grogginess, or impaired alertness, especially if combined with alcohol, opioids, benzodiazepines, or other CNS depressants (additive sedation; do not drive/operate machinery). Caveats

THE HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE STORY. (1) No identified receptor, gene, or precursor — a 2006 J Neurochem review literally titled DSIP "a still unresolved riddle" calls the sleep hypothesis "extremely poorly documented and still weak" (PMID 16539679). Every mechanism here is inferred from downstream effects, not from a confirmed binding target. (2) All human efficacy data are small (n=6 to n=14), double-blind but single-lab, IV-infusion studies from 1981-1987 by the peptide's own discoverers (PMID 6895513, 7028502, 3622582). No independent replication; no modern powered RCT exists. (3) Direct contradiction: a 2009 RCT (n=24) found DSIP *reduced* EEG delta and lightened anaesthesia — opposite to its "delta sleep-inducing" name (PMID 19142086). (4) DSIP itself frequently fails to alter sleep in animals; in several rodent studies only certain synthetic analogues ([Pro2]/[NMeAla2]DSIP) or phosphorylated DSIP were active, raising the possibility the "real" active molecule is a DSIP-like peptide, not DSIP. (5) Most mechanistic work (anti-stress, antioxidant, anticonvulsant, geroprotective) is rodent/in-vitro, much from a single Russian research lineage (Deltaran product), and not independently confirmed in humans. (6) Biohacker subcutaneous dosing is completely unstudied — human evidence used controlled IV; nothing is known about SC bioavailability, long-term safety, purity of gray-market peptide, or interactions. (7) DSIP also influences LH release, gut hormones, and appears in milk — it is pleiotropic and poorly specific, not a clean "sleep drug." NOT medical advice; this is a mechanistic teaching summary of published research, not a recommendation to use an unapproved peptide.

A tiny peptide that gets into the brain — but with no known "lock". DSIP is a 9-amino-acid peptide first pulled from the blood of sleep-deprived rabbits in 1977 and named for boosting EEG "delta" (deep slow-wave) sleep. It crosses the blood-brain barrier. The honest headline: despite decades of work, no one has isolated a DSIP gene, a precursor protein, or a dedicated DSIP receptor — so we literally don't know the "lock" this "key" fits. That makes every downstream mechanism below a correlation/effect, not a confirmed receptor pathway. IMPORTANT: better-controlled trials found the sleep effect statistically weak and clinically insignificant — the positive reports are mostly older/uncontrolled. PMID 16539679 (Kovalzon & Strekalova, J Neurochem 2006, review — abstract only)
