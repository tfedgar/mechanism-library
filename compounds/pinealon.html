Pinealon

Main target: No conventional receptor. Evidence is animal- and lab-only — no human trials yet.

EvidenceRodent / preclinical
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A synthetic Khavinson tripeptide (Glu-Asp-Arg / EDR) marketed for neuroprotection and cognition; its "gene-regulator" story rests almost entirely on Russian in-vitro and rodent work from one research group, with essentially no independent human trial evidence.

Human status
Not an approved drug in the US, EU, or UK, and not evaluated by FDA/EMA. There are no registered clinical trials on ClinicalTrials.gov and no ChEMBL entry (both verified: a compound search returns nothing, and the sole ClinicalTrials.gov hit under 'amino acids' is an unrelated Texas A&M metabolism study, NCT05400733). Human data are limited to a handful of small, unblinded, mostly Russian-language studies of aging/polymorbid or occupational-stress populations, often testing Pinealon within a bundle of Khavinson peptides rather than in isolation — none are placebo-controlled cognition RCTs. In practice Pinealon is sold as a research chemical / grey-market injectable or intranasal 'peptide' to the biohacking, longevity, and PED-adjacent communities. It should be treated as an experimental/emerging compound: preclinically interesting, clinically unproven.

At a glance

  • Marketed for neuroprotection, cognition/memory and anti-aging (geroprotection) — claims driven mostly by preclinical work from one research network
  • Consistent preclinical signal: reduces reactive oxygen species and apoptosis (caspase-3) in stressed neural cells and increases hypoxia tolerance
  • Proposed 'epigenetic'/gene-regulatory action via direct DNA binding — biophysically plausible in vitro but unproven in humans
  • Possible serotonergic/neurotransmitter effects in rodent cortex (raised serotonin-synthesis machinery)
  • Human evidence is minimal, unblinded, small, and largely Russian-language; no placebo-controlled cognition RCT and no ClinicalTrials.gov registrations
  • Not an approved drug anywhere; sold as a research chemical / grey-market 'peptide,' so identity, purity and dose of consumer product are unverified

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps7 rodent / preclinical 1 contested / speculative

  1. Small enough to enter the cell and its nucleus Evidence-in vitro

    Pinealon is a 3-amino-acid peptide (Glu-Asp-Arg). Fluorescently-labeled Pinealon added to HeLa cells was seen in the cytoplasm, nucleus and nucleolus, meaning the peptide can physically reach DNA rather than acting only at the cell surface. How it crosses membranes is not established. PMID 22117547

  2. Binds DNA in a sequence-preferring way Evidence-in vitro

    In fluorescence-quenching and biophysical (NMR, viscometry, molecular dynamics) experiments the EDR peptide interacted with DNA, appearing to slot partly into the major groove and contact guanine bases (N7/O6); binding was favored at CNG/CAG-type sequences and helped by magnesium ions. This is the physical basis for the 'gene-regulator' claim, shown only in cell-free/in-vitro systems. PMID 22117547; PMID 30762356

  3. Proposed epigenetic switching of specific genes Speculative

    The developers argue that by binding promoter regions Pinealon can raise or lower transcription of genes relevant to neurons. In an Alzheimer mouse study, molecular docking placed EDR at promoter regions of CASP3, NES, GAP43, APOE, SOD2, PPARA and PPARG; a review by the same group also names GPX1 (an antioxidant enzyme). This is a computational/hypothesized mechanism, not demonstrated gene regulation in living human tissue. PMID 34071923; PMID 33396470

  4. Lowers reactive oxygen species and cell death in stressed cells Evidence-in vitro

    In cerebellar granule cells, neutrophils and PC12 cells under oxidative stress, Pinealon dose-dependently limited ROS accumulation and reduced necrotic cell death, with a shifted time-course of ERK1/2 activation and cell-cycle changes. The authors infer both a direct antioxidant action and a genome-level action. Cell-culture only. PMID 21978084

  5. Upregulates the cell's own antioxidant enzymes Evidence-rodent

    Rather than acting purely as a chemical antioxidant, Pinealon is proposed to stimulate synthesis of endogenous antioxidant enzymes and to limit NMDA-receptor-mediated excitotoxicity, which is offered as the reason it raised neuronal resistance to hypoxia in rodent models. Rodent/in-vitro; enzyme-induction shown indirectly. PMID 18546825; PMID 33396470

  6. Increases serotonin-synthesis machinery in cortical cells Evidence-rodent

    In aging rat cortical-cell cultures EDR raised serotonin expression, and docking suggested EDR is complementary to a CCTGCC motif in the tryptophan hydroxylase (TPH) gene, the rate-limiting serotonin-synthesis enzyme. This is a candidate route to mood/cognition effects, shown in rodent cells with computational support. PMID 24909721

  7. Reduces apoptosis and neuroinflammation in stressed rodent brain Evidence-rodent

    In aged rats under acute hypoxia/hypothermia, Pinealon shifted caspase-3 activity, neuro-mediator levels and cytokines (IL-6, TNF) toward reference values and was associated with better performance on learning/behavior tasks. Effects were modest and often weaker than the comparator Cortexin. Rodent stress models only. PMID 25051764; PMID 28509493; PMID 28976148

  8. Protects developing rodent brain from prenatal oxidative insult Evidence-rodent

    In a rat model of prenatal hyperhomocysteinemia (an oxidative/vascular insult), maternal Pinealon reduced markers of oxidative damage in offspring brain and improved offspring spatial learning, consistent with the antioxidant/antihypoxic theme. Single rodent study; relevance to adult human cognition is indirect. PMID 22567179

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. No classical receptor. Pinealon = Glu-Asp-Arg (EDR), a short acidic/basic peptide. Proposed action is (1) cell/nucleus penetration and direct, sequence-preferring binding to DNA — partly inserting into the major groove and contacting guanine N7/O6, with binding enhanced by Mg2+ screening of DNA phosphate charge (PMID 22117547; PMID 30762356) — and (2) possible binding to histone proteins/RNA (PMID 33396470). It is also described as a direct ROS scavenger and inducer of endogenous antioxidant enzymes.

Signalling cascade. Hypothesized flow: nuclear entry -> promoter/major-groove DNA binding -> altered transcription of SOD2, GPX1 (antioxidant), CASP3/p53 (apoptosis), PPARA/PPARG, calmodulin, and TPH (serotonin synthesis) -> modulated MAPK/ERK1/2 signaling, reduced apoptosis, preserved dendritic spines -> neuroprotection. Non-genomic ROS scavenging runs in parallel (PMID 33396470; PMID 21978084; PMID 24909721; PMID 34071923).

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Studied preclinically by injection (i.p./i.m.) and intranasally; consumer use is typically subcutaneous injection or intranasal. Oral bioavailability is expected to be poor for an unprotected tripeptide (peptidase degradation), though the Khavinson group claims short peptides survive to act — not rigorously demonstrated in humans.
Absorption / bioavailability
No validated human PK. No published Cmax/Tmax/AUC. Absorption and CNS delivery in humans are unquantified.
Half-life
Unknown in humans; short peptides of this size typically have plasma half-lives of minutes. The proposed durable 'epigenetic' effect is invoked to explain activity despite presumed short half-life — this remains unproven.
Metabolism
Expected rapid hydrolysis by plasma and tissue peptidases into constituent amino acids (Glu, Asp, Arg); no human ADME dataset exists.
Excretion
Not characterized in humans; presumed renal handling of amino-acid metabolites. No half-life data.
Notes
Absence of any human PK is itself a key limitation: dose-exposure-response cannot be defined, and no ChEMBL/ADMET record exists for the compound.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

In vitro, ROS suppression and reduction of cell death saturate at LOW concentrations, whereas cell-cycle modulation continues to increase at HIGHER concentrations — the authors read this as two distinct mechanisms (a saturable antioxidant effect plus a separate genome-level effect) (PMID 21978084). This is a cell-culture concentration-response, not a human dose-response. No human dose-finding, no exposure-response, and no ceiling/toxic dose have been established. Per harm-reduction policy, no human dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Epitalon (AEDG, pineal tetrapeptide, in this library)
    same: Same Khavinson bioregulator family; both claim nuclear penetration, DNA/CNG-motif binding, antioxidant and geroprotective effects; studied side by side in the same nuclear-penetration paper (PMID 22117547)
    different: Epitalon is pitched around telomerase/pineal-melatonin/anti-aging; Pinealon (EDR) is pitched around neuroprotection/cognition and NMDA-excitotoxicity/hypoxia tolerance. Epitalon has a larger (though still Khavinson-dominated) literature.
  • vs Cortexin (polypeptide brain extract, common comparator)
    same: Both marketed for neuroprotection in Russia; tested head-to-head in aged-rat hypoxia models
    different: Cortexin is a multi-peptide tissue extract, not a defined single tripeptide; in head-to-head rat work Cortexin often showed a stronger effect on free-radical processes and caspase-3 than Pinealon (PMID 28509493)
  • vs Semax / Selank (Russian regulatory peptides)
    same: Russian-developed neuroactive peptides used off-label as nootropics with limited Western RCT evidence
    different: Semax/Selank have more defined mechanisms (melanocortin/BDNF; GABAergic/anxiolytic) and more behavioral data; Pinealon's mechanism is a less-validated direct-DNA-binding hypothesis (PMID 24738258)
  • vs GHK-Cu (copper tripeptide)
    same: Both are short tripeptides linked to antioxidant/anti-aging and gene-modulation narratives
    different: GHK-Cu has a defined copper-carrier chemistry and broader independent literature; Pinealon's evidence is narrower and more single-network

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Pinealon is part of the 'Khavinson peptide' program begun by Prof. Vladimir Khavinson and colleagues at the St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology, which from the 1970s isolated peptide complexes from animal tissues (e.g., pineal Epithalamin) and later synthesized defined short peptides (Epitalon/AEDG, Pinealon/EDR, Vesugen, Cortagen, Vilon). In Russia these are marketed variously as dietary/parapharmaceutical 'cytamins/cytogens' or geroprotectors rather than as rigorously approved drugs. Pinealon has NO FDA/EMA approval, no US/EU marketing authorization as a medicine, no ClinicalTrials.gov registration, and no ChEMBL record (both verified in this audit). Western availability is via research-chemical/peptide vendors, placing it in an unregulated grey market.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Whether Pinealon actually alters gene expression in living human cells (vs. in-vitro binding + docking predictions) — the central mechanistic claim is unconfirmed in humans
  • Human pharmacokinetics: absorption, CNS penetration, half-life, metabolism — essentially no data
  • Whether any cognitive/neuroprotective benefit exists in a placebo-controlled human trial (none has been done)
  • Independent (non-Khavinson-network) replication of the key efficacy and mechanism findings
  • The meaning and generality of the human prooxidant + hematopoietic-suppression signal (PMID 26390612) — is it a real safety flag?
  • Long-term safety of chronically nudging a DNA-binding/epigenetic mechanism, including in dividing/progenitor cells
  • Real-world product identity, purity, dose and sterility of grey-market Pinealon

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This is harm-reduction information, not an endorsement or protocol, and it contains no doses, frequencies, or administration schedule. Because human data are thin and one human study flagged a prooxidant/hematopoietic signal, anyone using grey-market Pinealon should do so under clinician supervision, who may consider: (1) CBC with differential — given the reported drop in CD34+/hematopoietic markers, watch for unexplained cytopenias; (2) injection-site and systemic infection signs (redness, swelling, fever) — sterility of grey-market injectables is unverified; (3) general markers a clinician might follow for any unregulated peptide, e.g. basic metabolic panel and liver/renal function; (4) mood/serotonergic effects and interactions if combined with serotonergic medications (theoretical, from rodent TPH data); (5) product provenance — third-party identity/purity testing where possible, since the biggest realistic harm is a mislabeled or contaminated product rather than the peptide's known pharmacology. Stop and seek medical review for any new bruising/bleeding, persistent fever, or systemic symptoms. None of this substitutes for individual medical advice.

Caveats

Evidence quality is the headline caveat. Nearly all mechanistic and efficacy data originate from a single research network (St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology / Khavinson group and close collaborators), which has a clear intellectual and commercial stake in these peptides; independent replication is scarce. The signature 'binds DNA and epigenetically regulates genes' mechanism is supported by in-vitro binding assays and molecular docking (a computational prediction), not by demonstrated gene-expression changes in living humans — treat 'gene regulator' as a hypothesis, not an established fact. Human studies are small, unblinded, often non-English, and frequently confound Pinealon with other peptides. Critically, one human geroprophylaxis study (PMID 26390612) reported a PROOXIDANT chemiluminescence signal and reduced CD34+ hematopoietic progenitor markers — directly at odds with the 'antioxidant/safe' marketing, and a reason not to assume the antioxidant framing generalizes to people. Because it is a grey-market product, the actual identity, purity, dose, and sterility of what a consumer buys are unverified. This monograph explains proposed mechanisms and what to monitor; it is not medical advice and contains no dosing protocol.
