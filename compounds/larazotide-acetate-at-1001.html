Larazotide acetate

Main target: The zonulin signaling pathway at the intestinal… Backed by human data.

EvidenceHuman evidence
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

An 8-amino-acid "gut zipper-keeper" peptide that tries to hold the intestinal tight junctions shut — the most clinically advanced "leaky gut" drug ever made, and a cautionary tale: it sailed through mechanism studies and a positive Phase 2b, then flat-out failed its Phase 3 trial in celiac disease.

Strong human data 17 citations
Class
Synthetic octapeptide; tight-junction regulator / zonulin antagonist (oral, gut-restricted, non-absorbed)
Primary target
The zonulin signaling pathway at the intestinal epithelial tight junction — functionally a zonulin-receptor antagonist that prevents zonulin-triggered opening of the spaces between gut-lining cells (the paracellular pathway)
Human status
Most clinically advanced tight-junction / 'leaky gut' drug to date, but NOT approved for any indication. Tested almost exclusively in CELIAC DISEASE. Human arc: proof-of-concept (2007, PMID 17697209) → gluten-challenge RCTs (PMID 22825365, 23163616) → a positive Phase 2b in 342 patients on a gluten-free diet where ONLY the 0.5 mg dose beat placebo for symptoms (PMID 25683116) → Phase 3 (NCT03569007, 307 patients, 160 sites) which was TERMINATED in 2022 and did not lead to approval. A 2022 systematic review/meta-analysis of 4 RCTs (626 patients, PMID 34339872) found some symptom benefit during gluten challenge but NO significant effect on actual measured gut permeability (lactulose/mannitol). Bottom line for viewers: every claim about larazotide as a general 'leaky gut' or anti-inflammatory cure is preclinical (cells/rats/pigs); in the one disease with real human trials, it failed Phase 3.

At a glance

  • Reduces paracellular ('between-cell') intestinal permeability in cell and animal models — the core 'seal the leaky gut' effect
  • Lowers MLC-2 phosphorylation and stabilizes tight-junction protein architecture (claudins, ZO-1, actin)
  • In the positive Phase 2b celiac trial (342 patients on a gluten-free diet), the 0.5 mg dose modestly reduced GI symptoms vs placebo — but 1 mg and 2 mg did NOT (bell-shaped dose-response)
  • Meta-analysis (4 RCTs, 626 patients): some GI-symptom benefit during gluten challenge, but NO significant effect on the actual permeability marker (lactulose/mannitol ratio)
  • Well tolerated, safety comparable to placebo; most common side effects headache and UTI
  • Preclinical barrier protection in ischemia, acute pancreatitis, and acute liver failure models — hypothesis-generating only, no human data

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

7 steps5 human 2 rodent / preclinical

  1. The leak it's designed to plug Established (zonulin/gluten-permeability link); Evidence-human

    Your gut lining is a single layer of cells glued together by 'tight junctions' — protein seams that decide what slips between cells into the bloodstream. In celiac disease, gluten fragments trigger release of a signaling protein called zonulin, which pries those seams open ('leaky gut'). The open seams let more gluten through, feeding the immune attack. PMID 17697209; PMID 26770266; PMID 40579122

  2. Larazotide blocks the 'open up' signal Evidence-rodent / Evidence-in vitro (mechanism); proposed in human trials

    Larazotide is a tiny 8-amino-acid peptide that sits in the gut and acts as a zonulin antagonist — it blocks zonulin from delivering its 'open the junctions' message to the gut-lining cells. No open signal, less leak. Think of it as jamming the remote that unlocks the door rather than nailing the door shut. PMID 33881350; PMID 33397225; PMID 34791921

  3. It calms the molecular 'motor' that yanks junctions apart Evidence-in vitro (cell monolayers); Evidence-rodent

    Tight junctions are pulled open by a contractile motor: myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK) phosphorylates myosin light chain (MLC-2), tightening actin cables that tug the seams apart. Larazotide reduces MLC-2 phosphorylation (likely via the ROCK/MLCK pathway), releasing that tension so the junctions can stay closed. PMID 33881350; PMID 41153766

  4. It helps tight-junction proteins re-assemble in the right place Evidence-in vitro / Evidence-animal (porcine, rodent)

    Beyond just blocking opening, larazotide promotes redistribution and rearrangement of tight-junction proteins (like claudins and ZO-1) and the actin scaffold back into an organized, sealed arrangement — actively helping a damaged barrier rebuild, not just preventing new damage. PMID 33881350; PMID 33886649; PMID 41153766

  5. In injured gut, it speeds barrier repair Evidence-animal (porcine, rodent) — NOT shown in humans for these conditions

    In pig intestine damaged by loss of blood flow (ischemia), larazotide restored barrier strength (electrical resistance) and cut leakage of bacterial toxin (LPS), with better localization of the sealing protein claudin-4. Similar barrier-protective effects show up in rat models of pancreatitis and liver failure. This is why people speculate about uses beyond celiac. PMID 33886649; PMID 38441784; PMID 34791921

  6. The catch: a strange dose-response and self-sabotaging fragments Evidence-in vitro / Evidence-animal, consistent with Evidence-human dosing

    It doesn't get stronger with more drug — there's a 'sweet spot.' In pig studies only the middle dose (1 µM) worked; higher doses didn't. The reason: gut enzymes chop larazotide into fragments, and some fragments actually block its own benefit. This bell-shaped curve is exactly why the human celiac trials found the LOW 0.5 mg dose worked while higher 1–2 mg doses did not. PMID 33886649; PMID 25683116

  7. What it does NOT do Established (pharmacology); Evidence-human (permeability endpoint negative)

    Larazotide is not absorbed into the blood — it works locally in the gut, so it's not a systemic 'leaky gut' fix or a metabolic peptide. It does not replace a gluten-free diet, does not stop the underlying autoimmune reaction to gluten, and human trials never showed it reliably reduced measured gut permeability (the lactulose/mannitol test) — the symptom benefit, where seen, may run partly through other routes. PMID 34339872; PMID 22825365; PMID 25683116

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Larazotide acetate (AT-1001) is a synthetic octapeptide (sequence Gly-Gly-Val-Leu-Val-Gln-Pro-Gly, derived from the Vibrio cholerae zonula occludens toxin / zonulin region). It is best described as a tight-junction REGULATOR / functional ANTAGONIST of the zonulin pathway rather than a classic high-affinity receptor ligand. CERTAINTY/HONESTY NOTE: despite being routinely called a 'zonulin receptor antagonist,' the actual molecular binding partner of larazotide has never been definitively identified and no published Kd/Ki/IC50 against zonulin or a named receptor exists (Established that the target is unconfirmed). It does NOT have a canonical small-molecule affinity value. Functional potency is reported instead: in cell and tissue assays the effective concentration is in the LOW MICROMOLAR range with a characteristic bell-shaped (U-shaped) curve — ~1 µM is maximally active in injured porcine jejunum, while 0.1 µM and 10 µM are markedly less effective (PMID 33886649; Evidence-in vitro/ex vivo). It acts apically (lumen side) on the enterocyte brush border and is gut-restricted (Established).

Signalling cascade. Upstream biology (the pathway larazotide opposes): Zonulin was identified as pre-haptoglobin-2 (pre-HP2), the uncleaved precursor of haptoglobin-2 (PMID 19805376; Established). Zonulin carries an EGF-like motif; it activates proteinase-activated receptor 2 (PAR2), which transactivates the EGF receptor (EGFR). PAR2/EGFR co-activation triggers PLC/PKC-alpha signaling and Rho-GTPase-dependent actin cytoskeleton rearrangement, leading to displacement of the scaffolding protein ZO-1 from the junctional complex, disengagement of occludin/claudins, and OPENING of the paracellular space (increased intestinal permeability). PAR2 knockout or siRNA silencing prevents this barrier loss (PMID 19805376; Evidence-rodent/in vitro). In celiac disease, gliadin peptides (via CXCR3) drive zonulin release, amplifying this loop. Larazotide's actions (what it does to oppose the cascade): (1) It inhibits zonulin/gliadin/AT-1002-induced redistribution of ZO-1 and actin, preserving normal tight-junction architecture (PMID 22401908; Evidence-in vitro/in vivo mouse). (2) It actively PROMOTES tight-junction ASSEMBLY — in calcium-switch assays it accelerates junctional relocalization of ZO-1, occludin, claudins and E-cadherin and drives actin reorganization, i.e. it is pro-assembly, not merely blocking (PMID 22401910; Evidence-in vitro). (3) More recently it has been linked to inhibition of myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK); reduced MLC phosphorylation lowers perijunctional actomyosin tension, favoring junction closure (PMID 33881350; Evidence-in vitro/mechanistic review, Contested as a primary mechanism). (4) It enhances localization of the sealing claudin claudin-4 during epithelial repair (PMID 33886649; Evidence-ex vivo porcine). Net effect: reduced paracellular permeability and restored barrier integrity, blocking translocation of immunogenic gliadin peptides into the lamina propria.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral; the clinical form is larazotide acetate, formulated as a delayed/targeted-release capsule designed to dissolve in the mid-duodenum and jejunum (the site of celiac injury). It is intended to act LOCALLY/topically in the gut lumen, not systemically (Established).
Absorption / bioavailability
Essentially negligible systemic absorption — this is by design and is central to its safety profile. As an 8-mer hydrophilic peptide it is poorly absorbed across the intact epithelium and is largely confined to the gut lumen and brush border. In a porcine PK study, after a 1 mg oral dose, larazotide appeared time-dependently in luminal fluid with peak duodenal/proximal-jejunal concentrations of ~0.32–1.76 µM at ~1 h, persisting at 0.02–0.47 µM out to 2–4 h — confirming it reaches the target site at pharmacologically relevant (low-micromolar) concentrations without needing systemic exposure (PMID 33844694; Evidence-porcine). Oral systemic bioavailability is effectively near-zero (Established by design; plasma levels generally not quantifiable in trials).
Half-life
No meaningful systemic plasma half-life is reported because systemic exposure is negligible; the relevant parameter is luminal residence time, on the order of a few hours in the small intestine (PMID 33844694; Evidence-porcine). A true systemic t1/2 is essentially undefined/not applicable.
Metabolism
Degraded LOCALLY in the gut by brush-border peptidases — specifically aminopeptidase M (aminopeptidase N / CD13) cleaves larazotide into peptide fragments. Critically, a fragment lacking BOTH N-terminal glycines is an INHIBITOR of larazotide's own activity, which is the proposed molecular basis for the bell-shaped dose-response (excess peptide generates more self-inhibitory fragment). A D-amino-acid (chirally modified) analog (Analog #6) resists this degradation and is active at ~10-fold lower concentration (PMID 33886649; Evidence-ex vivo/in vitro). No hepatic CYP metabolism is relevant because the drug does not reach the systemic circulation.
Excretion
Predominantly fecal — unabsorbed peptide and its luminal peptidase-generated fragments are eliminated in the stool. Minimal-to-no renal/biliary systemic clearance because of negligible absorption (inferred from gut-restricted PK; Evidence-porcine/Established by design).
Notes
It is an acetate salt of the parent octapeptide (the acetate is a counter-ion, NOT a prodrug ester). Larazotide is NOT a prodrug — it is the active species, although its activity is shaped by in-situ fragmentation. It does NOT cross the blood-brain barrier and has no appreciable systemic distribution. The defining PK feature is gut-restriction: efficacy and safety both follow from acting topically on the intestinal epithelium.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Larazotide shows a distinctive NON-MONOTONIC, bell-shaped (U-shaped) dose-response in BOTH preclinical and human data — more is not better, and the lowest effective dose tends to be optimal. EX VIVO PORCINE: in ischemia-injured jejunum, 1 µM significantly restored transepithelial resistance and reduced LPS flux, whereas 0.1 µM and 10 µM did NOT; the loss of effect at 10 µM is attributed to accumulation of self-inhibitory peptidase fragments (PMID 33886649; Evidence-ex vivo). HUMAN (the clearest dose-response signal): In the 342-patient Phase 2b trial of patients symptomatic despite a gluten-free diet, the LOWEST dose tested, 0.5 mg three times daily, met the primary symptom endpoint, while 1 mg and 2 mg three times daily were NO different from placebo on any endpoint (PMID 25683116; Evidence-human). In the gluten-challenge trials, lower doses (e.g. 1 mg) tended to limit symptoms and blunt the anti-tTG antibody rise, while higher doses showed less benefit (PMID 23163616, 22825365; Evidence-human). IMPORTANT GAP: a clean human dose-response CURVE for the mechanistic biomarker (intestinal permeability, lactulose-mannitol ratio) could NOT be established — LAMA was too variable in outpatients to detect a dose effect, and across trials larazotide did NOT separate from placebo on the LAMA permeability endpoint (PMID 22825365, 23163616, 34339872; Evidence-human). So the human efficacy that exists is on SYMPTOM scales at ~0.5 mg TID; the permeability dose-response in humans is effectively UNKNOWN/unconfirmed. There is no validated dose-response for any non-celiac indication in humans.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Latiglutenase (IMGX003 / ALV003) — oral gluten-degrading endopeptidases (a glutenase)
    same: Both are oral, gut-restricted, non-systemic, non-immunosuppressive adjuncts to a gluten-free diet for celiac disease; both reached Phase 2 and aim to prevent gliadin-driven injury.
    different: Mechanism is upstream and different: latiglutenase DESTROYS immunogenic gliadin peptides in the stomach/lumen (proteolysis) so they never reach the epithelium, whereas larazotide leaves gliadin intact but TIGHTENS the barrier to block its paracellular entry and downstream zonulin signaling. Larazotide is a single short peptide; latiglutenase is a pair of engineered enzymes.
  • vs AT-1002 (zonula occludens toxin / zonulin agonist fragment)
    same: Structurally related — both derive from the zonulin/ZOT region of the same tight-junction signaling system; both act on the paracellular junction.
    different: OPPOSITE pharmacology: AT-1002 is a permeability ENHANCER (it OPENS tight junctions, used experimentally as a paracellular drug-delivery absorption enhancer), while larazotide is the functional ANTAGONIST that CLOSES/stabilizes junctions and blocks AT-1002's effect (PMID 22401908).
  • vs Zinc / glutamine / other 'leaky gut' barrier supplements
    same: Marketed/used for the same conceptual goal — restoring intestinal barrier integrity and reducing 'leaky gut'.
    different: Larazotide is a defined synthetic octapeptide with a specific (if unconfirmed) molecular target in the zonulin pathway and actual RCT data; the supplements lack a defined receptor mechanism and rigorous controlled efficacy data for permeability. Larazotide also has the unique self-limiting bell-shaped PK; supplements do not.
  • vs Anti-IL-15 / immunomodulators (e.g. AMG 714) for celiac disease
    same: Both are non-dietary celiac therapeutics that progressed to clinical trials targeting the gluten-injury cascade.
    different: AMG 714 is a SYSTEMIC monoclonal antibody acting on the adaptive/innate immune effector arm (IL-15), with systemic immunosuppression risks; larazotide is a non-absorbed, non-immunosuppressive peptide acting purely on the physical barrier, hence its much cleaner systemic safety profile but more modest, biomarker-negative efficacy.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

"Origin: Larazotide acetate (AT-1001; research name from the zonula occludens toxin/zonulin work of Alessio Fasano's group at the University of Maryland) emerged from the discovery that zonulin (later identified as pre-haptoglobin-2, PMID 19805376) physiologically regulates intestinal tight junctions. It was developed by Alba Therapeutics, then advanced by Innovate Biopharmaceuticals and finally 9 Meters Biopharma. Clinical program: It has been the most clinically advanced non-dietary celiac therapy and the FIRST tight-junction-regulator drug to reach Phase 3. Trial chronology includes Phase 1 in healthy volunteers and celiac patients (NCT00386490, NCT00386165), multiple Phase 2/2b gluten-challenge and symptomatic studies (NCT00362856, NCT00492960, NCT00620451, NCT00889473), the positive low-dose Phase 2b NCT01396213 (Leffler 2015), and the pivotal Phase 3 NCT03569007. NOTE on the 'AT-1001' name collision: Amicus Therapeutics' migalastat (a Fabry disease pharmacological chaperone) is ALSO coded AT1001 — those Fabry trials are a DIFFERENT molecule and must not be conflated with larazotide. Regulatory status: Larazotide received FDA Fast Track designation for celiac disease. However it is NOT approved anywhere — the Phase 3 trial was TERMINATED in 2022 after an interim futility analysis indicated it would not meet its primary endpoint, and 9 Meters discontinued the program. As of now there is no approved larazotide product; remaining activity is investigational (e.g., a Phase 2a long-COVID study, NCT05747534, and assorted preclinical work in arthritis, NAFLD, pancreatitis, ischemia). It is not a controlled/banned substance, but it is also not a legally marketed approved drug — any consumer 'larazotide' product is unapproved/research-grade."

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • What is larazotide's actual molecular binding target? No receptor has been identified and no Kd/Ki/IC50 exists — 'zonulin antagonist' is functional shorthand, and even whether it binds zonulin, a receptor, or acts on the epithelial surface/cytoskeleton directly is unresolved.
  • Why did the positive low-dose (0.5 mg) Phase 2b symptom signal fail to replicate in Phase 3? Was it dose selection, the bell-shaped self-inhibition, placebo response, or a genuine lack of clinically meaningful effect?
  • Why does larazotide consistently improve SYMPTOMS but NOT the mechanistic permeability biomarker (lactulose-mannitol) in humans? This biomarker/symptom disconnect undercuts the proposed mechanism and is unexplained.
  • How important is MLCK inhibition versus zonulin-pathway antagonism as the PRIMARY mechanism? The MLCK link is recent and not firmly established as causal.
  • Do the non-celiac indications (long COVID, arthritis, NAFLD, pancreatitis, sepsis/ischemia) translate to humans? Nearly all supporting data are rodent/porcine; human efficacy outside celiac is essentially untested.
  • Can chiral modification or microdosing (to avoid self-inhibitory aminopeptidase fragments) salvage a clinically meaningful, reproducible effect — i.e., is the bell-shaped PK a fixable formulation problem or a fundamental ceiling?

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction framing (NOT dosing advice): Larazotide is gut-restricted with negligible systemic absorption, so classic systemic drug-toxicity bloodwork is largely uninformative and no characteristic organ toxicity has been identified. What to actually watch: (1) Symptom diary — because the only demonstrated human benefit is on GI symptom scales (abdominal pain, bloating, diarrhea, plus tiredness/headache), track whether symptoms genuinely change; lack of improvement is expected given Phase 3 futility. (2) For anyone using it in the celiac context: it is an ADJUNCT, never a replacement for a gluten-free diet — monitor celiac serology (anti-tissue transglutaminase IgA, anti-deamidated gliadin) and, where clinically indicated, mucosal healing, since the drug does not reliably normalize the permeability biomarker and will not protect against ongoing gluten exposure. (3) Nutritional status in celiac patients generally (iron/ferritin, B12, folate, vitamin D) — relevant to the underlying disease, not the drug. (4) Headache and general tolerability as the most-reported (largely placebo-comparable) adverse events. (5) Provenance/contamination risk: because no approved product exists, any 'larazotide' obtained is unapproved research-grade material of unverified identity and purity — this sourcing/contamination risk is the dominant real-world hazard, not on-target pharmacology. Watch for signs of the gut-restricted assumption being violated only with non-oral or compromised-barrier use. No routine liver/renal monitoring is mechanistically indicated for properly oral, gut-restricted use, but baseline labs are reasonable given unverified product quality."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE: outside celiac disease, all the exciting uses (intestinal ischemia, acute pancreatitis, acute liver failure, arthritis, gut-brain axis) are animal or test-tube only — do not imply human benefit. Even within celiac, the drug FAILED Phase 3 (NCT03569007 terminated, 2022) and is not approved anywhere. The permeability endpoint (the thing it's mechanistically supposed to fix) was negative in pooled human RCTs — the modest symptom signal that did appear was at only the lowest 0.5 mg dose and not the higher doses, an unusual bell-shaped response explained by gut enzymes chopping the peptide into self-inhibiting fragments. Larazotide is NOT systemically absorbed, so forum claims of it fixing whole-body 'leaky gut,' autoimmunity, or 'detox' have no human support. 'Zonulin' itself is a contested biomarker — commercial zonulin ELISA assays have been criticized for measuring other proteins, so 'zonulin antagonist' is a mechanistic label, not a validated clinical readout. Several pivotal mechanism and trial papers have authors affiliated with the developer (Alba Therapeutics / Innovate Biopharmaceuticals / 9 Meters Biopharma) — note the commercial interest. Retraction status checked live: none retracted (PMIDs 33881350, 25683116, 33886649, 34339872 all clear).
