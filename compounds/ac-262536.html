AC-262536

Main target: Androgen receptor. Human evidence is still limited.

Works in humans?Limited human data
Approved?Not FDA-approved

A preclinical "mild" non-steroidal SARM from essentially a single 2008 rat study — popular online as an ostarine alternative, but with zero human evidence behind the hype.

Limited human data 10 citations
Class
Non-steroidal selective androgen receptor modulator (SARM); azabicyclo/naphthalene ("tropanol"-type) AR partial agonist
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR, gene NR3C4) — binds the ligand-binding pocket as a partial agonist, producing submaximal AR activation relative to testosterone/DHT
Human status
No human data. AC-262536 has never been tested in a registered human clinical trial (ClinicalTrials.gov returns zero records), and there are no published human pharmacology, efficacy, or safety studies. The entire compound-specific evidence base is one 2008 in vitro/rat characterization from ACADIA Pharmaceuticals (PMID 18164613) plus a metabolism/PK study in horses (published 2021, electronic 2020; PMID 32959959) done for anti-doping detection purposes. It was never approved anywhere, is not a licensed medicine, and is sold only as a gray-market 'research chemical.' Any claims about human muscle gain, 'mildness,' or safety are extrapolation from rodent/equine data and unverified community anecdote — not clinical evidence. Like all SARMs it is a WADA Prohibited Substance (S1 anabolic agents), and marketing it for human consumption is illegal in the US (FDA) and many other jurisdictions.

At a glance

  • Muscle-selective anabolic signaling in rats (levator ani growth) with weak prostate/androgenic activity — the basis for its 'mild SARM' reputation (rodent evidence)
  • Partial agonist: submaximal AR activation, weaker than testosterone by design (in vitro)
  • Suppressed LH in castrated rats — implies it can suppress the body's own testosterone production (HPTA suppression); human magnitude is entirely unmeasured
  • Expected HDL ('good cholesterol') suppression as an oral non-steroidal SARM (class effect in humans; not measured for this compound)
  • Potential liver injury risk shared across orally-used SARMs (idiosyncratic; documented in humans for the class, not for AC-262536 specifically)
  • No proven benefit or safety in humans — all human effects are extrapolated from rat/horse data and community anecdote

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

6 steps1 human-evidenced 5 preclinical

  1. Binds the androgen receptor as a partial agonist Evidence-in vitro

    AC-262536 is a small non-steroidal molecule that slots into the same ligand-binding pocket of the androgen receptor (AR) that testosterone and DHT use. In the original ACADIA characterization, using a functional cell-based assay, it was identified as a potent, selective AR ligand with partial agonist activity relative to testosterone — meaning it only partially switches the receptor on compared with the natural androgen. Note: the primary paper does NOT publish a numeric binding affinity (Ki); the '~5 nM' figure circulating online is secondary-sourced and unverified. PMID 18164613 (Piu 2008, J Steroid Biochem Mol Biol); DOI 10.1016/j.jsbmb.2007.11.001

  2. Partial, submaximal activation of AR-driven transcription Evidence-in vitro

    Once bound, the AR-AC-262536 complex acts as a partial agonist in a functional cell-based reporter assay, driving transcription of androgen-responsive genes to a lesser maximal degree than a full agonist like testosterone. This 'weaker than testosterone but still active' signaling is what community users interpret as 'mild.' This is an in vitro finding. PMID 18164613 (Piu 2008); DOI 10.1016/j.jsbmb.2007.11.001

  3. Tissue-selective anabolic effect in rats: muscle > prostate Evidence-rodent

    In castrated (orchidectomized) male Sprague-Dawley rats given AC-262536 for 2 weeks, it significantly stimulated growth of the levator ani muscle (an anabolic readout) while having only weak effects on prostate and seminal vesicle weight (androgenic readouts). This muscle-favoring, prostate-sparing dissociation is the entire pharmacological rationale for the 'SARM' label. It has been shown in rats only, not humans. PMID 18164613 (Piu 2008); DOI 10.1016/j.jsbmb.2007.11.001

  4. Suppresses luteinizing hormone (LH) in rats Evidence-rodent

    In the same rat study, AC-262536 suppressed elevated LH levels in castrated rats. Because LH is the pituitary signal that tells the testes to make testosterone, an AR agonist providing negative feedback at the hypothalamic-pituitary axis is expected to down-regulate the body's own testosterone production — the mechanistic basis for the 'HPTA suppression' that SARM users worry about. Demonstrated as LH suppression in the castrated-rat (elevated-LH) model, and consistent with the class; the magnitude in humans is entirely unknown for this specific compound. PMID 18164613 (Piu 2008); DOI 10.1016/j.jsbmb.2007.11.001

  5. As an oral non-steroidal SARM, expected to lower HDL cholesterol (class inference) Evidence-human

    AC-262536 itself has NO lipid data in any species. But oral non-steroidal SARMs as a class suppress HDL ('good') cholesterol in humans — a controlled human study of the SARM OPK-88004 showed HDL-C suppression, with an increase in hepatic triacylglycerol lipase (HTGL) as one contributing mechanism. Importantly, that same study found the effect on HDL function/atherogenic potential was minimal and concluded the HDL drop is NOT necessarily proatherogenic. Applying any of this to AC-262536 is an inference from the class, not a direct measurement. PMID 35822201 (Guo 2022, J Endocr Soc — OPK-88004, human, class exemplar); DOI 10.1210/jendso/bvac099

  6. Hepatic metabolism (glucuronidation) in animals — a plausible route to liver stress (class) Evidence-rodent

    In horses dosed orally, AC-262536 was extensively metabolized (nine phase-I metabolites), conjugated mainly to glucuronides, and appeared in urine and plasma; parent drug even incorporated into hair. This confirms hepatic metabolism and systemic exposure of the intact compound IN A NON-HUMAN SPECIES (horse). Separately, non-steroidal SARMs as a class have caused documented drug-induced liver injury in HUMANS (numerous case reports for RAD-140, LGD-4033, etc.), plausibly via oxidative stress and disrupted lipid handling. The compound-specific metabolism data are equine; the human hepatotoxicity risk is a class inference, not measured for AC-262536. Metabolism/PK (equine, in vitro + in vivo): PMID 32959959 (Cutler 2021, DOI 10.1002/dta.2932). Class hepatotoxicity (human): PMID 40625511 (Habeb 2025, Cureus, DOI 10.7759/cureus.85500); PMID 39755947 (Vasireddi 2025, Am J Sports Med, DOI 10.1177/03635465241252435)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Non-steroidal small-molecule ligand of the androgen receptor (AR, NR3C4). Occupies the orthosteric ligand-binding pocket used by testosterone/DHT. Characterized in a functional cell-based (reporter) assay as a potent, selective AR ligand with PARTIAL AGONIST activity relative to testosterone. IMPORTANT: the primary paper (Piu 2008) does not report a numeric binding constant; the '~5 nM Ki' quoted in community/secondary sources is NOT reproduced in the peer-reviewed primary text and should be treated as approximate/unverified. Chemically it belongs to an azabicyclo/naphthalene SARM series (MeSH indexes it under Azabicyclo Compounds and Naphthalenes; anti-doping literature classes it as a 'tropanol' derivative), developed at ACADIA Pharmaceuticals, from the same discovery program that produced ACP-105.

Signalling cascade. AR binding -> nuclear translocation of the ligand-receptor complex -> binding to androgen response elements (AREs) -> submaximal transcription of androgen-responsive genes. Tissue selectivity (anabolic muscle effect with weak prostate effect) is thought to arise from tissue-specific coregulator recruitment and the partial-agonist conformation of AR, as described generally for the SARM class — the AC-262536-specific coregulator profile has not been mapped.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Orally active (demonstrated in rats and horses by oral administration). Sold to consumers as an oral liquid 'research chemical.' No human PK exists.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally bioavailable in animals (anabolic effects and systemic exposure achieved via oral dosing in rats and horses). Quantitative human bioavailability: unknown.
Half-life
Not determined in humans. No validated human half-life exists; any figure circulating in community sources is unsubstantiated.
Metabolism
Extensively hepatically metabolized in the horse. In equine in vitro and in vivo work, nine phase-I metabolites were identified; parent drug and metabolites circulated primarily as glucuronide conjugates. An epimer of the parent compound gave the longest urinary/plasma detection window. This confirms substantial hepatic processing in a non-human species; human metabolism has not been directly studied.
Excretion
Renal excretion of glucuronide conjugates (parent + phase-I metabolites detected in urine and plasma in horses). Parent compound also incorporated into hair, enabling retrospective detection. Human elimination half-life: not established.
Notes
All PK/metabolism data derive from an anti-doping study in Thoroughbred horses (PMID 32959959, published 2021 / electronic 2020) — species-appropriate for detection science but only a rough guide to human handling. The practical takeaway is hepatic metabolism + glucuronidation, consistent with the liver being the main site of processing (and thus a plausible site of toxicity for the class).

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

No human dose-response data exist and NO dosing protocol is provided or endorsed here. The only quantitative dose-response signal is from the original rat study: a 2-week course produced anabolic effects (levator ani muscle growth) and LH suppression while sparing prostate/seminal vesicle tissue, establishing the muscle-vs-androgenic tissue-selectivity window in rodents. Translating any rat dose to a human dose is not scientifically valid for this compound, and gray-market product mislabeling makes real-world dose entirely uncertain. This entry deliberately gives no mg figures or 'how to run it' guidance.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Ostarine (MK-2866 / enobosarm)
    same: Both non-steroidal AR partial agonists marketed as 'mild' muscle-selective SARMs; both suppress the HPTA and are expected to lower HDL.
    different: Ostarine is far better studied — multiple human clinical trials (muscle wasting, cancer cachexia) give it real human efficacy/safety data. AC-262536 has ZERO human trials; its 'ostarine alternative' status rests only on rat pharmacology and anecdote.
  • vs ACP-105
    same: Sister compound from the SAME ACADIA non-steroidal SARM discovery program; both potent AR partial agonists with anabolic activity in castrated rats.
    different: Different chemotype/potency within the series; ACP-105 was characterized in a dedicated medicinal-chemistry SAR paper (PMID 19856921). Both remain preclinical.
  • vs RAD-140 (testolone) / LGD-4033 (ligandrol)
    same: All non-steroidal SARMs sold online; all suppress natural testosterone and carry class hepatotoxicity/HDL risks.
    different: RAD-140 and LGD-4033 are more potent/strongly suppressive and are the SARMs most often implicated in published human liver-injury case reports (e.g., RAD-140 PMID 36561105, 39328701, 40761329; LGD-4033 PMID 39421081); AC-262536 is positioned by users as milder — but this is a partial-agonist-in-rats claim, not a demonstrated human safety advantage.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

AC-262536 was discovered and characterized by ACADIA Pharmaceuticals in the mid-2000s as part of a non-steroidal SARM program (first published 2008, PMID 18164613). ACADIA did not develop it as a marketed drug and later pivoted to CNS programs; AC-262536 was effectively shelved at the preclinical stage. It has NO approval from FDA, EMA, or any regulator, and no orphan/therapeutic designation. Like all SARMs it is a WADA Prohibited Substance (S1, anabolic agents) banned in and out of competition. In the US, the FDA has warned that SARMs are unapproved drugs and it is illegal to market them as dietary supplements or for human consumption; legislative efforts (the SARMs Control Act) have sought to schedule them. In practice AC-262536 exists only as a gray-market 'research chemical,' and its main appearance in the recent scientific literature is in anti-doping detection methods (e.g., PMID 32959959, PMID 32519780, PMID 33383501).

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Human potency, effective exposure, and half-life — no human PK exists.
  • Whether the rat muscle-vs-prostate selectivity translates to any real anabolic benefit in humans.
  • Degree and reversibility of testosterone suppression in humans (only LH suppression in rats is known).
  • Magnitude of HDL suppression and true cardiovascular impact for THIS compound (inferred from class only; even for the class the atherogenic significance is uncertain).
  • Hepatotoxicity risk specific to AC-262536 (class risk is documented in humans; compound-specific incidence is unstudied).
  • A reliable binding affinity — no Ki is published in the primary literature; the quoted '~5 nM' is secondary-sourced/unverified.
  • Long-term safety, effects in women, drug interactions, and consequences of the frequent product mislabeling.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

For harm-reduction only (this is not an endorsement of use, and no dosing is provided). Given the class pharmacology, anyone who has used a product sold as AC-262536 and is being clinically evaluated would reasonably have: (1) Liver function tests — ALT, AST, ALP, bilirubin — at baseline and if any symptoms of hepatotoxicity appear (fatigue, dark urine, jaundice, right-upper-quadrant pain, nausea); SARMs can cause idiosyncratic drug-induced liver injury, so new symptoms warrant prompt medical assessment and stopping the compound. (2) Lipid panel — particularly HDL-C, which oral SARMs characteristically suppress. (3) Hormonal panel — total/free testosterone, LH, FSH — to detect HPTA suppression; persistent suppression after cessation should prompt endocrinology review. (4) Awareness that gray-market products are frequently mislabeled or adulterated, so effects (and toxicities) may come from an undisclosed compound entirely. Red-flag symptoms (jaundice, severe abdominal pain, chest pain, marked mood change) should trigger immediate medical care. Use in tested athletes will cause an anti-doping violation. No dosing, cycling, titration, or 'PCT' guidance is provided.

Caveats

Honesty about the evidence tier is the single most important point: AC-262536 is a PRECLINICAL compound with a razor-thin dataset — essentially one rat/in vitro study for pharmacology and one horse study for metabolism. It is NOT a well-characterized drug. Specific cautions: (1) There is NO published binding affinity — the primary paper reports 'potent, selective, partial agonist' without a Ki, so the widely-quoted '~5 nM' is secondary/community-sourced and should not be treated as an established value. (2) The 'milder than ostarine/other SARMs' reputation rests on partial-agonist pharmacology in rats plus anecdote — there is NO head-to-head human comparison, no human potency data, and no human safety margin. 'Milder' does not mean 'safe.' (3) Testosterone suppression, HDL suppression, and liver injury are real, documented hazards of the SARM CLASS in humans; the absence of AC-262536-specific human reports reflects that almost nobody has studied it, not that it is free of these risks. (4) Gray-market SARM products are frequently mislabeled, underdosed, or contain entirely different compounds — so real-world 'AC-262536' may not even be AC-262536. (5) This is not medical advice and contains no dosing guidance; possession/use may be illegal and is prohibited in tested sport.
