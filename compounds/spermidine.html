Spermidine

Main target: No single receptor. Human evidence is still limited.

EvidenceLimited human data
ApprovalNot FDA-approved

A natural polyamine that acts as a "cellular cleanup" signal — it switches on autophagy, the process that recycles damaged proteins and worn-out mitochondria. Spectacular at extending lifespan in yeast, worms, flies, and mice; in humans the data are mostly dietary-correlation plus a couple of small trials, and the one larger, longer cognition RCT came back null.

Established that spermidine is a normal dietary polyamine (wheat germ, soy, aged cheese, mushrooms) and that intracellular levels fall with age. Lifespan/healthspan extension is robust in model organisms (Evidence-rodent and below). In humans: dietary spermidine intake correlates with lower cardiovascular mortality in observational cohorts (Evidence-human, correlational — confounded by overall diet). Supplements are generally well tolerated short-term. Bottom line: promising mechanism, strong animal data, human clinical benefit unproven.

At a glance

  • Induces autophagy and mitophagy — the cell's recycling of damaged proteins and mitochondria (robust across yeast→mice; the core mechanism)
  • Extends lifespan in yeast, worms, flies, and mice (Evidence-rodent and below)
  • Cardioprotective in aged and hypertensive rodents: less cardiac hypertrophy, better diastolic function, lower blood pressure (Eisenberg 2016); dietary intake correlates with lower CVD in humans
  • Reduces oxidative stress and dampens low-grade inflammation in animal models
  • Memory/cognition: positive in animals and a small human pilot, but NULL in the larger 12-month SmartAge RCT — net human cognitive benefit is unproven
  • Acts as a caloric-restriction mimetic, overlapping with the autophagy effects of fasting, rapamycin, and resveratrol

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

6 steps2 human / established 4 rodent / preclinical

  1. Spermidine enters cells; levels normally decline with age Established

    Spermidine is a small positively-charged polyamine the body makes and also gets from food (wheat germ, soy, aged cheese, mushrooms). Intracellular polyamine levels drop as we age, and the idea is that topping them back up restores youthful 'housekeeping.' This decline-with-age is well documented. Eisenberg 2009, Nat Cell Biol, PMID 19801973; Uemura 2026 review, PMID 41617890

  2. It blocks acetyl-transfer (inhibits HATs / EP300) and feeds eIF5A hypusination Evidence-in vitro / Evidence-rodent

    Two molecular handles. First, spermidine inhibits histone acetyltransferases — in yeast it deacetylates histone H3, and in mammals it inhibits the acetyltransferase EP300/p300, a brake on autophagy; releasing that brake turns autophagy on. Second, spermidine is the unique chemical building block used to modify translation factor eIF5A (a reaction called hypusination), which the cell needs to make certain proteins. Eisenberg 2009, PMID 19801973; Pietrocola 2015 (EP300/CR-mimetic concept), reviewed in Abdellatif 2018, PMID 30355077

  3. Hypusinated eIF5A and AMPK/mTOR signaling switch on the autophagy program Evidence-rodent / Evidence-in vitro

    Hypusinated eIF5A is needed to efficiently translate the master autophagy transcription factor TFEB, so more functional eIF5A means more autophagy machinery gets built. In parallel, spermidine nudges the cell's energy sensors — activating AMPK and easing off mTOR (the growth/'fed' signal) — which is the same switch that fasting and rapamycin flip. Together these turn on the autophagy gene program. eIF5A→TFEB axis, PMID 31474573; AMPK/SIRT1 route reviewed in Borsky 2025, PMID 41404767; Uemura 2026, PMID 41617890

  4. Autophagy and mitophagy clear damaged proteins and worn-out mitochondria Evidence-rodent

    Autophagy is the cell wrapping up junk — misfolded protein clumps, damaged organelles — and digesting it for recycling. Mitophagy is the same thing aimed specifically at broken mitochondria. In spermidine-fed mice this boosts mitochondrial respiration and cleans the cellular slate. Crucially, when researchers genetically delete the autophagy gene Atg5, spermidine's benefits disappear — strong evidence autophagy is the real mechanism, not a side-effect. Eisenberg 2016, Nat Med, PMID 27841876 (Atg5-knockout abolishes benefit); Rubinsztein 2011, PMID 21884931

  5. Downstream: cardioprotection, reduced oxidative stress/inflammation, longer life in animals Evidence-rodent

    Cleaner cells translate to organ-level benefits in animals: aged mice fed spermidine live longer, have less heart enlargement and better heart relaxation; hypertensive rats have lower blood pressure and delayed heart failure. Oxidative stress and chronic low-grade inflammation also drop. These are the effects driving the longevity hype. Eisenberg 2016, PMID 27841876; Eisenberg 2009, PMID 19801973

  6. In humans: dietary correlation is encouraging, but the best trial was null Contested (human efficacy); Evidence-human (dietary correlation only)

    Here's the honest part. In people, higher dietary spermidine intake correlates with lower blood pressure and less cardiovascular disease — but that's observational and confounded (spermidine-rich diets are just healthier overall). A small 3-month pilot (n=30) suggested memory improvement, yet the larger, longer SmartAge RCT (n=100, 12 months) found NO benefit on its main memory measure. So human efficacy is not established — animal mechanism is strong, human proof is thin. Schwarz 2022 SmartAge RCT (null), PMID 35616942; Wirth 2018 pilot (positive), PMID 30388439; CVD correlation in Eisenberg 2016, PMID 27841876

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Spermidine is NOT a classical receptor ligand — it has no single high-affinity drug-target with a clean Ki. It is a small (MW 145.2), triamine polycation (net +3 at physiological pH) that acts through multiple low-affinity, high-abundance interactions. The NAMED mechanistic target for autophagy is EP300 (E1A-binding protein p300, a lysine acetyltransferase / HAT). Pietrocola et al. 2015 (PMID 25526088) showed spermidine directly inhibits the acetyltransferase activity of recombinant EP300 in vitro and, in an siRNA screen of 43 acetyltransferases, EP300 (and NAA20) were the only ones whose knockdown both activated autophagy AND inhibited mTORC1 — establishing EP300 as the principal pro-autophagic target. Certainty: Established (in vitro/cellular). However, a precise published IC50/Ki for spermidine on EP300 is NOT well characterized in the human literature retrieved here — spermidine is a weak, millimolar-range modulator, not a nanomolar inhibitor (contrast the pharmacological EP300 inhibitor C646). State this gap explicitly. Spermidine ALSO binds nucleic acids and ribosomes electrostatically (a major intracellular sink), and serves as the obligate substrate for deoxyhypusine synthase (DHPS) in hypusination of eIF5A — a covalent enzymatic role, not reversible binding. Certainty: Established. Action type at EP300 = INHIBITOR (HAT inhibitor); at DHPS = SUBSTRATE.

Signalling cascade. Two partly-overlapping cascades converge on increased autophagic flux. (1) EP300/acetylation arm (Pietrocola 2015, PMID 25526088; Eisenberg 2009, PMID 19801973): spermidine inhibits EP300 (cytoplasmic) and histone acetyltransferases → global protein and histone H3 HYPOacetylation. In yeast this de-represses transcription of ATG genes (ATG7, ATG11, ATG15); in mammalian cells cytoplasmic EP300 inhibition relieves acetylation of core autophagy proteins (e.g., ATG5, ATG7, LC3) and inhibits mTORC1, jointly driving LC3-I→LC3-II conversion, GFP-LC3 puncta formation, and SQSTM1/p62 depletion (markers of flux). Certainty: Established (in vitro/rodent). (2) eIF5A-hypusination/TFEB arm (Hofer/Madeo 2024, PMID 39212197): spermidine is the unique substrate for hypusination of translation factor eIF5A; hypusinated eIF5A is required to translate the master autophagy/lysosome transcription factor TFEB. Spermidine → eIF5A hypusination → TFEB translation → CLEAR-network lysosomal/autophagy gene transcription → autophagic flux. This arm is the obligatory downstream effector of fasting- and rapamycin-induced autophagy and longevity (loss of ODC1 or hypusination enzymes abolishes those benefits). Certainty: Established (yeast/fly/worm/mouse). Downstream physiology: enhanced macroautophagy and mitophagy → improved mitochondrial respiration, reduced proteotoxic/oxidative load, suppressed inflammasome/cytokine output, and (cardiac-specific) increased titin phosphorylation improving myocyte compliance (Eisenberg 2016, PMID 27841876). Autophagy-INDEPENDENT actions also exist: direct antioxidant/anti-LDL-oxidation (PMID 40005266) and AMPK/SIRT1 modulation. The cardioprotective phenotype is Atg5-dependent (abolished in cardiomyocyte Atg5-knockout mice), proving autophagy is causal there. Certainty: Evidence-rodent.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Oral (diet or supplement; wheat-germ extract is the most-studied clinical form, typically 0.9–1.2 mg/day in trials). Also endogenously synthesized de novo from putrescine via spermidine synthase, so plasma levels reflect diet + biosynthesis + gut-microbial production combined. No approved injectable human formulation.
Absorption / bioavailability
Dietary polyamines are absorbed almost entirely in the upper small intestine, rapidly and largely intact, then distributed to tissues (Larqué 2007 review, PMID 17113752). A precise, validated human oral bioavailability percentage for spermidine is NOT established in the retrieved literature — this is a genuine gap. A key confound: much ingested spermidine is taken up and metabolized by enterocytes and gut bacteria, and circulating levels are buffered by endogenous synthesis, so plasma spermidine rises only modestly and variably after supplementation. Reviewers explicitly attribute the variable/null human clinical outcomes partly to uncertain bioavailability and metabolic conversion (Uemura 2026, PMID 41617890). Certainty: Evidence-human (qualitative) / quantitative bioavailability Unknown.
Half-life
Not reliably established for whole-body spermidine in humans in the retrieved sources. Because it is an endogenous metabolite under tight homeostatic control (synthesis, interconversion, catabolism, and cellular sequestration onto RNA/DNA), simple plasma half-life is not a meaningful single number — intracellular pools turn over slowly while free plasma fractions are cleared faster. Treat any specific half-life figure with caution. Certainty: Unknown.
Metabolism
Tightly homeostatically regulated via the polyamine interconversion/catabolic pathway. Key enzymes: (1) Spermidine/spermine-N1-acetyltransferase 1 (SAT1/SSAT) acetylates spermidine, the rate-limiting committed step of catabolism; (2) acetyl-spermidine is then oxidized by peroxisomal acetylpolyamine oxidase (PAOX/APAO) back to putrescine, generating H2O2 and 3-acetamidopropanal; (3) spermine oxidase (SMOX) acts mainly on spermine but feeds the same pool and, when upregulated with age, produces the toxic aldehyde ACROLEIN plus H2O2 (Uemura 2026, PMID 41617890); (4) extracellular/dietary spermidine is also degraded by copper-containing amine oxidases — diamine oxidase (DAO) in gut/plasma and serum amine oxidases — again yielding H2O2, aminoaldehydes and acrolein. Biosynthetic counter-enzymes: ornithine decarboxylase (ODC1) and spermidine synthase (SRM), with S-adenosylmethionine decarboxylase (AMD1) supplying the aminopropyl donor. Certainty: Established (biochemistry).
Excretion
Polyamines and their oxidative/acetylated catabolites are excreted in urine in all metabolic forms (Larqué 2007, PMID 17113752). Certainty: Established (qualitative); quantitative renal handling not detailed in retrieved sources.
Notes
BBB: spermidine is reported to influence the gut-brain axis and CNS endpoints, and animal cognition data imply functional CNS access, but DIRECT quantitative human BBB-penetration data were NOT found here — CNS effects may be partly indirect (gut-brain, peripheral inflammation, microbial). Certainty: Contested/Unknown. No ester prodrug is used clinically; the practical 'delivery vehicle' is spermidine-rich food/wheat-germ extract. Plasma levels are strongly buffered by endogenous synthesis and gut microbiota, which is the central PK caveat for interpreting supplement trials.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Human dose-response is essentially UNCHARACTERIZED — a critical honesty point. The clinical trials used a single low nutritional dose (~0.9–1.2 mg spermidine/day from wheat-germ extract), with NO dose-ranging, so no human dose-response curve exists. At that dose: the 3-month pilot RCT (n=30) saw a moderate memory signal (Cohen's d ≈ 0.77), but the definitive 12-month phase 2b SmartAge RCT (n=100, 0.9 mg/day) found NO effect on the primary memory outcome (between-group diff −0.03; 95% CI −0.11 to 0.05; p=0.47) (Schwarz 2022, PMID 35616942). The trialists themselves concluded any future benefit would need HIGHER doses. ANIMAL dose-response: rodents/flies/yeast respond to far higher relative exposures (e.g., 3 mM spermidine in drinking water extended mouse lifespan and produced cardioprotection; Eisenberg 2016, PMID 27841876) — orders of magnitude above human supplement doses on a body-weight basis. In vitro, spermidine is explicitly BIPHASIC: in polyamine-depleted colorectal cancer cells, LOW concentrations (<20 µM) promoted proliferation via eIF5A hypusination, whereas HIGH concentrations (>100 µM) were cytotoxic (driven by extracellular amine-oxidase generation of H2O2/acrolein), and high doses transiently INHIBITED autophagic flux rather than enhancing it (Tahara 2025, PMID 41408852). Net: 'more is better' is false; effects are concentration- and context-dependent, and the human-effective dose for any hard endpoint is unknown. Certainty: Evidence-human (null at low dose) + Evidence-rodent (effective at high relative dose) + Evidence-in vitro (biphasic).

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Rapamycin (sirolimus)
    same: Both are 'caloric-restriction mimetics' that converge on autophagy induction and lifespan extension across model organisms; rapamycin's longevity/autophagy effects actually REQUIRE a spermidine surge and eIF5A hypusination downstream (Hofer/Madeo 2024, PMID 39212197) — spermidine is an obligatory effector of rapamycin.
    different: Rapamycin is a potent, specific nanomolar mTORC1 inhibitor (FKBP12-binding, prescription immunosuppressant with defined PK, real immunosuppression and metabolic side effects). Spermidine is a weak, multi-target endogenous metabolite with a benign short-term safety profile but unproven hard human efficacy. Mechanistic entry point differs: rapamycin acts upstream at mTORC1; spermidine acts via EP300/acetylation and eIF5A/TFEB.
  • vs Putrescine and spermine (the other natural polyamines)
    same: Same biosynthetic/interconversion pathway (putrescine → spermidine → spermine), same polycationic nucleic-acid/ribosome binding, all decline with age and are diet-derived.
    different: Spermidine is uniquely the SOLE substrate for eIF5A hypusination (spermine and putrescine cannot substitute), making it the specific autophagy/longevity-relevant polyamine. Spermine is the preferred substrate of spermine oxidase (SMOX) and thus the bigger source of toxic acrolein on catabolism; putrescine is the diamine precursor handled by diamine oxidase. (PMID 41617890)
  • vs Other dietary acetyltransferase-inhibiting autophagy inducers (curcumin, anacardic acid, garcinol)
    same: All four were shown in the SAME study to directly inhibit recombinant EP300 and induce autophagy with mTORC1 inhibition — a shared 'EP300-inhibitor' pharmacology (Pietrocola 2015, PMID 25526088).
    different: Spermidine is the only one that is an endogenous human metabolite with longevity epidemiology and dedicated human RCTs; the others are exogenous phytochemicals with their own additional targets and generally poorer bioavailability/specificity.
  • vs Urolithin A (mitophagy inducer, frequently co-marketed for 'longevity')
    same: Both are food-derived 'cellular-renewal' supplements promoted for healthspan and muscle aging, acting on autophagy-family processes.
    different: Urolithin A selectively induces MITOPHAGY via the PINK1/Parkin pathway, whereas spermidine induces general macroautophagy via EP300/eIF5A-TFEB (and mitophagy secondarily). They are mechanistically distinct and often discussed as complementary rather than interchangeable (PMID 41404767).

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

"Spermidine was first isolated historically from semen (hence the name, with spermine) and is a ubiquitous endogenous polyamine found in all eukaryotic cells and in many foods (wheat germ, soybeans/natto, aged cheese, mushrooms, legumes). Its modern 'longevity' era began with Eisenberg/Madeo et al. 2009 (Nature Cell Biology, PMID 19801973) demonstrating autophagy-mediated lifespan extension, followed by the EP300 mechanism (2015) and the Nature Medicine cardioprotection paper (2016). Regulatory status: spermidine is NOT an approved drug for any indication anywhere. It is marketed as a DIETARY SUPPLEMENT / food ingredient, most commonly as spermidine-rich wheat-germ extract; such products are regulated as foods/supplements (e.g., in the EU and US), not as medicines, and carry no approved disease claims. It is generally treated as having food-grade/GRAS-adjacent status at dietary doses; there are no bans, but as a non-drug supplement it is not subject to drug-level efficacy review. Human clinical work remains in phase 2 (SmartAge; ClinicalTrials.gov NCT03094546, NCT02755246). Certainty: Established (regulatory facts)."

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • Human dose-response is unmapped: every RCT used a single low nutritional dose (~0.9–1.2 mg/day) and the definitive phase 2b trial was NULL — the dose (if any) that produces measurable autophagy or clinical benefit in humans is unknown, and higher-dose safety/efficacy is untested.
  • True oral bioavailability, plasma/tissue half-life, and the degree of blood-brain-barrier penetration of supplemental spermidine in humans are not reliably quantified; circulating levels are heavily buffered by endogenous synthesis and gut microbiota, confounding interpretation.
  • Causality of the mortality/cardiovascular epidemiology: Kiechl 2018 (Bruneck/SAPHIR) found a strong protective association, but the large Japanese Takayama cohort (Nagata 2024) found no benefit and a hint of higher cancer mortality in women — is the Bruneck signal spermidine itself, or a marker of healthy whole-food diets?
  • The cancer double-edge: polyamines support tumor proliferation and spermidine is elevated in cancers, yet it is promoted for cancer prevention. The net effect of chronic supplementation on cancer risk in humans (and whether it differs by existing tumor burden, dose, or sex) is genuinely unresolved.
  • Relative contribution of the EP300/acetylation arm vs the eIF5A-hypusination/TFEB arm to any human benefit, and whether the named EP300 target is actually rate-limiting at achievable human exposures (no human EP300-occupancy or acetylome data exist).
  • Whether long-term (multi-year) supplementation is safe given age-related upregulation of spermine oxidase and the potential for acrolein/H2O2 generation from polyamine catabolism — chronic human safety beyond ~12 months is uncharacterized.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only — no dosing guidance. In trials, spermidine-rich wheat-germ extract at ~1.2 mg/day was well tolerated for up to 12 months with no signal in vital signs, body weight, routine clinical chemistry, hematology, or self-reported health, and adverse events balanced vs placebo (Schwarz 2018, PMID 29315079; Schwarz 2022, PMID 35616942). Reasonable things to WATCH if someone is using it: (1) General bloodwork at baseline and periodically — complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel (liver and kidney function), since these were the safety parameters tracked in trials. (2) Histamine-intolerance / DAO-related symptoms — flushing, headache, palpitations, GI upset, or low blood pressure after high biogenic-amine intake; people with known histamine intolerance or on MAO-inhibitor/DAO-affecting medications warrant extra caution because polyamines and histamine share amine-oxidase catabolism. (3) Cancer history — given the unresolved tumor-proliferation concern and the suggestive cancer-mortality signal in one cohort, anyone with active or prior malignancy should treat chronic high-dose use as unstudied and discuss with their oncologist. (4) Source quality — wheat-germ products carry gluten and theoretical mycotoxin/contaminant concerns; product testing matters. (5) Be skeptical of 'more is better' — in vitro data show high concentrations flip to cytotoxic and can inhibit autophagy, so megadosing is not supported. None of these replace clinician oversight; this is monitoring, not a protocol."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP IS THE HEADLINE: nearly all dramatic lifespan and cardioprotection results are in yeast, worms, flies, and mice. The strongest human cognition trial (SmartAge, n=100, 12 months) was NULL on its primary endpoint — do not let the 30-person positive pilot stand in for proof. Human cardiovascular evidence is observational/correlational and confounded by overall diet quality. (2) Dose translation is murky: the SmartAge dose was ~0.9 mg/day from wheat-germ extract; many forum/supplement claims cite far higher doses with no controlled human safety data at those levels. (3) The polyamine 'double-edged sword': polyamines are elevated in and can fuel cancer cells, and spermine catabolism generates toxic acrolein via spermine oxidase — context and dose matter (PMID 41617890; PMID 41408852 shows opposite effects depending on molecular target). (4) Mechanism is multi-pronged and not fully mapped — the eIF5A→TFEB and EP300/AMPK routes are the best-supported, but spermidine also has autophagy-independent actions (e.g. directly inhibiting LDL oxidation, PMID 40005266). (5) Several supporting items are reviews/preprints; preprints (bioRxiv/Preprints.org) are NOT peer-reviewed. (6) Not medical advice — this explains mechanism, it does not recommend supplementation. Core primary sources (PMID 19801973, 27841876, 30388439, 35616942) were live retraction-checked this run: none retracted or under expression of concern.
