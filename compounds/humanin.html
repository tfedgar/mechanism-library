Humanin

The first mitochondrial-derived peptide — a 24-amino-acid cellular "rescue factor" that blocks stress-induced apoptosis and sensitizes the body to insulin, with circulating levels that track human aging. Mechanistically rich but almost entirely preclinical: no approved therapeutic use in humans.

Human / approved 15 citations
Class
Mitochondrial-derived peptide (MDP); 24-aa cytoprotective/anti-apoptotic microprotein encoded within the mitochondrial 16S rRNA (MT-RNR2) gene
Primary target
Dual-compartment signaling
Human status
PRECLINICAL / RESEARCH ONLY for any therapeutic use. Humanin is an endogenous human peptide, so it and its analogs (e.g. the potent S14G-humanin/HNG and HNGF6A) are studied extensively in cell culture and rodents, but there is NO approved humanin drug and no interventional efficacy trial for any disease. On ClinicalTrials.gov the only registered human studies are OBSERVATIONAL biomarker studies — measuring endogenous plasma/tissue humanin, e.g. as a candidate marker in acute kidney injury (NCT06105229, observational, status listed as unknown) and after cardiac surgery (NCT03431844, observational, completed) — not trials that administer humanin as a treatment. It is not FDA-approved for anything and is not a dietary supplement; peptide vials sold online for 'longevity stacks' are unregulated research chemicals with no human efficacy or safety data. Claims that injecting humanin extends human lifespan, reverses aging, or treats Alzheimer's/diabetes are NOT supported by human trial evidence — the human data are correlational (higher endogenous levels or specific variants associate with longevity and health).

At a glance

  • Anti-apoptotic / cytoprotective: sequesters BAX/tBid/Bim and blocks the mitochondrial death pathway (best-established action)
  • Neuroprotective against amyloid-beta and diverse familial-Alzheimer toxicity in cell/rodent models
  • Improves insulin sensitivity via a central (hypothalamic STAT3) mechanism; enhances glucose-stimulated insulin secretion (rodent)
  • Antioxidant and endothelium-protective; reduced atherosclerotic plaque progression in ApoE-null mice (no cholesterol effect)
  • Activates STAT3 and PI3K/AKT survival signaling through FPRL1/FPR2 and the CNTFR-alpha/gp130/WSX-1 receptor complex
  • Circulating levels are an age/mitochondrial-stress biomarker; a rare P3S variant is enriched in centenarians (association, not proven cause)
  • Potential downside signals: in-vitro exacerbation of the senescent-cell SASP and tumor-promoting activity in glioblastoma models

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Encoded inside the mitochondrial genome Established

    Humanin is a 24-amino-acid peptide translated from a small open reading frame within the 16S ribosomal RNA (MT-RNR2) region of mitochondrial DNA. It was the first 'mitochondrial-derived peptide' ever found, discovered in 2001 by functional screening of a brain cDNA library from an Alzheimer's patient — a clone that rescued neurons was named 'Humanin.' This is why it is grouped with MOTS-c and the small humanin-like peptides (SHLP1-6) as a mitochondria-to-body signaling molecule, not a classic drug. PMID 11371646; PMID 38160808

  2. Directly muzzles the pro-apoptotic protein BAX inside the cell Evidence-rodent

    Intracellularly, humanin binds pro-apoptotic BCL-2-family proteins — BAX, tBid, and Bim — and prevents BAX from translocating into the mitochondrial membrane, where it would otherwise punch holes, release cytochrome c, and trigger the caspase 'self-destruct' cascade. In stressed rat testis in vivo, giving humanin reduced BAX in the mitochondrial fraction and cut germ-cell apoptosis, with endogenous humanin physically co-precipitating with BAX in the cytoplasm. This BAX-sequestration is the core of its 'cytoprotective rescue factor' identity. PMID 23686888; PMID 35372353

  3. Secreted humanin acts on cell-surface receptors to switch on survival signaling Evidence-in vitro

    When released from cells, humanin behaves like a hormone. It is an agonist at the G-protein-coupled formyl-peptide receptors FPRL1/FPR2 (and FPRL2), which suppresses the ASK1/JNK and p38-MAPK death pathways. It also engages a three-part receptor — CNTFR-alpha paired with gp130 and WSX-1 — to activate JAK2/STAT3 and PI3K/AKT. The net effect on the receiving cell is 'stay alive, resist stress.' Most receptor mapping is from cell-culture and biochemical work. PMID 35372353; PMID 22010475

  4. Protects neurons from amyloid-beta and Alzheimer's-linked toxicity Evidence-rodent

    In the founding experiments, humanin abolished neuronal death caused by a wide range of familial Alzheimer's disease genes and by amyloid-beta, while having no effect on unrelated forms of cell death (e.g. from polyglutamine or SOD1 mutants) — showing the protection is specific, not generic. That founding work was in cultured neuronal cells; the neuroprotection has since been reproduced across many cell and rodent models but has never been shown to change the course of Alzheimer's disease in a human trial. PMID 11371646; PMID 22010475

  5. Acts on the brain to improve whole-body insulin sensitivity Evidence-rodent

    Infusing humanin into the brain ventricles of rats improved overall insulin sensitivity, and this depended on hypothalamic STAT3 signaling — blocking STAT3 in the brain abolished even the liver effects, implying the action is centrally coordinated rather than a direct effect on hepatocytes. Potent analogs given intravenously reproduced the insulin-sensitizing effect and lowered blood glucose in diabetic (Zucker) rats. A humanin analog (HNGF6A) also directly boosted glucose-stimulated insulin secretion from pancreatic beta cells. PMID 19623253; PMID 23995290

  6. Reduces oxidative stress and protects blood-vessel lining cells Evidence-rodent

    Humanin is naturally expressed in the endothelial (inner) layer of human arteries and veins. In human aortic endothelial cells it cut oxidized-LDL-induced reactive oxygen species and apoptosis by about half. In ApoE-deficient mice on a high-cholesterol diet, a humanin analog preserved endothelial function and reduced atherosclerotic plaque size over 16 weeks — without lowering cholesterol — pointing to an antioxidant/anti-apoptotic vascular mechanism rather than a lipid effect. PMID 20562421; PMID 21763658

  7. Circulating levels track aging — and a rare variant tracks human longevity Evidence-human

    Endogenous humanin is detectable in human blood and tissue. In rodents, tissue and circulating humanin fall with age. In humans the picture is more nuanced: in healthy people plasma humanin RISES with age (interpreted as an adaptive mitochondrial-stress response) but is lower in Alzheimer's disease. A rare humanin variant (P3S), enriched in APOE4-carrying centenarians of Ashkenazi descent, binds APOE4 and reduces amyloid pathology in an APOE4 mouse model — the strongest human-genetic link between humanin and exceptional longevity. Higher humanin is associated with, not proven to cause, longevity. PMID 33131010; PMID 38520065

  8. Cytoprotection is a double-edged sword: it can shield the wrong cells Evidence-rodent

    The same 'keep cells alive under stress' program is not uniformly beneficial. Humanin (with MOTS-c) has been reported in vitro to exacerbate the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) — increasing inflammatory cytokines like IL-6, IL-1-beta and TNF-alpha from senescent cells. Separately, in glioblastoma cell and orthotopic mouse models, humanin activates an ITGAV-TGF-beta axis that promotes invasion and tumor progression. So in contexts with senescent or malignant cells, protection can be counterproductive. This is a key reason a naive 'more humanin = anti-aging' framing is not supported. PMID 30058454; PMID 38942749

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Two-compartment mechanism. Intracellular: humanin binds pro-apoptotic BCL-2-family proteins BAX, tBid and Bim, preventing BAX mitochondrial translocation/pore formation, and interacts with IGFBP3; the tripartite-motif protein TRIM11 binds and destabilizes humanin. Extracellular/secreted: agonist at G-protein-coupled formyl-peptide receptors FPRL1/FPR2 and FPRL2/FPR3, and at a trimeric cytokine receptor composed of CNTFR-alpha + gp130 + WSX-1. The secreted protein VSTM2L is an antagonist that binds humanin and reduces its availability. Homo-dimerization/oligomerization appears important for activity. (Note: the compound is a peptide, so classic ChEMBL small-molecule mechanism/ADMET records do not apply.)

Signalling cascade. FPRL1/FPR2 agonism suppresses the ASK1->JNK and p38-MAPK stress/death cascades; CNTFR-alpha/gp130/WSX-1 engagement activates JAK2->STAT3 and PI3K->AKT pro-survival signaling. Downstream: BAX is kept out of mitochondria, cytochrome c release and caspase-3/9 activation are prevented, reactive oxygen species fall, and (in brain) hypothalamic STAT3 signaling improves peripheral insulin sensitivity. Autoinduction has been reported — humanin treatment can upregulate mitochondrial biogenesis and its own expression via PI3K/AKT.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Experimental only: intracerebroventricular, intraperitoneal, subcutaneous, intravenous, intratesticular, and intranasal (rodents). No approved human route.
Absorption / bioavailability
As a 24-aa peptide, native humanin has poor oral bioavailability and is expected to be degraded in the GI tract; no validated human oral or injectable product exists. Intranasal delivery reached rodent brain mainly via the trigeminal pathway (PMID 37351170).
Half-life
Native humanin is reported to have a short circulating half-life (minutes-range typical for unmodified peptides); precise validated human PK half-life is not established. Engineered analogs were designed for greater stability.
Metabolism
Presumed proteolytic degradation by peptidases (typical for small peptides); formal metabolism studies are lacking. Position-14 substitution (S14G, 'HNG') and other analog modifications were engineered specifically to increase potency and metabolic stability.
Excretion
Not formally characterized in humans; small peptides/fragments are expected to undergo renal handling and proteolytic clearance.
Notes
Because native peptide is short-lived and orally inactive, essentially all in-vivo efficacy data use stabilized analogs (S14G-humanin/HNG, HNGF6A) at pharmacologic doses — a major caveat for translating any result to 'humanin' the endogenous molecule.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

No human dose-response data exist. In rodents, effects are dose-dependent: HNGF6A enhanced glucose-stimulated insulin secretion dose-dependently and in a K-ATP-channel-independent manner (PMID 23995290); central humanin infusion improved insulin sensitivity with effects negated by co-inhibition of hypothalamic STAT3 (PMID 19623253); chronic HNGF6A (16 weeks) reduced atherosclerotic plaque in mice (PMID 21763658). Potency ranking of analogs (S14G-HNG and HNGF6A much greater than native humanin) reflects engineered stability/receptor affinity, not a human therapeutic window. There is NO established human dose, and none should be inferred — describing a protocol here would be inappropriate given the absence of human safety data.

How it compares

  • vs MOTS-c (companion 16-aa MDP, MT-RNR1/12S rRNA)
    same: Both mitochondrial-derived peptides, both improve insulin sensitivity in rodents, both promoted for longevity 'stacks,' both circulate as mitochondrial-stress signals.
    different: MOTS-c is a metabolic/exercise-mimetic peptide acting largely via AMPK activation and nuclear translocation to reprogram gene expression; humanin is primarily a cytoprotective/anti-apoptotic peptide acting via BAX sequestration and FPRL1/CNTFR-STAT3 survival signaling. MOTS-c is exercise-inducible and skeletal-muscle-centric; humanin is neuron/endothelium/beta-cell-centric. Both share the SASP-exacerbation caveat.
  • vs SHLP2 / SHLP1-6 (small humanin-like peptides, same MT-RNR2 region)
    same: Encoded near humanin in the 16S rRNA region; cytoprotective, reduce ROS/apoptosis, improve mitochondrial metabolism; SHLP2 is also an insulin sensitizer and declines with age.
    different: SHLPs are distinct peptides with partly different receptor/potency profiles (e.g. SHLP2/SHLP3 are protective while SHLP6 can be pro-apoptotic); humanin remains the best-characterized and the only one with a defined human longevity-variant (P3S) and the richest neuroprotection literature.
  • vs GDF15 / FGF21 (nuclear-encoded 'mitokines')
    same: All are stress-responsive circulating signals of mitochondrial/metabolic state used as aging biomarkers; plasma levels rise with age in healthy people.
    different: GDF15 and FGF21 are nuclear-genome-encoded secreted proteins with defined receptors (GFRAL; FGFR/beta-Klotho) and advanced drug-development programs; humanin is mitochondrial-genome-encoded, dual intracellular/extracellular, and has NO clinical development program. In the same cohorts humanin was lower in AD whereas GDF15 was elevated in T2D — different disease signatures.

History &amp; legal status

Humanin was discovered in 2001 by Nishimoto's group (Hashimoto et al., PNAS) via functional screening of a cDNA library from the occipital lobe of an Alzheimer's patient — a surviving neuroprotective clone was named 'Humanin.' It became the founding member of the mitochondrial-derived peptide (MDP) class; MOTS-c (2015) and the SHLPs (Cobb et al. 2016) followed. Much of the metabolic and aging biology came from the Cohen/Barzilai/Muzumdar groups (USC/Einstein). Regulatory status: humanin has NEVER been an approved drug in any jurisdiction, has no completed interventional efficacy trial, and is not a recognized dietary supplement. Registered human studies are observational biomarker studies only (e.g. NCT06105229 acute kidney injury; NCT03431844 cardiac surgery). It circulates in the 'research peptide'/longevity-biohacking market as an unapproved, unregulated injectable with no human efficacy or safety dossier.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether administering exogenous humanin (or analogs) produces ANY clinical benefit in humans — no interventional trial has ever tested it.
  • Whether the age-associated changes in circulating humanin are cause, consequence, or bystander of aging/disease (association only).
  • Human pharmacokinetics: real half-life, bioavailability by any route, tissue distribution, and metabolism of native peptide vs analogs.
  • The net effect of chronic cytoprotection — does shielding stressed cells help healthspan, or does it preserve senescent/pre-malignant cells (SASP and glioblastoma signals)?
  • Receptor stoichiometry and tissue-specific receptor usage (FPRL1/2 vs CNTFR-alpha/gp130/WSX-1) that determine which effect dominates where.
  • Safety of unregulated injectable products (purity, endotoxin, immunogenicity of a peptide given at supraphysiologic doses).
  • Whether the P3S longevity association reflects the peptide's action or linkage to other protective mitochondrial-genome features.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Framed as harm-reduction for someone who has encountered or is using an unregulated humanin product — NOT an endorsement or protocol, and deliberately containing no doses, routes, frequencies, or titration guidance. Because no human safety data exist, the priorities are: (1) recognize there is no evidence base — a clinician cannot dose or titrate this safely, and 'longevity peptide' marketing outruns the science. (2) Given rodent insulin/glucose effects, anyone with diabetes or on glucose-lowering therapy should be alert to hypoglycemia symptoms and monitor blood glucose with their clinician; there is a theoretical additive effect with insulin/sulfonylureas/GLP-1 agents. (3) The theoretical cancer-safety concern (anti-apoptotic biology plus glioblastoma tumor-promotion and SASP signals) means a personal or family history of cancer is a strong reason for caution and physician discussion. (4) Injectable research peptides carry infection, injection-site, and immunogenic/allergic risks and possible endotoxin contamination — watch for fever, injection-site reactions, or systemic allergic signs. (5) Product-quality due diligence (third-party purity/sterility testing) matters more than any 'dose,' because contamination is the dominant real-world hazard. Anyone considering or using it should do so under medical supervision and disclose it to their physician, especially before surgery or if on other medications.

Caveats

Read the evidence tier before believing any benefit. (1) Almost everything is rodent or in-vitro — the anti-apoptotic, neuroprotective, insulin-sensitizing, and vascular effects come from cell lines and mice, not controlled human trials; human data are limited to observational biomarker associations. (2) The 'higher humanin = longevity' story is an association, not proven causation, and is nuanced: in healthy people plasma humanin actually RISES with age (an adaptive stress response), while it is lower in Alzheimer's; the strongest human-longevity link is a rare genetic variant (P3S) in APOE4 centenarians of Ashkenazi descent, not injected peptide. (3) Cytoprotection can backfire — the same anti-death signaling has been reported in vitro to worsen the senescence-associated inflammatory secretome (SASP) and, in glioblastoma cell/mouse models, to promote tumor progression via an ITGAV-TGF-beta axis, so a blanket 'anti-aging/anti-cancer' framing is wrong and there is a theoretical cancer-safety concern. (4) Native humanin has a short circulating half-life and poor oral bioavailability; almost all rodent efficacy used engineered, more-stable analogs (S14G-HNG, HNGF6A) at supraphysiologic doses — results do not transfer cleanly to native peptide or to humans. (5) No human PK, dosing, drug-interaction, or long-term safety data exist for exogenous administration; injectable 'humanin' products are unregulated. This monograph is mechanism education, NOT medical advice or a protocol.
