PeakATP

A patented oral ATP salt sold for "pump," strength and recovery — with a genuine multi-trial human record, but built on a paradox: swallowed ATP is barely absorbed intact, so any benefit must work through indirect purinergic/blood-flow signaling, not by refilling your muscles' ATP.

Human / approved 13 citations
Class
Exogenous purinergic nucleotide / ergogenic dietary supplement (branded, patented ingredient; not a drug)
Primary target
Extracellular ATP acting as a purinergic (P2, notably endothelial P2Y2) signal to promote vasodilation and skeletal-muscle blood flow — plus proposed maintenance of intramuscular adenine-nucleotide pools during exercise
Human status
Widely sold, patented (Peak ATP) dietary-supplement ingredient, typically 400 mg/day, featured in pre-workout and strength formulas. Not a drug and not FDA-approved for any indication; marketed under DSHEA. Human evidence exists and is unusual for a supplement in that it includes multiple placebo-controlled RCTs, an acute dose-response study, an independent bioavailability study, and a 2024 independent meta-analysis — but most positive mechanistic and chronic-outcome trials come from a small manufacturer-affiliated author group (Jäger, Purpura, Rathmacher, Wilson; TSI/Increnovo/Metabolic Technologies), the key blood-flow/artery-dilation human readout is from an uncontrolled pilot, studied populations are almost entirely young resistance-trained men, and the only independently pooled benefit (maximal strength) is real but modest. The core mechanistic paradox — oral ATP is not bioavailable as intact ATP — is acknowledged even in the manufacturer's own papers, so the ingredient is best described as an emerging ergogenic with a plausible-but-unproven indirect (purinergic/blood-flow) mechanism, not a proven ATP-delivery system.

At a glance

  • Increased maximal strength (1RM) in resistance-trained men — the most reproducible effect; independent meta-analysis +8.13 kg vs placebo (PMID 38535745)
  • Greater single-session lifting volume / reps in some acute studies at 400 mg (PMID 29045315; PMID 34957398, industry-affiliated)
  • Blunted decline in Wingate peak power and muscle excitability across repeated sprints in one industry RCT (PMID 28080323)
  • Reported increase in post-exercise blood flow / brachial-artery dilation with chronic dosing — but from an UNCONTROLLED human pilot plus rat data (PMID 25006331)
  • Attenuated performance loss and lower protein-breakdown markers during a high-volume overreaching block (PMID 24330670, industry-affiliated)
  • No reliable benefit to maximum repetitions or peak anaerobic power on meta-analysis (PMID 38535745)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. You swallow ATP, but almost none reaches the blood intact Established

    Oral ATP (400 mg disodium salt) does NOT raise circulating (plasma or whole-blood) ATP. Multiple independent human studies measured blood ATP after dosing and found no increase — including an independent trial using doses up to 5000 mg enteric-coated and an early independent trial at 150-225 mg. ATP is degraded in the gut; a rise in uric acid (its end catabolite) shows the molecule is broken down and its purine backbone absorbed, not that ATP itself enters the circulation. This is the central paradox of the ingredient: whatever it does, it is NOT by delivering ATP into your bloodstream — a point conceded even in the manufacturer's own papers. PMID 22510240; PMID 15179168; PMID 28080323

  2. Proposed trigger: ATP (or breakdown products) in the gut/local tissue act as a purinergic signal Contested

    The manufacturer's mechanistic model is that ATP released in the small intestine, or ATP/adenosine appearing locally, acts as a signaling molecule on purinergic receptors rather than as fuel. Extracellular ATP is a well-established signaling ligand at P2 receptors. This step is plausible physiology, but the exact species that reaches receptors after an oral dose in humans has never been directly demonstrated — it is inferred from downstream blood-flow changes, not measured. PMID 25006331; PMID 22510240

  3. Extracellular ATP binds endothelial P2Y2 receptors -> nitric oxide and prostacyclin -> vasodilation Established

    This is solid vascular biology, independent of the supplement: ATP in the vessel/muscle interstitium binds P2Y (especially P2Y2) receptors on endothelial cells, triggering nitric oxide (NO) and prostacyclin release, which relax vascular smooth muscle and widen arteries. Applying ATP directly to skeletal muscle at exercise-like interstitial concentrations raises blood flow by up to ~400%, an effect abolished when NO-synthase and prostanoid production are blocked. This is the mechanistic engine PeakATP is claimed to tap into — but note it is proven with locally applied/infused ATP, which reaches receptors intact, unlike an oral dose. PMID 23761642; PMID 18339690

  4. In the body, red blood cells are a major natural source of this ATP signal (the physiological analogy) Established

    Normally, a strong driver of exercise blood flow via this pathway is the red blood cell itself: as hemoglobin offloads oxygen in working muscle, RBCs release ATP (and S-nitrosohemoglobin), matching blood delivery to oxygen demand. PeakATP's rationale borrows this endogenous 'ATP-as-oxygen-signal' logic — but the physiological signal is generated inside the circulation by RBCs, which is exactly what an oral, non-absorbed dose cannot straightforwardly replicate. PMID 16439430

  5. Chronic oral ATP is REPORTED to increase post-exercise blood flow and artery dilation — but the human data are uncontrolled Contested

    Despite the bioavailability problem, a manufacturer-affiliated report found that 12 weeks of 400 mg/day increased ultrasound-measured brachial-artery blood flow and vessel dilation after exercise in resistance-trained men. Crucially, the paper explicitly describes this human arm as a PILOT with NO placebo group, alongside a rat leg-blood-flow dose-response. This is the readout used to link the ingredient to the vasodilation pathway, but an uncontrolled human pilot cannot establish causation, and it comes from an industry-affiliated group with no independent replication. PMID 25006331

  6. Oral ATP blunts the exercise-induced fall in muscle adenine nucleotides (single industry RCT) Evidence-human

    In a manufacturer-affiliated RCT (n=42, 2 weeks of 400 mg/day), oral ATP prevented the normal post-sprint decline in ATP, ADP and AMP and preserved muscle excitability (a power-to-activation ratio) in later sprint bouts, alongside higher Wingate peak power in those later bouts. The proposed mechanism is better substrate salvage / reduced net nucleotide loss rather than direct ATP delivery. This is a single-study, industry-affiliated human finding awaiting independent replication. PMID 28080323

  7. Net downstream effect: modestly greater maximal strength in trained lifters (narrow, replicated only for one endpoint) Evidence-human

    The proposed chain — better blood flow + preserved nucleotide pools -> more work per set, less performance decay during overreaching -> greater strength adaptation over weeks. A 12-week industry RCT reported larger gains in total strength, vertical-jump power and muscle thickness. A 2024 independent meta-analysis of 5 RCTs (121 trained men) confirmed a real max-strength benefit (+8.13 kg, 95% CI 3.36-12.90) but found NO effect on maximum reps or peak anaerobic power — so the pooled, independently-verified effect is real but narrow. PMID 24330670; PMID 38535745

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. The intended pharmacophore is extracellular ATP acting at ionotropic P2X and metabotropic P2Y purinergic receptors, with the vasodilatory ergogenic rationale centered on endothelial P2Y2 receptors. Activation couples through Gq/PLC and downstream endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) and cyclooxygenase pathways.

Signalling cascade. Extracellular ATP -> endothelial P2Y2 (and other P2X/P2Y subtypes) -> increased intracellular Ca2+ and eNOS/COX activation -> nitric oxide + prostacyclin (PGI2) -> vascular smooth-muscle relaxation -> vasodilation and increased skeletal-muscle blood flow. Direct application of exercise-relevant interstitial ATP concentrations to skeletal muscle raises flow by up to ~400%, an effect abolished by combined NO-synthase and prostanoid inhibition, confirming NO/prostacyclin as the effectors. ATP infusion also attenuates alpha-adrenergic vasoconstriction (functional sympatholysis). The unresolved issue is upstream: because oral ATP is not absorbed intact, it is unclear what ligand actually reaches these receptors after a swallowed dose.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (disodium salt; various trials used uncoated or enteric-coated forms).
Absorption / bioavailability
Effectively zero for intact ATP. Human studies show no rise in plasma or whole-blood ATP after oral dosing (150-225 mg, 400 mg, and even 5000 mg enteric-coated). A robust ~50% rise in plasma uric acid after PROXIMAL small-intestine release (not seen with distal release) indicates ATP is broken down and its purine backbone absorbed and catabolized, not that ATP itself enters circulation.
Half-life
Intact extracellular ATP has a plasma half-life on the order of seconds due to ecto-nucleotidase clearance; no meaningful systemic ATP exposure is achieved orally, so a conventional half-life is not applicable to the parent molecule.
Metabolism
Rapid luminal/mucosal dephosphorylation by ecto-nucleotidases (ATP -> ADP -> AMP -> adenosine -> inosine -> hypoxanthine -> xanthine -> uric acid). Circulating ATP is also cleared within seconds by vascular ecto-ATPases.
Excretion
Purine end-products (chiefly uric acid) excreted renally.
Notes
The manufacturer hypothesizes chronic dosing may improve local/indirect signaling or nucleotide salvage over time, but a pharmacokinetic pathway delivering intact ATP systemically has not been demonstrated. This bioavailability gap is the single most important pharmacologic fact about the ingredient.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

400 mg/day is the studied and marketed dose and the empirically identified minimal effective single dose. A crossover dose-finding study (100 vs 200 vs 400 mg) found only 400 mg significantly increased set-1 repetitions; 200 mg gave a non-significant numeric trend; 100 mg gave no performance benefit (though 100 mg did lower perceived exertion). Note this dose-finding study is manufacturer-affiliated. A 2024 independent meta-analysis concluded 400 mg improves maximal strength regardless of whether dosing was acute or chronic. Rodent blood-flow work showed a dose-response with the strongest recovery blood-flow effects at higher human-equivalent doses (~1000-1600 mg HED). No human efficacy or safety rationale supports exceeding 400 mg; higher doses are not established as more effective in humans.

How it compares

  • vs L-citrulline / citrulline malate
    same: Both marketed for 'pump' and vasodilation-mediated performance; both act ultimately via NO-dependent blood flow
    different: Citrulline reliably raises plasma arginine and is orally bioavailable, feeding the NO pathway directly; oral ATP is NOT bioavailable as ATP, so its link to NO/vasodilation is indirect and mechanistically less certain. Both have modest, somewhat inconsistent human ergogenic data.
  • vs Dietary nitrate / beetroot
    same: Shared downstream endpoint (NO-mediated vasodilation, blood flow, performance)
    different: Nitrate->nitrite->NO is a well-characterized, dose-responsive, orally bioavailable pathway with strong endurance data; ATP's upstream delivery step is the weak link. Nitrate's evidence is broader across endurance; ATP's clearest signal is maximal strength.
  • vs Creatine monohydrate
    same: Both are ergogenic and involve the ATP/phosphagen energy system conceptually
    different: Creatine is orally bioavailable, loads intramuscular phosphocreatine, and has large, independent, reproducible strength/power evidence; oral ATP does neither (does not raise muscle ATP) and has a far smaller, more industry-weighted evidence base. Creatine is the benchmark; ATP is emerging and weaker.
  • vs Intravenous/intra-arterial ATP (research infusions)
    same: Same purinergic vasodilation biology is the intended target
    different: Infused ATP produces large, immediate, dose-dependent hyperemia and sympatholysis because it reaches receptors intact; oral dosing cannot replicate this delivery, which is why infusion studies prove the mechanism but do not validate the oral product.

History & legal status

Oral ATP as an ergogenic aid was studied from the early 2000s (Jordan/Cooper Institute 2004, largely null). The branded ingredient 'Peak ATP' (disodium ATP; TSI Group/Increnovo/Metabolic Technologies) was developed and studied through a cluster of trials from ~2012-2021 led by an affiliated author group (Wilson, Jäger, Purpura, Rathmacher), including the pivotal 12-week RCT (NCT01508338, a 4-arm HMB/ATP study) and an acute dose-response study. It is sold as a dietary-supplement ingredient under DSHEA (not an FDA-approved drug and not reviewed for efficacy by FDA); safety support includes a 90-day rodent toxicology study (2020). It is not a WADA-banned substance. A 2024 independent meta-analysis provided the first pooled, non-industry synthesis, affirming a maximal-strength benefit while narrowing the claims.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • What molecular species actually reaches purinergic receptors after an oral dose, given intact ATP is not systemically bioavailable — the mechanism is essentially inferred, not directly traced.
  • Whether the manufacturer-affiliated blood-flow (uncontrolled pilot), nucleotide-preservation and hypertrophy findings replicate in fully independent, placebo-controlled labs.
  • Effects in women, older adults, endurance athletes, and untrained individuals — nearly all data are in young trained men.
  • Long-term (beyond 12 weeks) human safety, including chronic uric-acid effects in susceptible people.
  • Whether the modest maximal-strength benefit translates to meaningful real-world training or body-composition outcomes beyond study conditions.
  • Whether any benefit is specific to ATP signaling or a nonspecific purine/uric-acid effect (uric acid itself has been hypothesized to be ergogenic).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Educational, not a protocol. Reasonable things to watch and discuss with a clinician: (1) Serum uric acid — because oral ATP demonstrably raises it; especially relevant for anyone with gout, prior hyperuricemia, or kidney stones. (2) Symptoms of a gout flare (joint pain/swelling). (3) Blood pressure/lightheadedness if combined with other vasodilators or NO boosters (citrulline, nitrate, PDE5 inhibitors), given the theoretical additive vasodilation. (4) Renal function in anyone with kidney disease, since purine load is renally cleared. (5) General practice: choose third-party-tested product (supplements are lightly regulated), disclose use to any prescriber, and stop and seek care if unexpected symptoms occur. No dosing or 'how to run it' guidance is provided here.

Caveats

CRITICAL — this is not medical or dosing advice. Key honesty points: (1) The name is misleading — swallowing ATP does NOT raise your blood or muscle ATP and does not 'give cells energy'; any effect must be indirect signaling (PMID 22510240; PMID 15179168; PMID 28080323). (2) Evidence bias: the mechanistic (blood-flow, nucleotide-preservation) and chronic-hypertrophy findings come mainly from one manufacturer-affiliated research group and single small studies, and the human artery-dilation result specifically comes from an uncontrolled pilot (no placebo arm) (PMID 25006331); independent replication is limited. (3) The one independently meta-analyzed, reproducible effect is a modest gain in maximal strength — with NO benefit shown for endurance reps or peak power (PMID 38535745). (4) Nearly all data are in young, trained men; effects in women, older adults, endurance athletes and untrained people are essentially untested. (5) Long-term human safety data beyond ~12 weeks are lacking; reassurance rests largely on rodent toxicology and short trials. (6) Because uric acid rises after dosing, people with gout, hyperuricemia or kidney concerns should be cautious and consult a clinician.
