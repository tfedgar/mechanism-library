Cortexin

A Russian-made animal-brain peptide extract marketed for stroke and cognition — a \"Cerebrolysin alternative\" whose lab mechanisms are real, whose human trials are almost all small, manufacturer-linked Russian RCTs (some double-blind), and whose most rigorous blinded rodent test found it no better than saline.

Human status
Approved and marketed as a prescription drug in Russia and several post-Soviet/CIS and a few other countries (manufacturer Geropharm), where it is used for ischemic stroke, TBI, encephalopathies, chronic cerebrovascular disease, cognitive impairment, epilepsy (adjunct) and pediatric neurology. It is NOT approved by the FDA or EMA and has no place in Western stroke or cognition guidelines. Contrary to a common overstatement, double-blind placebo-controlled RCTs of Cortexin DO exist — e.g., a 62-patient multicenter double-blind placebo-controlled acute-stroke trial (PMID 19431244) and a 272-patient multicenter double-blind placebo-controlled trial (PMID 24874316) — plus a 490-patient double-blind IV-vs-IM equivalence trial in which both arms received Cortexin (PMID 41524350), and open-label/observational programs (e.g., 90-patient PMID 27845314; 309-patient PMID 30874524). However, these are small-to-modest, single-country (Russian), manufacturer-linked, and not independently replicated in the West; there are NO registered ClinicalTrials.gov trials (confirmed: 0 records). Critically, the 2023 Cochrane review of Cerebrolysin-and-Cerebrolysin-like agents incorporated the 272-patient Cortexin RCT and concluded there is probably no benefit on all-cause death and a possible increase in non-fatal serious adverse events for the class (PMID 37818733). The most rigorous independent preclinical experiment — a blinded, placebo-controlled rodent embolic-stroke comparison — found Cortexin no better than saline (PMID 30665068). In the fitness/nootropic/longevity community it is used off-label and unstudied as an injectable, cheaper 'Cerebrolysin alternative.'

At a glance

  • Marketed for ischemic stroke (acute + recovery), traumatic brain injury, chronic cerebrovascular disease / 'encephalopathy', and cognitive impairment; in Russia also widely used in pediatric neurology (perinatal CNS injury, epilepsy adjunct).
  • Mechanistically (mostly rodent/in vitro): anti-excitotoxic and anti-apoptotic (caspase-8 inhibition), antioxidant (restores SOD/catalase/glutathione), and glutamate/GABA-receptor modulation.
  • Human evidence exists but is low-quality and conflicted: there ARE double-blind, placebo-controlled Russian RCTs (e.g., 62-patient PMID 19431244; 272-patient PMID 24874316) reporting faster deficit regression — but they are small, single-country, manufacturer-linked and not independently replicated in the West.
  • The Cochrane review that pooled Cortexin's own RCT data with Cerebrolysin found no benefit on all-cause death and a possible increase in non-fatal serious adverse events — so 'neuroprotection in humans' is not established for this class.
  • Practical community appeal: an injectable, cheaper, easier-to-source 'Cerebrolysin alternative' for nootropic/longevity use — but this is off-label, non-approved outside a few countries, and unstudied for healthy-person cognitive enhancement.

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It is a crude peptide soup, not a designed molecule Established

    Cortexin is a lyophilized water-soluble extract of cattle/pig cerebral cortex containing a mix of low-molecular-weight (<10 kDa) neuropeptides, amino acids and trace elements (possibly with chromatin/DNA fragments). There is no single active ingredient and no fixed, sequenced structure — the 'drug' is a standardized-by-process biological fraction, which is why its mechanism is described as pleiotropic and why batch composition is a genuine question. PMID 26356623; PMID 30499504

  2. A fraction reaches the brain Evidence-rodent

    Radiolabeled Cortexin injected into mice crossed the blood-brain barrier, reaching brain concentrations equal to about 6-8% of blood levels. So some peptide material does get into the CNS after peripheral injection, rather than acting only in the periphery. Uptake was measured in mice, not humans. PMID 34260655

  3. Binds glutamate and GABA receptors in a test tube Evidence-in vitro

    In an in vitro receptor-binding panel, Cortexin (10 ug/mL) showed high-to-moderate binding to AMPA receptors (~80%), kainate receptors (~74%), mGluR1 (~49%), GABA-A1 (~44%) and mGluR5 (~40%). This is the concrete basis for calling it a glutamatergic/GABAergic modulator — but it is displacement in a binding assay, not proven functional agonism/antagonism or a proven mechanism in a living brain. PMID 34260655

  4. Blocks an apoptosis trigger (caspase-8) Evidence-in vitro

    Cortexin selectively inhibited brain caspase-8 (an initiator of programmed cell death) while barely affecting caspase-1, -3, -9, cathepsin B or calpain. A purified peptide sub-fraction kept this activity, and both the whole drug and the fraction prevented glutamate-induced neuron death in culture. This gives a plausible anti-apoptotic, anti-excitotoxic mechanism at the molecular/cellular level. PMID 28251948

  5. Props up the brain's antioxidant defenses Evidence-rodent

    In rats with chronic brain ischemia, Cortexin restored superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase and reduced glutathione and lowered malondialdehyde (a lipid-peroxidation marker). In a rat ischemia-reperfusion model it also lowered total oxidant status, raised total antioxidant status, and reduced ischemia-driven OPG/RANK/RANKL and TRPC1 (calcium-signalling) expression. Consistent antioxidant/anti-inflammatory signal — all in rodents. PMID 34460162; PMID 40783844

  6. Reduces tissue damage and cognitive deficits in rodent models Evidence-rodent

    In rat middle-cerebral-artery-occlusion (acute) and carotid-stenosis (chronic) models, Cortexin improved neurological/motor recovery, reduced necrosis size, and improved Morris-water-maze memory, with effects comparable to Cerebrolysin and better than Actovegin, plus less hippocampal (CA1/CA4) damage. This is the animal efficacy story the marketing leans on — but note these are from a single manufacturer-affiliated group. PMID 34260655; PMID 32929929

  7. The neurotrophic (BDNF/NGF) step is inferred, not shown for Cortexin Speculative

    Cortexin is grouped with Cerebrolysin as a 'neurotrophic' brain hydrolysate, and reviews propose BDNF/NGF-like signalling. But the direct BDNF/NGF/TrkB induction data belong to related peptides (Semax, Cerebrolysin) — not to Cortexin itself. For Cortexin specifically the neurotrophic-factor mechanism is an extrapolation, and a comparative analysis noted Cerebrolysin is better standardized and richer in identifiable active peptide fragments. PMID 30499504; PMID 31793555

  8. When tested blinded against saline in rodents, it did not clearly work Evidence-rodent

    In a prospective, randomized, blinded, placebo-controlled US rat embolic-stroke study, Cortexin produced functional recovery and infarct volumes no different from saline; only Cerebrolysin significantly beat placebo. This is the most important preclinical reality check: the mechanistic steps above are real in a dish, but they did not translate into a benefit over placebo in the most rigorous, independent head-to-head animal experiment. PMID 30665068

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No single molecular target — a mixture of low-MW (<10 kDa) neuropeptides, free amino acids and trace elements (and possibly small chromatin/DNA fragments) extracted from cattle and pig cerebral cortex. In an in vitro receptor panel, Cortexin (10 ug/mL) showed high/moderate displacement at AMPA (80.1%), kainate (73.5%), mGluR1 (49.0%), GABA-A1 (44.0%) and mGluR5 (39.7%) receptors, implying glutamatergic + GABAergic modulation. It also selectively inhibits brain caspase-8 (with a purified peptide sub-fraction retaining the activity), while sparing caspase-1/-3/-9, cathepsin B and calpain. Proposed intracellular partners include beta5-tubulin, creatine kinase B and 14-3-3 alpha/beta.

Signalling cascade. Working model (mostly rodent/in vitro): CNS-penetrant peptides simultaneously (a) dampen glutamate excitotoxicity via glutamate/GABA receptor modulation, (b) block the caspase-8 apoptosis initiator to promote neuron survival, and (c) restore antioxidant enzymes (SOD, catalase, glutathione) and reduce lipid peroxidation and ischemia-driven OPG/RANK/RANKL and TRPC1 (Ca2+ signalling) — converging on reduced infarct size, preserved hippocampus, and better motor/memory recovery. A BDNF/NGF-like neurotrophic/plasticity arm is claimed but inferred from related peptides, not shown for Cortexin itself.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Intramuscular injection (reconstituted lyophilized powder); an intravenous form has also been trialed (PMID 41524350). No oral or nasal form with established bioavailability.
Absorption / bioavailability
Not characterized as a classic single-analyte PK. As a peptide mixture it is presumed subject to peptidase degradation; formal human bioavailability data are lacking.
Half-life
Not established in humans for the mixture.
Metabolism
Presumed proteolytic breakdown to constituent amino acids/small peptides; no defined CYP metabolism (it is not a small-molecule drug).
Excretion
Not characterized; peptide/amino-acid catabolism assumed.
Notes
Key PK datum: radiolabeled Cortexin crossed the blood-brain barrier in mice, reaching ~6-8% of whole-blood concentration in brain (PMID 34260655) — evidence that some material reaches the CNS after peripheral dosing, but this is a rodent tracer study, not human PK.

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

Rodent studies show a dose-dependent pattern: lower doses (~0.3 mg/kg) were often ineffective while ~1-3 mg/kg improved memory, antioxidant status and tissue outcomes in chronic/acute ischemia models (PMID 32929929, PMID 34460162, PMID 34260655), and in an in-vitro sensory-neuron model protection increased with concentration up to 40 ug/mL (PMID 37080184). In humans, one trial suggested repeated/second courses gave more complete deficit regression than a single course (PMID 27845314; also PMID 24874316). No validated human dose-response curve exists, and NO dosing protocol is provided here — this is a prescription biologic that should only be used under a clinician in jurisdictions where it is approved.

How it compares

  • vs Cerebrolysin
    same:
    different: Same organ-hydrolysate class and marketing niche, but Cerebrolysin is porcine-brain-derived, better standardized, and richer in identifiable active peptide fragments; crucially, in the one blinded rodent head-to-head, Cerebrolysin beat placebo while Cortexin did not (PMID 30665068, PMID 31793555). Both nonetheless lack convincing independent Western RCT support — Cochrane finds no clear stroke benefit and a possible non-fatal SAE increase for the class (PMID 37818733).
  • vs Actovegin
    same:
    different: Actovegin is a deproteinized calf-blood extract (not a cortex peptide), acting more on metabolism/oxygen utilization; in direct rodent comparisons Cortexin and Cerebrolysin outperformed Actovegin on recovery and antioxidant endpoints (PMID 34260655, PMID 32929929).
  • vs Semax / Selank (Russian regulatory peptides)
    same:
    different: Semax/Selank are single, defined synthetic peptides with characterized sequences and documented BDNF/NGF/TrkB induction; Cortexin is an undefined multi-component extract whose neurotrophic action is inferred rather than shown (PMID 16996037, PMID 19633950 describe Semax's BDNF effects, not Cortexin's).
  • vs Citicoline (recognan)
    same:
    different: Citicoline is a defined small molecule (phospholipid precursor) with a larger Western evidence base; in Russian practice it is often combined with Cortexin, which confounds attributing benefit to Cortexin (PMID 30874524).

History &amp; legal status

Developed in the Soviet/Russian tradition of organ-specific peptide bioregulators; manufactured by Geropharm (Russia) as a lyophilized cortex-peptide injectable. Approved and clinically used across Russia and several CIS and neighboring countries for stroke, TBI, encephalopathies, chronic cerebrovascular disease, cognitive disorders, epilepsy (adjunct), and pediatric neurology. It has never been approved by the FDA or EMA, appears in no major Western guideline, and has no registered ClinicalTrials.gov trials (confirmed 0 records). Its Western footprint is almost entirely as a grey-market 'Cerebrolysin alternative' in nootropic/longevity circles. The regulatory story of its class is cautionary: the 2023 Cochrane review, which incorporated the 272-patient Cortexin RCT alongside Cerebrolysin trials, found no clear stroke benefit and a possible non-fatal serious-adverse-event signal (PMID 37818733).

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Does Cortexin improve clinical outcomes versus placebo in a LARGE, INDEPENDENT (non-manufacturer-linked), Western double-blind RCT? Small Russian double-blind RCTs exist and report benefit (PMID 19431244, PMID 24874316), but Cochrane's pooled analysis found no mortality/functional benefit and a possible SAE increase — so independent confirmation is lacking.
  • What is the actual, reproducible molecular composition batch-to-batch, and how standardized is it? (Called an undefined mixture even in supportive reviews.)
  • Is the claimed BDNF/NGF/neurotrophic mechanism real for Cortexin specifically, or only borrowed from Cerebrolysin/Semax data?
  • Human pharmacokinetics (does the 6-8% BBB penetration seen in mice hold in humans; half-life; which peptides are active)?
  • Real-world immunogenicity and any prion/TSE risk from bovine CNS sourcing under current manufacturing — essentially uncharacterized in the public literature.
  • Any effect in healthy people for cognitive enhancement (the community use-case) — no evidence at all.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Framed as harm-reduction, not endorsement — and this is a prescription biologic that should only be used under medical supervision where it is legally approved. If a clinician is overseeing use, sensible things to WATCH (not a protocol): (1) injection-site reactions and any signs of systemic hypersensitivity/allergy (rash, swelling, wheeze, anaphylaxis) — animal-derived peptides can be immunogenic, so first exposures deserve caution and access to emergency care; (2) the class-level signal of possible non-fatal serious adverse events seen in Cochrane's pooled RCT analysis — meaning any serious new symptom warrants prompt medical review; (3) neuro-stimulant-type effects such as insomnia, agitation or (in seizure-prone individuals) any change in seizure control; (4) product provenance and sterility — grey-market/self-sourced injectables carry infection, contamination and counterfeit risks independent of the drug itself; (5) avoid in pregnancy, breastfeeding, known peptide/protein allergy, and without a real diagnosis. Above all, recognize the evidence ceiling: mechanistic plausibility in rodents is NOT proof of human benefit, the best independent blinded animal study was negative, and the Cochrane review that included Cortexin's own RCT found no clear benefit — so expectations should be modest and any use discussed with a physician.

Caveats

Certainty is deliberately low. (1) Mechanism vs outcome gap: the anti-apoptotic (caspase-8), antioxidant and receptor-binding data are real but almost all in vitro or in rodents; the one blinded, placebo-controlled, INDEPENDENT rodent stroke study showed NO benefit over saline (PMID 30665068). (2) Human evidence is weak, not absent: double-blind placebo-controlled Russian RCTs exist (PMID 19431244 n=62; PMID 24874316 n=272) and report benefit, but they are small, single-country, from groups with manufacturer ties, prone to publication/design bias, and not independently replicated — and when the 272-patient Cortexin RCT was pooled by Cochrane it showed no benefit on death and a possible rise in non-fatal serious adverse events (PMID 37818733). (3) The BDNF/NGF 'neurotrophic' framing is largely inferred by analogy to Cerebrolysin/Semax, not directly demonstrated for Cortexin (PMID 30499504; PMID 31793555; Semax data PMID 16996037, PMID 19633950). (4) It is an animal-brain-derived biological of undefined exact composition — raising theoretical concerns about batch-to-batch variability, immunogenicity and (for bovine cortex tissue) prion/TSE plausibility, none of which are well characterized in the public literature. (5) There is essentially zero evidence in healthy people for cognitive enhancement; community use is extrapolation. This entry synthesizes published evidence and is not medical advice or a protocol.
