17-alpha-Estradiol

A non-feminizing mirror-image of the main human estrogen that reliably extends lifespan in male mice by mimicking mild calorie restriction — with essentially zero human longevity data.

Rodent / preclinical 17 citations
Class
Non-feminizing estrogen stereoisomer (C17 epimer of 17β-estradiol) / candidate geroprotector; also marketed topically outside the US as alfatradiol, a weak 5α-reductase inhibitor for androgenetic alopecia
Primary target
Estrogen receptor alpha (ERα)
Human status
Not approved or studied as a systemic geroprotector in humans — the entire longevity/metabolic evidence base is in mice (chiefly male UM-HET3 and C57BL/6J), with essentially no controlled human data on lifespan, healthspan, insulin sensitivity, or adiposity. The compound does have a separate human regulatory footprint: TOPICAL 17α-estradiol (alfatradiol, e.g. Ell-Cranell alpha / Pantostin) is approved in Germany and some other European countries as a scalp lotion for androgenetic alopecia, where it acts as a weak local 5α-reductase inhibitor; efficacy there is modest and it is not FDA-approved in the US. A ClinicalTrials.gov search for systemic 17α-estradiol / alfatradiol returns no aging or metabolic-disease trials (only unrelated 17β-estradiol / assisted-reproduction studies). It is sold as a research chemical and appears in longevity-biohacker communities, but self-experimentation is not supported by any human safety or efficacy data, and its most striking property — the male-only benefit that depends on intact male gonadal hormones — has never been tested for translation to humans.

At a glance

  • Robustly extends MALE mouse median lifespan (~12% at original ITP dose; median AND maximal lifespan extended at a 3x-higher dose) — replicated across ITP cohorts; NO lifespan benefit in females
  • Improves insulin sensitivity, glucose tolerance and reduces adiposity in obese/aged male mice (males only)
  • Reduces food intake via hypothalamic POMC neurons (partial calorie-restriction mimic)
  • Lowers age-related hypothalamic/brain neuroinflammation (male-specific, partly gonad-dependent)
  • Preserves skeletal muscle mass and improves late-life physical function (grip strength, rotarod) in males only
  • Reshapes liver and multi-organ lipid handling toward a younger/fat-oxidizing profile (male-biased)
  • 'Non-feminizing' is relative, not absolute: at higher exposures it retains measurable estrogenic/uterotrophic activity (shown in ovariectomized female mice)
  • As topical alfatradiol, weakly inhibits scalp 5α-reductase — the basis of its (modest) use for androgenetic alopecia in some countries
  • Human longevity, metabolic, or healthspan benefit: NOT demonstrated

Mechanism, step by step

  1. A weak, non-classical estrogen enters the brain and liver Established

    17α-estradiol is the C17 stereoisomer (epimer) of ordinary 17β-estradiol. It binds the same receptor, ERα, but with far lower potency and without the strong feminizing/uterus-growing activity of 17β-estradiol. In mice it acts mainly in the hypothalamus, liver, and fat, and its longevity benefit appears only in males and depends on the presence of male gonadal hormones. PMID 24245565; PMID 28834262

  2. Acts on POMC neurons in the hypothalamus to curb appetite Evidence-rodent

    In obese and aged male mice, 17α-E2 works through pro-opiomelanocortin (POMC) neurons in the arcuate nucleus to reduce food intake and body weight. Part — but not all — of the metabolic benefit is driven by this mild, self-imposed calorie reduction; some peripheral improvements persist even when this appetite pathway is disabled. PMID 29168299

  3. Improves insulin sensitivity and glucose tolerance in males only Evidence-rodent

    In male mice, treatment increases insulin sensitivity and glucose tolerance and enhances hepatic mTORC2 signaling, Akt activity, and FOXO1a phosphorylation — a metabolically 'healthy' liver signature. Females show no such benefit. Castration removes most of these male responses and ovariectomy shifts females toward some male-like responses, showing the effect is gated by gonadal hormones. PMID 28834262

  4. Lowers hypothalamic and brain inflammation in a sex-specific way Evidence-rodent

    Aging drives 'gliosis' (activation of microglia and astrocytes) and TNF-α production in the hypothalamus. 17α-E2 blunts this in male mice (reduced reactive microgliosis and astrogliosis) but not females; the effect is brain-region specific and partly reversed by castration. It correlates with higher hypothalamic ERα expression specifically in intact males. PMID 28544365; PMID 34309657

  5. Suppresses NF-κB (p65) driven inflammatory signaling — in vitro only Evidence-in vitro

    In cultured cells, both 17α- and 17β-estradiol reduce LPS-driven TNF-α and IL-6 by acting through ERα and lowering NF-κB p65. This gives a plausible molecular route for the anti-inflammatory effects — but in vivo the story is incomplete: a Miller-lab survey found that, unlike rapamycin, acarbose, canagliflozin and calorie restriction, 17α-E2 did NOT lower liver p65, so the NF-κB mechanism is real in vitro but not confirmed in the whole animal. PMID 28877759; PMID 39666139

  6. Dials down mTORC1 and the MAPK/ERK stress-translation axis Evidence-rodent

    In male mouse liver, 17α-E2 (like rapamycin and acarbose) reduces mTORC1 activity and shifts protein-making from cap-dependent toward cap-independent translation (via lower METTL3). It also opposes age-related activation of the MEK–ERK–MNK cascade in males specifically, while dampening the p38-MAPK inflammatory pathway in both sexes. The male-specific ERK changes match the male-only lifespan effect and overlap with signatures seen in long-lived dwarf and GH-resistant mice. PMID 33742521; PMID 35105357

  7. Remodels lipid handling toward a younger, fat-oxidizing liver Evidence-rodent

    Multi-omic studies show 17α-E2 tightens regulation of fatty-acid oxidation in male liver, alters skeletal-muscle lipid and inflammatory signaling, and shifts tissue lipid profiles across multiple organs back toward those of younger or calorie-restricted mice. This links the drug's metabolic and longevity effects to improved lipid homeostasis rather than to a single receptor event, and the shifts are generally stronger in males. PMID 37481675; PMID 37653270; PMID 37398463; PMID 40129070

  8. Net result in mice: extended male lifespan and healthspan Established

    The downstream payoff in male mice is longer median (and, at higher dose, maximal) lifespan, better late-life grip strength and coordination, preserved muscle mass, and less frailty — a coordinated healthspan benefit replicated across ITP cohorts. None of this is established in females, and none of it has been demonstrated in humans. PMID 24245565; PMID 27312235; PMID 30740872

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. 17α-estradiol is the C17-epimer (stereoisomer) of 17β-estradiol, differing only in the orientation of the C17 hydroxyl. It binds estrogen receptor alpha (ERα) but with markedly lower affinity/potency than 17β-estradiol and comparatively little classical uterotrophic (feminizing) activity — hence 'non-feminizing estrogen.' In vitro it engages ERα to suppress NF-κB p65 and pro-inflammatory cytokines; in vivo its male-specific effects correlate with increased hypothalamic ERα expression in gonadally intact males. Topically (alfatradiol) it additionally acts as a weak inhibitor of 5α-reductase, lowering local dihydrotestosterone in scalp skin.

Signalling cascade. Proposed geroprotective cascade (male mouse): weak ERα engagement + hypothalamic POMC activation → reduced food intake and improved central metabolic tone → hepatic reprogramming (↑mTORC2/Akt/FOXO1a phosphorylation, ↓mTORC1, ↓METTL3 with a shift to cap-independent translation, ↓MEK-ERK-MNK in males and ↓p38-MAPK in both sexes) → enhanced fatty-acid oxidation, altered lipid profiles, and lower age-related neuroinflammation → improved insulin sensitivity, reduced adiposity, preserved muscle, and extended lifespan. The pathway is convergent with rapamycin, acarbose, canagliflozin and long-lived GH/IGF-1 mutant mice.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Preclinical longevity work: dietary/oral admixture in chow (systemic). Human clinical use: topical scalp lotion (alfatradiol). No validated systemic human PK.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally absorbed in mice at the dietary concentrations used by the NIA ITP; systemic human oral bioavailability not formally characterized in the aging context. As a topical, systemic absorption is intended to be low/local.
Half-life
Not reliably established for systemic human use; steroid estrogens generally have short plasma half-lives with metabolite recirculation. Do not rely on any specific published human t1/2 for the α-epimer.
Metabolism
As a natural estradiol epimer, subject to hepatic estrogen metabolism (Phase I/II conjugation, glucuronidation/sulfation); the C17-α configuration reduces affinity for ER and alters metabolic handling relative to 17β-estradiol. Detailed human metabolic mapping is lacking.
Excretion
Presumed renal/biliary elimination of conjugated estrogen metabolites, analogous to other estradiol species; not specifically quantified for the α-epimer in humans.
Notes
Because the compound is a stereoisomer of an endogenous hormone, assays must distinguish 17α- from 17β-estradiol; some historical literature and supplement labeling conflate them. Dose scaling from mouse chow to humans is not established.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

In the NIA ITP, 17α-estradiol showed a clear dose-response: the original dose extended male median lifespan ~12% (P=0.002) with no maximal-lifespan effect (Harrison 2014, PMID 24245565), whereas a 3-fold-higher dose robustly extended both median AND maximal lifespan, still only in males (Strong 2016, PMID 27312235). In the first study the benefit was notably stronger at one of three ITP sites, but the male-specific effect was reproduced and strengthened at higher dose. The dose-dependence and male specificity are consistent with a threshold of ERα-mediated hepatic/hypothalamic reprogramming that requires male gonadal hormones. Metabolic effects (reduced adiposity, improved glucose tolerance) are seen in obese/aged males at longevity-relevant exposures. No human dose-response exists, and this monograph intentionally gives no dosing protocol.

How it compares

  • vs 17β-estradiol (the main human estrogen)
    same: Same receptor (ERα), overlapping anti-inflammatory signaling (↓NF-κB p65 in vitro)
    different: 17α-E2 is the C17 epimer — far weaker/less uterotrophic, 'non-feminizing,' and uniquely extends MALE mouse lifespan; 17β-E2 is a full feminizing hormone used in HRT/gender-affirming therapy
  • vs Rapamycin
    same: ↓mTORC1, shift to cap-independent translation, anti-inflammatory signatures; both extend mouse lifespan
    different: Rapamycin extends lifespan in BOTH sexes (more in females); 17α-E2 is male-only and gonad-dependent, and unlike rapamycin does NOT lower liver p65
  • vs Acarbose
    same: Male-biased ITP lifespan extension; overlapping hepatic MAPK/mTOR and metabolic signatures
    different: Acarbose is an α-glucosidase inhibitor acting on carbohydrate absorption (with some female benefit and a maximal-lifespan effect in both sexes); 17α-E2 acts via ERα/hypothalamus and is male-exclusive
  • vs Canagliflozin
    same: Male-specific ITP lifespan extension; shared metabolic/lipid signatures
    different: Canagliflozin is an SGLT2 inhibitor (renal glucose excretion); different primary target, and it lowers liver p65 whereas 17α-E2 does not
  • vs Alfatradiol (its own topical form)
    same: Chemically identical molecule
    different: Topical/local use targets scalp 5α-reductase for hair loss; systemic geroprotection is a distinct, unproven-in-humans use with different exposure and risk profile

History & legal status

17α-estradiol is a naturally occurring minor estrogen epimer. Its longevity profile emerged from the NIA Interventions Testing Program: Harrison et al. reported male-specific median-lifespan extension in 2014 (PMID 24245565), and Strong et al. replicated and strengthened it at a higher dose in 2016, adding a maximal-lifespan effect (PMID 27312235). Since then the Miller (Michigan), Stout (Oklahoma), Garratt (Otago), Sadagurski (Wayne State) and collaborating groups have built a deep mechanistic literature (metabolic, neuroinflammatory, signaling, and multi-omic). Separately and much earlier, topical 17α-estradiol was developed as alfatradiol (e.g., Ell-Cranell alpha, Pantostin) for androgenetic alopecia and studied in controlled dermatology trials (PMID 17451383); it is approved in Germany and some other European markets. There is NO FDA approval for systemic human use, and no completed ClinicalTrials.gov trials of systemic 17α-estradiol for aging or metabolic disease. It is widely available as a research chemical and discussed in longevity/biohacker circles.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether ANY benefit (lifespan, metabolic, cognitive) translates to humans — completely untested systemically.
  • Human systemic pharmacokinetics, effective/safe exposure range, and long-term safety, including thromboembolic and endocrine risks.
  • The precise molecular reason for strict male specificity and gonadal-hormone dependence — how the testosterone/estrogen milieu gates ERα-mediated hepatic and hypothalamic responses.
  • How much of the effect is a genuine 'estrogen/ERα' action versus a calorie-restriction mimic driven by appetite suppression.
  • Whether the residual estrogenic activity seen in female mice (uterine rescue) causes clinically meaningful feminization or HPG-axis suppression in men at longevity-relevant exposures.
  • Whether the required target tissue is liver, hypothalamus, adipose, or a combination — mechanistic studies implicate all three.
  • Interactions with existing testosterone/estrogen manipulation common in the fitness/PED population (uncharacterized and potentially additive on the HPG axis).

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

General harm-reduction principles for anyone considering an unapproved, human-untested estrogenic compound (NOT an endorsement, and NO dosing is provided): (1) Recognize there is no established safe human dose or safety database. (2) Estrogenic red flags to watch for in men — gynecomastia/breast tenderness, reduced libido or erectile changes, mood changes; a clinician-ordered baseline and periodic sex-hormone panel (total/free testosterone, estradiol with an assay that distinguishes estradiol species, LH/FSH) is reasonable. (3) Metabolic tracking — fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel, and body-weight/appetite changes (the drug can suppress appetite). (4) Liver — given hepatic ERα action and estrogen metabolism, baseline and periodic liver function tests are reasonable. (5) Thrombosis risk — be alert to estrogen-class venous thromboembolism warning signs (unilateral leg swelling, chest pain, shortness of breath) and personal/family clotting and cardiovascular history. (6) Anyone with hormone-sensitive conditions (e.g., history of estrogen-responsive cancers) should regard systemic estrogenic exposure as high-risk. (7) This is a mouse-based mechanism library entry for education; decisions about any human use belong with a qualified clinician, and this monograph deliberately excludes protocols or 'how to run it' guidance.

Caveats

"'Non-feminizing' is a comparative, not an absolute, claim. 17α-E2 is far less estrogenic than 17β-estradiol, but it still binds ERα and retains measurable estrogenic activity: in ovariectomized female mice it rescued uterine mass and lining (without inducing hypertrophy) and prevented adiposity/bone loss (PMID 33065227). In men, meaningful systemic estrogenic exposure could plausibly cause gynecomastia, reduced libido, testicular effects, or thromboembolic risk — none of which have been characterized. | The headline benefit is MALE-ONLY and gonad-dependent: it fails to extend female lifespan and its metabolic/anti-inflammatory effects shrink after castration (PMID 28834262; PMID 34309657). This makes naive extrapolation to all humans unsafe and scientifically unjustified. | Everything load-bearing is rodent data. Human pharmacokinetics, safety, and any efficacy are unknown. Mouse dietary exposures do not map cleanly to human dosing. | Part of the metabolic effect is a calorie-restriction mimic driven by appetite suppression (PMID 29168299) — some benefit may not be a unique 'estrogen' effect. | Not all longevity-drug signatures are shared: unlike rapamycin/acarbose/canagliflozin/CR, 17α-E2 did NOT lower liver NF-κB p65 (PMID 39666139), so mechanistic claims should not be over-unified. | This entry is educational mechanism synthesis, NOT medical advice, and deliberately contains no dosing or 'how to run it' guidance."
