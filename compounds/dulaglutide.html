Dulaglutide

Main target: Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor. Human evidence is still limited.

A once-weekly GLP-1 receptor agonist built as a peptide-IgG4-Fc fusion: lowers blood sugar without causing hypoglycemia on its own, drives modest weight loss, and is one of the few glucose-lowering drugs proven in a large trial to cut cardiovascular events.

At a glance

  • Lowers HbA1c (roughly 1.0-1.5% depending on dose and baseline), with low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk when used without insulin or sulfonylureas
  • Modest weight loss (a few kg at 1.5 mg; somewhat more at higher doses)
  • Reduces major adverse cardiovascular events (HR 0.88 in REWIND), including in a large primary-prevention population
  • Reduces new-onset macroalbuminuria / composite kidney outcomes (exploratory, hypothesis-generating)
  • Weekly subcutaneous injection via prefilled pen; no glucose-guided dose titration, only escalation for efficacy/tolerability
  • Gastrointestinal side effects (nausea, diarrhea, vomiting) are common and are mechanistic, not incidental
  • Possible signal for reduced cognitive decline (exploratory; significant only after post-hoc baseline adjustment)

Mechanism, step by step

The step-by-step story of how it works — each step tagged by how sure we are.

8 steps8 human / established

  1. Fc fusion + DPP-4-resistant peptide = weekly action Established

    Native GLP-1 is destroyed within ~1-2 minutes by the enzyme DPP-4 and by the kidneys. Dulaglutide fuses two modified GLP-1 analog peptides to a human IgG4 Fc fragment; substituted amino acids block DPP-4 cleavage and the large Fc slows renal clearance, extending half-life to about 5 days so one injection covers a week. This is engineering, not a different receptor. Min JS et al., Drug Des Devel Ther 2025, PMID 40330819 (DOI 10.2147/DDDT.S506957)

  2. Agonism at the GLP-1 receptor Established

    Dulaglutide binds and activates the GLP-1 receptor, a class B GPCR expressed on pancreatic islet cells, in the gut, and in the brain and heart. ChEMBL curates it as a direct GLP-1R AGONIST (direct interaction, disease efficacy, max phase 4). This receptor engagement is the single upstream event from which every downstream effect follows. ChEMBL mechanism mec_id 2276, molecule CHEMBL2107841, target CHEMBL1784 (FDA label 125431s000)

  3. Glucose-dependent insulin secretion (beta cells) Established

    On pancreatic beta cells, GLP-1R activation raises intracellular cAMP, which amplifies glucose-stimulated insulin release. Crucially this is glucose-dependent: it boosts insulin mainly when blood sugar is high, which is why the drug alone rarely causes hypoglycemia. Nauck MA et al., Mol Metab 2021, PMID 33068776 (DOI 10.1016/j.molmet.2020.101102)

  4. Suppression of glucagon (alpha cells) Established

    GLP-1R activation lowers secretion of glucagon, the hormone that tells the liver to release glucose. Less glucagon means less hepatic glucose output, especially overnight and fasting. This suppression is also glucose-dependent and does not block the normal glucagon rescue response to true hypoglycemia. Nauck MA et al., Mol Metab 2021, PMID 33068776; review Madsbad S, Diabetes Obes Metab 2016, PMID 26511102

  5. Slowed gastric emptying Established

    Acting on gut and vagal GLP-1 receptors, dulaglutide slows how fast the stomach empties, blunting the post-meal glucose spike and increasing fullness. For long-acting agents this effect partially wanes over weeks (tachyphylaxis), unlike short-acting GLP-1 drugs. It is also the direct cause of most nausea. Nauck MA et al., Mol Metab 2021, PMID 33068776; Saha B et al., Mayo Clin Proc 2025, PMID 41324524

  6. Central appetite suppression -> weight loss Established

    GLP-1 receptors in hypothalamic and hindbrain appetite centers are engaged, reducing hunger and calorie intake. The net result is modest weight loss (a few kg in trials at 1.5 mg), less than newer agents like semaglutide 2.4 mg or tirzepatide but consistent. Nauck MA et al., Mol Metab 2021, PMID 33068776; Dungan KM et al. (AWARD-6), Lancet 2014, PMID 25018121

  7. Cardiovascular risk reduction (partly beyond glucose) Evidence-human

    In the REWIND trial, weekly dulaglutide reduced major adverse cardiovascular events (nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, CV/unknown death) with HR 0.88 (12.0% vs 13.4%) over median 5.4 years, including in people WITHOUT prior heart disease. The exact mechanism is not fully pinned down: proposed contributors are anti-inflammatory, anti-atherogenic, endothelial and modest blood-pressure/lipid effects, and the benefit appears only partly explained by glucose lowering. Gerstein HC et al. (REWIND), Lancet 2019, PMID 31189511 (DOI 10.1016/S0140-6736(19)31149-3)

  8. Kidney protection (mainly anti-albuminuria) Evidence-human

    In a prespecified exploratory REWIND analysis, dulaglutide cut a composite renal outcome (HR 0.85), driven mostly by less new macroalbuminuria (HR 0.77); the effect on the eGFR-decline component was weaker and not statistically conclusive (HR 0.89, p=0.066). Mechanism is thought to be partly hemodynamic and anti-inflammatory rather than purely glucose-driven. Gerstein HC et al. (REWIND renal, exploratory), Lancet 2019, PMID 31189509 (DOI 10.1016/S0140-6736(19)31150-X)

Receptor pharmacology

How tightly it grabs its targets, and what that binding does.

Binding & selectivity. Direct agonist at the glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor (GLP-1R; ChEMBL1784), a class B secretin-family GPCR. The molecule is a homodimer: two modified human GLP-1(7-37) analog peptides, each covalently linked via a small peptide linker to one chain of a modified human IgG4 Fc fragment. Peptide substitutions (notably at positions that would be cleaved by DPP-4) confer enzymatic stability and reduce immunogenicity; the IgG4 Fc is engineered to reduce effector/FcgammaR and complement engagement. ChEMBL curates action_type AGONIST, direct_interaction true, disease_efficacy true, max_phase 4.

Signalling cascade. GLP-1R is Gs-coupled: activation stimulates adenylate cyclase, raising intracellular cAMP and activating downstream PKA and Epac2 signaling. In pancreatic beta cells this potentiates glucose-stimulated insulin secretion in a glucose-dependent manner (minimal effect at normoglycemia, hence low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk). In alpha cells it suppresses glucagon. Peripheral/vagal and central GLP-1R signaling slows gastric emptying and reduces appetite. Proposed cardiovascular mechanisms include anti-inflammatory, anti-atherogenic, endothelial-protective, and modest blood-pressure and lipid effects.

Pharmacokinetics

How the body absorbs it, moves it around, and clears it out (ADME).

Route / form
Subcutaneous injection (abdomen, thigh, or upper arm) once weekly via single-dose prefilled pen or syringe.
Absorption / bioavailability
Absolute bioavailability after SC dosing approximately 47-65% (per label/PK reviews); median time to peak concentration on the order of 1-3 days. Steady state reached after ~2-4 weeks of weekly dosing.
Half-life
Approximately 5 days (~4.5-4.7 days reported), which supports once-weekly dosing.
Metabolism
Degraded into its component amino acids by general protein catabolism pathways, like other large peptide/protein therapeutics. Not metabolized by cytochrome P450 enzymes, so classic CYP-mediated drug-drug interactions are not expected.
Excretion
Cleared by proteolytic catabolism rather than intact renal or hepatic elimination; the IgG4 Fc fragment markedly slows clearance versus native GLP-1.
Notes
The main PK-relevant interaction is mechanism-based: slowed gastric emptying can alter absorption of co-administered oral drugs. For dulaglutide this has generally been found clinically insignificant for most drugs, though caution/monitoring is reasonable for narrow-therapeutic-index oral agents. No clinically significant CYP or transporter drug-drug interactions reported.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

How the effect changes with dose, where the data exists.

Marketed strengths are 0.75 mg and 1.5 mg weekly, with higher 3.0 mg and 4.5 mg strengths added later (AWARD-11 program) for additional glycemic and weight effect. Glycemic and weight benefit increase with dose but with a shallow slope and more GI adverse events at higher doses. REWIND used the 1.5 mg dose. Unlike insulin, there is no glucose-guided up-titration; escalation exists mainly to improve tolerability and to extract incremental HbA1c/weight benefit. This entry deliberately gives no titration schedule or dosing protocol.

How it compares

How it stacks up against similar compounds.

  • vs Semaglutide (weekly)
    same: Both weekly GLP-1 RAs with proven CV benefit and glucose lowering
    different: Semaglutide (esp. 2.4 mg) produces substantially greater weight loss and larger HbA1c reductions; dulaglutide is generally milder on weight. Semaglutide uses albumin binding via fatty-acid acylation, dulaglutide uses IgG4-Fc fusion.
  • vs Liraglutide (daily)
    same: Comparable HbA1c efficacy; both CV-proven (LEADER for liraglutide, REWIND for dulaglutide)
    different: Dulaglutide is once-weekly vs once-daily; AWARD-6 showed noninferior glycemic control with similar tolerability, favoring convenience.
  • vs Tirzepatide (dual GIP/GLP-1, weekly)
    same: Both weekly injectables; SURPASS-CVOT directly compared them
    different: Tirzepatide adds GIP receptor agonism, giving markedly greater HbA1c and weight reduction; in SURPASS-CVOT it was noninferior (not superior, p=0.09) to dulaglutide for MACE. Dulaglutide is the pure-GLP-1 benchmark.
  • vs Exenatide once-weekly / albiglutide
    same: Same weekly long-acting GLP-1 RA class with shared mechanism
    different: Dulaglutide generally showed stronger/more consistent HbA1c and CV data; albiglutide was withdrawn commercially, and exenatide QW is less potent on HbA1c and weight.
  • vs DPP-4 inhibitors (e.g., sitagliptin)
    same: Both act on the incretin/GLP-1 axis
    different: DPP-4 inhibitors only modestly raise endogenous GLP-1 (weaker HbA1c effect, weight-neutral, no established MACE benefit); dulaglutide is a direct receptor agonist with greater efficacy and proven CV benefit.

History & legal status

Where it came from, and its approval and legal status.

Developed by Eli Lilly as a weekly GLP-1 RA using an IgG4-Fc fusion platform. FDA approved 2014 and EMA approved for type 2 diabetes; marketed as Trulicity. The AWARD phase 3 program (AWARD-1 through -11 and regional trials) established glycemic efficacy vs metformin (AWARD-3), sitagliptin, exenatide, insulin glargine, and liraglutide (AWARD-6). In 2019-2020 REWIND supported an expanded FDA indication to reduce major adverse cardiovascular events in adults with T2D and established CVD or multiple CV risk factors — notable for including a large primary-prevention population. Higher 3.0/4.5 mg doses were later approved for additional efficacy. Dulaglutide carries a boxed warning for thyroid C-cell tumors (rodent data) and is contraindicated in personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN 2. It served as the active comparator in SURPASS-CVOT (tirzepatide; PMID 41406444), cementing its role as the reference weekly GLP-1 RA.

Key unknowns / open questions

What we still don't know.

  • The precise mechanism of cardiovascular benefit — how much is anti-inflammatory/anti-atherogenic vs glucose/weight/blood-pressure mediated — is not resolved.
  • Whether the exploratory renal and cognitive signals in REWIND represent true hard-outcome benefits (dedicated trials, not exploratory analyses) remains unproven for dulaglutide specifically; the cognitive signal in particular depended on post-hoc adjustment.
  • Whether rodent thyroid C-cell carcinogenicity translates to any human medullary thyroid cancer risk is unresolved; human calcitonin data for the class have not shown a signal, but no long-term causal answer exists.
  • Long-term (beyond ~5-6 years) safety and durability of effect are less characterized than for older agents.
  • Comparative effectiveness in non-T2D obesity is not an approved use and is not well studied for dulaglutide (unlike semaglutide/tirzepatide).
  • Magnitude and mechanism of the modest heart-rate increase and its long-term significance.

What to monitor

What to keep an eye on if it's used — bloodwork and symptoms.

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

What to watch (surveillance, not a protocol): (1) GI tolerability — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration; persistent severe GI symptoms warrant medical review. (2) Hypoglycemia when combined with sulfonylureas or insulin — glucose monitoring and clinician review of concomitant therapy. (3) Pancreatitis — new severe, persistent abdominal pain (which may radiate to the back) should prompt stopping the drug and urgent evaluation. (4) Thyroid — any neck mass, dysphagia, or persistent hoarseness should be evaluated; contraindicated in personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN 2. (5) Gallbladder — right-upper-quadrant pain, especially with rapid weight loss. (6) Kidney — dehydration from GI losses can transiently worsen renal function; hydration and monitoring in at-risk patients. (7) Injection-site reactions and, rarely, hypersensitivity. (8) Because gastric emptying is slowed, be alert to altered absorption of narrow-therapeutic-index oral drugs and, per anesthesia/endoscopy guidance, retained gastric contents around sedation/surgery. None of this constitutes dosing advice; decisions belong with the treating clinician.

Caveats

GI side effects are the rule, not the exception: in REWIND 47.4% of dulaglutide patients reported a GI adverse event vs 34.1% on placebo (p<0.0001). The cardiovascular benefit is a genuine primary-endpoint result of a large RCT, but the renal and cognitive findings are EXPLORATORY (hypothesis-generating, not primary endpoints) — the cognitive signal was non-significant unadjusted (HR 0.93, p=0.11) and reached significance only after post-hoc baseline adjustment. Weight loss is real but modest and clearly inferior to semaglutide 2.4 mg and tirzepatide. The boxed thyroid C-cell tumor warning rests on RODENT carcinogenicity data (dulaglutide causes dose-dependent C-cell hyperplasia/tumors in rats; no such neoplasia was seen in the 26-week transgenic-mouse study); a causal link to human medullary thyroid cancer has NOT been established, and long-term human calcitonin data for the class (liraglutide) did not show a signal, but the warning stands as precaution. This is an evidence summary, not medical advice; it contains no dosing protocol. Pancreatitis is a labeled risk (discontinue if suspected), though high-quality studies have not confirmed an increased incidence. All pivotal trials cited were funded by Eli Lilly, the manufacturer.
