Amycretin

One molecule, two hunger brakes: amycretin fuses GLP-1 and amylin signaling into a single peptide that, in early trials, drove weight loss rivaling or beating the best injectables — but the human data is still tiny and early-phase.

Limited human data 9 citations
Class
Unimolecular (single-peptide) GLP-1 receptor + amylin receptor co-agonist; investigational anti-obesity agent (Novo Nordisk)
Primary target
Dual agonist at the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) and the amylin receptor (AMYR = calcitonin receptor [CTR] + RAMP heterodimers); in cell systems it also activates the calcitonin receptor itself
Human status
Investigational / early clinical. As of mid-2026, the published human evidence is limited to early-phase, Novo Nordisk-funded trials: a first-in-human phase 1 of ORAL amycretin (NCT05369390, n=144, PMID 40550229) and a phase 1b/2a of SUBCUTANEOUS amycretin (NCT06064006, n=125, PMID 40550231), both published in The Lancet, July 2025. No phase 3 efficacy/outcome data published yet; not FDA/EMA approved. A network meta-analysis (PMID 42175595) ranks it highly for short-term weight loss but explicitly calls the evidence sparse and low-certainty. All weight-loss figures come from small, short, dose-finding studies with notable participant withdrawals in the SC trial.

At a glance

  • Large body-weight reduction: ~22-24% at high subcutaneous doses over 28-36 weeks (phase 1b/2a, n=125); oral form ~13% at 12 weeks per review
  • Appetite suppression via dual hindbrain satiety signaling (GLP-1 + amylin) plus slowed gastric emptying
  • Improved insulin sensitivity and reduced fasting glucose (human exploratory + rodent clamp data)
  • Reduced liver fat / MASLD steatosis (rodent histology)
  • Predominantly gastrointestinal side effects (nausea, vomiting, constipation), dose-dependent, mostly mild-moderate
  • Exists as BOTH an oral pill and a weekly injection — unusual flexibility for a peptide

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Two gut/pancreas satiety hormones, mimicked by one peptide Established

    Your body makes two appetite-controlling hormones after eating: GLP-1 (an incretin from the gut) and amylin (co-released with insulin from the pancreas). Each curbs hunger through its own receptor. Amycretin is a single engineered peptide that switches ON both receptor systems at once, instead of needing two separate drugs. PMID 40550229; PMID 40550231

  2. Hits GLP-1, amylin AND calcitonin receptors at the molecular level Evidence-in vitro

    In lab dish (cell-based) tests, amycretin activated the human, mouse and rat GLP-1 receptor, amylin receptor, and calcitonin receptor. The amylin receptor is itself built from a calcitonin receptor paired with helper proteins (RAMPs), which is why a single molecule can light up this whole family. PMID 40706446

  3. Acts on hindbrain satiety centers + slows the stomach Evidence-rodent

    The GLP-1 and amylin signals converge on appetite-regulating regions of the hindbrain (the brainstem feeding centers), telling the brain you are full sooner and for longer. They also slow gastric emptying, so food leaves the stomach more slowly. The net effect is you eat less without consciously trying. In mice, amycretin was shown to reach and target the brain areas that control food intake. PMID 40706446; PMID 41850421

  4. Massive cut in food intake drives the weight loss (animal data) Evidence-rodent

    In diet-induced obese rats, 21 days of amycretin slashed total calorie intake by about 47% and dropped body weight by about 18%, while energy expenditure (calorie burning) was maintained — meaning the weight loss came from eating less, not from a metabolic burnout. It also improved insulin sensitivity and reduced fatty-liver (MASLD) markers, mainly by cutting liver fat (steatosis). PMID 40706446

  5. Translates to large weight loss in humans — early phase Evidence-human

    In a subcutaneous (injected) phase 1b/2a trial of 125 people with overweight/obesity, weekly amycretin produced roughly 22-24% body-weight loss over 28-36 weeks at the higher doses, versus near-zero on placebo. The oral form in a separate first-in-human phase 1 trial also produced meaningful weight loss (a review cites up to ~13% at 12 weeks). These are early, small, dose-finding studies — not large outcome trials. PMID 40550231; PMID 40550229; PMID 41850421

  6. Side-effect profile is the familiar incretin GI pattern Evidence-human

    The dominant side effects were gastrointestinal — nausea, vomiting, constipation — mostly mild to moderate, dose-dependent, and consistent with other GLP-1/amylin drugs. No deaths occurred in the trials. A network meta-analysis ranked high-dose amycretin among the strongest for weight loss but also flagged more GI adverse events, and the data as sparse and low-certainty. PMID 40550229; PMID 40550231; PMID 42175595

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Amycretin is a single-molecule (unimolecular) co-agonist that activates BOTH the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) and the amylin-receptor system in one engineered, lipidated peptide — not a mix of two drugs. GLP-1R is a class B/secretin-family GPCR (beta cells, hindbrain/hypothalamus, gut); amycretin is a full agonist there. The 'amylin receptor' is not one protein: it is the calcitonin receptor (CTR, a class B GPCR) heterodimerised with a receptor-activity-modifying protein (RAMP1/2/3), forming AMY1R (CTR+RAMP1), AMY2R (CTR+RAMP2), AMY3R (CTR+RAMP3); amycretin also activates the bare CTR. The pivotal preclinical paper confirmed amycretin activates HUMAN, MOUSE and RAT GLP-1, amylin AND calcitonin receptors in cell-based reporter assays [Established — Kuhre 2025, EBioMedicine, PMID 40706446]. IMPORTANT GAP: exact numeric Ki/EC50 values and the GLP-1R-vs-amylin potency ratio for amycretin are NOT in the public peer-reviewed record as constants — activation is reported qualitatively/cross-species, not as a head-to-head affinity table. Any specific nanomolar figure circulating online for amycretin is therefore unverified [Contested/unknown]. For calibration, the structurally related SELECTIVE amylin analogue eloralintide was reported with explicit numbers: it preferentially activated human AMY1R ~12-fold over CTR and ~11-fold over AMY3R [Evidence-in vitro/human — Briere 2025, Mol Metab, PMID 41109426].

Signalling cascade. Both target families are Gs-coupled GPCRs: agonism activates adenylate cyclase, raises intracellular cAMP and drives PKA/Epac signalling. (1) GLP-1R on pancreatic beta cells => glucose-DEPENDENT insulin secretion (low hypoglycaemia risk because it is glucose-gated) plus suppression of glucagon; on hindbrain/hypothalamic and vagal neurons => reduced food intake, slowed gastric emptying, increased satiety. (2) Amylin/calcitonin-receptor agonism acts largely in the HINDBRAIN — the area postrema and nucleus tractus solitarius (circumventricular organs lacking a tight blood-brain barrier) — to amplify satiation, slow gastric emptying, and suppress prandial glucagon; amylin signalling is thought to restore leptin sensitivity, which may explain synergy. The dual mechanism 'synergistically engages hindbrain-mediated satiety pathways and delays gastric emptying to overcome the efficacy plateau of GLP-1 monotherapy.' Animal data: amycretin reached key food-intake-regulating brain areas, cut total energy intake ~47% and body weight ~18% in diet-induced obese rats over 21 days while MAINTAINING energy expenditure (i.e., weight loss was via reduced intake, not increased burn), and improved insulin sensitivity and hepatic steatosis [Evidence-rodent — Kuhre 2025, PMID 40706446; mechanism synthesis — Fu 2026, Metabolism, PMID 41850421].

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Two parenteral/enteral forms taken into humans by Novo Nordisk: (a) ORAL tablet, once daily, co-formulated with the absorption enhancer SNAC (salcaprozate sodium — the same technology as oral semaglutide/Rybelsus); and (b) SUBCUTANEOUS injection, once weekly. [Established — Gasiorek 2025 PMID 40550229 (oral); Dahl 2025 PMID 40550231 (subcutaneous)]
Absorption / bioavailability
Oral peptide absorption is intrinsically very low and variable: SNAC transiently raises local gastric pH and promotes monomeric transcellular uptake, but as with oral semaglutide only a small single-digit percentage (or less) of an oral dose is absorbed — this is WHY oral maintenance doses (up to ~50 mg, even 2x50 mg/day) are orders of magnitude larger than the subcutaneous doses (mg-to-sub-mg). Exact oral bioavailability % for amycretin is not published [Evidence-human, magnitude inferred; oral dosing range — PMID 40550229]. Oral plasma exposure (AUC, Cmax) was dose-proportional across groups [Established — PMID 40550229].
Half-life
The subcutaneous program uses ONCE-WEEKLY dosing, which implies a multi-day plasma half-life consistent with fatty-acid (albumin-binding) acylation that prolongs circulation — the same design principle as semaglutide/cagrilintide. A precise numeric t1/2 in hours is not stated in the primary trial abstracts [Evidence-human, qualitative — Dahl 2025 PMID 40550231]. The oral form is dosed once DAILY, consistent with shorter effective exposure from low/variable absorption.
Metabolism
As an acylated peptide, amycretin is expected to be cleared by ubiquitous proteolytic degradation into peptide fragments and amino acids (as for GLP-1 analogues and amylin analogues), NOT by cytochrome-P450 hepatic metabolism — so classic CYP drug-drug interactions are not the main concern. Specific degradation enzymes/sites are not detailed in the primary human papers [Evidence-human, class-inferred; no amycretin-specific enzymatic data published].
Excretion
Not explicitly reported for amycretin; peptide drugs of this class are eliminated mainly via metabolic breakdown to fragments with renal handling of small fragments rather than intact renal/biliary excretion [class-inferred, not amycretin-specific].
Notes
Lipidation (long-chain fatty-acid conjugation) drives albumin binding and the long half-life enabling weekly subcutaneous dosing. The fatty-acid tail keeps the peptide largely peripheral; amylin/calcitonin-receptor effects are exerted at hindbrain circumventricular organs (area postrema) that lie OUTSIDE the tight blood-brain barrier, so deep CNS penetration is not required for the central anorectic effect. Rodent work confirmed access to food-intake-regulating brain regions [Evidence-rodent — PMID 40706446]. No ester/prodrug moiety; SNAC is a formulation enhancer, not a covalent prodrug.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

HUMAN — SUBCUTANEOUS (once-weekly, phase 1b/2a, NCT06064006, up to 36 weeks): clear dose-response in placebo-subtracted weight loss. 1.25 mg => -9.7% vs +2.0% placebo (week 20); 5 mg => -16.2% vs +2.3% (week 28); 20 mg => -22.0% vs +1.9% (week 36); 60 mg => -24.3% vs -1.1% (week 36); all p<=0.0003 [Evidence-human — Dahl 2025, PMID 40550231]. HUMAN — ORAL (once-daily, phase 1, NCT05369390, 12-week part C/D, titrated up to 50 mg or 2x50 mg/day): a mechanistic review summarises up to ~13.1% weight loss at 12 weeks; the primary phase-1 paper's stated objective was safety/PK/tolerability and it reports dose-dependent, dose-proportional exposure and dose-dependent adverse events rather than a definitive efficacy curve [Evidence-human, efficacy magnitude from review — Gasiorek 2025 PMID 40550229; Fu 2026 PMID 41850421]. RODENT: diet-induced obese rats, 21 days => ~47% reduction in total energy intake and ~18% body-weight reduction vs vehicle, energy expenditure preserved; dose-dependent food-intake and fat-mass reduction [Evidence-rodent — Kuhre 2025 PMID 40706446]. KEY UNKNOWNS: no head-to-head dose-equivalence between oral and subcutaneous forms; no published EC50-anchored exposure-response model; longer-term (>=68-week) human dose-response and the plateau/ceiling are not yet established; phase 3 dosing not finalised.

How it compares

  • vs CagriSema (cagrilintide + semaglutide, Novo Nordisk)
    same: Same dual pharmacology target set — GLP-1R agonism plus amylin-receptor agonism — and same developer; both aim to beat GLP-1 monotherapy on weight loss.
    different: CagriSema is TWO separate molecules co-formulated (the amylin analogue cagrilintide + the GLP-1 agonist semaglutide); amycretin packs BOTH activities into ONE peptide (unimolecular), and amycretin uniquely has an ORAL form. Amycretin's early subcutaneous weight-loss figures (-24.3% at 36 wk) are numerically large but come from a small phase 1b/2a, not comparable head-to-head to CagriSema phase 3.
  • vs Tirzepatide (GLP-1 + GIP dual agonist, Mounjaro/Zepbound)
    same: Also a unimolecular incretin co-agonist for obesity/T2D; high-efficacy weight loss; GI side-effect profile dominated by on-target satiety mechanisms.
    different: Second target differs: tirzepatide adds GIP-receptor agonism, whereas amycretin adds AMYLIN/calcitonin-receptor agonism (a different appetite axis acting via hindbrain area postrema). Tirzepatide is approved; amycretin is investigational phase 1/2.
  • vs Eloralintide (LY3841136, Eli Lilly) and cagrilintide (amylin analogues)
    same: Share the amylin-receptor arm of amycretin's mechanism; centrally acting anorectic peptides; weekly subcutaneous designs.
    different: These are PURE amylin-receptor agonists with NO GLP-1 activity. Eloralintide is AMY1R-SELECTIVE (~12x over CTR) and showed less conditioned taste avoidance than non-selective cagrilintide, suggesting selectivity may improve GI tolerability; amycretin instead combines amylin activity WITH full GLP-1 agonism in one molecule, trading single-pathway selectivity for dual-pathway potency [Briere 2025 PMID 41109426].
  • vs Semaglutide (GLP-1 monotherapy, Wegovy/Ozempic/Rybelsus)
    same: Shares the GLP-1R arm; same SNAC oral-delivery technology for the oral form; same GI tolerability themes and glucose-dependent (low-hypoglycaemia) insulin mechanism.
    different: Semaglutide is single-target (GLP-1 only); amycretin adds the amylin axis, and early data suggest larger weight loss than GLP-1 monotherapy typically achieves — but from much smaller, earlier-phase trials.

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • Clenbuterol compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Amycretin raises resting heart rate modestly via the incretin class effect (amycretin-specific magnitude unquantified), and its GI side effects can cause vomiting/diarrhea with fluid and electrolyte loss. Clenbuterol is a beta-2 agonist that raises heart rate and drives hypokalemia (intracellular potassium shift). The tachycardia stacks, and amycretin-driven GI fluid/electrolyte loss can worsen clenbuterol's hypokalemia, a combined cardiac-strain and arrhythmia-risk concern. amycretin.json (class heart-rate rise, magnitude unquantified; GI vomiting/dehydration/electrolyte risk); clenbuterol.json (beta-2 agonist: tachycardia, arrhythmia risk, drop in blood potassium) Watch: Watch for palpitations, resting tachycardia, tremor and muscle cramps; monitor heart rate, blood pressure and serum potassium/electrolytes (especially during GI illness or dehydration).
  • Insulin compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Amycretin's GLP-1R arm lowers glucose (glucose-dependent, so low intrinsic hypoglycemia risk alone) and its amylin/calcitonin arm suppresses prandial glucagon, which can blunt counter-regulation. Added to exogenous insulin, which lowers glucose independent of glucose level, the glucose-lowering effects stack and hypoglycemia risk rises. Amycretin also slows gastric emptying, delaying meal-carbohydrate absorption so insulin action can mismatch food timing, further raising hypo risk. amycretin.json (glucose-dependent insulin, glucagon suppression, slowed emptying; hypoglycemia risk rises with insulin); insulin-exogenous.json (glucose-independent glucose lowering; hypoglycemia) Watch: Watch for hypoglycemia symptoms (shakiness, sweating, confusion); monitor blood glucose closely, especially around meals given delayed gastric emptying. Harm-reduction only.
  • Retatrutide compounds risk ⚠ Evidence-human
    Retatrutide is a GLP-1R+GIPR+GCGR triple agonist and amycretin is a GLP-1R+amylin agonist, so they share the GLP-1R arm (redundant/additive appetite suppression and GI effects). Both also raise resting heart rate via the incretin class effect (retatrutide's HR rise is human-observed; amycretin's own magnitude is not separately quantified), so the tachycardia and GI-driven dehydration risk compound. amycretin.json (GLP-1R agonist; class heart-rate rise, magnitude unquantified); retatrutide.json (GLP-1R agonist; mild heart-rate increase); PMID 41582189 (GLP-1 RA heart-rate rise) Watch: Watch for additive resting heart-rate rise/palpitations, severe combined GI symptoms and dehydration, and excessive/too-fast weight loss; monitor heart rate, blood pressure and hydration.
  • Tirzepatide compounds risk ⚠ Established
    Both are unimolecular incretin co-agonists that share the GLP-1 receptor arm (tirzepatide adds GIP, amycretin adds amylin). Overlapping GLP-1R agonism plus each drug's second appetite axis produces strongly additive hindbrain satiety and markedly slowed gastric emptying, compounding on-target GI adverse events and, because both delay emptying, potentially reducing or making erratic the absorption of co-ingested ORAL drugs. Tirzepatide's gastric-emptying delay is documented to alter oral-drug (e.g. contraceptive) absorption. amycretin.json (GLP-1R + amylin; slowed gastric emptying); tirzepatide.json (delayed gastric emptying reduces oral drug absorption; PMID 37940101, 39418085) Watch: Watch for severe additive nausea/vomiting, dehydration, and unreliable absorption of any oral medications taken alongside; monitor heart rate and hydration/electrolytes during GI illness.
  • Ligandrol counteracts ↓ Speculative
    High-efficacy incretin weight loss like amycretin's drives a fraction of lost weight from lean/muscle mass because profound appetite suppression cuts protein intake (in amycretin's case this lean-mass loss is inferred from the magnitude of intake reduction, not yet quantified in humans). Ligandrol is a SARM that activates the muscle androgen receptor and is discussed in the literature as a candidate to preserve/rebuild lean mass lost on GLP-1 agonists, so it would oppose amycretin's lean-mass-loss side effect (not its target). No human study of this specific combination exists. amycretin.json (lean-mass loss inferred from intake reduction, amycretin-specific quantification Speculative); ligandrol.json (AR agonist, increases lean body mass); PMID 40739991 (SARMs proposed for GLP-1-induced lean-body-mass loss); PMID 41022269 (GLP-1 RA sarcopenia risk) Watch: Track body composition (not just scale weight) to gauge lean-mass preservation; monitor lipid panel/HDL and liver enzymes, since SARMs suppress HDL and are linked to cholestatic liver injury.
  • MK-677 opposes Evidence-human
    Amycretin IMPROVES insulin sensitivity and lowers fasting glucose. MK-677 (a ghrelin-receptor/GH secretagogue) raises GH/IGF-1 and is documented to impair glucose homeostasis, causing insulin resistance and higher fasting glucose. The two push glucose metabolism in OPPOSITE directions, so MK-677 partly counteracts amycretin's glycemic benefit; conversely amycretin may mask MK-677's glucose-worsening on labs while the underlying insulin resistance persists. amycretin.json (improved insulin sensitivity, lower fasting glucose); mk-677.json (raises fasting glucose, reduces insulin sensitivity; GH opposes insulin) Watch: Monitor fasting glucose, HbA1c and fasting insulin to detect MK-677-driven glucose impairment that amycretin's benefit could be hiding; watch the net glycemic effect rather than assuming amycretin fully offsets it.
  • Orforglipron shares target Established
    Orforglipron is an oral small-molecule GLP-1 receptor agonist and amycretin is a peptide GLP-1 receptor agonist. Although orforglipron binds a different (upper-helical-bundle) pocket than peptide agonists, both converge on the identical GLP-1R appetite/gastric pathway, so the GLP-1 arm is redundant while the shared on-target GI side-effect burden (nausea, vomiting, slowed emptying) compounds. amycretin.json (GLP-1R agonist); orforglipron.json (non-peptide GLP-1R agonist, Gs/cAMP) Watch: Watch for additive nausea/vomiting and dehydration; track heart rate and fasting glucose. No distinct mechanism is added by combining two GLP-1R agonists.
  • Semaglutide shares target Established
    Amycretin is a full GLP-1 receptor agonist and semaglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist, so combining them means two drugs occupying the identical GLP-1R (hindbrain satiety, slowed gastric emptying, glucose-dependent insulin release). This is redundant on the GLP-1 arm and additively compounds the shared on-target GI effects (nausea/vomiting) and gastric-emptying delay without engaging a new pathway. Both oral forms also share the same SNAC (salcaprozate) absorption technology. amycretin.json (GLP-1R full agonist; shared SNAC oral tech vs semaglutide); semaglutide.json (GLP-1R agonist; SNAC oral formulation) Watch: Watch for stacked GI adverse events and dehydration; monitor fasting glucose/HbA1c and resting heart rate (small class-typical rise). No added benefit expected from doubling the same receptor arm.
  • Survodutide shares target Established
    Survodutide is a GLP-1R+GCGR dual agonist and amycretin is a GLP-1R+amylin agonist, so they share the GLP-1 receptor arm: appetite suppression, slowed gastric emptying and on-target GI side effects are additive/redundant. Both are also reported to lower liver fat, but note this convergence is human/biopsy-confirmed for survodutide (via the glucagon arm) versus rodent-only for amycretin, so the shared GLP-1R/GI overlap is the load-bearing interaction. amycretin.json (GLP-1R agonist; reduced liver fat/MASLD in rodents only); survodutide.json (GLP-1R+GCGR; MASH/liver-fat reduction in humans) Watch: Watch for compounded nausea/vomiting and dehydration; track liver enzymes/steatosis markers and fasting glucose. No new receptor arm is added on the GLP-1 side.
  • Cagrilintide redundant Established
    Both act on the SAME amylin/calcitonin-receptor axis: cagrilintide is a long-acting amylin analogue (a Dual Amylin and Calcitonin Receptor Agonist, agonizing AMY1R/AMY3R = CALCR+RAMP heterodimers plus the bare calcitonin receptor), and amycretin is a unimolecular peptide that already contains a full amylin-receptor + calcitonin-receptor agonist arm alongside its GLP-1 arm. Adding cagrilintide layers a second drug onto a receptor system amycretin is already fully activating, so it is largely redundant on the amylin side while stacking amylin-driven nausea, slowed gastric emptying and prandial-glucagon suppression rather than adding a new mechanism. amycretin.json (dual GLP-1R + amylin/calcitonin-receptor agonist); cagrilintide.json (DACRA at AMY1R/AMY3R + CTR); PMID 40204768 (cagrilintide binds amylin + calcitonin receptors) Watch: Watch for compounded GI intolerance (persistent nausea/vomiting), signs of dehydration (dizziness, dark/reduced urine), and excessive/too-rapid weight loss; track fasting glucose since both suppress prandial glucagon.

History & legal status

"Origin: developed by Novo Nordisk as a next-generation, single-molecule GLP-1 + amylin receptor co-agonist to push past the efficacy plateau of GLP-1 monotherapy; engineered as a lipidated peptide deliverable both orally (SNAC enhancer) and subcutaneously. First-in-human results were published July 2025 in The Lancet (oral, NCT05369390/PMID 40550229; subcutaneous phase 1b/2a, NCT06064006/PMID 40550231), with the pivotal preclinical characterisation in EBioMedicine (PMID 40706446). A completed phase-1 study is also registered (NCT06049329). Regulatory status as of mid-2026: INVESTIGATIONAL — amycretin is NOT approved by the FDA, EMA, or any regulator for any indication; it is in early/mid clinical development (phase 1/2, advancing toward larger trials). It has no marketing authorisation and is not a controlled/scheduled substance. It is not legally available as a prescription medicine; any 'amycretin' sold by research-chemical/grey-market peptide vendors is unapproved, unverified in identity/purity, and outside any clinical safety monitoring."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Exact receptor pharmacology constants: published Ki/EC50 values and the GLP-1R-vs-amylin potency ratio for amycretin are not in the peer-reviewed record — the GLP-1-dominant vs amylin-dominant balance is unquantified.
  • Long-term (phase 3, >=68-week) efficacy, the weight-loss ceiling/plateau, and durability/weight-regain after stopping are unknown; current human data are small, single-site, early-phase.
  • Body-composition safety: how much of amycretin-induced weight loss is fat vs lean/muscle mass in humans is not yet quantified.
  • Cardiovascular and cardio-renal outcomes (MACE, blood pressure, heart rate magnitude) have not been tested in outcome trials; benefits are extrapolated from class data.
  • The very high trial discontinuation seen in the subcutaneous study (largely for non-AE reasons) leaves real-world adherence and tolerability at top doses uncertain.
  • Oral-vs-subcutaneous dose equivalence, precise human half-life/bioavailability numbers, metabolising enzymes, and excretion route are not published.
  • Whether combining amycretin with agents like SGLT2 inhibitors meaningfully improves cardio-renal outcomes is hypothesised but untested.
  • Safety in pregnancy, adolescents, hepatic/renal impairment, and any amyloid-aggregation risk over years are unstudied.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring (educational — NOT dosing advice; no protocol implied). Because the dominant effects are on-target GI and metabolic: WATCH FOR clinically — persistent or severe nausea/vomiting/diarrhoea and signs of DEHYDRATION (dizziness, dark urine, reduced urination); rapid or excessive weight loss and signs of muscle loss/weakness; symptoms of gallbladder disease (right-upper-quadrant pain, after rapid weight loss, a class signal); pancreatitis warning signs (severe persistent abdominal pain radiating to back with vomiting — seek urgent care); resting heart-rate increase/palpitations; and, if combined with insulin or sulfonylureas, hypoglycaemia symptoms. BLOODWORK/measures that clinicians track for this drug class: fasting plasma glucose and HbA1c (glycaemic response, and to catch hypos in at-risk users), renal function (creatinine/eGFR — especially if dehydrated), liver enzymes / hepatic steatosis markers (amycretin improved MASLD in animals), lipid panel, heart rate and blood pressure, hydration/electrolytes during GI illness, and body composition (not just scale weight) to track lean-mass preservation. Note this is an INVESTIGATIONAL drug with no approved monitoring guideline; grey-market product additionally carries identity/purity/dosing-accuracy risks that no bloodwork fully mitigates. This is not medical advice; decisions belong with a qualified clinician."

Caveats

HUMAN-DATA GAP: every efficacy number here is from tiny, short, early-phase trials (≤144 oral / 125 SC participants) funded and run largely by the manufacturer — these are dose-finding/safety studies, not the large phase 3 outcome trials that establish real-world efficacy and durability. The headline ~24% SC weight loss came alongside a high participant-withdrawal rate, so attrition could inflate the per-protocol figure; treat it as preliminary. The ~13% oral figure is quoted via a review, not a head-to-head. Long-term safety, cardiovascular outcomes, lean-mass loss, and weight regain after stopping are all UNKNOWN for amycretin specifically. PERSONA MISMATCH FLAG: amycretin is a peptide incretin drug — it has nothing to do with anabolic steroids, 17-alpha-alkylation, androgen-receptor signaling, or oral-steroid liver toxicity; do not import that framework. Calcitonin/amylin-receptor activation in vitro is established, but the precise human receptor stoichiometry driving the effect is not fully resolved. No dosing guidance is provided. Forum/anecdote claims (e.g. comparisons to retatrutide or 'best ever' weight loss) outrun the published evidence and should not be presented as established.
