Ecdysterone

The flagship "natural anabolic" — one suggestive human strength RCT, a plausible ERbeta-and-Akt story, and a supplement market where most products barely contain it.

Limited human data 13 citations
Class
Phytoecdysteroid (polyhydroxylated sterol); marketed as a non-hormonal / "hormone-receptor-independent" putative anabolic
Primary target
Proposed to act via estrogen receptor beta (ERbeta) rather than the androgen receptor (Parr 2014, cell/rodent; Tariq 2025, in-silico), driving Akt/mTOR-dependent muscle protein synthesis (Gorelick-Feldman 2008)
Human status
Emerging / mostly preclinical with one suggestive human efficacy RCT. The human anabolic case rests almost entirely on a single 10-week, single-center controlled trial in 46 young men (Isenmann 2019, PMID 31123801) reporting greater muscle mass and bench-press gains vs placebo — enough to prompt WADA to add ecdysterone to its Monitoring Program (monitored, not formally prohibited) around 2020. Counter-evidence: a 2025 12-week RCT of a commercial phytoecdysteroid product (Dissemond 2025, PMID 40781783; 28 enrolled/24 completed) found no benefit over placebo — but the capsules contained <1% of the labeled 20E with no biological activity, so it tested a near-empty product, not the molecule. A separate small trial (Denben 2023, PMID 37755852; n=20, 30 mg/day) found no strength/mass benefit during training but lower cortisol and possible muscle preservation during detraining. The pharma-grade form BIO101 (Biophytis) completed a Phase 2 sarcopenia trial (NCT03452488, SARA-INT, n=233, COMPLETED) — but that trial's primary mobility endpoint was severely compromised by COVID-era missing data (>50% of endpoint data missing per the registry record), so it does not constitute clean positive efficacy evidence. A Phase 2 trial of BIO101 for GLP-1-agonist (semaglutide)-associated muscle loss is registered but NOT YET RECRUITING (NCT07411378, n=164, start 2026); a COVID-19 respiratory trial (NCT04472728) was terminated. Bottom line: biologically plausible and safe-appearing in humans over the short term, one positive strength RCT, but efficacy is NOT established and is badly confounded by supplement quality.

At a glance

  • Increased muscle protein synthesis via PI3K/Akt/mTOR (robust in vitro and in rodents; one supportive human strength RCT, one null human trial)
  • Greater gains in muscle mass and 1-rep-max bench press vs placebo in a single 10-week human trial (Isenmann 2019)
  • Pro-differentiation of muscle cells (raised MyoD/myogenin, myotube size) in vitro/rodent
  • Possible anti-catabolic effect — lower cortisol/corticosterone, higher IGF-1 (rodent + two small human trials); one human trial suggested preserved muscle during detraining rather than added hypertrophy
  • Proposed to be independent of the androgen axis: does not raise testosterone, does not aromatize, no classical steroid-hormone signaling demonstrated in humans
  • Metabolic/anti-aging signals in rodents (calorie-restriction-mimetic-like effects) — preclinical/review-level only
  • Pharma form (BIO101) under development for sarcopenia and muscle wasting; Phase 2 sarcopenia trial completed but primary endpoint confounded by COVID-era missing data
  • Real-world effect heavily confounded by widespread under-dosing / mislabeling of commercial supplements

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It is NOT an androgen — no androgen-receptor pathway Evidence-rodent

    Unlike testosterone-type anabolic steroids, ecdysterone does not appear to work through the androgen receptor. In muscle-cell (C2C12) work an anti-androgen did not block its hypertrophic effect, and computational docking finds it does not engage the androgen receptor productively. This is the basis of the 'non-hormonal / natural' marketing claim — it does not raise or mimic testosterone. All supporting data are cell, rodent, or in-silico; this has not been tested at the receptor level in humans. Parr 2014, PMID 24974955 (rat + C2C12/HEK293); Tariq 2025, PMID 40455734 (in-silico)

  2. Proposed binding to estrogen receptor beta (ERbeta) Evidence-in vitro

    The leading academic hypothesis is that ecdysterone binds and activates ERbeta (the estrogen receptor subtype found in muscle), NOT ERalpha. In transfected HEK293 cells it transactivated an estrogen-response reporter, an ERbeta-selective agonist reproduced the muscle-cell growth, and an ERbeta-selective antagonist blocked it (Parr 2014). A 2025 all-atom molecular-dynamics study (in-silico, 250 ns) found ecdysterone forms stable, ERbeta-preferring complexes (Tariq 2025). This is a hypothesis built on cell and computational data — it has not been confirmed as the operative mechanism in humans, and ecdysterone is not a classical high-affinity estrogen. Parr 2014, PMID 24974955 (cell/rodent); Tariq 2025, PMID 40455734 (in-silico)

  3. Activation of PI3K/Akt and mTOR-driven protein synthesis Evidence-in vitro

    Downstream, ecdysterone increases muscle protein synthesis by turning on the PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway — the same 'build muscle' signaling that IGF-1 and resistance exercise use. In C2C12 and human primary myotubes it raised protein synthesis (up to ~20%) and the effect was blocked by a PI3K inhibitor; pharma-grade 20E (BIO101) rapidly phosphorylated Akt/mTOR and grew myotube diameter (+26%). This is the most reproducible molecular finding, but almost entirely in cultured cells and rodents. Gorelick-Feldman 2008, PMID 18444661; Serova 2024, PMID 38064183

  4. Competing upstream node: MAS receptor / Ang-(1-7) Evidence-rodent

    The company developing the drug form (Biophytis) reports a different entry point: in muscle cells BIO101 acts like angiotensin-(1-7) through the MAS receptor (the protective arm of the renin-angiotensin system), and a MAS-receptor antagonist (A779) abolishes its effect. This does not necessarily contradict the ERbeta story — both converge on Akt/mTOR — but it means the true proximal target in mammals is genuinely unsettled and mechanism claims should be read cautiously. Evidence is cell and mouse only. Serova 2024, PMID 38064183 (C2C12 + mouse)

  5. Pro-differentiation and anti-catabolic effects in muscle Evidence-rodent

    Beyond raw protein synthesis, ecdysterone pushes muscle precursor cells to mature (raising MyoD/myogenin, fusion index) and may blunt muscle breakdown. In rats it raised serum IGF-1 and lowered the catabolic hormone corticosterone; small human trials reported lower cortisol. These findings suggest a 'help muscle grow and resist wasting' profile, but the human anti-catabolic signal rests on two small trials (one showed lower cortisol only, with no hypertrophy after training). Parr 2014, PMID 24974955; Serova 2024, PMID 38064183; Denben 2023, PMID 37755852 (small human, cortisol only)

  6. Human performance signal — one suggestive RCT Evidence-human

    In the single supportive human RCT (46 young men, 10 weeks of strength training), the ecdysterone groups gained more muscle mass and, notably, more one-rep-max bench press than placebo, with no rise in liver or kidney injury markers. This is the entire human efficacy case, and it is what put ecdysterone on the WADA Monitoring Program. It is one modest, single-center controlled trial — encouraging but far from established, and a separate small human trial (Denben 2023) found no hypertrophy or strength gain during training. Isenmann 2019, PMID 31123801

  7. The supplement-quality confound: most products barely contain it Established

    A critical honesty check: when researchers assayed a commercial phytoecdysteroid supplement in a 12-week RCT, capsules contained less than 1% of the labeled 20E, showed zero biological activity in muscle cells, and produced no benefit over placebo. Anti-doping surveys likewise find many 'ecdysterone' products contain little or none (only 5 of 16 supplements had detectable 20E in one survey). So much of the real-world 'it did nothing for me' — and 'it worked' — experience may reflect what is actually in the bottle, not the molecule's pharmacology. Dissemond 2025, PMID 40781783; Kraiem 2021, PMID 33759363

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. No confirmed mammalian receptor. Leading hypothesis: agonism at estrogen receptor beta (ERbeta) — not ERalpha and not the androgen receptor. Evidence: in HEK293 cells ecdysterone transactivated ERalpha/ERbeta reporters; an ERbeta-selective agonist reproduced myotube hypertrophy and an ERbeta-selective antagonist blocked it; an anti-androgen did not block the effect (Parr 2014, PMID 24974955, cell + rat). A 2025 all-atom molecular-dynamics study (in-silico, 250 ns/system) found ecdysterone forms stable, low-fluctuation, high-H-bond-occupancy complexes preferentially with ERbeta over ERalpha or AR (Tariq 2025, PMID 40455734). Competing hypothesis from the pharma developer: agonism of the MAS receptor (mimicking angiotensin-(1-7)); the MAS antagonist A779 abolishes BIO101's effect on muscle cells (Serova 2024, PMID 38064183, C2C12 + mouse). Ecdysterone is not a classical high-affinity steroid ligand, and no proximal target is confirmed in humans.

Signalling cascade. Convergent output regardless of proximal receptor: activation of PI3K -> Akt -> mTOR, increasing muscle protein synthesis (blocked by a PI3K inhibitor in vitro), plus induction of myogenic transcription factors (MyoD, myogenin) driving myoblast differentiation and myotube growth; associated rise in IGF-1 and fall in catabolic corticosteroids in rodents (Gorelick-Feldman 2008, PMID 18444661; Serova 2024, PMID 38064183; Parr 2014, PMID 24974955).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (supplements and the BIO101 drug candidate); ecdysterone is also ingested unintentionally via foods such as spinach and quinoa.
Absorption / bioavailability
Poor systemic delivery. After spinach ingestion, only ~1.4% of ingested ecdysterone plus metabolites is recovered in urine (Yuliandra 2023, PMID 37161586). Cyclodextrin complexation has been explored to improve aqueous solubility (~100-fold) and potentially bioavailability (Temirgaziyev 2019, PMID 30458190).
Half-life
Not well characterized in humans; urinary detection windows imply relatively rapid clearance of parent (hours to a few days), with metabolites persisting somewhat longer. Formal plasma PK is being collected in the BIO101 GLP-1 program (NCT07411378).
Metabolism
Hepatic; principal human urinary metabolites include 14-deoxy-ecdysterone and 14-deoxy-poststerone, detectable after ingestion (Yuliandra 2023, PMID 37161586; Kraiem 2021, PMID 33759363). Poststerone is a further downstream metabolite with some independent muscle activity in rats (Csabi 2019, PMID 30923008).
Excretion
Renal. Parent and 14-deoxy metabolite detectable in urine within 2-5 h; after a low-dose supplement, parent detectable ~70 h and metabolite ~48 h; after a higher dose, parent ~96 h and metabolite ~48 h (Kraiem 2021, PMID 33759363).
Notes
Molecular formula C27H44O7, MW ~480.6, ChEMBL CHEMBL224128, max clinical phase 2, natural product; 1 Rule-of-Five violation, QED 0.35 — modestly drug-like. Dietary sources (spinach, quinoa, Rhaponticum/Leuzea teas, some topical creams) can raise urinary ecdysterone to hundreds-to->2000 ng/mL, complicating anti-doping interpretation (Kwiatkowska 2024, PMID 36480213).

Dose–response &amp; effect magnitudes

No validated human dose-response for anabolism. The one positive human RCT used ecdysterone-containing supplements and reported a dose-related trend toward greater muscle-mass and strength gains over placebo (Isenmann 2019, PMID 31123801). A negative RCT used a label-recommended regimen of a commercial product, but the capsules contained <1% of the claimed 20E, so it provides no usable dose-response information (Dissemond 2025, PMID 40781783). A small trial using 30 mg/day 20E showed no hypertrophy after training (Denben 2023, PMID 37755852). The BIO101 sarcopenia program tested defined oral doses (175 mg and 350 mg twice daily; NCT03452488). Preclinical work shows dose-dependent myotube hypertrophy in C2C12 cells. Interpreting supplement 'doses' is unreliable because actual content frequently diverges wildly from the label. No dosing protocol is provided here.

How it compares

  • vs Testosterone / classical anabolic-androgenic steroids
    same: Both aim to increase muscle mass/strength and both ultimately engage Akt/mTOR protein-synthesis signaling
    different: AAS act through the androgen receptor with potent, established, dose-dependent human anabolism and well-documented endocrine/cardiovascular/hepatic harms; ecdysterone does NOT appear to use the androgen receptor, does not aromatize or suppress the HPG axis, and has only one suggestive human efficacy trial with a benign short-term safety profile
  • vs Turkesterone (the other flagship 'natural anabolic')
    same: Both are phytoecdysteroids marketed together as non-hormonal natural anabolics with the same proposed Akt/mTOR mechanism
    different: Turkesterone has essentially NO controlled human efficacy trials (even weaker evidence than ecdysterone), is not the compound WADA singled out for monitoring, and its supplements are at least as prone to under-dosing/mislabeling
  • vs BIO101 (pharma-grade 20E, same molecule)
    same: Identical active molecule (20-hydroxyecdysone); same Akt/mTOR anabolic rationale
    different: BIO101 is a GMP, content-verified (>=97% purity), defined-dose drug candidate in formal Phase 2 trials (sarcopenia; GLP-1-associated muscle loss); OTC ecdysterone supplements are unregulated, frequently near-empty, and cannot be assumed equivalent
  • vs Diosgenin / other 'phytosteroid' stack partners
    same: Co-formulated in supplements on the theory of additive anabolic signaling through distinct pathways
    different: The combination has no positive human RCT; the one 20E+diosgenin RCT was null and the product was grossly under-dosed

History &amp; legal status

Ecdysterone was characterized as an arthropod molting hormone decades ago and studied since the 1980s-2000s as a putative mammalian anabolic/adaptogen (notably in Soviet/Russian sports-nutrition literature). Western mechanistic interest was catalyzed by Gorelick-Feldman 2008 (PI3K/protein synthesis) and Parr 2014 (ERbeta). The pivotal event was Isenmann 2019, whose positive human strength RCT led the authors to formally recommend inclusion on the WADA Prohibited List. Consequently, WADA placed ecdysterone on its Monitoring Program (around 2020) to gauge prevalence/potential for abuse; it is monitored, not formally prohibited. It remains legal and widely sold as a dietary supplement in most countries. In parallel, Biophytis is developing pharma-grade 20E (BIO101) as a drug for sarcopenia and muscle-wasting indications, with a completed Phase 2 sarcopenia trial (NCT03452488, endpoint confounded by COVID), a registered but not-yet-recruiting Phase 2 trial for semaglutide-associated muscle loss (NCT07411378), and a terminated COVID-19 respiratory trial (NCT04472728).

Key unknowns / open questions

  • What the true proximal mammalian target is — ERbeta, MAS receptor, both, or neither (no mammalian ecdysteroid receptor identified).
  • Whether the single positive human efficacy RCT replicates in larger, multi-center, content-verified trials — i.e., whether the anabolic effect is real in humans at all (one small human trial already found no hypertrophy).
  • How a compound with ~1% oral bioavailability produces meaningful systemic anabolism (mechanism-vs-exposure gap).
  • Long-term safety of chronic use, including any consequences of chronic ERbeta engagement if that mechanism is correct.
  • Whether reported anti-catabolic/anti-aging/metabolic (calorie-restriction-mimetic) effects seen in rodents and reviews translate to humans.
  • Real efficacy of OTC products given pervasive under-dosing — how much observed benefit/lack of benefit is pharmacology vs bottle contents.
  • Whether pharma-grade BIO101 clinical results (with the COVID-confounded sarcopenia trial re-read and new trials) will validate or undercut the supplement-market anabolic claims.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

Harm-reduction monitoring only (not a dosing protocol). Because short-term human safety looks benign but data are thin: (1) Product verification is the single most important step — seek third-party-assayed / certified-for-sport products with a Certificate of Analysis confirming actual ecdysterone content and screening for undeclared anabolic steroids or stimulants, since many products are near-empty or potentially adulterated. (2) Baseline and periodic liver and kidney function panels (ALT/AST, bilirubin, creatinine/eGFR) are reasonable given supplement-adulteration risk, even though ecdysterone itself did not raise these in the RCT. (3) Watch for any unexpected estrogen-like effects (none are documented in humans) and stop if unexplained symptoms arise. (4) Competitive athletes should treat it as a WADA-monitored substance: dietary and supplemental intake can produce urinary ecdysterone, so account for anti-doping exposure and record all supplement use. (5) People with hormone-sensitive conditions or on estrogen-modulating therapy should be cautious given the unresolved ERbeta hypothesis and discuss with a clinician. Anyone considering it as a drug-like agent for muscle wasting should do so within a clinical trial or under medical supervision rather than via OTC supplements. This is monitoring guidance only and deliberately provides no doses, titration, or 'how to run it' information.

Caveats

The primary mammalian target is genuinely unresolved: the two leading mechanisms (ERbeta vs MAS/Ang-(1-7)) come from different labs, rest on cell, rodent, and in-silico data, and no mammalian ecdysteroid receptor has been identified — so 'ERbeta agonist' should be read as a leading hypothesis, not a fact. Human efficacy is a single positive controlled trial (n=46) against one null human trial (n=20) and a well-conducted negative RCT that was undermined by a near-empty test product; this is not a settled effect. Real-world 'results' are dominated by a supplement-quality problem — assays repeatedly find commercial products with a fraction of, or none of, the labeled ecdysterone (e.g., only 5 of 16 products with detectable 20E in one survey), and some may contain undeclared or prohibited substances, so brand/lot matters enormously and cannot be verified by the user. Oral bioavailability is very low (only ~1.4% of an ingested dose from spinach is recovered in urine as parent+metabolites), which sits uneasily with claims of potent systemic anabolism. Because it is on the WADA Monitoring Program, competitive athletes can produce detectable urinary ecdysterone even from ordinary spinach or supplements, complicating anti-doping interpretation. This monograph describes mechanism and evidence; it is not dosing guidance.
