BPC-157

A 15-amino-acid fragment of a stomach protein that seems to help the body build new blood vessels and repair tissue faster — spectacular in rats, barely tested in humans.

Limited human data 23 citations
Class
Synthetic pentadecapeptide; experimental cytoprotective / pro-angiogenic tissue-repair agent (gastric-juice-derived)
Primary target
No single classical receptor
Human status
Overwhelmingly an animal story. The mechanism (VEGFR2/Akt/eNOS angiogenesis, NO modulation, GHR up-regulation, anti-inflammation) is built almost entirely on rat and cell-culture studies. Direct human evidence is thin: (1) ONE human-tissue mechanism study showing NO-dependent vessel relaxation in leftover bypass-surgery artery rings (PMID 42123221); and (2) only THREE small human pilot/case studies of actual use — intra-articular injection for knee pain (PMID 34324435), a symptom pilot in interstitial cystitis (PMID 39325560), and an IV-safety/pharmacokinetics pilot (PMID 40131143). A 2025 scoping review states plainly that human data are 'extremely limited' and BPC-157 should be considered investigational (PMID 40789979). No large randomized controlled trials, no regulatory approval. A heavily-cited older link to inflammatory bowel disease ('PL14736' clinical trials) traces to the original Croatian developer group (Sikiric et al.) and should be read with that conflict in mind. The most-cited mechanistic literature comes disproportionately from one research group, which is a real source-concentration caveat.

At a glance

  • Accelerated healing of tendon, ligament, muscle, bone and skin wounds (rodent models)
  • Promotion of new blood-vessel formation (angiogenesis) and improved blood flow to injured tissue
  • Protection of the GI lining; counteracts NSAID-, alcohol- and other drug-induced gut damage (rodent)
  • Anti-inflammatory: lowers inflammatory cytokine activity
  • Blood-vessel relaxation / improved microvascular function via nitric oxide (rodent + isolated human artery)
  • Reported analgesic and mood/gut-brain effects (rodent; mechanistically plausible, unproven in humans)

Mechanism, step by step

  1. It is a tiny, unusually stable peptide Established

    BPC-157 is a lab-made string of 15 amino acids copied from a protein found in human stomach juice. Its claim to fame is that it resists being chewed up by stomach acid and enzymes, so unlike most peptides it can survive in the gut — which is why people take it orally as well as by injection. Note: 'stable' here means stable in gastric juice, not necessarily a long half-life in blood. PMID 40005999; PMID 30915550

  2. It switches on the VEGFR2 angiogenesis pathway Evidence-rodent

    Its central action is to ramp up new blood-vessel growth. It does NOT raise the growth factor VEGF-A itself; instead it increases the amount of the VEGFR2 receptor on blood-vessel cells, helps that receptor get pulled inside the cell (internalization) where it signals, and fires the downstream VEGFR2 -> Akt -> eNOS cascade. Blocking that internalization abolishes the effect — so this is the load-bearing step. PMID 27847966

  3. It works through the nitric oxide (NO) system Evidence-rodent

    A lot of BPC-157's effects run through nitric oxide, the body's own 'open the blood vessels and protect tissue' molecule. It activates eNOS (the enzyme that makes NO) via a Src-Caveolin-1 route, which relaxes vessels and improves blood flow to injured areas. Drugs that block NO production (L-NAME) blunt its effects, and NO donors (L-arginine) mimic them — the classic fingerprint of an NO-dependent mechanism. PMID 33051481; PMID 23755725

  4. This vessel-relaxing effect has now been shown in actual human artery tissue Evidence-human

    In one of the very few human-tissue experiments, leftover internal mammary artery from bypass surgery relaxed in a dose-dependent way when exposed to BPC-157, and that relaxation depended on an intact vessel lining and was knocked down by an NO blocker. This is real human-tissue confirmation of the NO mechanism — but it is isolated artery rings in a dish, not a living person. PMID 42123221

  5. It makes tendon and connective-tissue cells more 'growth-hormone-ready' and more mobile Evidence-rodent

    In rat tendon fibroblasts it strongly up-regulates the growth hormone receptor, making those repair cells more responsive to growth hormone, and it boosts their survival, migration, and outgrowth (partly via FAK-paxillin, the cell's 'crawling and gripping' machinery). In plain terms it tells the cells that rebuild tendon and ligament to multiply, move into the wound, and lay down new tissue. PMID 25415472; PMID 21030672

  6. It calms inflammation and acts as a gut-and-vessel 'cytoprotectant' Evidence-rodent

    Across many rat injury models it reduces inflammatory signaling, stabilizes the gut lining and its permeability, and protects cells lining the stomach and blood vessels from damage (the 'cytoprotection' idea it was originally built around). This is why it counteracts NSAID and other drug-induced gut and organ injury in animals. PMID 41898733; PMID 32445447

  7. It nudges brain chemistry through a gut-brain axis Evidence-rodent

    Beyond healing, rat studies show it interacts with dopamine and serotonin systems and changes brain serotonin synthesis, which is proposed to underlie reported effects on mood, the gut-brain axis, and counteracting some drug side-effects. This is the most speculative arm — interesting signal in rodents, with no human confirmation. PMID 27138887; PMID 15531385; PMID 9073154

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. BPC-157 (sequence GEPPPGKPADDAGLV; MW ~1419 Da) has NO known classical high-affinity receptor — no measured Ki/Kd/EC50 against a defined receptor protein exists in the literature, which is a genuine and important gap [Established that none is published: PMID 42198317, 40005999]. It is best described as acting on a signalling HUB rather than a single target. (1) Pro-angiogenic axis: it does NOT act as a direct VEGF mimetic — in cell culture there is no direct angiogenic effect on cells; instead in vivo it UP-REGULATES VEGF/VEGFR2 expression and downstream signalling [Evidence-rodent/in vitro: PMID 20388964]. Effect is therefore indirect agonism of the VEGFR2 pathway, not receptor binding. (2) NO system: functional 'agonist-like' effect on eNOS-derived NO with a distinctive bidirectional/competitive relationship — it competes with BOTH L-NAME (NOS inhibitor) AND L-arginine (NOS substrate), behaving as a NO-system modulator rather than a pure agonist [Evidence-rodent: PMID 23755725, 33051481]. (3) Growth hormone receptor: up-regulates GHR transcription/protein in tendon fibroblasts (one of the most abundantly induced genes by cDNA microarray), sensitising cells to endogenous GH [Evidence-in vitro: PMID 25415472]. No affinity values are reported for any of these interactions.

Signalling cascade. Convergent, receptor-upstream-unclear cascade. ANGIOGENESIS/NO ARM: BPC-157 → activation of the non-receptor tyrosine kinase Src → phosphorylation of Caveolin-1 (Cav-1) → release of eNOS from its inhibitory Cav-1 complex (co-IP shows reduced Cav-1:eNOS binding) → eNOS phosphorylation/activation → NO generation (directly imaged by DAF-FM) → vasodilation + endothelial cell migration [Evidence-rodent/in vitro, Src-Cav-1-eNOS: PMID 33051481]. In parallel BPC-157 up-regulates VEGFR2 and feeds the VEGFR2–Akt–eNOS angiogenesis axis, plus engages ERK1/2 [Evidence-rodent, summarised: PMID 40789979]. CYTOPROTECTION/HUMAN VASCULAR: in human internal mammary artery rings, vasorelaxation is concentration-dependent and largely abolished by the NOS inhibitor L-NAME and by endothelial denudation — confirming an endothelium-dependent NO mechanism in HUMAN tissue (residual non-NO component remains) [Evidence-human ex vivo: PMID 42123221]. TENDON/FIBROBLAST ARM: BPC-157 → ↑GHR → permits GH→JAK2 signalling → ↑proliferation (PCNA); independently it drives FAK-paxillin phosphorylation → F-actin assembly → fibroblast spreading, migration and survival under H2O2 oxidative stress [Evidence-in vitro: PMID 25415472, 21030672]. CNS/gut-brain arm overlays the NO system onto dopaminergic and serotonergic tone [Evidence-rodent: PMID 34380875, 27138887].

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Studied/used by oral (drinking water in rodents), intramuscular, intraperitoneal, intragastric, intravenous, intra-articular, intravesical, and topical routes. Activity has been reported across all of these in preclinical work; human exposure to date is IV infusion and local injection only [PMID 42198317, 40131143, 39325560, 34324435].
Absorption / bioavailability
Unusual gastric-juice stability is the signature property (it was isolated from and is stable in human gastric juice), which underpins claimed oral activity [Established for stability: PMID 23755725, 34267654]. A formal preclinical ADME study in TWO species (cited in the 2026 biopharmaceutical review) reports LINEAR, dose-proportional kinetics and intramuscular bioavailability of 14–51% depending on species [Evidence-rodent/preclinical, via review: PMID 42198317]. No validated human bioavailability, no BCS classification, no permeability characterisation exists.
Half-life
Plasma half-life UNDER 30 minutes — confirmed in the two-species preclinical ADME study AND in a preliminary two-subject human pilot [Evidence-rodent + minimal-human: PMID 42198317]. This short half-life starkly contrasts with biological effects lasting hours to days — the central 'PK–PD disconnect' for this compound.
Metabolism
As a 15-residue linear peptide it is expected to undergo proteolytic/peptidase degradation (aminopeptidase/endopeptidase hydrolysis to constituent amino acids); however, NO specific metabolising enzymes, metabolite identification, or degradation-pathway studies are published [gap explicitly noted: PMID 42198317].
Excretion
Not formally characterised in humans; no renal/biliary excretion or mass-balance data are published. Small peptide fragments/amino acids would be expected to follow normal amino-acid handling, but this is inferential, not measured [gap: PMID 42198317].
Notes
No prodrug/ester forms are established. Claims of blood-brain-barrier penetration are inferred from CNS effects (stroke, schizophrenia-model, spinal-cord models) rather than from direct CNS PK measurement [Evidence-rodent, indirect: PMID 34380875]. The PK–PD disconnect (sub-30-min half-life vs multi-day effects) suggests effects are driven by triggered downstream cascades (gene expression, eNOS activation) rather than sustained plasma exposure. No pharmaceutical-grade, validated formulation exists; marketed product is research-chemical / compounding-pharmacy grade [PMID 42198317, 40005999].

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Rodent dose-response is wide and famously flat/non-monotonic: efficacy is reported across an extraordinary ~6-log range, from ~10 ng/kg up to ~10 µg/kg in many models, often with similar effect magnitude (a hallmark that complicates dose-finding) [Evidence-rodent: PMID 18818478 used 10 µg/kg AND 10 ng/kg with effect; 23755725]. Tendon-fibroblast effects (GHR up-regulation, migration, FAK-paxillin phosphorylation) are dose- and time-DEPENDENT in vitro [Evidence-in vitro: PMID 25415472, 21030672]. Human ex-vivo artery: concentration-dependent vasorelaxation over 0.01–1 mg/mL on IMA rings [Evidence-human ex vivo: PMID 42123221]. HUMAN in-vivo dose-response is essentially UNKNOWN: the only human exposures are an IV safety pilot (10 mg then 20 mg infusions, n=2) [PMID 40131143], a single 10 mg intravesical injection for interstitial cystitis (n=12) [PMID 39325560], and a retrospective intra-articular knee series (n=16) [PMID 34324435] — none designed to establish a dose-response curve, none placebo-controlled. No validated human dosing regimen exists [PMID 42198317, 40789979]. (No dosing protocol is provided here — these figures describe what was studied, not a recommendation.)

How it compares

  • vs TB-500 / Thymosin beta-4
    same: Both are 'repair/regenerative' peptides popular in the same fitness/PED community, both used off-label by injection for tendon/muscle/wound healing, both lack FDA approval and robust human RCTs; commonly stacked together (e.g., the knee-pain series combined BPC-157 + TB4)
    different: Thymosin beta-4 is a 43-residue actin-sequestering protein with a defined molecular target (G-actin) and an angiogenic/cell-migration mechanism via actin dynamics; BPC-157 is a 15-residue gastric-derived peptide with NO defined receptor, acting through VEGFR2/NO/GHR hubs. BPC-157's signature is gastric-juice stability and oral activity, which TB4 lacks
  • vs Standard angiogenic growth factors (e.g., recombinant VEGF)
    same: Share the angiogenesis endpoint and converge on the VEGF/VEGFR2 pathway to promote vascularised healing
    different: Recombinant VEGF is a direct receptor agonist driving maximal neovascularisation (with oedema/tumour-feeding concerns); BPC-157 has NO direct angiogenic effect on cells and instead MODULATES/up-regulates endogenous VEGF, claimed to preserve 'angiogenic privilege' (no corneal neovascularisation) and show anti-tumour effects — a regulatory vs maximal-stimulus distinction
  • vs PL14736 / PLD-116 (the pharmaceutical BPC-157 program, Pliva/Croatia)
    same: Same pentadecapeptide molecule; PL14736 IS BPC-157 developed for inflammatory bowel disease and reached Phase II
    different: PL14736 was a regulated, GMP, defined-indication clinical program (topical/local IBD) with quality control; today's consumer BPC-157 is research-chemical/compounding-grade with no validated formulation, no completed Phase II, and broad off-label injectable use

Interactions & stacking mechanism only — not a protocol

  • CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin potentiates ↑ Evidence-rodent
    Mechanistic complementarity on the GH axis. BPC-157 up-regulates the growth-hormone RECEPTOR (GHR) on tendon/connective-tissue fibroblasts, making those repair cells more responsive to GH (one of the most abundantly induced genes in rat Achilles-tendon fibroblasts). CJC-1295 (GHRH-receptor analog) + Ipamorelin (GHS-R1a agonist) raise circulating GH and therefore IGF-1, supplying more LIGAND for the receptor BPC-157 has sensitized. Since the GH/IGF-1 axis drives collagen synthesis in connective tissue, 'more receptor' (BPC-157) plus 'more hormone' (secretagogues) is a plausible potentiation of connective-tissue repair rather than two redundant effects. BPC-157 GHR up-regulation PMID 25415472; CJC-1295+Ipamorelin monograph (GHRHR + GHS-R1a raise GH/IGF-1) Watch: Watch fasting glucose and HbA1c (GH excess drives insulin resistance), and watch for fluid retention, carpal-tunnel symptoms, or joint aching from elevated GH/IGF-1. Given the shared pro-growth signaling, keep cancer screening current. The GHR-sensitization data are rat-tendon in-vitro only; human magnitude is unproven.
  • GHK-Cu potentiates ↑ Evidence-rodent
    These two do NOT share a molecular target: BPC-157 works through the VEGFR2->Akt->eNOS/NO angiogenesis hub and up-regulates the growth hormone receptor on connective-tissue fibroblasts, whereas GHK-Cu is a copper-carrying SPARC-derived matrikine that chemoattracts macrophages/capillary cells and feeds copper into fibroblast collagen/elastin/GAG synthesis and MMP/TIMP remodeling. They CONVERGE on the same downstream endpoints - angiogenesis plus extracellular-matrix rebuild - by different routes, so the pairing is additive/potentiating on tissue repair rather than a shared receptor. Mechanistic rationale is animal/in-vitro only; no controlled human trial has tested the pair against either alone. PMID 18644225 (GHK-Cu chemoattraction/angiogenesis/matrix remodeling); PMID 27847966 (BPC-157 VEGFR2 angiogenesis); PMID 20388964 (BPC-157 VEGF up-regulation in vivo) Watch: Both are unapproved gray-market injectables - watch injection sites for redness, swelling, warmth, induration or discharge (infection/sterile abscess/contamination). Because two pro-angiogenic stimuli stack, be alert to any new or growing lump and stop; there is no validated human safety data for the combination.
  • MK-677 potentiates ↑ Evidence-rodent
    Same GHR-sensitization logic as with injectable secretagogues, but oral and sustained: BPC-157 raises GHR expression on connective-tissue repair cells, while MK-677 (oral GHS-R1a/ghrelin-receptor agonist) chronically elevates GH and IGF-1, supplying persistent ligand for the up-regulated receptor and plausibly amplifying the GH/IGF-1-driven collagen-synthesis arm of repair. The interaction is on GH SIGNALING, not a shared receptor: BPC-157 changes receptor density, MK-677 changes hormone level. BPC-157 GHR up-regulation PMID 25415472; MK-677 monograph (GHSR-1a raises GH/IGF-1, insulin resistance) Watch: MK-677 notably worsens insulin sensitivity and raises fasting glucose, monitor glucose/HbA1c and watch for edema and increased appetite. Elevated IGF-1 plus pro-angiogenic BPC-157 both argue for current cancer screening. The GHR-upregulation evidence is rodent/in-vitro; treat the potentiation as mechanistically plausible, not proven in humans.
  • Anadrol counteracts ↓ Evidence-rodent
    Opposing actions on the liver. Anadrol is a 17-alpha-alkylated oral androgen that is markedly hepatotoxic (cholestasis, transaminase elevation, peliosis risk). BPC-157 is a broad cytoprotectant that, in numerous rodent models, counteracts drug-induced LIVER and gut injury (it protects hepatocytes and gut lining against NSAIDs, alcohol, and even paracetamol overdose). So BPC-157 could in principle blunt (counteract) the hepatocellular stress a 17aa oral imposes. IMPORTANT: this is a rodent cytoprotection story against DIFFERENT toxins, not proof it neutralizes anabolic-steroid cholestasis in humans, and it must never be read as license to run a hepatotoxic oral. BPC-157 hepatoprotection/cytoprotection PMID 32445447 (monograph step 6/expand); Anadrol monograph (17aa hepatotoxicity PMID 12646793, 27372877) Watch: This is where the harm-reduction point matters most: do NOT assume BPC-157 makes a hepatotoxic oral 'safe.' Track liver function (ALT, AST, GGT, bilirubin) at baseline and periodically, and stop the androgen for rising enzymes or jaundice/dark urine regardless of peptide use. Anadrol also raises hematocrit, monitor CBC/hematocrit independently.
  • Superdrol counteracts ↓ Evidence-rodent
    Same hepatic axis as Anadrol. Superdrol is one of the most hepatotoxic oral androgens (rapid cholestatic transaminitis, often with pruritus and high bilirubin). BPC-157's demonstrated rodent hepatoprotection against toxin-induced liver injury (NSAIDs, alcohol, paracetamol) gives a mechanistic basis for it partially counteracting the hepatocellular insult, but only in animal models against OTHER hepatotoxins, so the protective effect against 17aa-steroid cholestasis in humans is unproven and cannot be assumed. BPC-157 hepatoprotection PMID 32445447 (monograph); Superdrol monograph (cholestatic 17aa hepatotoxicity, lowers HDL) Watch: Do not treat BPC-157 as hepatoprotective cover for a very hepatotoxic oral. Baseline and periodic ALT/AST/GGT/bilirubin; discontinue the androgen for enzyme elevation, malaise, jaundice, or itching. Superdrol also crushes HDL, check a lipid panel independently.
  • Clenbuterol opposes Evidence-rodent
    Opposing effects on vascular tone and the cardiovascular system. BPC-157 is an endothelium-dependent, NO-mediated VASODILATOR, it relaxes vessels (confirmed in human internal mammary artery, abolished by the NOS blocker L-NAME) and helps maintain/normalize blood pressure via the eNOS/NO pathway. Clenbuterol is a beta-2 adrenergic agonist that drives sympathetic cardiovascular stimulation: tachycardia, palpitations, raised blood pressure, and cardiac strain. Their acute vascular directions oppose (NO-vasodilation vs adrenergic cardiovascular stimulation). This is opposition on vascular/cardiovascular tone, not a shared target. BPC-157 NO vasodilation/BP maintenance PMID 42123221 (human IMA), 33051481 (Src-Cav-1-eNOS, rodent), 23755725; Clenbuterol monograph (beta-2 agonist, tachycardia, hypertension, hypokalemia) Watch: Monitor blood pressure and heart rate (clenbuterol drives both up; BPC-157's NO effect leans the other way, so net effect is unpredictable). Clenbuterol independently causes hypokalemia and cardiac strain, check potassium and be alert to palpitations/arrhythmia. BPC-157's vascular data are largely rodent plus one ex-vivo human artery study.
  • TB-500 redundant Evidence-rodent
    Both are grey-market repair peptides that converge on the SAME downstream outcomes by DIFFERENT molecular routes: BPC-157 drives angiogenesis via VEGFR2/Akt/eNOS-NO and primes fibroblasts via GHR up-regulation, while TB-500 sequesters G-actin to speed repair-cell migration, up-regulates the Ang1/Tie2 + VEGF angiogenesis axis, and suppresses NF-kB inflammation. They share the angiogenesis and anti-inflammatory endpoints (redundant/overlapping) but have NO shared molecular target, which is why they are marketed together as the 'Wolverine stack.' Because both independently promote new blood-vessel growth, they also ADD to the same theoretical pro-angiogenic concern (could in principle feed an occult tumor or pathological neovascularization). BPC-157 monograph (VEGFR2/eNOS PMID 27847966, 33051481; pro-angiogenic tumor concern PMID 40005999); TB-500 monograph (G-actin PMID 23084823; Ang1/Tie2 angiogenesis PMID 30500399; tumor-migration concern PMID 11311852) Watch: Both are WADA-banned and non-pharmaceutical-grade (endotoxin/purity risk at each injection site). Given the additive pro-angiogenic biology, keep age-appropriate cancer screening current and investigate any new or growing mass; avoid with active or recent malignancy. Watch injection sites for redness, swelling, nodules, or infection. No human trial has ever compared the combination to either peptide alone, so any 'synergy' claim is anecdote, not evidence.

History & legal status

"BPC-157 is a synthetic 15-amino-acid fragment (GEPPPGKPADDAGLV) of 'Body Protection Compound', a protein isolated from human gastric juice; preclinical research dates back over three decades, largely from Sikiric and colleagues at the University of Zagreb [Established: PMID 23755725, 34267654]. It entered formal pharmaceutical development as PL-10 / PLD-116 / PL14736 (Pliva, Croatia) for inflammatory bowel disease and reached Phase II trials [PMID 18818478, 17713731]. Regulatory status: NOT approved by the FDA or other major regulators for any indication, owing to absence of adequate human clinical trials; no completed Phase II is on record in the modern review literature [PMID 42198317, 40005999]. WADA: temporarily/effectively flagged around 2022 but is NOT currently on the WADA Prohibited List per the 2025 patent-and-literature review [PMID 40005999] — verify against the live current WADA list before any sport-context claim. In 2023 the FDA placed BPC-157 in a category of substances it considered not eligible for compounding (rising regulatory scrutiny of consumer/compounded peptide products) — reflected in the translational-barriers review's discussion of compounding regulation [context: PMID 42198317]. It remains widely sold online as a 'research chemical' [PMID 40005999, 40789979]."

Key unknowns / open questions

  • No identified high-affinity receptor or binding target — the actual molecular initiator upstream of Src/VEGFR2/GHR is unknown, so no Ki/EC50 can be assigned [PMID 42198317]
  • The PK–PD disconnect: how a sub-30-min plasma half-life produces effects lasting hours-to-days is unexplained (triggered gene-expression cascade? tissue retention? active metabolite?) [PMID 42198317]
  • Human in-vivo dose-response and efficacy are essentially unknown — total human data are <30 subjects in 3 uncontrolled, mostly unblinded pilots [PMID 40789979, 42198317]
  • Long-term safety, including the theoretical tumour-angiogenesis question, is unresolved — originating-lab anti-tumour data vs independent-reviewer concern remain contested [PMID 40573323 vs 40005999]
  • Metabolism and excretion pathways (which peptidases, what metabolites, renal vs biliary clearance) are uncharacterised [PMID 42198317]
  • Whether oral dosing yields meaningful systemic exposure in humans (vs purely local gut effects) is unproven despite gastric-juice stability [PMID 42198317, 34267654]
  • Real-world product identity/purity: most consumer material is unvalidated, so reported anecdotal effects/harms cannot be cleanly attributed to BPC-157 itself [PMID 42198317, 40005999]

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

"Harm-reduction monitoring only — no dosing guidance. Given the NO/vascular mechanism and the (very thin) human data, reasonable parameters to watch with a clinician include: blood pressure and heart rate (NO-mediated vasodilation could lower BP) [PMID 42123221, 33051481]; the biomarker panels used in the human IV pilot — comprehensive metabolic panel (liver enzymes, renal function/creatinine), fasting glucose, thyroid panel, and CBC — as a baseline-and-follow-up safety screen [PMID 40131143]; signs related to altered haemostasis (unusual bruising/bleeding or, conversely, clotting symptoms), since rodent data show context-dependent effects on thrombosis/bleeding especially alongside anticoagulants like aspirin/warfarin/heparin [PMID 23755725]; injection-site reactions, infection, or systemic reaction (product is non-pharmaceutical-grade, raising contamination/endotoxin risk) [PMID 42198317]; and, given pro-angiogenic biology and the unresolved tumour question, a low threshold to investigate any new or growing mass and to avoid use in those with active or recent malignancy until human safety is established [Contested: PMID 40005999, 40573323]. Sport-tested individuals should re-check the CURRENT WADA Prohibited List before use [PMID 40005999]. None of this substitutes for individualized medical supervision."

Caveats

EVIDENCE QUALITY: Direction and targets are reasonably consistent (pro-angiogenic via VEGFR2/NO, pro-repair via GHR/fibroblasts), but MAGNITUDE in humans is unknown — dosing in people is extrapolated from rodents with no validated human pharmacokinetics beyond one tiny IV pilot. SOURCE CONCENTRATION: A large fraction of the favorable mechanistic and therapeutic literature originates from a single group (Sikiric and colleagues, the compound's developers) — independent replication exists for the core VEGFR2/eNOS angiogenesis findings (e.g. the Taiwanese groups, PMID 27847966, 33051481) but is much thinner for the broader 'protects every organ' claims. NO 'RECEPTOR': there is no identified BPC-157 receptor; it acts through pathway modulation, so 'primary target' is a best-supported hub, not a clean lock-and-key. SAFETY/REGULATORY: Not approved by FDA/EMA; FDA has flagged it and it cannot be legally compounded; banned by WADA (prohibited in sport). Long-term safety in humans is essentially unstudied, and a theoretical concern exists that a pro-angiogenic agent could feed tumor or unwanted vessel growth — unproven but not ruled out. Retraction check (live): the three load-bearing primaries (PMID 27847966, 20388964, 25415472) are NOT retracted as of this run. ANECDOTE: strong fitness/PED-community enthusiasm exists but is anecdote, not evidence, and must be kept separate from the rodent data above.
