Methyltrienolone

The lab's reference standard for "how tightly can a molecule grab the androgen receptor" — and, in the body, one of the most potent and most liver-toxic oral anabolic steroids ever made.

Human / approved 18 citations
Class
17alpha-methylated 19-nor (estrane) trienolone; oral anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS); non-aromatizable, essentially non-metabolized synthetic androgen; laboratory AR radioligand ([3H]R1881)
Primary target
Androgen receptor (AR / NR3C4) — very high-affinity full agonist; used as the benchmark radioligand for measuring AR
Human status
Not an approved therapeutic drug. Methyltrienolone was developed by Roussel-Uclaf in the 1960s-70s (the code 'R1881'/'R-1881' is the origin of its common lab name). Its enduring, best-documented scientific role is as a laboratory tool: [3H]methyltrienolone (R1881) is a classic reference radioligand for quantifying the androgen receptor in tissue homogenates and for prostate-cancer receptor assays, including prognostic receptor-content studies (PMID 84428, 698975, 2578085, 2422446, 3490602). It is frequently stated in secondary/lore sources that metribolone was trialed as an oral therapeutic for advanced prostate and breast cancer and abandoned because of hepatotoxicity; this specific therapeutic-use-then-abandonment narrative is NOT well documented in the primary literature indexed here (the traceable human work is receptor-assay and prognostic, not administration as a treatment), so it should be treated as unverified rather than established. Today it circulates only as a research chemical / illicit ultra-potent oral AAS in bodybuilding, with no legitimate human medical use, no pharmaceutical quality control, and no dosing standard. Direct controlled human efficacy/safety trials as a performance drug do not exist; human evidence is old oncology receptor work plus class-level AAS toxicology and anecdote.

At a glance

  • Very high milligram-for-milligram androgenic/anabolic potency — historically described as active at low (sub-milligram) doses, among the most potent oral AAS ever characterized (potency figures come from historical bioassay/lore, not modern controlled human trials)
  • Non-aromatizable and a poor 5-alpha-reductase substrate: effects are not modulated into estrogen or DHT the way testosterone's are
  • Severe hepatotoxicity expected as a potent 17alpha-alkylated oral (cholestasis, raised liver enzymes, risk of peliosis hepatis / hepatic tumors) — the dominant clinical concern; the specific data are class-level for 17aa orals
  • Cross-receptor activity shown in vitro/rodent: glucocorticoid-receptor binding, progesterone-receptor (progestogenic) activity, and mineralocorticoid-receptor antagonism, so the effect profile is not purely 'androgen'
  • Strong HPG-axis suppression expected (shutdown of natural testosterone) as a class effect of potent AR agonists
  • Its most durable, best-documented real-world role is as a laboratory benchmark: [3H]R1881 is the classic radioligand for quantifying androgen receptor in tissue and for prostate-cancer receptor assays

Mechanism, step by step

  1. Binds the androgen receptor with very high affinity Established (in vitro / rodent tissue)

    Metribolone locks onto the androgen receptor tightly. In castrated-rat prostate cytosol its dissociation constant was measured at Kd = 1.25 x 10^-10 M (~0.125 nM), a stronger grip than testosterone or DHT. Because it binds so well and is not broken down, [3H]R1881 became the standard radioligand labs use to *measure* the androgen receptor itself. (Note: affinity is tissue-dependent — in rat liver cytosol reported Kd is far weaker, ~2.5-3.3 x 10^-8 M, so the sub-nanomolar figure is the prostate/AR value, not universal.) PMID 8866837; PMID 84428; PMID 2422446

  2. Resists metabolism, so it stays around and works orally Established (in vitro / rodent)

    The 17-alpha-methyl group blocks the liver from quickly clearing it (making it orally active), and the 19-nor 4,9,11-triene structure means it is not aromatized to estrogen and not a good 5-alpha-reductase substrate. It is repeatedly described as a 'not-metabolized-in-vivo' androgen — in male rat liver cytosol, 97% of [3H]R1881 was recovered chemically unchanged after 20 h of incubation. PMID 3872380; PMID 3495627

  3. Activated AR moves to the nucleus and switches on anabolic genes Established (mechanism in vitro; downstream physiologic outputs are class-level for AR agonists)

    Like all androgens, the ligand-bound receptor sheds chaperones, translocates to the nucleus, dimerizes, and binds androgen response elements (AREs) in DNA to turn genes on. R1881 does this potently: in LNCaP prostate cells it drove PSA gene expression within ~6 h at the transcriptional level, and a -1600/+12 PSA-promoter reporter gave ~110-fold induction at 1 nM R1881. This transcriptional program is what produces muscle protein synthesis, prostate growth, and red-cell stimulation. PMID 1724287; PMID 37717994

  4. It is NOT clean for AR — it also binds glucocorticoid, progesterone and mineralocorticoid receptors Established (in vitro / rodent binding); human physiologic consequence uncharacterized

    R1881's 'reference ligand' status comes with a well-documented caveat: it also binds the glucocorticoid receptor in rat muscle and liver (which is why labs add triamcinolone to block cross-binding), binds progesterone receptors (human PR Kd ~1 nM, rat ~6 nM), and binds the mineralocorticoid receptor with affinity equal to aldosterone — where it acts as an MR *antagonist*. These cross-reactivities are established from in-vitro and rodent-tissue binding studies; the net in-vivo consequence in humans is not characterized. PMID 6975208; PMID 6331850; PMID 17105867; PMID 2457134

  5. As a 19-nortestosterone, it is expected to carry progestogenic activity Established for 19-nor class (nandrolone/trenbolone); inferred for metribolone — no direct human data

    Metribolone is a 19-nor (estrane) steroid, the same chemical family as nandrolone and trenbolone. For that family, intrinsic progesterone-receptor (progestogenic) activity plus a tendency to raise prolactin is an established route to gynecomastia and sexual side effects independent of estrogen — which is relevant because R1881 cannot be aromatized. IMPORTANT: this progestin/prolactin mechanism is established for nandrolone/trenbolone as a class; it has NOT been demonstrated for metribolone specifically, so its application here is an inference from shared structure, not direct data. corpus MoA: progesterone_receptor; corpus MoA: prolactin_dopamine

  6. The 17-alpha-methyl group makes it directly toxic to liver cells Established (17aa-oral class effect; human case-level + rodent hepatocyte data)

    The same modification that lets it survive first-pass metabolism makes it hepatotoxic. 17-alpha-alkylated oral steroids as a class impair bile transport (BSEP/ABCB11, MRP2) causing cholestasis, are directly toxic to cultured hepatocytes (LDH release, glutathione depletion, oxidative stress), and at the severe end are linked to peliosis hepatis and hepatic tumors. Given metribolone's high potency per milligram, this class liver risk is its defining safety problem — though the specific toxicity data are class-level (17aa AAS), not metribolone-specific. PMID 27372877; PMID 29521706; PMID 8527826; corpus MoA: hepatotoxicity_17aa_orals

  7. Strong AR agonism suppresses the body's own hormone axis Established (class effect of AR agonists; not measured for metribolone in humans)

    Any potent AR agonist feeds back negatively on the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, shutting down endogenous LH/FSH and testosterone production. Because metribolone is a potent, non-aromatizable AR agonist, HPG suppression is expected — this is a class-level established effect of AR agonists / AAS, not a metribolone-specific human measurement. corpus MoA: androgen_receptor; corpus MoA: hpg_axis; PMID 37717994

Receptor pharmacology

Binding & selectivity. Methyltrienolone (17beta-hydroxy-17alpha-methyl-estra-4,9,11-trien-3-one) is a very high-affinity full agonist of the androgen receptor (AR/NR3C4). Reported affinity: Kd = 1.25 x 10^-10 M (~0.125 nM) in castrated-rat prostate cytosol (PMID 8866837); high-affinity binding also demonstrated in human benign and malignant prostate cytosol (PMID 84428). Affinity is tissue-dependent — in rat liver cytosol the reported Kd is far weaker (~2.5-3.3 x 10^-8 M; PMID 3872380, 6331850). Its defining feature is that it binds AR tightly AND resists metabolism/aromatization, which is why [3H]R1881 became the standard AR radioligand. Critically it is NOT AR-monospecific: it binds the glucocorticoid receptor in rat skeletal muscle and liver (PMID 6975208, 6331850, 3486322), binds progesterone receptors (human PR Kd ~1 nM, rat ~6 nM; PMID 2457134), and binds the mineralocorticoid receptor with affinity equal to aldosterone where it acts as an MR antagonist (PMID 17105867). Some tissues (e.g., placenta) also contain a non-AR high-affinity R1881 binding protein (PMID 3259998), underscoring that R1881 binding does not equal 'AR only.'

Signalling cascade. Ligand binding -> AR conformational change and chaperone release -> nuclear translocation -> receptor dimerization -> binding to androgen response elements (AREs) -> recruitment of coactivators and RNA Pol II -> transcription of anabolic/androgenic target genes. R1881 is a benchmark agonist in this cascade: in LNCaP prostate cells it induced PSA expression at the transcriptional level within ~6 h, and a PSA-promoter reporter (-1600 to +12) gave ~110-fold induction at 1 nM R1881 via a defined ARE (PMID 1724287). Downstream physiologic outputs (established for AR agonists as a class, not measured for metribolone in humans): skeletal-muscle hypertrophy, erythropoiesis stimulation, prostate growth, sebaceous/hair effects, and negative HPG feedback (PMID 37717994; corpus MoA androgen_receptor, hpg_axis).

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Route / form
Oral (17alpha-methylation confers oral bioavailability); [3H]R1881 also used in vitro / as a research radioligand.
Absorption / bioavailability
Orally active due to 17alpha-methyl group blocking rapid first-pass hepatic inactivation. Precise human bioavailability figures for this specific compound are not established in the literature.
Half-life
Not characterized in humans; functionally long-acting relative to testosterone because it is not degraded quickly.
Metabolism
Distinctively resistant to metabolism — described as 'not metabolized in vivo.' Not aromatized to estrogen and a poor 5-alpha-reductase substrate (19-nor 4,9,11-triene A/B-ring). In male rat liver cytosol, 97% of [3H]R1881 was recovered chemically unchanged after 20 h (PMID 3872380). This metabolic stability is exactly why it is used as an AR tracer and contributes to its per-milligram potency.
Excretion
Human excretion/metabolite data specific to metribolone are sparse (it never became a marketed human drug); as a 17aa oral it undergoes slow hepatic handling, which contributes to hepatotoxicity rather than rapid clearance.
Notes
The same metabolic resistance that makes it a clean lab ligand makes it hazardous in vivo: it is not detoxified quickly, and its receptor cross-reactivity (GR/PR/MR, shown in vitro/rodent) means circulating drug could act at multiple nuclear receptors simultaneously.

Dose–response & effect magnitudes

Historically characterized as active at very low doses (commonly cited as under ~1 mg/day for anabolic/androgenic effect), reflecting its high AR affinity and metabolic stability — this is the basis of the 'most potent oral AAS' description. IMPORTANT: this potency figure comes from historical pharmacology/comparative bioassay and lore, NOT from modern controlled human dose-response trials of this compound; treat it as order-of-magnitude, not precise. In-vitro AR occupancy is essentially maximal at low-nanomolar concentrations (AR/prostate Kd ~0.1 nM). Hepatotoxicity from 17aa orals is dose- and duration-dependent (corpus MoA hepatotoxicity_17aa_orals; PMID 29521706), and given metribolone's per-milligram potency the therapeutic-to-toxic window is expected to be extremely narrow. NO dosing protocol is provided or implied here.

How it compares

  • vs Testosterone
    same: Both are AR agonists producing anabolic/androgenic gene programs and HPG suppression
    different: Metribolone is orally active, non-aromatizable (no estrogen), a poor 5a-reductase substrate, and — as a 17aa oral — markedly more hepatotoxic; testosterone is injectable, aromatizes to estradiol, and is not liver-toxic in normal use. Note: high AR affinity does not guarantee proportionally higher activity at every androgen endpoint (in castrated rats R1881 was a weak restorer of mating behaviour vs testosterone).
  • vs Trenbolone (its non-methylated cousin)
    same: Both are 19-nor 4,9,11-trienolones — same core, both potent non-aromatizable AR agonists; the 19-nor progestogenic/prolactin liability is characterized for trenbolone
    different: Metribolone adds a 17alpha-methyl group making it ORAL and (as a 17aa) hepatotoxic; trenbolone is used as injectable esters and is not 17aa-hepatotoxic
  • vs Nandrolone / other 19-nors
    same: Shared 19-nor structure; nandrolone-class progestogenic activity drives AI-resistant gyno and sexual side effects (metribolone shares the structure, so the same liability is inferred)
    different: Metribolone is far more potent per mg and, being 17aa oral, far more hepatotoxic than injectable nandrolone
  • vs DHT
    same: Both non-aromatizable high-affinity AR agonists
    different: DHT is the endogenous potent androgen and is rapidly metabolized; R1881 resists metabolism, is orally active, cross-reacts with GR/PR/MR (in vitro), and is used as the lab tracer
  • vs SARMs
    same: Both act through AR to drive anabolic transcription
    different: SARMs are designed for tissue selectivity (more muscle/bone, less prostate/liver) and are non-steroidal; metribolone is the opposite — a highly potent, non-selective, hepatotoxic steroid, and its radioligand R1881 is used to *characterize* AR/SARM binding

History & legal status

Developed by Roussel-Uclaf in the 1960s-70s (the code 'R1881'/'R-1881' is the origin of its common lab name). Its durable, best-documented role has been as a biochemical reagent: tritiated methyltrienolone ([3H]R1881) is a classic reference radioligand for androgen-receptor quantification, prostate-cancer receptor assays, and characterization of AR ligands (PMID 84428, 698975, 2578085, 2422446, 3490602). Claims that it was trialed and then abandoned as an oral cancer therapeutic specifically because of hepatotoxicity are widely repeated in secondary sources but are not well substantiated in the primary literature indexed here, and should be treated as unverified. It has no legitimate marketed human use. As an anabolic-androgenic steroid it falls under controlled-substance / anti-doping frameworks (a WADA-prohibited anabolic agent) and circulates illicitly as an ultra-potent 'research chemical' oral in bodybuilding, with no pharmaceutical quality control.

Key unknowns / open questions

  • Whether metribolone was ever genuinely used as a cancer therapeutic (vs solely a receptor-assay reagent) is not clearly documented in the primary literature — the 'abandoned drug' story is largely secondary/lore.
  • Precise human pharmacokinetics (bioavailability, half-life, clearance, metabolite profile) are uncharacterized — it never completed drug development.
  • Quantitative human dose-response and the true toxic threshold are not established from controlled trials; potency claims rest on historical bioassays.
  • The net in-vivo consequence in humans of the in-vitro GR / PR / MR activity (electrolytes, cortisol-like signaling, progestin effects) is undefined.
  • Whether the progestogenic/prolactin gyno liability of 19-nors actually manifests with metribolone specifically has not been demonstrated (it is inferred from nandrolone/trenbolone).
  • Long-term cardiovascular and hepatic tumor risk specific to metribolone (versus 17aa AAS as a class) is unquantified.
  • Whether its high AR affinity translates to a proportionally higher anabolic response in humans, or is offset by cross-receptor and toxicity ceilings, is unknown — and rodent data show affinity does not predict every endpoint.
  • Real-world illicit products are frequently mislabeled/underdosed or contaminated, so anecdote cannot be mapped to the pure compound.

What to monitor

Harm-reduction context — not a dosing protocol.

This section describes what a clinician would MONITOR for harm reduction — it is not medical advice and contains NO dosing guidance. Given the dominant risk, liver is the priority: liver function tests (ALT, AST, GGT, ALP), bilirubin, and watchfulness for jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, right-upper-quadrant pain, or unexplained fatigue/nausea (signs of cholestasis or hepatotoxicity). Because 17aa orals are linked to peliosis hepatis and hepatic tumors, unexplained abdominal pain or a palpable mass warrants imaging. Cardiovascular/metabolic: lipid panel (expect HDL suppression and raised LDL), blood pressure, and hematocrit/hemoglobin (erythrocytosis risk). Endocrine: LH, FSH, total/free testosterone, and prolactin (as a 19-nor, a progestin/prolactin route could drive gyno/sexual dysfunction that aromatase inhibitors will NOT fix — this liability is inferred from the 19-nor class). In women, watch for virilization (voice change, hirsutism, menstrual disruption), some of which is irreversible. The single most useful 'monitoring' insight from the evidence is preventive: the liver risk of any potent 17alpha-alkylated oral is severe and largely a function of the 17aa oral structure itself, so the real mitigation is avoiding/limiting oral 17aa AAS rather than relying on liver-support supplements (TUDCA/NAC have only limited evidence). Any concerning LFT rise or symptom should prompt cessation and medical evaluation.

Caveats

Several things are frequently overstated or conflated. (1) \"Highest-affinity AR ligand\" is a reasonable shorthand but not a clean fact: R1881 is the conventional *reference* radioligand and binds AR very tightly in prostate/AR tissue (Kd ~0.1 nM; PMID 8866837), yet it is explicitly NOT AR-selective — it also binds glucocorticoid, progesterone and mineralocorticoid receptors in vitro/rodent tissue, which is why AR assays must chemically block cross-binding (PMID 6975208, 6331850, 17105867, 2457134). Affinity is also tissue-dependent (much weaker in liver cytosol, ~10^-8 M; PMID 3872380, 6331850). Any potency claim should be read as \"reference-standard high affinity,\" not \"cleanest.\" (2) The famous sub-milligram potency and \"worst hepatotoxicity\" framing come from historical pharmacology/lore and class-level 17aa toxicology, NOT from modern controlled human dose-response trials of this specific compound — precise human potency and toxicity numbers are not established. (3) Because it is non-aromatizable, people assume \"no estrogen side effects = safe on that front,\" but as a 19-nor it is *expected* to retain progestogenic/prolactin-driven risks (gyno, sexual dysfunction) that estrogen-blockers do not fix — though this is an inference from the nandrolone/trenbolone class and has not been shown for metribolone directly. (4) A rat study found R1881 only marginally restored mating behaviour versus testosterone (PMID 3495627), a reminder that high AR affinity does not translate cleanly into every androgen-dependent endpoint. (5) Much of the mechanistic detail is from in-vitro binding and rodent tissue, not from humans taking it as a drug. This entry describes mechanism and what to monitor; it deliberately gives NO dosing or \"how to run it\" guidance, and none should be inferred.
