The amylin class: cagrilintide, petrelintide, eloralintide, amycretin

The big next mechanism after incretins — a satiety hormone that works alongside GLP-1, not through it — and an honest look at where each drug actually sits.

Why amylin is "the next mechanism"

For a decade the weight-loss conversation has been an incretin conversation: GLP-1 (semaglutide), then GLP-1 plus GIP (tirzepatide). Amylin is the answer to an obvious next question — is there another hunger brake we haven't pulled yet? There is, and it has been sitting in plain sight since the 1980s.

Amylin (islet amyloid polypeptide) is co-secreted with insulin from pancreatic beta-cells at every meal. Its job is to tell the brain you have eaten enough Established (PMID 34798060). It does this through a receptor family built on the calcitonin receptor (CTR): pair CTR with a helper protein called a RAMP and you get the amylin receptors AMY1R (CTR+RAMP1) and AMY3R (CTR+RAMP3). These sit on calcitonin-receptor–expressing neurons in the hindbrain dorsal vagal complex — the area postrema and nucleus tractus solitarius — a brainstem hub with a leaky blood-brain barrier that samples the blood directly [Established/Evidence-rodent] (PMID 34288673, PMID 39426704).

The crucial fact for this whole class: amylin's satiety wire is separate from GLP-1's. In rodents, knocking down the specific NTS Calcr/Prlh neurons abolishes an amylin agonist's weight effect but not semaglutide's — a parallel circuit into the same appetite hub [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 42260119, PMID 40609154). That separateness is the entire strategic thesis. If two drugs hit two different receptors, you can stack them for additive effect. That is why the marquee amylin drug so far is not a standalone but a combination.

Two design philosophies: broad vs selective, and "add GLP-1"

The class splits along two axes, and understanding them explains most of the differences.

Axis 1 — broad DACRA vs selective amylin. Cagrilintide and petrelintide are Dual Amylin and Calcitonin Receptor Agonists (DACRAs): they activate AMY1R, AMY3R and the bare calcitonin receptor Established (PMID 34288673). Eloralintide is Lilly's contrarian bet — engineered to preferentially hit human AMY1R (about 12-fold over CTR and 11-fold over AMY3R in cell assays) on the theory that a "cleaner" signal keeps the appetite benefit while trimming the aversive/nausea component [Evidence-in vitro] (PMID 41109426). In lean rats, eloralintide did cause less conditioned taste avoidance (a malaise proxy) than cagrilintide [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 41109426). But — and this is the honest part — that is rodent taste avoidance, not human nausea, and eloralintide's own phase 2 hit ~64% nausea at the 6 mg fixed dose. What actually tamed nausea was slow dose-escalation: a gradual 3→9 mg arm reached −16% weight loss with only 25% nausea versus 64% for the fixed 6 mg arm [Evidence-human] (PMID 41207310). So "selective = less nausea" remains a plausible but unproven-in-humans hypothesis Contested.

Axis 2 — standalone amylin vs "just add GLP-1." Cagrilintide, petrelintide and eloralintide are pure amylin-pathway drugs. Amycretin is different in kind: it fuses GLP-1 and amylin agonism into a single engineered peptide that in cell systems lights up GLP-1R, the amylin receptor, and CTR all at once [Evidence-in vitro] (PMID 40706446). It even comes in an oral form (using the same SNAC absorption technology as oral semaglutide). CagriSema does the same job with two molecules co-formulated (cagrilintide + semaglutide); amycretin does it with one. Petrelintide splits the difference — it was deliberately built to be co-formulable at neutral pH, and Roche is testing it alongside the dual GLP-1/GIP agonist enicepatide in the phase 2 ZYNERGY trial [Evidence-human, planned] (PMID 41217931).

Why it combines with GLP-1 — and the CagriSema reality check

The combination logic is mechanistically clean: amylin and GLP-1 converge on overlapping hindbrain/hypothalamic circuits through different receptors, so their satiety signals add rather than overlap Established (PMID 34288673). CagriSema was the proof of concept, and it worked — but the reality is more sober than the hype was.

In the phase 3 REDEFINE 1 trial (N=3,417, 68 weeks), CagriSema gave −20.4% weight loss versus −3.0% placebo [Evidence-human] (PMID 40544433). That is strong. Two honest asterisks, though. First, it undershot the ~25% that early expectations had set — a real-world reminder that preliminary signals oversell. Second, the increment over the components was modest: in the same trial, semaglutide alone was −14.9% and cagrilintide alone −11.5%, so the combination beat semaglutide monotherapy by only a handful of points. And ~80% of participants had a GI adverse event [Evidence-human] (PMID 40544433). Combining mechanisms adds efficacy, but not without adding the tolerability cost of both.

The uncomfortable truth about "gentler than GLP-1"

The most-repeated selling point for the amylin class is that it might cause less nausea than incretins. Read the mechanism and you see why that is not guaranteed: the satiety signal and the nausea signal share the same brainstem real estate — the area postrema is simultaneously the appetite-integration zone and the chemoreceptor trigger zone for emesis. Nausea here is on-target, not an off-target accident Established (PMID 39426704, PMID 34798060).

The human data reflect that. Petrelintide's phase 1 looked genuinely mild (nausea ~17–33% vs ~17% placebo, mostly mild, one GI discontinuation) — but that is a small, short, low-exposure safety study, and the sponsor's own framing calls the tolerability edge a hope, not a result [Evidence-human] (PMID 42017294). Eloralintide's phase 2 showed nausea is squarely dose-dependent and reached ~64% at high fixed dosing [Evidence-human] (PMID 41207310). The honest read: amylin agonists may be better-tolerated per unit of efficacy, and titration clearly helps — but "gentler than GLP-1" has no head-to-head confirmation in humans yet Contested.

Evidence tiering — this is where the drugs really differ

If you take one thing from this piece, make it this: the four drugs are at wildly different evidence maturities, and that matters more than any weight-loss percentage.

  • - Cagrilintide is the only one with phase 3 data, and only as half of CagriSema. Its monotherapy ceiling above 4.5 mg is unknown, and the human mechanism (which receptor, which neurons) is proven causally only in mice — RAMP1/3 knockouts blunt its weight loss and cut area-postrema neuron activation by 57% [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 40609154).
  • - Eloralintide has the most striking standalone number — up to ~20% at 48 weeks — but it rests on one 263-person phase 2 [Evidence-human] (PMID 41207310). A broad phase 3 program (ENLIGHTEN) launched in late 2025; results are not expected before ~2028, and there is no long-term, durability, or cardiovascular-outcome data yet.
  • - Petrelintide has advanced to phase 2 (the ZUPREME dose-finding trial completed, with topline reported by the sponsor), but the pivotal efficacy magnitude is still pending full peer-reviewed publication; its published number remains the 8.6% phase 1 figure [Evidence-human] (PMID 42017294).
  • - Amycretin posts the biggest headline — ~22–24% subcutaneous — but from a phase 1b/2a of ~125 people with notable trial dropout, so attrition could flatter the per-protocol figure. Its oral ~13% comes via a review, not a head-to-head [Evidence-human] (PMID 40550231, PMID 40550229, PMID 41850421). Worth stating plainly for a PED-literate audience: amycretin is a peptide incretin drug, not an anabolic steroid — no androgen receptor, no 17-alpha-alkylation, no oral-steroid liver-tox framework applies.

Cross-trial rankings that put these side by side (e.g. a network meta-analysis numerically ranking high-dose amycretin ~24% > eloralintide ~18% > CagriSema ~17% > semaglutide ~11%) are indirect, non-randomized, and low-certainty — the authors say so themselves Contested (PMID 42175595, PMID 41207310). Do not read them as a leaderboard.

Bottom line

Amylin is a real, mechanistically distinct second hunger brake, and the class is legitimately the most interesting thing after incretins — both because standalone amylins (eloralintide, petrelintide) are posting large numbers and because the pathway stacks with GLP-1 (CagriSema, amycretin, the petrelintide+enicepatide combos). But the field is young: one drug has phase 3, two are mid-phase-2, one is barely out of phase 1, the "less nausea" advantage is unproven, and the fine-grained mechanism is a rodent story we are extrapolating onto humans. Every compound here is investigational and unapproved; grey-market versions are unregulated and unverified. The science is exciting and the honest ledger is thin — both are true at once.

Key takeaways

  • Amylin is a second satiety hormone, co-released with insulin, that signals fullness through calcitonin-receptor complexes in the hindbrain — a wire into the appetite-control system that is separate from, and complementary to, GLP-1 [Established] (PMID 34798060, PMID 39426704).
  • That separateness is the whole strategic point: because amylin and GLP-1 hit different receptors, you can stack them. CagriSema (cagrilintide + semaglutide) gave ~20.4% weight loss vs ~3.0% placebo at 68 weeks in phase 3 — though it notably undershot the ~25% hype and beat semaglutide-alone (~14.9%) only modestly [Evidence] (PMID 40544433).
  • The class splits by design: cagrilintide and petrelintide are broad dual amylin/calcitonin-receptor agonists (DACRAs); eloralintide is engineered to be AMY1R-selective (the 'cleaner signal, less nausea' bet); amycretin fuses GLP-1 + amylin into one molecule [Established/Evidence-in vitro] (PMID 34288673, PMID 41109426, PMID 40706446).
  • Evidence tiers are very different and matter more than the headline percentages. Cagrilintide has phase 3 (as CagriSema); eloralintide has one striking 48-week phase 2 (~20% monotherapy, N=263); petrelintide has phase 2 topline pending full publication; amycretin has only tiny early-phase trials (~24% SC but N=125, high dropout) [Evidence] (PMID 40544433, PMID 41207310, PMID 42017294, PMID 40550231).
  • The 'amylin causes less nausea than GLP-1' story is a hypothesis, not a proven fact. Nausea is on-target — the satiety circuit sits next to the brainstem nausea trigger zone — and eloralintide's own phase 2 hit ~64% nausea at a high fixed dose. Slow titration, not the drug class, did most of the tolerability work [Contested] (PMID 41207310).
  • The deep-mechanism 'which receptor, which neurons' story is proven in rodents (RAMP knockouts, NTS Calcr/Prlh neurons), not humans — we infer the human mechanism by analogy. Every drug here is investigational; none is approved, and grey-market 'research peptide' versions are unregulated and unverified [Established] (PMID 40609154, PMID 42260119).

Every compound discussed is investigational and unapproved as of mid-2026; nothing here is a dosing protocol or medical advice. Cross-trial weight-loss percentages (e.g. cagrilintide vs eloralintide vs amycretin) come from separate trials with different populations, durations, and estimands — they are NOT randomized head-to-head comparisons, and the one network meta-analysis attempting to rank them explicitly flags its own evidence as sparse and low-certainty (PMID 42175595). The detailed neuron- and receptor-level mechanism is established mainly in rodents and in-vitro; human mechanism is inferred by analogy. Amycretin's ~24% figure rests on ~125 people with notable trial dropout, and petrelintide's pivotal phase 2 is topline-only pending full peer-reviewed publication. Grey-market "research chemical" amylin peptides are of unverified identity, purity, and sterility and sit entirely outside clinical safety monitoring.
