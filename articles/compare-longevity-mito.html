Longevity & mitochondrial compounds, compared

NAD+ precursors vs mTOR inhibition vs autophagy vs mitophagy — and which ones actually have human data

adversarially verified · revised37 citations

The setup: one shelf, four different machines

Walk into any longevity-supplement conversation and these five get thrown into the same bucket: nicotinamide riboside (NR), NMN, rapamycin, spermidine, and urolithin A. "Anti-aging stack." But under the hood they are doing four genuinely different jobs, and the honest verdict on each depends entirely on which job you're asking about and whether anyone has tested it in an actual human.

Here's the one-sentence version of each machine:

  • - NR and NMN are fuel-line toppers. They raise NAD+, the coenzyme every cell burns for energy and repair Established.
  • - Rapamycin is a switch-flipper. It jams mTORC1, the cell's "grow now, store now" master switch, forcing it into a fasting-like state Established.
  • - Spermidine is a cleanup signal. It turns on autophagy — the cell digesting and recycling its own junk [Evidence-rodent].
  • - Urolithin A is a specialist recycler. It turns on mitophagy — autophagy aimed specifically at worn-out mitochondria [Established mechanism].

The trap the marketing sets is treating "it changes a molecule in your blood" as the same thing as "it makes you healthier or live longer." For most of this shelf, those are two completely different claims — and only the first one is proven.

Reconciling the mechanisms: why they aren't interchangeable

If you zoom out, three of these compounds (rapamycin, spermidine, urolithin A) all end up pulling on the same lever — autophagy, the cell's self-cleaning system — but from completely different starting points, and that difference matters.

Rapamycin works from the top down. mTORC1 is the master switch that, when active, actively suppresses autophagy so the cell can build and grow instead. Block mTORC1 and you release that brake — autophagy ramps up, protein-building slows, and the cell behaves as if it's fasting even after a meal Established. This is the most "upstream" and most powerful intervention on the shelf, which is exactly why it's the only one with real side effects: chronic dosing also drags down mTORC2, the source of its glucose and lipid problems [Evidence-human] (PMID 22461615).

Spermidine works from the side. Instead of hitting the master switch, it inhibits the acetyltransferase EP300 (a brake on autophagy genes) and supplies the raw material to modify a translation factor (eIF5A) the cell needs to build its autophagy machinery [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 25526088). Interestingly, the pathways connect: rapamycin's own longevity effect in animals actually requires a downstream spermidine/eIF5A step [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 39212197) — so spermidine is less a competitor to rapamycin than one of its obligatory downstream errand-runners.

Urolithin A is the specialist. It doesn't induce general housekeeping; it targets one specific problem — damaged mitochondria — flagging them via PINK1/Parkin and clearing them via TFEB-driven lysosomes [Established mechanism] (PMID 27400265). Narrower job, but a job that maps cleanly onto muscle aging, which is why it's the one with actual human muscle trials.

NR and NMN sit apart from those three entirely. They don't touch autophagy or mTOR. They top up NAD+, the fuel that other enzymes (sirtuins, PARPs) burn. The theory is that more fuel = more sirtuin activity = better mitochondria and repair. And here is the single most important honest point in this entire comparison: the NAD+ precursors have the strongest, cleanest biomarker data of the whole group — and the weakest functional payoff.

Across placebo-controlled trials, oral NR raises blood NAD+ by roughly 22% to 142% dose-dependently Established (PMID 31278280), and NMN does the same up to a plateau around 600 mg/day Evidence (PMID 36482258). Target engagement is not in doubt. But when researchers looked for the consequences — in obese insulin-resistant men (PMID 29992272), in muscle mitochondria (PMID 31710095), and in a 2025 meta-analysis of muscle outcomes (PMID 40275690) — the results were null or mixed. NAD+ going up is simply not the same as you getting better. (There are isolated positive signals for NMN, like improved muscle insulin sensitivity in prediabetic women, PMID 33888596 — but they're single-trial, and pooled metabolic meta-analyses come back null, PMID 39531138.) A 2026 head-to-head even suggests the human NAD+ rise from NR/NMN is partly gut-microbiome-mediated, not the clean "swallow NMN → cell gets NMN" story on the label (PMID 41540253).

Which ones actually have human data — and which are hype

Rank these by the quality of human functional evidence and the picture inverts from the marketing:

Genuinely have human functional data (modest, narrow):

  • - Rapamycin — but read the fine print. The real human RCTs (Mannick 2014, 2018) used rapalogs (everolimus, RTB101), not rapamycin itself, and measured immune/vaccine response, not longevity — where they showed better flu-vaccine responses and ~fewer infections in older adults Evidence (PMID 25540326, 29997249). The first proper rapamycin-in-healthy-adults trial, PEARL, found weekly low-dose safe over a year but failed its primary efficacy endpoint (no change in visceral fat) [Evidence, null on primary] (PMID 40188830).
  • - Urolithin A — the standout "supplement with real RCTs." It raises human muscle mitophagy markers and gives modest endurance/strength gains Evidence (PMID 32694802). But be precise: both flagship RCTs missed their primary endpoints — Singh 2022 missed peak power, Liu 2022 missed 6-minute walk and ATP production; the wins were secondary endurance measures and blood biomarkers (PMID 35584623, 35050355). A human systematic review found no effect on mitochondrial biogenesis or physical function (PMID 39002645), and athlete trials are mixed (positive PMID 39487653, limited PMID 40839339).

Strong biomarker data, weak-to-null functional payoff:

  • - NR and NMN — reliably raise NAD+, repeatedly fail to move hard outcomes (see above).

Mostly preclinical, human proof thin or absent:

  • - Spermidine — spectacular in yeast, worms, flies, and mice [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 27841876); in humans the dietary-mortality correlation is observational and confounded (PMID 29955838) and didn't replicate in a large Japanese cohort (PMID 37964604); and the best trial — the 12-month SmartAge cognition RCT — came back null on its primary endpoint (PMID 35616942).

And the claim that unites all five in the marketing — live longer, reverse aging — has zero human data for any of them. Rapamycin's mouse lifespan result is genuinely impressive and replicated (PMID 19587680), spermidine's and the NAD+ precursors' animal data are real, urolithin A extends worm and mouse lifespan (PMID 27400265) — but every one of these is a mouse (or worm) telling you something might be true in a human. It hasn't been shown. Some rigorous rodent work even argues rapamycin's lifespan gain may partly reflect suppressing specific lethal cancers rather than "slowing aging" per se [Evidence-rodent, dissenting] (PMID 23863708).

What to monitor (not how to run it)

This is educational, not a protocol. But if the honesty brand means anything, it means knowing what would actually tell you something:

  • - For NR/NMN: the temptation is to measure NAD+ and declare victory. Don't. A NAD+ rise is guaranteed and proves nothing about benefit — track the hard markers (glucose/HbA1c, lipids) the trials failed to move, not the biomarker the marketing sells.
  • - For rapamycin: this is the one with real medical downside. Fasting glucose/HbA1c and lipids (mTORC2-driven metabolic shifts), CBC (cytopenias), proteinuria, mouth ulcers, infections, and its heavy CYP3A/grapefruit drug-interaction profile all belong under clinician supervision. It's a prescription drug for a reason.
  • - For spermidine and urolithin A: generally benign at the low doses actually tested in trials. Watch GI tolerance; hs-CRP is the marker most consistently moved by UroA. Both carry unresolved theoretical flags — polyamine/cancer double-edge for spermidine, estrogenic/SERM activity in vitro for UroA — that matter most for anyone with a cancer or hormone-sensitive history.

The through-line: for this whole shelf, the biology is real and often elegant, the animal data range from suggestive to genuinely impressive, and the human proof runs from "modest and narrow" (rapamycin, urolithin A) to "biomarker-only" (NR, NMN) to "the best trial was null" (spermidine). None of them has earned the word longevity in a human yet.

Key takeaways

  • These five compounds are marketed as one "longevity" bucket, but mechanistically they split into four different jobs: NR and NMN feed the NAD+ fuel line, rapamycin turns the mTOR growth switch OFF, spermidine turns on general cellular recycling (autophagy), and urolithin A specifically recycles worn-out mitochondria (mitophagy).
  • The gap between "changes a biomarker" and "makes a person better" is the whole story. NR and NMN reliably raise blood NAD+ in humans [Established] — but repeatedly fail to move insulin sensitivity, muscle, or mitochondrial function in randomized trials [Contested].
  • Only two of the five have meaningful human functional data: urolithin A (modest, biomarker-level muscle and immune effects — and its two flagship RCTs missed their primary endpoints) [Evidence] and rapamycin (real human RCTs, but for immune/vaccine response, not longevity) [Evidence].
  • Every headline lifespan claim in this group is a mouse (or worm/fly) result. Rapamycin extends mouse lifespan even started in old age [Evidence-rodent]; spermidine and the NAD+ precursors extend lifespan in animals [Evidence-rodent]; NO human lifespan or aging-reversal data exist for any of them [Speculation].
  • Rapamycin is the only prescription drug here with a genuine downside profile (glucose/lipid changes, immunosuppression, mouth ulcers). The four supplements are well tolerated short-term, but that safety is only established at the low doses actually tested — not the megadoses forums promote.
  • The honest ranking by human evidence quality: rapamycin and urolithin A have the most real (if narrow and modest) human data; NR and NMN have rock-solid biomarker data but a null-to-mixed functional record; spermidine's best human trial came back null.

This is educational content, not medical advice, and contains no dosing protocols. Several important honesty flags: (1) Most positive urolithin A trials (Ryu 2016, Singh 2022, Liu 2022, Denk 2025) were funded by Amazentis/Timeline, the manufacturer — weight them accordingly; independent athlete trials (Zhao 2024, Whitfield 2025) show smaller/mixed effects. (2) Much of the foundational NR human work is ChromaDex-affiliated. (3) The PEARL rapamycin trial is published in the journal Aging and tied to the longevity-clinic ecosystem — treat as suggestive safety data, not efficacy proof. (4) Spermidine's human cardiovascular signal is observational and confounded by overall diet quality, and it did not replicate in a large Japanese cohort. (5) NMN's US supplement status is legally disputed (FDA drug-exclusion position), and its human NAD+ rise may be partly gut-microbiome-mediated rather than direct cellular uptake. (6) The polyamine and NAD+ pathways both carry an unresolved theoretical cancer-proliferation concern at chronic high exposure. None of these compounds is proven to extend human lifespan.
