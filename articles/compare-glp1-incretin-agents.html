GLP-1 & incretin agents, compared

Seven drugs, four receptors: what actually differs under the hood — and where the hype outruns the human data

adversarially verified · revised37 citations

The one idea that ties all seven together

Your gut and pancreas release hormones after you eat — GLP-1, GIP, glucagon, amylin — that between them say "make insulin," "you're full," and "burn some fuel." They're powerful and they're gone in minutes, chewed up by enzymes and cleared from the blood. Every drug on this page is the same basic hack: take one or more of those signals, re-engineer it so it lasts a week (peptides, via a fatty tail that grips onto blood albumin) or so it survives being swallowed (small molecules), and hold the "button" down. Established (PMID 40281304)

So the class isn't really seven different ideas. It's one idea — long-acting incretin agonism — with the knob turned to different combinations of receptors. Once you see which receptors each drug grabs, almost everything else (how much weight comes off, whether it "burns" fat, whether it's a pill or a needle, how solid the evidence is) follows. Let's lay them side by side, then explain why the differences fall out the way they do.

Why "add a receptor, add an effect" — and where it breaks down

The tidy-looking ladder — GLP-1 ~15% → GLP-1+GIP ~21% → GLP-1+GIP+glucagon ~24% — is real, and it's the industry's whole thesis: each receptor arm adds a somewhat independent lever. [Evidence-human] But two honesty checks:

First, those numbers aren't a fair race. Semaglutide's 15% is at 68 weeks, tirzepatide's ~21% at 72 weeks, retatrutide's ~24% at 48 weeks — and retatrutide hadn't clearly plateaued when its trial ended (PMID 37366315). They're different trials, different doses, different populations. The direction of the ladder is trustworthy; the exact gaps are not a leaderboard. Contested

Second, what GIP actually contributes is genuinely unsettled. Adding GIP to GLP-1 clearly helps — but both switching GIP on (tirzepatide) and switching it off (GIP-antagonist combos) added to GLP-1 produce weight loss in humans. Anyone who tells you a clean "GIP does X" story is overselling it. Contested (PMID 42166683)

Why retatrutide "burns differently": the glucagon arm

Here's the mechanistic heart of the comparison. GLP-1 and GIP work almost entirely by the eat-less route — appetite down, fullness up, stomach empties slower. Glucagon is the odd one out.

Glucagon is normally a sugar-raising hormone — the opposite of what you'd want in a weight drug. But turned on in a controlled way (with GLP-1 there to keep blood sugar in check), the glucagon receptor tells the liver to burn its own fat and nudges up whole-body energy expenditure — calories burned at rest. So retatrutide and survodutide stack two forces: the GLP-1/GIP arms push calorie intake down, the glucagon arm pushes calorie burn up. That "eat less and burn more" combination is the entire design rationale — and it's why retatrutide clears liver fat so aggressively and why survodutide's whole program is aimed at fatty-liver disease (MASH). [Evidence-rodent for the mechanism] (PMID 35985340; 40364529)

The critical honesty flag: the "burns more calories" half is demonstrated cleanest in mice. In obese-mouse studies you can cleanly show the glucagon arm's energy-expenditure boost adds to the appetite arm (PMID 35985340). In humans, we see the outcomes — huge weight loss, dramatic liver-fat clearance (PMID 38858523), a body-composition substudy (PMID 40609566), a dose-dependent heart-rate rise consistent with glucagon's action on the heart (PMID 40613938) — but we infer the neat "so-much-from-burning, so-much-from-eating-less" split rather than measure it directly. Do not narrate the mouse energy-expenditure math as if it were filmed in a human. [Evidence-rodent]

One more precision point people get wrong: retatrutide is not "strongest at glucagon." In the dish it's roughly balanced at glucagon and GLP-1, with actually its most activity at GIP. The glucagon arm is the differentiator, not the dominant one. Established (PMID 35985340)

The amylin branch: cagrilintide and amycretin

Two of these seven aren't incretins at all in the GLP-1 sense. Amylin is a separate satiety hormone (co-released with insulin) that signals fullness through receptors in the brainstem's "appetite control room." Cagrilintide is a long-acting amylin analogue; amycretin fuses amylin and GLP-1 into one peptide. Established (PMID 34288673; 40550229)

The logic is convergence: amylin and GLP-1 suppress appetite through different but overlapping brainstem routes, so combining them should add up — which is exactly the CagriSema bet (cagrilintide + semaglutide, ~20% in phase 3). [Evidence-human] (PMID 40544433) But note the hype-check baked into that number: CagriSema's ~20% undershot the ~25% that early chatter had promised — a clean real-world example of preliminary signals overselling. [Evidence-human] And the "which receptor, which neuron" causal wiring for cagrilintide is proven in mice (RAMP-knockout studies), inferred in humans. [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 40609154)

Amycretin is the one to be most cautious about. Its ~22–24% subcutaneous figure is genuinely striking — but it comes from a single early-phase trial of 125 people over 28–36 weeks with notable participant withdrawals (PMID 40550231). That's a dose-finding/safety study, not a phase-3 outcome trial. Forum claims that it's already "the best ever" run miles ahead of the evidence; a network meta-analysis that ranks it highly also explicitly calls the evidence sparse and low-certainty. Contested (PMID 42175595)

Oral vs injectable: two totally different chemistries

Most of these are peptides, and peptides get destroyed in the gut — which is why they're weekly injections. There are two escape routes to a pill, and they're not equal:

  • - The SNAC trick (oral semaglutide, and oral amycretin): bolt on an absorption enhancer, take it fasted with a sip of water, and only a sliver sneaks across the gut lining intact — for oral semaglutide that's roughly 1% or less. Amycretin's oral form rides the same SNAC route (its exact oral bioavailability isn't published), so it inherits the same finicky, wasteful fasting ritual. [Evidence-human] (PMID 40550229)
  • - The small-molecule route (orforglipron): don't use a peptide at all. Orforglipron is a non-peptide designed from scratch to hit GLP-1R — so it survives digestion natively, hits ~79% oral bioavailability, and has no food or water restrictions. [Evidence-human] (PMID 39693407; 41994902) That's the genuinely new thing here: an oral GLP-1 drug that behaves like a normal pill. Its trade-off is that oral exposure plateaus at higher doses, which may cap how much effect you can push out of it. [Evidence-human] (PMID 41325139)

The lean-mass question — the one this audience most needs straight

Across the whole class, a meaningful chunk of the weight lost is lean/muscle mass, not just fat. This is real and worth flagging for a physique audience. [Evidence-human] (PMID 38629387; 39996356)

But here's the mechanism, told honestly: this is not a special toxic effect of GLP-1 (or GIP, or glucagon) on muscle. It's the ordinary, known consequence of losing a lot of weight fast in a calorie deficit — some fraction of any rapid weight loss is lean tissue, whether the deficit comes from a drug, a diet, or bariatric surgery. Because these drugs suppress appetite hard, people often under-eat protein, which makes it worse. [Evidence-human] The retatrutide body-composition substudy suggests its lean-to-total loss ratio is similar to other obesity drugs (if anything a greater share of loss is fat, given how much total comes off) — so the triple agonist isn't uniquely muscle-sparing or uniquely muscle-wasting; it's the same physiology at a bigger magnitude. [Evidence-human] (PMID 40609566; 39996356)

The practical read: you don't dodge lean-mass loss by picking a different molecule off this list. It's blunted by resistance training and adequate protein — the levers are behavioural, not which receptor you agonize. [Evidence-human]

What to monitor (not how to dose)

Common threads worth watching across all seven: GI effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) are the dominant, dose-related side effect for every one — which is why trials use slow multi-week escalation. The glucagon agents (retatrutide, survodutide) add a dose-dependent heart-rate rise and a counter-regulatory glucose push the GLP-1 arm has to offset. [Evidence-human] (PMID 38330987; 40613938) Lean-mass loss, gallbladder events, and weight regain after stopping recur across the class. None of these are dosing instructions — they're what a clinician tracks.

Two hard lines. (1) Everything here except semaglutide and tirzepatide is not an approved product, and anything sold as gray-market "retatrutide," "survodutide," "cagrilintide," or "amycretin" (or knock-off "semaglutide") is unregulated and unverified for identity, purity, and dose — none of the trial safety data apply to that vial. Established (2) This is mechanistic education, not medical or dosing advice.

Key takeaways

  • The whole class works by one core trick: mimic gut/pancreas 'I'm full' hormones that normally die in minutes, and re-engineer them to last a week (or, for the small molecules, to survive being swallowed). The differences are which receptors each one hits. [Established]
  • Add receptors, add effect: GLP-1 alone (semaglutide) ~15%, GLP-1+GIP (tirzepatide) ~21%, GLP-1+GIP+glucagon (retatrutide) ~24% mean weight loss in trials. But these are dose- and trial-specific averages measured at DIFFERENT durations, not a guaranteed ranking any one person gets. [Evidence-human]
  • Retatrutide 'burns differently' because of its glucagon arm: GLP-1/GIP make you eat less, glucagon (GCGR) makes the liver burn fat and nudges up calories-burned-at-rest. The eat-less-plus-burn-more stacking is the design rationale — but the 'burn more' half is cleanest in mice; in humans it's inferred from liver-fat clearance and weight loss. [Evidence-rodent for mechanism, Evidence-human for outcomes]
  • Oral is the real frontier. Orforglipron is a non-peptide small molecule with ~79% oral bioavailability and no food/water rules — a genuinely different chemistry from the peptides, which either need a weekly needle or (oral semaglutide, and oral amycretin on the same route) absorb at only ~1% behind a strict fasting ritual. [Evidence-human]
  • The lean-mass loss everyone worries about is NOT a special drug toxicity — it's the normal consequence of losing a lot of weight fast in a calorie deficit. A chunk of ANY rapid weight loss is muscle, across the whole class; it's blunted by resistance training and protein, not by picking a different injection. [Evidence-human]
  • Evidence tiers are wildly uneven: semaglutide and tirzepatide have huge phase-3 outcome data; retatrutide, survodutide, cagrilintide, orforglipron have solid phase-2/phase-3 efficacy but immature long-term safety; amycretin's eye-popping ~22-24% number comes from a single small early-phase trial with notable dropout. Do not treat them as equally proven. [Evidence-human / Contested]
  • Every non-approved agent here (retatrutide, survodutide, cagrilintide, amycretin, and gray-market 'versions' of the approved ones) sold as a research peptide is unregulated and unverified for identity, purity and dose — none of the trial safety data transfer to that product. This is education, not medical or dosing advice. [Established]

Weight-loss percentages are trial-specific means at particular doses and durations (semaglutide 68wk, tirzepatide 72wk, retatrutide 48wk, etc.) and are NOT a head-to-head ranking — no single trial compares all seven.
