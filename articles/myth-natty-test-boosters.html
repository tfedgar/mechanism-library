Do natural test boosters actually work?

Tongkat, fadogia, ashwagandha, boron, DHEA — what the human data actually shows, and how it stacks up against a real HPG-axis drug.

adversarially verified · revised20 citations

Walk into any supplement shop, or scroll any "boost your T naturally" video, and you'll meet the same cast: tongkat ali, fadogia agrestis, ashwagandha, boron, DHEA. The pitch is intoxicating — herbs and minerals that supposedly crank your testosterone the way a steroid would, minus the needles, the shutdown, and the risk.

Here's the honest version. Most of these do something measurable. But "something measurable in a trial" and "a change you'd feel, that meaningfully moves your testosterone" are very different claims — and the gap between them is where the marketing lives. To see how thin the effect really is, it helps to hold these supplements up against a drug that genuinely hijacks your hormone axis. That's enclomiphene, and we'll get to it.

The myth

The myth is a single sentence repeated a thousand ways: tongkat / fadogia / ashwagandha / boron / DHEA meaningfully raise your testosterone. The subtext is that they're a natural stand-in for TRT or a steroid cycle — a way to run your own testosterone up into "beast mode" without touching a prescription.

The reality

The human evidence is thin, the effect sizes are small and inconsistent, and the biggest effects show up in exactly the people who aren't the target audience: older, stressed, overweight, or already-low men. In healthy young guys with normal testosterone, the needle barely moves. Let's go compound by compound.

Tongkat ali is the strongest of the bunch, and it still isn't strong. It genuinely has human RCTs and a 2022 meta-analysis of 5 pooled trials showing a statistically significant rise in total testosterone (standardised mean difference ~1.35) Evidence (PMID 36013514). But the effect is driven by hypogonadal, older, and stressed men; in healthy young men the testosterone bump is small and the mechanism looks more like stress-axis relief than a true testicular kick Contested (PMID 33559971). And the mechanism everyone repeats — that eurycomanone blocks aromatase so less testosterone converts to estrogen — is shown in rat Leydig cells and molecular docking, not in living humans [Evidence, rodent] (PMID 23810842). Tellingly, in the young-men trial, estradiol went up, not down Evidence (PMID 33559971). A broad systematic review of "testosterone boosters" as a category found the evidence inconsistent and riddled with small, industry-funded trials on proprietary extracts Evidence (PMID 37697053).

Ashwagandha is real, but it's a stress drug wearing a testosterone costume. Its most replicated human effect by far is lowering cortisol and easing anxiety and poor sleep Evidence (PMID 39348746). A 2026 meta-analysis of 23 RCTs did find testosterone rose about +57 ng/dL in men — but not in women, and the signal leans on stressed, overweight, aging, or sub-fertile populations, not healthy young lifters Evidence (PMID 41740946). Some of that rise may just be cortisol getting out of testosterone's way, not direct stimulation Speculation. It is not a steroid-equivalent booster, and the "GABA-A / anti-inflammatory / longevity" mechanisms are largely rodent and test-tube work [Evidence, in vitro] (PMID 26068424).

Boron is a masterclass in how a headline outruns its data. The famous study is eight men taking ~10 mg/day for one week — free testosterone rose, estradiol fell, SHBG dropped Evidence (PMID 21129941). Eight men, one week, and no parallel placebo arm at that one-week endpoint — it's a within-subject before/after, not a controlled trial. An older study in postmenopausal women found boron raised estradiol and testosterone Evidence (PMID 3678698). It's a plausible SHBG/hormone nudge in deficiency, not a proven testosterone lever in well-fed men. Note what else went up in that eight-man study: dihydrotestosterone Evidence (PMID 21129941) — more on that below.

DHEA is the clearest miss in men. It's a hormone precursor, so it sounds like it should convert straight to testosterone. In the DAWN trial (225 adults, 50 mg/day for a year), testosterone and estradiol rose in women — and did not rise in men Evidence (PMID 18084691). A second year-long RCT is messier: in men it nudged the free-testosterone index and estradiol up slightly, but produced no bone or body-composition benefit — so even where a number moves, nothing useful follows Contested (PMID 19321570). DHEA is a genuine androgen source in women (which is exactly why it causes androgenic side effects there); as a male testosterone booster the controlled data are, at best, unimpressive.

Fadogia agrestis is the one with essentially no human evidence at all. The entire hype rests on a single 2008 rat study — and that study is a warning, not a green light: at higher doses, Fadogia extract produced changes the authors explicitly called "adverse effects on... testicular function" [Evidence, rodent] (PMID 18023305). There are no human RCTs. Selling a compound whose only real study flags testicular toxicity in rats as a "natural test booster" is the myth at its most reckless.

What a real HPG-axis intervention looks like

To feel the difference, look at enclomiphene. It's the purified anti-estrogen isomer of clomiphene — a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM) that blocks the estrogen "off-switch" in your hypothalamus Established (PMID 19204885; PMID 27337642). With that brake released, the brain ramps up GnRH, the pituitary pushes out more LH and FSH, and the testes make more of your own testosterone — while sperm production is preserved, unlike TRT Established (PMID 25044085; PMID 26496621).

The magnitude is the whole point. Across SERM trials (clomiphene and enclomiphene pooled), total testosterone rose about +274 ng/dL versus placebo — comparable to a testosterone gel Evidence (PMID 41066380). That's a drug-sized move through your own axis. The supplements above, at their honest best, produce a fraction of that in a narrow subgroup. This is the contrast the marketing hopes you never draw: a genuine hormonal intervention has a large, consistent, mechanism-backed effect. The "natty boosters" have small, population-dependent, often rodent-inferred ones.

And enclomiphene is not a free lunch either — it's not FDA-approved, its development was halted, real-world access is off-label via compounding pharmacies, and long-term safety (cardiovascular, clot risk, mood, vision) is genuinely unstudied Established (PMID 26496621). It's a real drug with real unknowns, not a supplement. It's here as a yardstick, not a recommendation.

The hidden risks nobody puts on the label

"Natural" is not "consequence-free," and a few of these carry quiet costs:

  • - Liver (ALT/AST). Ashwagandha has the best-documented harm of the group: repeated case reports of herb-induced liver injury — jaundice, itching, raised liver enzymes — graded "probable," with rare progression to liver failure Established (PMID 37631044; PMID 37982556). For tongkat the liver signal is weaker; rodent toxicology was largely benign, but products vary wildly and tongkat has documented contamination/adulteration issues Established (PMID 29154863) — so a bad batch, not the herb itself, can be the hepatotoxin. If you use any of these, baseline and periodic liver enzymes are the sensible move.
  • - DHT and hair loss. That eight-man boron study didn't just raise free testosterone — DHT went up too Evidence (PMID 21129941). DHEA is a direct precursor to androgens including DHT, which is part of why it drives acne and hair effects, especially in women Speculation. DHT is the androgen behind male-pattern balding, so anything that raises it is a real consideration if you're genetically prone.
  • - Estrogen surprises. These don't reliably lower estrogen. In young men, tongkat raised estradiol (PMID 33559971); boron and DHEA can shift estradiol up too (PMID 3678698; PMID 18084691). The "natural aromatase inhibitor" framing is often backwards in practice.

The bottom line

Tongkat and ashwagandha have real human trials and can produce a modest, real effect — mostly in men who are low, old, or stressed, and mostly by calming the stress axis rather than supercharging the testicles. Boron is an eight-man curiosity. DHEA doesn't meaningfully raise testosterone in men. Fadogia's only study is a rat toxicity warning. None of them is a natural steroid, and none approaches what an actual HPG-axis drug like enclomiphene does — which is precisely why enclomiphene is a prescription-grade intervention with prescription-grade unknowns, and these are shelf supplements.

The useful reframe: some of these are decent stress-and-sleep tools with a small hormonal side effect. Sold that way, they're honest. Sold as "natty TRT," they're the myth. Watch your liver enzymes and your DHT, be skeptical of any effect size that only shows up in already-low men, and remember that the loudest testosterone claim in the room is usually the one with the thinnest human data behind it.

This is mechanism education, not medical advice. It describes what the evidence says and what to monitor — not how to dose anything.

Key takeaways

  • Tongkat ali and ashwagandha have genuine human RCTs, but the testosterone effect is modest and concentrated in older, stressed, overweight, or already-low men — in healthy young men the needle barely moves (PMID 36013514, 33559971, 41740946).
  • Ashwagandha is really a cortisol/stress-and-sleep supplement; its testosterone bump (~+57 ng/dL in men, not women) is small and population-dependent, not steroid-like (PMID 41740946).
  • Boron's headline rests on a single 8-man, 1-week uncontrolled study; DHEA did not raise testosterone in men in the DAWN trial (a second RCT showed only a small free-T-index bump with no clinical benefit); Fadogia has zero human trials and its only rat study flags testicular toxicity (PMID 21129941, 18084691, 19321570, 18023305).
  • For scale: a real HPG-axis drug (enclomiphene, a SERM) raises testosterone ~+274 ng/dL vs placebo in pooled clomiphene/enclomiphene trials — comparable to a T-gel and far beyond any supplement — but it's unapproved, off-label, and long-term safety is unstudied (PMID 41066380, 26496621).
  • Hidden risks: ashwagandha has documented liver injury (monitor ALT/AST); boron and DHEA can raise DHT (hair-loss/androgenic concern); and several of these raise estradiol rather than lowering it (PMID 37631044, 21129941, 33559971).

Human effect sizes are drawn from monograph-verified and freshly re-fetched PubMed abstracts; full texts were not all re-retrieved this run, so present effect sizes (SMD 1.35, +57 ng/dL, +274 ng/dL) as abstract-grounded. The +274 ng/dL enclomiphene figure is from a 2025 meta-analysis explicitly titled 'Clomiphene OR enclomiphene citrate' (PMID 41066380), i.e. pooled SERM data, not enclomiphene alone — worded accordingly and tagged [Evidence] rather than [Established] because it is a heterogeneous pooled estimate. Boron (PMID 21129941, 3678698) and Fadogia (PMID 18023305) lack dedicated verified monographs; they are grounded in freshly fetched PubMed abstracts only, so treat those specific claims as single-study / abstract-level evidence. DHEA has no monograph; the 'no rise in men' claim is anchored on the DAWN RCT (PMID 18084691, which reports no testosterone rise in men), while the second RCT (PMID 19321570) actually showed a small free-testosterone-index and estradiol rise in men with no bone/body-composition benefit — so it is presented honestly as mixed/[Contested], NOT as clean support for 'no rise in men.' The tongkat liver claim is hedged: the strong Established liver-injury signal belongs to ashwagandha (PMID 37631044, 37982556); tongkat rodent toxicology was largely benign and its real-world liver risk is tied to product contamination/variability (PMID 29154863). No dosing protocols included.
