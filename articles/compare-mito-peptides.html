The pitch, and the problem

"Mitochondrial support" is one of the most crowded corners of the longevity market, and five molecules dominate the conversation: elamipretide (SS-31), MOTS-c, humanin, MitoQ, and SkQ1. They get lumped together because they all claim to fix the same thing — tired, leaky, oxidatively-damaged mitochondria — but they are three completely different kinds of molecule with three different mechanisms, three different levels of human evidence, and three different honest verdicts.

This piece sorts them into their real families:

  • - Cardiolipin-targeting peptide: SS-31 (elamipretide) — binds a lipid, stabilizes the inner membrane's architecture.
  • - Mitochondrial-derived peptides (MDPs): MOTS-c and humanin — tiny peptides your own mitochondria transcribe and release as signals.
  • - TPP+-targeted antioxidants ("Skulachev ions"): MitoQ and SkQ1 — a redox head-group bolted to a positive charge that drags it inside mitochondria to scavenge radicals.

The single most important honest frame up front: the mechanistic stories are genuinely elegant, and almost all of the efficacy evidence is in rodents, cells, or worms. Where good human trials exist, the results have been humbling — a string of missed primary endpoints, with two narrow, non-systemic exceptions (Barth syndrome for SS-31; topical dry-eye for SkQ1). Nothing here is a validated human anti-aging intervention.

Family 1 — Cardiolipin targeting: SS-31 stands apart

SS-31 is the odd one out and arguably the most mechanistically interesting. It is a synthetic aromatic-cationic tetrapeptide that binds cardiolipin, the signature lipid of the inner mitochondrial membrane, through combined electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions Established (PMID 24117165, PMID 32273339). Critically, it does not rely on membrane potential to get in — so it can reach damaged, depolarized mitochondria that a charge-driven molecule would skip Established (PMID 24117165, PMID 39940712).

Modern biophysics has reframed its mechanism away from simple "antioxidant scavenging" and toward membrane structural/electrostatic stabilization — it preserves the tightly-folded cristae that house the respiratory chain, and is proposed to keep cytochrome c working as an electron carrier rather than flipping into a peroxidase [Established for the electrostatics; Evidence-in vitro for the cytochrome-c step] (PMID 32273339, PMID 40294492). In aged mouse muscle a single dose restored ATP production and coupling to youthful levels within an hour [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 23692570).

Here's where honesty matters. SS-31 has one of the largest clinical programs of any molecule on this page, which is exactly why we can check the hype against real Phase 2/3 data — and the data are mostly negative at the primary-endpoint level:

  • - Heart failure (HFrEF): PROGRESS-HF found no improvement in LV end-systolic volume at either dose [Evidence-human] (PMID 32068002).
  • - Primary mitochondrial myopathy: MMPOWER-3 missed its 6-minute-walk primary; only a nuclear-DNA/CPEO genotype subgroup showed benefit [Evidence-human] (PMID 39574155).
  • - Dry AMD: ReCLAIM-2 missed both co-primaries [Evidence-human] (PMID 39605874).

The one real win: in September 2025 the FDA granted accelerated approval (brand Forzinity) for Barth syndrome on a muscle-strength endpoint — the first-ever disease-specific Barth therapy Established (PMID 41335372, PMID 41260682). That is a genuine milestone, but it is an ultra-rare disease, an intermediate endpoint, and still requires a confirmatory trial. It is not validation for healthy-aging use, and gray-market "SS-31" is unregulated research chemical, not the approved medicine.

Family 2 — Mitochondrial-derived peptides: MOTS-c and humanin

These two are conceptually beautiful: short peptides encoded by hidden reading frames inside mitochondrial rRNA genes, released as "mitokines" that signal metabolic and stress status to the rest of the body Established (PMID 25738459, PMID 11371646). Both circulate in humans and both change with age. But they work through entirely different biology, and — this is the load-bearing caveat — neither has a single completed randomized trial of the administered peptide in humans.

MOTS-c (16-aa) is the metabolic/exercise-mimetic one. It converges on AMPK, the cell's low-fuel master switch. The working model is that it disrupts the folate/de-novo-purine cycle so the AMPK-activating intermediate AICAR/ZMP builds up and tips AMPK on [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 25738459), and it also directly binds and activates the kinase CK2 in muscle [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 39559755). Under stress it translocates to the nucleus and switches on NRF2/ARE antioxidant genes [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 29983246). In mice it prevents diet- and age-induced insulin resistance and boosts running capacity even when started late in life [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 33473109). In humans it rises with exercise and falls with age — but a 2026 human study found that exercising skeletal muscle may not even be the source of the circulating rise, complicating the tidy "muscle exerkine" story [Evidence-human] (PMID 41520850).

Humanin (24-aa) is the cytoprotective/anti-apoptotic one — the first MDP ever found (2001) Established (PMID 11371646). Intracellularly it sequesters pro-apoptotic BAX to block the mitochondrial death pathway [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 23686888); when secreted it acts on FPRL1/FPR2 and a CNTFRα/gp130/WSX-1 receptor complex to drive STAT3/AKT survival signaling [Evidence-in vitro] (PMID 35372353). It improves insulin sensitivity via a central, hypothalamic STAT3 mechanism [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 19623253), and a rare P3S variant is enriched in APOE4 centenarians — the strongest human-genetic link to longevity on this page [Evidence-human] (PMID 38520065).

The honest reconciliation for both: the "more MDP = anti-aging" framing is oversimplified and possibly wrong in some contexts. The very same cytoprotective program can shield the wrong cells — humanin (and MOTS-c) have been reported in vitro to worsen the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) [Evidence-in vitro] (PMID 30058454), and humanin promotes tumor progression in glioblastoma models [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 38942749). Higher humanin is associated with longevity; that is not the same as injecting it being beneficial. And in healthy people plasma humanin actually rises with age (an adaptive stress response), which cuts against the naive "restore youthful levels" pitch [Evidence-human] (PMID 33131010).

Family 3 — TPP+ antioxidants: MitoQ and SkQ1

These are the "Skulachev ion" cousins: a redox-active head-group (ubiquinone for MitoQ, plastoquinone for SkQ1) tethered to triphenylphosphonium (TPP+), a lipophilic cation. Because the inner mitochondrial membrane is negative inside, the positive charge drags them several-hundred-fold into mitochondria, where the respiratory chain reduces them to the active radical-scavenging form and then recharges them — so a tiny amount works catalytically Established (PMID 11092892, PMID 20307489). This charge-driven delivery is the whole point (plain CoQ10 doesn't do it) — and it's the mechanistic opposite of SS-31's potential-independent lipid binding.

MitoQ has the best independent human data of any molecule here — which is still modest. Its flagship is a 6-week crossover RCT in 20 older adults with impaired endothelial function: flow-mediated dilation was ~42% higher on MitoQ, with lower oxidized LDL [Evidence-human] (PMID 29661838). An ancillary analysis showed part of the benefit is systemic, via reduced oxLDL/LOX-1 signaling [Evidence-human] (PMID 37326998). But this is one small trial of a surrogate marker — and the largest hard-endpoint trial, a 128-patient Parkinson's RCT, was flatly negative [Evidence-human] (PMID 20568096). The much-cited "lifespan extension" is in C. elegans worms, not mammals.

SkQ1 is the one with the dramatic longevity headline — "roughly doubles median lifespan" in rodents [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 19120018) — and the biggest asterisk. That effect appeared mainly in mice in non-sterile vivaria, where much of the benefit coincided with reduced infection-related mortality; in clean housing, simply improving hygiene reproduced much of the gain [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 22166671). Most foundational work comes from a single group. Its only human efficacy is topical: Visomitin eye drops improved dry-eye signs in an industry-funded RCT (n=240) and is approved in Russia [Evidence-human] (PMID 26660938) — nothing systemic, and not FDA-approved.

The unifying mechanistic caveat: antioxidant is not automatically good

The most important scientific point that reconciles all five: blunting mitochondrial ROS is a double-edged intervention, and "more is better" is false for every molecule here.

  • - For MitoQ, a redox-crippled analog that cannot scavenge radicals still inhibited the respiratory chain — meaning at higher concentrations the TPP+ scaffold itself becomes a bioenergetic (OXPHOS) inhibitor, an off-target effect that has nothing to do with antioxidant chemistry [Evidence-in vitro] (PMID 37321281).
  • - For SkQ1, the dose window is a genuine knife-edge: antioxidant at nanomolar concentrations, pro-oxidant and mitochondriotoxic at micromolar [Evidence-in vitro] (PMID 19159610).
  • - For the MDPs, exogenous dosing bypasses the physiological gating that normally controls when these signals fire, and the cytoprotection can preserve senescent or malignant cells [Evidence-in vitro] (PMID 30058454).
  • - Even SS-31, the least "antioxidant" of the group, is now understood to work by membrane electrostatics rather than radical scavenging — the old antioxidant framing was incomplete Established (PMID 32273339).

So the toolkit splits cleanly. If you want the compound with a real (narrow) approval, it's SS-31 — but for Barth syndrome, not aging. If you want the best independent human data, it's MitoQ — but on a surrogate, in 20 people. Everything else is compelling rodent biology sold well ahead of the human evidence. Key takeaways

  • These five molecules are three different families, not one: SS-31 binds cardiolipin (potential-INDEPENDENT); MOTS-c and humanin are endogenous mitochondrial-derived signaling peptides; MitoQ and SkQ1 are TPP+ charge-driven antioxidants (potential-DEPENDENT). Same marketing bucket, opposite mechanisms.
  • SS-31 (elamipretide) has the only real regulatory approval — FDA accelerated approval for ultra-rare Barth syndrome in Sept 2025 (PMID 41335372) — but it MISSED its primary endpoint in heart failure, general mitochondrial myopathy, and dry AMD (PMID 32068002, 39574155, 39605874). No healthy-aging benefit is proven.
  • MOTS-c and humanin have essentially NO completed randomized trials of the administered peptide in humans; all efficacy (insulin sensitization, healthspan, neuroprotection) is rodent/cell data (PMID 25738459, 11371646). Human evidence is observational biomarker associations only.
  • MitoQ has the best independent human data — but it is a single small (n=20) surrogate-endpoint FMD trial (PMID 29661838), and its largest hard-outcome trial (Parkinson's, n=128) was negative (PMID 20568096).
  • SkQ1's 'doubles lifespan' rodent claim is largely housing-dependent (reduced infection mortality in dirty vivaria; PMID 22166671); its only human efficacy is topical dry-eye drops approved in Russia (PMID 26660938), nothing systemic.
  • 'Antioxidant = good' is false for all of them: MitoQ's TPP+ scaffold inhibits the respiratory chain at high concentration independent of antioxidant action (PMID 37321281); SkQ1 flips pro-oxidant at micromolar doses (PMID 19159610); MDP cytoprotection can shield senescent/tumor cells (PMID 30058454, 38942749). More is not better.

Every efficacy claim above is certainty-tagged to its evidence tier, and the tiers are steep. For SS-31 the mechanistic depth (cardiolipin binding, membrane electrostatics) is well-established biophysically, but clinical benefit is proven only for ultra-rare Barth syndrome on an intermediate endpoint requiring confirmation — most larger trials failed. For MOTS-c and humanin, there are no interventional human trials at all; every therapeutic magnitude is a rodent number and must not be presented as a human effect. For MitoQ and SkQ1, human efficacy is limited to surrogate markers (endothelial function) or a topical route (dry eye), never systemic anti-aging outcomes. Gray-market versions of all five are unregulated research chemicals of unverified identity, purity, and dose — not the studied or approved products. This is mechanism education and harm-reduction only; it is not medical advice and contains no dosing protocol. Anyone with cardiovascular, hepatic, renal, metabolic, or oncologic disease, or who is pregnant/breastfeeding, or on medication, should involve a clinician.
