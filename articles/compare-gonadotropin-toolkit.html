The gonadotropin toolkit: HCG vs Gonadorelin vs hMG vs FSH vs Triptorelin

Five injectables people lump together as "keep the balls working." They act at three different levels of the axis — and one of them does the exact opposite of what its reputation claims.

Why these five belong in one conversation

Walk into any TRT or "men's health" forum and you'll see hCG, gonadorelin, hMG, FSH, and triptorelin discussed as if they were interchangeable — five ways to "keep the testicles online" while on testosterone, or to "restart" after a cycle. They are not interchangeable. They act at three different levels of the hypothalamic–pituitary–testicular (HPT) axis, and understanding those levels is the whole game.

The axis runs top-down: the hypothalamus fires GnRH in pulses → the pituitary releases LH and FSH → LH tells testicular Leydig cells to make testosterone, and FSH tells Sertoli cells to nurse sperm to maturity. Exogenous testosterone (TRT or a steroid cycle) silences the top of that chain, so LH and FSH vanish, intratesticular testosterone collapses, and the testes shrink and stop making sperm.

Each drug in this toolkit re-enters the axis at a different rung:

  • - Triptorelin, gonadorelin → the pituitary (GnRH-receptor level). They need an intact pituitary and testes to do anything.
  • - hCG, hMG, FSH (follitropin) → the testis directly (gonadotropin-receptor level). They work even when the brain is fully shut down.

That single distinction — pituitary vs. testis — plus one more (does it supply the LH signal, the FSH signal, or both?) explains almost every practical difference between them.

hCG: the "fake LH" that bypasses the brain

hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) and LH are sister glycoprotein hormones that bind the same receptor — the LH/CG receptor (LHCGR), a Gs-coupled GPCR on Leydig cells. hCG just carries an extra sialylated tail that makes it last days instead of an hour, so one injection acts like a sustained LH signal Established (PMID 29566165). Because it acts at the testis, it works even when the pituitary is silenced — which is exactly the situation on TRT or a steroid cycle.

Downstream it's textbook: LHCGR → cAMP/PKA → StAR ferries cholesterol into the mitochondria (the rate-limiting step) → the CYP11A1 enzyme chain builds testosterone Established (PMID 29566165). Crucially, it restores intratesticular testosterone — the very high local concentration (~100× blood levels) that sperm production actually depends on. The cleanest human data come from a controlled trial in men whose axes were suppressed with testosterone: intratesticular testosterone fell 94%, and hCG restored it dose-dependently and linearly — a low dose left it slightly below baseline, a higher one pushed it ~26% above [Evidence-human] (PMID 15713727).

Two honest limits. First, hCG supplies no FSH — it is a pure LH-arm drug, which becomes the central plot point below. Second, driving Leydig cells hard raises estradiol (via aromatization) more than equivalent TRT does, and modern PK/PD work directly measured rising estradiol and DHT alongside testosterone Established (PMID 38446525). And "more is not more": pounding the receptor with high, frequent doses desensitizes it — a single dose already maximally drives the Leydig cell for ~48 hours, and redosing inside that window adds essentially nothing [Evidence-human] (PMID 447810).

Gonadorelin: the pulse that has to be a pulse

Gonadorelin is GnRH — the identical native decapeptide. It binds the pituitary GnRH receptor (a Gq-coupled GPCR), triggers the PLC/calcium cascade, and makes gonadotropes dump both LH and FSH Established (PMID 8563717). One rung higher than hCG, same downstream goal.

The defining feature is its minutes-long half-life, and this is not a bug — it's the mechanism. The pituitary is wired to read pulses. Give GnRH in brief on-off pulses and it stimulates; give it continuously and the receptor down-regulates and the axis goes silent — the same trick long-acting agonists use for chemical castration Established. That's why gonadorelin's brevity is essential: each dose comes and goes before it can desensitize anything.

Here's the catch that forums gloss over. The rigorous human evidence is from pump-delivered pulses roughly every 90–120 minutes in men with hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (HH), where pulsatile GnRH normalized LH in 97% and testosterone in 93% [Evidence-human] (PMID 12213860). The once- or twice-daily subcutaneous injection that surged through US TRT clinics after the 2020 hCG shortage is a different animal: pharmacology predicts infrequent dosing gives only weak, transient LH/FSH bumps, and there are no randomized trials showing it preserves fertility or testis size like hCG. When pulsatile GnRH was compared head-to-head with hCG/hMG from the start in HH men, it was, if anything, slightly worse for sperm output (40% vs 80% at two years) [Evidence-human] (PMID 3142911). As a peptide, it can also occasionally provoke anti-GnRH antibodies that abolish the response [Evidence-human] (PMID 3139467). Contested for the TRT-adjunct use specifically.

hMG and FSH: why the FSH arm is the point

Now the crux — why sperm need FSH, and why hCG alone often isn't enough.

hCG restores the LH arm: intratesticular testosterone, testis size, serum testosterone. But full, quantitatively normal spermatogenesis also needs the FSH signal hitting Sertoli cells — the "nurse" cells that determine how many sperm the testis can make. hCG delivers none of it.

hMG (menotropins) solves this by bundling both signals in one urine-derived vial: LH-like activity (in modern Menopur/HP-hMG, mostly from co-purified hCG) plus FSH activity. So it hits both the LHCGR on Leydig cells and the FSHR on Sertoli cells Established. Follitropin alfa is the opposite design philosophy: recombinant, "clean" FSH with essentially no LH activity — it acts only on the FSHR on Sertoli cells and does nothing directly to testosterone Established (PMID 26236283). You add it to hCG, never instead of it.

The human evidence that FSH adds real value is now solid. A 2024/25 meta-analysis of 41 studies and 1,673 gonadotropin-deficient men found combined hCG/FSH produced significantly higher mean sperm output and more men crossing every sperm-concentration threshold than hCG alone [Evidence-human] (PMID 39445789). A classic crossover study nailed the logic: hMG+hCG induced spermatogenesis in all men, while FSH plus exogenous testosterone failed — proving testosterone cannot substitute for the LH/intratesticular-T signal, and both arms are needed [Evidence-human] (PMID 8263139). And a maintenance study showed the practical pattern behind "induce with FSH, maintain with hCG": once primed, hCG alone kept sperm going in most men, but counts drifted down over ~12 months and one man went azoospermic — FSH is needed for full quantity, not just to switch things on [Evidence-human] (PMID 12444893).

Two caveats keep this honest. Testicular volume is a weak proxy for "the FSH arm working" — in the meta-analysis, volume gains were similar with hCG alone vs. hCG/FSH; inhibin B is the more FSH-specific readout [Evidence-human] (PMID 39445789, 17218732). And the Sertoli-cell molecular machinery (GDNF, stem-cell niche support) is characterized mostly in mice and in vitro; the human evidence sits at the level of sperm counts, not pathways [Evidence-rodent] (PMID 28100869). hMG vs. recombinant FSH is largely a wash on efficacy — the recombinant product just avoids urine-derived contaminants and has consistent batches, at higher cost.

Whichever you use, it is slow: a systematic review of ~1,583 azoospermic HH men found ~74% eventually produced sperm and ~52% of couples conceived, but mean time was ~12 months to first sperm and ~19 months to pregnancy, with final counts usually staying below the normal range [Evidence-human] (PMID 41987691). No gonadotropin is a fast switch.

Triptorelin: the mythologized one — and why the myth is dangerous

Triptorelin gets sold in fitness circles as a "one-shot PCT" that reboots your natural testosterone. Mechanistically it does the opposite.

It's a GnRH superagonist — a modified decapeptide (D-Trp at position 6) that binds the pituitary GnRH receptor far harder and longer than native GnRH. And there's the problem: remember that continuous GnRH-receptor occupancy suppresses. Triptorelin has two acts:

  1. 1. The flare (hours–days): the first hit behaves like a giant GnRH pulse, spiking LH/FSH and briefly raising testosterone. This is the "boost" people mistake for a restart Established (PMID 22691297).
  2. 2. The shutdown (weeks): sustained occupancy desensitizes and down-regulates the receptor, LH/FSH collapse, and testosterone falls to castrate levels. In pooled prostate-cancer data, 79% of men were below 20 ng/dL within a month Established (PMID 28028737).

That second act is the licensed use — this is a chemical-castration drug for prostate cancer and puberty blockade. And recovery is slow and duration-dependent: after long-term therapy, 38% of men were still castrate at 18 months, with mean testosterone recovery of ~14.5 to ~29.3 months depending on course length [Evidence-human] (PMID 27628623). (Those are older oncology patients on prolonged depots — a young man after a single dose is a different scenario — but the direction is not in doubt.)

So the flare isn't a feature; it's the leading edge of shutdown. The genuine "restart" tools do the reverse: SERMs like clomiphene/tamoxifen block estrogen negative feedback to raise your own LH/FSH, and hCG stimulates the testis directly. Using a GnRH agonist for this confuses agonism with feedback-blockade. Tellingly, the field engineered GnRH antagonists (degarelix, relugolix) specifically to suppress testosterone without any flare — proof the flare was always an unwanted side effect, not a therapeutic goal Established (PMID 19035858, 34409852). A mis-timed triptorelin dose can plausibly stack months of extra suppression onto steroid-induced hypogonadism. Established that it suppresses; the "safe one-shot restart dose" is unsupported by any controlled human data.

Putting it together

Two questions sort this entire toolkit:

1. Do you need the pituitary intact? If the pituitary is suppressed (on TRT, on a cycle) or absent, only the testis-level drugs — hCG, hMG, FSH — will do anything. Gonadorelin needs a working pituitary and correct pulsatile delivery; triptorelin needs a pituitary too, but only to shut it down.

2. Which signal do you need — LH, FSH, or both? For testosterone and testis size, the LH arm (hCG) does the heavy lifting. For sperm quantity, you need the FSH arm (hMG or follitropin) added on top. hCG alone can maintain an already-primed system for a while, but full induction of spermatogenesis wants FSH.

And two caveats sit over everything: nearly all the strong human evidence is in hypogonadotropic hypogonadism, not otherwise-healthy men restarting after anabolic steroids — so the PED "PCT" use is extrapolation, not proof — and none of these are fast. Fertility recovery is measured in months to a year-plus, under a clinician, with bloodwork and semen analysis, not in forum anecdotes.

Key takeaways

  • The whole toolkit is organized by *where in the axis* a drug acts. Gonadorelin/triptorelin hit the pituitary (need an intact pituitary + testes); hCG/hMG/FSH act directly on the testis (work even when the brain is shut down). [Established]
  • hCG is a long-acting LH mimic: it binds LHCGR on Leydig cells and restores intratesticular testosterone dose-dependently — in suppressed men a higher hCG dose pushed it ~26% above baseline in a controlled trial — but it supplies zero FSH (PMID 15713727, 29566165). [Established]
  • FSH is the arm that matters for *sperm quantity*. Adding FSH (as hMG or recombinant follitropin) to hCG produces higher sperm output and more men crossing fertility thresholds than hCG alone in a 1,673-man meta-analysis; hCG alone can often *maintain* but not fully *induce* (PMID 39445789, 12444893, 8263139). [Evidence]
  • Gonadorelin only works as pulses. Its minutes-long half-life is the whole point — pulsatile stimulates, continuous suppresses. The rigorous human data are pump-delivered in hypogonadotropic hypogonadism; the once-daily TRT-clinic injection use is off-label and under-evidenced, and it did not beat hCG/hMG head-to-head (PMID 12213860, 3142911). [Contested]
  • Triptorelin is the mythologized one. It is a GnRH *superagonist*: a brief testosterone flare, then desensitization and chemical castration for weeks-to-months (79% of men <20 ng/dL by 1 month). As a 'one-shot restart' it is mechanistically backwards (PMID 28028737, 22691297). [Established]
  • Fertility restart is slow everywhere — mean ~12 months to first sperm, ~19 to pregnancy even with hCG+FSH, and final counts often stay subnormal. No product is a fast switch (PMID 41987691). [Evidence]
  • Almost all high-quality human data are in hypogonadotropic hypogonadism, not otherwise-healthy men restarting after anabolic steroids. The PED 'PCT' application is extrapolation, not trial evidence. [Contested]

This is mechanism education and harm-reduction, not medical advice, and it contains no dosing protocols. Fertility restoration and any use of these drugs should be physician-supervised with bloodwork and semen analysis. Two honesty flags run through the whole piece: (1) the strong human evidence base for all of these is hypogonadotropic hypogonadism and, for the female drugs, ovulation induction — the anabolic-steroid "restart/PCT" scenario is largely extrapolated, with little dedicated randomized data; (2) several molecular details (Sertoli-cell support programs, ERK-driven Leydig trophic signaling) are rodent/in-vitro, while the human evidence sits at the level of hormones and sperm counts.
