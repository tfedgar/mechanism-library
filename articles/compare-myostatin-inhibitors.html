The one brake, five ways to release it

Your muscles carry a built-in growth limiter. It's a protein called myostatin (also named GDF-8), a member of the TGF-β superfamily secreted mostly by skeletal muscle, whose entire job is to cap how large muscle can get. Knock it out and animals — cattle, dogs, mice, and rare humans — become dramatically "double-muscled"; overexpress it and muscle wastes away Established (PMID 32911580). Myostatin doesn't work alone: it shares its receptor, the activin type II receptor (ActRIIB/ActRIIA), with the related ligands activin A and GDF-11. Ligand binding recruits type-I co-receptors (ALK4/ALK5), which phosphorylate the intracellular messengers SMAD2/3, and activated SMAD2/3 suppress the muscle-building program while switching on atrophy genes Established (PMID 32911580, PMID 42123420, PMID 41954678).

That single pathway — ligand → ActRII → SMAD2/3 — is the target every compound on this page attacks. But they attack it at different points, with wildly different evidence behind them. Two are gray-market fitness/PED options sold as muscle-builders; three are clinical biologics being trialled largely to preserve lean mass while people lose weight on GLP-1 drugs. The honest story is that the pathway is real and druggable, but "bigger muscle on a scan" has repeatedly failed to become "stronger or more functional" — and the two community favourites have the thinnest human evidence of all.

The gray-market pair: YK-11 and follistatin

YK-11 is the most misrepresented compound here. It is marketed as a "SARM," but it is chemically a steroid — a 19-norprogesterone-derived molecule — so the "clean, tissue-selective" branding is inaccurate Established (PMID 21372378). Its actual mechanism is indirect and clever: it binds the androgen receptor as a partial agonist without the normal N/C-terminal interaction, and in mouse muscle cells this raises follistatin, which then mops up myostatin. Blocking follistatin with an antibody abolished the effect, proving follistatin is the causal link [Evidence — in vitro, mouse C2C12 cells] (PMID 23995658). The problem is the ceiling of that evidence: there is not one human trial of YK-11, no human pharmacokinetics, and no efficacy or dose-response data in any species — the only human-derived data are anti-doping forensics confirming people use it [Established — data gap]. Meanwhile a rat study found YK-11 caused hippocampal oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction and impaired memory [Evidence — rodent] (PMID 37468001). So YK-11's muscle claims rest on cell-culture follistatin induction plus anecdote; its human risk profile is genuinely uncharacterised, not clean.

Follistatin is the endogenous version of what YK-11 tries to trigger — and it reveals why "more follistatin" is a double-edged strategy. Follistatin is a secreted ligand trap: two follistatin molecules wrap around one myostatin/activin dimer, burying the ligand so it can never reach ActRIIB Established (PMID 16198295). It is not selective — it also neutralises activins, GDF-11 and some BMPs, which is exactly why drug developers often prefer more targeted tools Established (PMID 32911580). The best human data come from tiny, uncontrolled AAV-follistatin gene-therapy trials in Becker muscular dystrophy and inclusion-body myositis, where some patients gained on the 6-minute walk test and biopsies showed hypertrophy and less fibrosis — proof-of-concept in disease, not proof of efficacy, with N in the single digits [Evidence — human, small/uncontrolled] (PMID 25322757, PMID 28279643). The locally-acting follistatin-Fc fusion ACE-083 was the cleaner test: in randomised Phase 2 trials it reliably grew muscle volume (+16% biceps, +13.5% in CMT) but produced no consistent strength or functional benefit — and its program did not advance [Evidence — human RCT] (PMID 35428982, PMID 35545446). Crucially, the injectable "follistatin peptide" sold to the fitness market is not any of these clinical constructs; it has essentially zero published human efficacy or safety data, and a short peptide is unlikely to reproduce a large glycoprotein's biology [Contested/Speculation].

The clinical trio: bimagrumab, trevogrumab, apitegromab

These three are where real human evidence lives — and where the field's central lesson keeps repeating.

Bimagrumab blocks the receptor itself (ActRIIA/ActRIIB), so one antibody shuts off myostatin, activin A and GDF-11 together — a broader hit than blocking myostatin alone Established (PMID 24298022). It is the most human-studied of the group, and it does something unusual for an anabolic: it drives large fat loss alongside muscle gain. In type-2-diabetes/obesity, monotherapy cut fat mass ~20% (≈7.5 kg) while raising lean mass ~3.6% and improving HbA1c over 48 weeks [Evidence — human] (PMID 33439265). In the Phase 2 BELIEVE trial, bimagrumab + semaglutide produced the largest weight loss of any arm (−17.8 kg vs −3.3 kg placebo at week 48), with the combination preserving lean mass [Evidence — human, Phase 2] (PMID 41772149). But bimagrumab is also the clearest cautionary tale: across a decade of trials in sarcopenia and inclusion-body myositis it repeatedly failed to improve strength, gait speed or function despite reliable mass gains, and a meta-analysis confirmed the "mass up, fat down, function flat" pattern Established (PMID 33074327, PMID 32690797, PMID 39251484). It is IV-only, investigational, and has no legitimate consumer supply.

Trevogrumab (Regeneron's REGN1033) is a mirror-image strategy: instead of the receptor, it neutralises the mature myostatin ligand in circulation — expected to be cleaner (sparing activin/BMP signalling) but likely smaller in magnitude [Established mechanism] (PMID 26457176). Here the honesty flag matters most: trevogrumab's human muscle-preservation data are not yet published. Its flagship Phase 2 COURAGE trial (NCT06299098) — trevogrumab ± the anti-activin-A antibody garetosmab, added to semaglutide — has co-primary endpoints of fat, lean and body-weight change but no posted results [Speculation for the human effect]. And the rodent data set the expectation honestly: antibody blockade in already-grown mice produced only ~20% more muscle with almost no proteomic remodelling — nothing like the ~2× "double-muscled" knockout that hype invokes [Evidence — rodent] (PMID 27214824). Any "51% muscle preserved" figures circulating on social media are not corroborated by any published trial Speculation.

Apitegromab (Scholar Rock's SRK-015) is the most selective and, so far, the only one with a genuine functional human win. It binds the prodomain of pro/latent myostatin, locking it in its inactive form so it can never be cleaved into active ligand — and it deliberately avoids mature myostatin, GDF-11 and activin A Established (PMID 32075906). In the Phase 3 SAPPHIRE trial in spinal muscular atrophy, adding apitegromab on top of SMN-directed drugs gave a small but statistically significant motor-function gain (+1.8 HFMSE points, p=0.019) in children — modest and additive, not a cure, and the 20 mg/kg-alone arm actually missed significance [Evidence — human] (PMID 40818473). In obesity, the Phase 2 EMBRAZE trial showed apitegromab preserved ~1.9 kg of lean mass (~55% retention) during tirzepatide weight loss without blunting total weight loss — but it measured DXA lean mass only, not strength or function [Evidence — human, single Phase 2] (PMID 42260100).

Reconciling the differences: why mechanism ≠ muscle you can use

Three threads tie this comparison together.

First, selectivity trades magnitude for safety. The broader the blockade, the bigger the effect — and the bigger the off-target risk. Follistatin's promiscuity gives dramatic rodent hypertrophy (a follistatin transgene quadruples muscle in myostatin-null mice) but also touches bone and vascular BMP9/10 signalling; the related decoy receptor ACE-031 was halted over bleeding/vascular signals Established (PMID 17726519, PMID 32911580). Bimagrumab (receptor block) is broader and larger than trevogrumab (single ligand) or apitegromab (latent ligand only), which are engineered to be cleaner at the cost of a smaller ceiling. There is also a plausible endocrine cost to broad blockade: muscle-derived myostatin was recently shown to drive pituitary FSH, so trapping it could perturb reproductive signalling [Evidence — rodent] (PMID 39818879).

Second — and this is the field's defining problem — extra muscle mass has repeatedly failed to become extra strength or function. Bimagrumab, ACE-083 and multiple selective myostatin antibodies all grew muscle volume on imaging without reliably improving how patients actually performed Established (PMID 33074327, PMID 35428982). Apitegromab's SMA result is notable precisely because it is one of the few to move a functional endpoint at all — and even that is modest and additive. For the GLP-1 "muscle-preservation" thesis, this means the honest readout so far is lean mass on a DXA scan is preserved — whether that translates into better strength, metabolic health or resistance to weight regain is genuinely unproven Contested.

Third, the evidence gap between the community options and the clinical drugs is enormous. YK-11 has zero human trials; injectable follistatin peptides have zero controlled human data. The clinical biologics — however imperfect — have Phase 2/3 randomised evidence. A fitness-audience reader should treat that gap as the headline, not a footnote: the compounds easiest to buy are the ones with almost nothing behind them, and the ones with real data are IV biologics available only in trials.

The muscle-preservation frontier is real, and the pathway is one of the best-validated growth "brakes" in physiology. But as of mid-2026 not a single one of these agents is approved, the flagship human-preservation trial (COURAGE) hasn't reported, and the most reliable finding in the entire class remains the uncomfortable one: you can make muscle bigger long before you can prove you've made it better.

Key takeaways

  • All five compounds hit the same pathway — myostatin/activin → ActRII receptor → SMAD2/3 — but at different points: YK-11 indirectly (via follistatin), follistatin as a ligand trap, bimagrumab at the receptor, trevogrumab at the mature ligand, apitegromab at the latent (pre-activation) ligand.
  • The evidence gap is the headline: the two gray-market options (YK-11, injectable follistatin peptide) have essentially zero controlled human data, while the three clinical biologics have Phase 2/3 randomised trials. The easiest to buy have the least behind them.
  • The field's defining problem is that muscle MASS repeatedly fails to become muscle STRENGTH or FUNCTION — bimagrumab, ACE-083 (follistatin-Fc) and selective myostatin antibodies all grew muscle volume without reliable functional benefit (PMID 33074327, 35428982, 39251484).
  • YK-11 is a steroid, not a 'clean SARM' (19-norprogesterone-derived), has never been tested in a single human trial, and showed hippocampal neurotoxicity in rats (PMID 21372378, 23995658, 37468001).
  • Bimagrumab is unique in driving large fat loss alongside muscle gain (~20% fat-mass loss; largest weight loss in BELIEVE combined with semaglutide, PMID 33439265, 41772149) — but a decade of trials shows mass up, function flat.
  • Trevogrumab's human muscle-preservation benefit is currently a hypothesis: the COURAGE Phase 2 trial (NCT06299098) has no published results, and rodent data show antibody blockade in adults gives only ~20% more muscle, not the ~2x knockout phenotype (PMID 27214824).
  • Apitegromab is the most selective and the only one with a functional human win (modest +1.8 HFMSE motor-function gain in SMA Phase 3, PMID 40818473); in obesity it preserved ~1.9 kg lean mass on tirzepatide but measured only DXA mass, not strength (PMID 42260100).
  • Broader blockade means bigger effect but more off-target risk (bone, vascular BMP9/10, reproductive FSH axis) — the reason follistatin's non-selectivity and the ACE-031 decoy receptor raised safety concerns (PMID 32911580, 39818879). None of these agents is approved anywhere as of mid-2026.

This is mechanism education and harm-reduction only — not medical advice, and it contains no dosing protocols. None of these five agents is approved by any regulator as of mid-2026. The two community options carry the weakest evidence: YK-11 has zero human trials of any kind and injectable "follistatin peptide" sold to the fitness market is not the AAV gene therapy or Fc-fusion construct studied in trials — it has essentially no published human efficacy or safety data, and gray-market products are frequently mislabelled or adulterated. Do not conflate the clinical biologics: bimagrumab's fat-loss and lean-mass numbers cannot be assumed for trevogrumab or apitegromab, which are different molecules hitting different points in the pathway. Human muscle-preservation benefit for trevogrumab is unpublished (COURAGE pending), and apitegromab/bimagrumab obesity data measure DXA lean mass, not strength or function.
