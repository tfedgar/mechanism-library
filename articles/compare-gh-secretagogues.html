GH secretagogues, compared

GHRH analogs vs ghrelin-receptor agonists vs the oral pill — same GH axis, three different switches, very different off-targets

adversarially verified · revised41 citations

The one thing they all have in common

Not one drug on this page is growth hormone. Every "GH secretagogue" is a switch that sits upstream of GH — it leans on your own pituitary to fire off your own GH, which then travels to the liver and raises IGF-1, the longer-acting hormone that does most of the actual tissue-building. That upstream position matters: because the pituitary and its feedback loops stay in the loop, secretagogues tend to preserve the natural pulsatile rhythm of GH and don't fully shut down your own axis, unlike injecting synthetic HGH [Evidence-human, PMID 18981485; PMID 17018654].

The entire family splits along one anatomical fact: there are only two receptors in this conversation, both on the same pituitary cell (the somatotroph).

  • - The GHRH receptor (Gs-coupled → cAMP/PKA). This is the "make GH" accelerator. GHRH analogs — CJC-1295, tesamorelin, sermorelin — bind here [Established, PMID 15817669; PMID 18057338].
  • - The ghrelin receptor, GHS-R1a (Gq-coupled → calcium). This is the "release the stockpile" lever, and it's the same receptor your hunger hormone ghrelin uses. Every peptide GHRP (ipamorelin, GHRP-2, GHRP-6, hexarelin) and the oral pill MK-677 bind here [Established, PMID 9849822; PMID 34737341].

Because those two receptors run different internal wiring on the same cell, hitting one of each at once produces a GH pulse bigger than either alone — a genuine, human-demonstrated synergy [Evidence-human, PMID 11549707]. That's the mechanistic reason "GHRH + GHRP" stacks (like CJC-1295 + ipamorelin) exist.

Reconciling the differences — it's all off-targets and timing

Once you accept that everything here ends in "more GH → more IGF-1," the interesting differences are entirely in which receptor the drug hits and how long it stays on.

GHRH side (CJC-1295, tesamorelin, sermorelin). These are clean. They lift the accelerator and nothing else — no hunger, no cortisol, no prolactin — because the GHRH receptor isn't wired into the appetite or stress circuits. Their signature adverse cluster is the generic GH-excess one: water retention, joint aches, paresthesia/carpal-tunnel, injection-site reactions — documented most rigorously for tesamorelin [Evidence-human, PMID 41545261; PMID 37328323]. Tesamorelin is the standout of the entire page because it's the one compound with FDA approval and a proper RCT meta-analysis behind it (visceral-fat loss in HIV lipodystrophy: ~−28 cm² VAT, IGF-1 up, and notably without glucose perturbation at therapeutic dose) [Established, PMID 41545261; PMID 18057338]. Sermorelin is the same idea but weaker and now compounded-only; its human data are small (an elderly RCT lifted integrated GH substantially but IGF-1 only ~28%), and its head-to-head performance against injected GH in kids was inferior [Evidence-human, PMID 18031173; PMID 9360512].

Ghrelin side (ipamorelin, GHRP-2, GHRP-6, hexarelin, MK-677). Here the "off-targets" are actually on-targets of the same receptor system, which is why they cluster together. GHS-R1a lives not just on the pituitary but on hunger neurons and near the stress axis — so activating it can bring appetite, cortisol, ACTH and prolactin along for the ride. The peptides differ mostly in how selectively they were engineered:

  • - Ipamorelin is the clean one — it was specifically selected to release GH without meaningfully raising cortisol, ACTH or prolactin, which is why it's the preferred partner in the CJC-1295 stack. Be honest about the evidence grade, though: that selectivity is rodent/swine data (Raun 1998), not a large human trial [Evidence-rodent, PMID 9849822].
  • - GHRP-2 is the dirtiest of the injectables: in a direct human study it raised ACTH/cortisol comparably to CRH itself, and in a separate human infusion study it increased food intake ~36% in healthy men [Established, PMID 9285939; PMID 15699539; PMID 15645295]. It's more potent per microgram than the others but the least "clean."
  • - GHRP-6 is the appetite king of the peptides and shares the cortisol/prolactin spillover; its non-GH claims (healing, cardioprotection) are rodent-only [Evidence-rodent].
  • - Hexarelin adds a genuinely different twist: a second receptor, CD36, in heart and vessel tissue, which is the basis of its (still preclinical) cardiac story — and it too bumps ACTH/cortisol/prolactin in people [Evidence-human, PMID 10404825; Established CD36 identification, PMID 11988484]. Two honesty flags: that CD36/cardiac-repair story is essentially all rodent and in-vitro, not shown in humans; and hexarelin's Achilles' heel is tachyphylaxis — the GH response tends to wane with repeated dosing, and while a short human study did not see desensitization, chronic use is unresolved [Contested, PMID 8921821].

Then there's the timing axis, which is where MK-677 stands apart. All the peptides are short-acting pulses. MK-677 is the oral non-peptide that occupies the same ghrelin receptor but with a long, once-daily duration — so instead of a brief pulse it produces sustained 24-hour GH/IGF-1 elevation (IGF-1 up ~40-90%) [Established, PMID 8954023; PMID 18981485]. That sustained exposure is a double-edged sword. It's convenient and gives the biggest, most reliable IGF-1 numbers — but continuous stimulation of an insulin-antagonistic axis is exactly what most consistently worsens insulin sensitivity and raises fasting glucose, and it drives strong appetite and fluid retention because it's leaning on the hunger receptor all day [Established, PMID 18981485; PMID 9467542]. The same logic applies to CJC-1295-with-DAC, whose multi-day albumin-tethered half-life turns discrete pulses into a sustained "GH-bleed" [Evidence-human, PMID 17018654] — which is why the no-DAC version is often preferred by people trying to preserve pulsatility.

The brand-honest bottom line

The mechanism is real and mostly human-established: these drugs raise your own GH and IGF-1. What's not established is that any of this reliably becomes muscle, fat loss, recovery or longevity in a healthy person. MK-677 is the cautionary tale precisely because it was tested the most: a full year of it raised lean mass but not strength or physical function, a large Alzheimer's RCT confirmed a robust (~70%) IGF-1 rise with zero clinical benefit, and a hip-fracture trial was halted early for a congestive-heart-failure signal [Evidence-human, PMID 18981485; PMID 19015485; PMID 21067829]. Raising IGF-1 is the easy part; making it matter is the part the data keep failing.

If you're comparing these to decide what to watch (not what to run — this isn't a protocol): the labs that separate signal from harm are IGF-1 (is it engaged, is it supraphysiologic), fasting glucose / insulin / HbA1c (the GH-axis tax, worst with the sustained agents MK-677 and DAC-CJC), and for the dirty ghrelin-side peptides, cortisol and prolactin. Watch for the acromegaly-spectrum flags — new edema, carpal-tunnel tingling, joint aches — that signal you've pushed the axis too far [Evidence-human, PMID 41545261; PMID 37328323]. And remember every one of these except tesamorelin is grey-market and WADA-banned, so what's in the vial is its own variable [PMID 39145153].

Key takeaways

  • Every GH secretagogue works by the same trick: none of them is growth hormone. They push your own pituitary to release your own GH, which then raises IGF-1. That upstream position is why they preserve pulsatility and feedback in a way injected HGH does not [Evidence-human].
  • There are only two receptors in this whole conversation. GHRH analogs (CJC-1295, tesamorelin, sermorelin) hit the Gs/cAMP GHRH receptor; the ghrelin-receptor agonists (ipamorelin, GHRP-2, GHRP-6, hexarelin, and the oral MK-677) hit the Gq/calcium GHS-R1a. Combining one of each is the mechanistic basis for the 'synergy' stacks, and that synergy is real in humans [Evidence-human, PMID 11549707].
  • The off-target profile is the whole game. GHRH analogs are clean but add nothing to appetite. Among the ghrelin-side drugs, ipamorelin is the selective one (little cortisol/prolactin, and that selectivity is animal-grade), while GHRP-2, GHRP-6 and hexarelin measurably bump cortisol and appetite in people [Established, PMID 9285939, 15699539]. MK-677 is the strongest appetite driver because it stimulates that ghrelin receptor around the clock.
  • Pulsatile vs sustained is the axis that predicts side effects. Short peptides (no-DAC CJC, ipamorelin, GHRP-2/6, hexarelin) give brief pulses. CJC-1295-with-DAC and oral MK-677 give sustained, all-day elevation — and it's the sustained exposure that most reliably worsens insulin sensitivity and drives fluid retention [Evidence-human, PMID 18981485].
  • Tesamorelin is the only one of the group that is FDA-approved and has real RCT/meta-analytic human data (visceral-fat loss in HIV lipodystrophy). Everything else is either discontinued (sermorelin, ipamorelin, GHRP-2, MK-677) or never developed as a therapy, and is sold grey-market [Established, PMID 41545261].
  • The honest limiter: raising IGF-1 is easy and proven; turning that into strength, function, or health outcomes is not. MK-677's year-long RCT raised lean mass but not strength, and both an Alzheimer's trial and a hip-fracture trial failed on hard endpoints (the latter stopping early for a heart-failure signal) [Evidence-human, PMID 18981485, 19015485, 21067829].

Almost none of the popular use-cases here have direct trial evidence. The GH-pulse and IGF-1 effects are human-established for most of these compounds, but the muscle/fat/recovery/anti-aging claims rest on the GH-to-IGF-1 axis plus anecdote, not RCTs on these specific drugs at these specific uses. Ipamorelin's defining 'selectivity' (low cortisol/prolactin) is rodent/swine-grade (PMID 9849822), not confirmed in a large human trial. The CJC-1295 + ipamorelin combination has never been tested as a combination in a controlled human trial — the popular "3-5x pulse" figure is an extrapolation from general GHRH+GHRP synergy data (PMID 11549707), not a measured number for that pair. Hexarelin's much-hyped cardiac/CD36 story is almost entirely rodent and in-vitro (PMID 11988484); the only thing proven in people for hexarelin is the acute GH (and cortisol/prolactin) response. All of these except tesamorelin are unapproved and sold as grey-market "research chemicals," where mislabeling and adulteration are documented (PMID 39145153) — so real-world effects may reflect the contents of the vial as much as the molecule. All GH secretagogues are WADA-prohibited at all times. This is mechanism education for comparison, not medical advice and not an endorsement of use; no dosing or protocols are given by design.
