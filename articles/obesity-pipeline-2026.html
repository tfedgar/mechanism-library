The one question the whole pipeline is arguing about

For a decade, "GLP-1 drug" was the whole story. That era is over. The 2025-2027 obesity pipeline is a set of bets on what you add to GLP-1, what you swap it for, and how you deliver it. And underneath the marketing, the field is quietly split by a single, genuinely unresolved mechanistic question:

Does the gut hormone GIP help you lose weight by being switched ON, or switched OFF?

Tirzepatide — the reigning champion — is a GIP receptor agonist. It turns GIP signaling up. MariTide, Amgen's most-watched asset, is a GIP receptor antagonist. It turns GIP signaling down. Both produce large weight loss in humans. That is a paradox, and reconciling it is the intellectual center of this whole generation of drugs Contested (PMID 36509857).

Below is the honest map: what's approved, what's pipeline, what each mechanism is actually doing, and where the evidence is strong versus where it's a press release with a receptor diagram attached. No dosing. No rankings you can't trust.

The GIP paradox: block it (MariTide) vs boost it (tirzepatide)

Start with the confusing one, because it defines the field.

Tirzepatide is a single peptide that agonizes both GIPR and GLP-1R. At its receptors it behaves as a full GIP-like agonist and an "imbalanced," cAMP-biased partial agonist at GLP-1R Established. It delivered ~20.9% mean weight loss at 15 mg over 72 weeks in SURMOUNT-1 (PMID 35658024) and beat semaglutide 1 mg head-to-head in diabetes (SURPASS-2, PMID 34170647). GIP-on works.

MariTide (maridebart cafraglutide, AMG 133) does the opposite with GIP. It is a bispecific: a fully human anti-GIPR antibody that blocks the GIP receptor, with two GLP-1 agonist peptides bolted onto the antibody scaffold [Established, molecular]. Its Phase 2 (n=592, NEJM 2025, PMID 40549887) delivered ~12.3-16.2% weight loss at 52 weeks in obesity without diabetes. GIP-off, on a GLP-1 background, also works.

So how can antagonizing and agonizing the same receptor both help? Nobody has proven the answer. The leading hypothesis is functional desensitization: chronic strong GIPR agonism may internalize and downregulate the receptor, so persistent agonism and outright antagonism converge on the same net "quiet GIPR" state Contested (PMID 36509857). Other proposed pieces — that GIP antagonism has central appetite effects, or blunts nausea — are hypothesis- or rodent-level, and the "MariTide is easier on the gut because it blocks GIP" claim is speculative; GI adverse events were in fact common in its trials Speculation. Be suspicious of anyone who states the mechanism as settled. It isn't.

VK2735 (Viking) sits on the tirzepatide side of this fence: it's a GLP-1R/GIPR co-agonist, same mechanistic class as tirzepatide, not MariTide [Established, class]. Its Phase 2 VENTURE study showed up to ~14.7% weight loss at 15 mg over just 13 weeks, with a curve that had not plateaued (PMID 41508550) Evidence.

The comparison trap you must not fall into

Here is the discipline this article insists on. VK2735's ~14.7% is at 13 weeks on a still-climbing curve, in a trial that excluded diabetics. Tirzepatide's ~20.9% is at 72 weeks at steady state (PMID 35658024). MariTide's ~16% is at 52 weeks. Pasting these numbers next to each other and concluding "tirzepatide > MariTide > VK2735" is apples-to-oranges-to-grapes [Established caution]. Different durations, populations, estimands, and sponsors. The only fair comparison is a head-to-head trial at the same timepoint — and none of the new agents has one against tirzepatide yet. When you see a "which is strongest" leaderboard online, it is almost always built from exactly this error.

Glucagon dual agonists: burning liver fat, maybe sparing muscle

A second branch swaps GIP for glucagon. This sounds backwards — glucagon raises blood sugar — but at the liver, glucagon drives fat oxidation and suppresses lipogenesis, and it may nudge energy expenditure up [Evidence, mechanistic]. The design only works because the GLP-1 arm keeps glucose in check.

  • - Pemvidutide (Altimmune) is a balanced GLP-1/glucagon dual agonist tuned toward liver-fat and lipid reduction over maximal weight loss. It stripped ~75% relative liver fat over 24 weeks and met MASH-resolution in its Phase 2b IMPACT trial — but missed the fibrosis-improvement co-primary at 24 weeks, and fibrosis is the endpoint that predicts hard liver outcomes (PMID 41237796) [Evidence, with a real miss]. Its physique hook — "spares lean mass better than GLP-1 drugs" — is not proven head-to-head and should not be treated as fact Contested.
  • - Survodutide (Boehringer) leans harder on glucagon; its pivotal data is a 320-patient biopsy-confirmed MASH RCT (NEJM 2024, PMID 38847460) plus obesity dose-finding (PMID 38330987) Evidence. Phase 3 is recruiting.
  • - Mazdutide (Innovent/Lilly) is a GLP-1-biased glucagon dual agonist and — importantly for a peptide audience — is actually approved, in China, on real Phase 3 data (~14% at 6 mg in GLORY-1; ~16.7% at 9 mg in GLORY-2) Evidence. Not FDA/EMA-approved; data are predominantly in Chinese adults.
  • - Retatrutide (Lilly) is the maximalist: a triple agonist adding glucagon on top of GIP and GLP-1, with the largest Phase 2 weight loss reported (~24% at 48 weeks, PMID 37366315) Evidence. Its "burns more calories" energy-expenditure story is most rigorously shown in mice (PMID 35985340) and only inferred in humans [Evidence-rodent for the mechanism]. Phase 3 TRIUMPH is now reporting.

The honest caveat across this branch: glucagon agonism is double-edged. It can raise resting heart rate and transiently nudge glucose and blood pressure, and its long-term cardiac safety at these ratios is not established Evidence.

The oral race: a pill, not a peptide

The biggest access story isn't magnitude — it's route. Peptides mean injections and cold chains. Small-molecule oral GLP-1 agonists could change who can be treated worldwide.

Orforglipron (Lilly) is the frontrunner: an oral non-peptide GLP-1R agonist that binds the receptor at an upper-helical-bundle pocket, not the deep peptide site (PMID 33177239, 39693407) [Established, structural]. Its Phase 3 obesity program is complete, showing significantly greater weight loss than placebo over 72 weeks (PMID 40960239, 42120723) Evidence. Because it's a manufactured small molecule with ~79% oral bioavailability and no food/water restrictions, it dodges the supply constraints of injectable peptides. Note the honest ceiling: injectable semaglutide generally produces larger weight loss than orforglipron 36 mg, and oral drug exposure appears to plateau at higher doses.

Aleniglipron (Structure Therapeutics; GSBR-1290) is the second entrant — same oral small-molecule GLP-1 class, Phase 2b ACCESS showing ~11% at 36 weeks with no plateau (PMID 42249138) Evidence. It is behind orforglipron and unproven at Phase 3. The class's "Gs-biased, cleaner-than-peptides" pitch is a design hypothesis extrapolated across molecules, not molecule-specific proof for aleniglipron Contested. And the class has a shadow: an earlier oral candidate, lotiglipron, was killed by transaminase elevations (PMID 39415344) — so liver safety at scale is an open question, not a solved one Evidence.

The amylin wave: the other satiety hormone

The final branch abandons GIP and glucagon entirely and reaches for amylin — a pancreatic hormone that signals fullness through hindbrain amylin receptors (calcitonin-receptor + RAMP heterodimers), a pathway independent of and complementary to GLP-1 [Established, molecular]. The bet: GLP-1-class weight loss with less nausea. (The fine-grained "which neurons" wiring is proven mostly in mice and inferred in humans.)

  • - Cagrilintide (Novo) is the lead, mostly developed as CagriSema (with semaglutide), which posted ~20.4% weight loss at 68 weeks in REDEFINE 1 (PMID 40544433) Evidence — a result that actually undershot the ~25% early expectations, a useful reminder that preliminary signals oversell. Cagrilintide alone reached only ~6-11% at 26 weeks in Phase 2 (PMID 34798060).
  • - Petrelintide (Zealand/Roche) is a monotherapy amylin analogue, ~10-day half-life, ~8.6% at 16 weeks in a Phase 1 safety/PK study (PMID 42017294), now in Phase 2 Evidence.
  • - Eloralintide (Lilly) is a selective AMY1R-preferring agonist posting up to ~20% at 48 weeks in a Phase 2 monotherapy trial (n=263, PMID 41207310) — a landmark for the class — but "selective" did not mean side-effect-free (nausea reached ~64% at the 6 mg fixed dose), and Phase 3 (ENLIGHTEN) has no readout yet Evidence.
  • - Amycretin (Novo) fuses GLP-1 and amylin into one peptide, in both oral and injectable forms, with early data rivaling the best injectables (~22-24% at high SC doses; PMID 40550229, 40550231) — but the trials are tiny and early (≤144 oral / 125 SC), came with high withdrawal, and a network meta-analysis explicitly rates the evidence sparse and low-certainty (PMID 42175595) [Evidence, low-certainty].

Monthly dosing, and why the half-life cuts both ways

MariTide's other differentiator is once-monthly (every-4-week) dosing, courtesy of its antibody scaffold's long half-life — in Phase 1, weight loss persisted up to ~150 days after the last dose (PMID 38316982) Evidence. That's a genuine convenience and adherence advantage over weekly injectables. But an antibody-length half-life is not free: if a serious adverse reaction occurs, you cannot quickly withdraw the drug Evidence. Long-acting and rapidly-reversible are opposing design goals, and MariTide has firmly chosen the former.

How to read this pipeline honestly

Three habits will keep you out of trouble:

  1. 1. Tag by phase, not by hype. Approved (semaglutide, tirzepatide; mazdutide in China only) means large randomized outcome data. Everything else is Phase 1-3 pipeline, most of it single-sponsor, short-duration, and — critically — without cardiovascular-outcome trials yet. Promise is not proof.
  2. 2. Refuse cross-trial rankings. The weight-loss column above is not a leaderboard. Timepoint, population, and estimand differences swamp the drug-to-drug differences.
  3. 3. The mechanism debate is live. Whether GIP should be blocked or boosted, whether glucagon agonists truly spare muscle, whether amylin genuinely means less nausea at equal efficacy — these are open, testable questions, and the Phase 3 readouts of 2026-2027 are what will actually answer them.

One last, non-negotiable point: none of this endorses self-sourcing. "VK2735," "GSBR-1290," "retatrutide" and the rest are sold as gray-market research chemicals of unverifiable identity, purity, and dose. The clean trial data on this page do not transfer to a vial bought online [Established harm-reduction]. This is a map of where the science is going — not a shopping list, and not medical advice.

Key takeaways

  • The field is splitting along one deep mechanistic question: does GIP help by being switched ON (tirzepatide, VK2735) or switched OFF (MariTide)? Both approaches produce large weight loss in humans, and the reason both work is genuinely unresolved [Contested].
  • Approved reality vs pipeline promise: only semaglutide and tirzepatide are FDA-approved for obesity [Established], and mazdutide is approved only in China [Evidence]. MariTide, VK2735, pemvidutide, retatrutide, survodutide, cagrilintide, amycretin, petrelintide and eloralintide are all investigational — most efficacy figures are 13-72 week Phase 1/2 numbers with no cardiovascular-outcome data yet [Evidence].
  • The oral small-molecule race is real: orforglipron completed Phase 3 as an oral non-peptide GLP-1 pill [Evidence] (PMID 40960239), with aleniglipron a Phase 2b second entrant [Evidence]. A pill that needs no cold chain and no needle is the single biggest access story in the pipeline.
  • Cross-trial weight-loss comparisons are a trap. VK2735's ~14.7% is at 13 weeks on a non-plateaued curve; tirzepatide's ~20.9% is at 72 weeks (PMID 35658024). You cannot rank drugs by pasting these numbers side by side [Established caution].
  • Glucagon dual agonists (pemvidutide, survodutide, mazdutide) trade some appetite suppression for a liver-fat-burning, possibly more lean-sparing profile — but the muscle-sparing claim is not proven head-to-head, and glucagon raises heart rate [Contested].
  • The amylin wave is the other satiety axis. Cagrilintide, petrelintide and eloralintide hit hindbrain amylin receptors, not GLP-1 — the bet is GLP-1-class weight loss with less nausea [Evidence], with eloralintide posting ~20% in Phase 2 (PMID 41207310) but no Phase 3 readout yet.
  • Monthly dosing (MariTide, every-4-week) is a convenience leap but cuts both ways: an antibody-length half-life means a serious adverse reaction cannot be quickly withdrawn [Evidence].
  • None of this is a dosing guide or an endorsement of gray-market peptides. Research-chemical 'VK2735' or 'GSBR-1290' sold online is of unverifiable identity, purity and dose, and the trial data do not transfer to it [Established harm-reduction].

Everything past semaglutide and tirzepatide is pipeline data — mostly single-sponsor Phase 1/2 trials of 13-72 weeks, no cardiovascular-outcome results, and no head-to-head superiority trials between the new agents.
